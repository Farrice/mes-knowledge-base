## YOUR EXECUTION

### Phase 1: Product Vehicle Classification

Not every idea should be a course. Determine the RIGHT format based on the transformation scope:

**Level 1 — Quick Win ($29-$99):**
Ebook, mini-course, template pack. Solves ONE specific problem in under 2 hours. Best for: first-time product creators, building trust, or lead generation.

**Level 2 — Skill Builder ($99-$349):**
Self-paced course, 5-10 modules. Teaches a complete skill with practice exercises. Best for: creators with some audience who want mid-tier recurring revenue.

**Level 3 — Cohort Experience ($349-$999):**
Live or semi-live course with community, accountability, and deadlines. Students go through together. Best for: creators who want engagement and testimonials.

**Level 4 — Community ($29-$199/month):**
Ongoing membership with content, access, and peer support. Best for: creators with proven content and existing audience.

**Level 5 — High-Ticket Group ($3K-$10K):**
Small group coaching with personalized attention and transformation guarantee. Best for: established experts with case studies and waitlists.

**Level 6 — Mastermind ($10K-$100K):**
Elite access, direct mentorship, network effects. Best for: authorities with significant track records and demand.

**Pricing Psychology Note:** Below $350, purchases are impulse decisions driven by curiosity. Above $350, purchases become investment decisions requiring justification. Your pricing should respect this threshold — don't price at $400 if you can't justify the investment decision.

**CLASSIFY** the user's product idea into the right level. If their audience size or existing products don't support the level they're aiming for, recommend starting lower and tell them honestly why.

### Phase 2: Student Identity Spectrum

Create a 4-level progression that students can use to self-identify their starting point:

**Level 1 — [Name for Entry-Level]:**
- Observable behaviors (what they do that keeps them stuck)
- Internal narrative (the story they tell themselves)
- This label should be slightly uncomfortable to claim — not insulting, but honest enough that people want to grow past it

**Level 2 — [Name for Aware-But-Stuck]:**
- Observable behaviors (they know what to do but don't consistently do it)
- Internal narrative (the excuses, the "I'll start Monday" loop)
- This is where most of your audience actually is

**Level 3 — [Name for Active-But-Plateaued]:**
- Observable behaviors (they're doing the work but hitting a ceiling)
- Internal narrative (frustration with diminishing returns)
- This is where the biggest breakthroughs happen

**Level 4 — [Name for Mastery]:**
- Observable behaviors (they operate from a different paradigm)
- Internal narrative (confidence without arrogance, capability without rigidity)
- Your course should bring students to the doorstep of this level

**Rules for naming levels:**
- Names should be psychologically loaded — people should FEEL something when they hear them
- Lower levels should be slightly embarrassing to claim publicly
- Higher levels should feel aspirational but achievable
- Your product should naturally sit at Level 3-4 without explicitly saying "buy this to reach Level 4"

**Self-Sorting Question:** Create ONE question that makes the reader instantly identify which level they're at. This becomes a powerful sales tool and content hook.

### Phase 3: Transformation-Based Course Outline

Design the course structure. Each module is an IDENTITY SHIFT, not a topic.

**Module Structure (repeat for each module):**

**Module Title:** Frame as a shift, not a subject
- BAD: "Module 3: Social Media Marketing"
- GOOD: "Module 3: From Random Poster to Strategic Communicator"

**Identity Shift:** What does the student believe/feel/do BEFORE this module vs. AFTER?
- Before: "[Specific limiting belief or behavior]"
- After: "[New belief or capability that changes how they operate]"

**Core Content:** What do they learn? (Keep this focused — one core concept per module, not 5)

**Proof Element:** Story, case study, data, or demonstration that makes the shift feel possible and real

**Implementation Gate:** What must the student DO before moving to the next module? This prevents passive consumption. It should be:
- Specific (not "reflect on what you learned")
- Completable in 15-30 minutes
- Produces a tangible artifact (a document, a decision, a published post, a conversation)
- Impossible to skip without the student knowing they skipped it

**Checkpoint Language:** Write 2-3 sentences the student should be able to say after completing this module. Use "I am" and "I do" language, not "I know" language.
- BAD: "I know the 5 principles of effective communication"
- GOOD: "I communicate with intention. I choose my words based on who I'm speaking to, not what sounds smart."

**Design 5-8 modules following this structure.** The first module should address the biggest internal blocker (usually a belief, not a skill). The final module should bridge to real-world application.

### Phase 4: The Inner Capability Reframe

Write a 200-word "welcome message" for the course that:
- Speaks to the student's FUTURE self, not their current struggles
- Uses assumptive language ("You're already the kind of person who...")
- Frames the course as REVEALING capability they already have, not building something from scratch
- Never uses language that reinforces limitation ("struggling," "stuck," "confused")
- Makes them feel seen, not sold to

### Phase 5: Launch Readiness Assessment

Be honest with the creator:
- **Ready to launch?** (Based on audience size, existing proof, and product-market fit)
- **If not, what's the minimum viable version?** (How to test demand before building the full course)
- **Recommended first step** (The one thing to do this week)
- **What the next product level looks like** (After this succeeds, where do they go?)

## YOUR OUTPUT

Deliver all 5 phases as clearly labeled sections. Be direct and specific throughout — no filler, no generic advice. If the creator isn't ready for what they want to build, say so and tell them what to do instead.

**Scenario: Photography course for beginners**

Variables used:
- [EXPERTISE] = Portrait and landscape photography, 12 years professional experience
- [AUDIENCE] = Hobbyist photographers who want to go from "auto mode" to confident manual shooting
- [TRANSFORMATION] = Before: Takes photos on auto mode, deletes 90% of shots, intimidated by manual settings. After: Shoots confidently in manual, understands light, and creates photos they're proud to print and share.
- [AUDIENCE SIZE] = 3,200 Instagram followers, 800 email subscribers
- [EXISTING PRODUCTS] = Free "5 Camera Settings" PDF download
- [PRIMARY GOAL] = Revenue

---

### Phase 1: Product Vehicle Classification

**Classification: Level 2 — Skill Builder ($149)**

Your numbers support this. 800 email subscribers is enough to validate a self-paced course if your open rates are healthy (above 35%). You already have a free lead magnet pulling people in, which means you have a functioning top of funnel. The transformation you described — auto mode to confident manual shooting — is a complete skill arc that fits naturally into 6-8 modules.

Why not Level 3 (Cohort): You don't have the audience depth yet. A $500+ cohort course needs at least 2,000 engaged email subscribers to fill a 20-person cohort reliably. You'd end up discounting to fill seats, which poisons your pricing forever.

Price recommendation: $149. Photography hobbyists spend freely on gear but resist spending on education unless the price feels like "one nice lens" territory. $149 hits below the impulse threshold and feels like a no-brainer compared to the $800 lens they bought last month.

### Phase 2: Student Identity Spectrum (2 of 4 shown)

**Level 1 — The Spray-and-Pray Shooter:**
- Observable behaviors: Shoots 200 photos at every event. Relies entirely on auto mode. Crops aggressively to "fix" composition after the fact. Judges photo quality by whether it's blurry or not.
- Internal narrative: "I just need a better camera." Blames equipment for results. Believes some people are born with 'an eye for photography' and they weren't.

**Level 3 — The Technical Plateauer:**
- Observable behaviors: Understands exposure triangle, shoots in manual, owns decent glass. But photos look "correct" without looking compelling. Gets consistent results but nothing that stops a scroll. Edits for 45 minutes per photo because the raw image never feels finished.
- Internal narrative: "I know what I'm doing technically, so why don't my photos look like theirs?" Watches YouTube tutorials that repeat what they already know. Starting to wonder if photography is just about expensive locations and lucky moments.

**Self-Sorting Question:** "When you take a photo you love, do you know exactly WHY it worked — or does it feel like an accident you can't repeat?"

### Phase 3: Course Modules (2 of 6 shown)

**Module 1: From Equipment Believer to Light Reader**

- Identity Shift:
  - Before: "Better photos require better gear. If I had that lens, my work would look professional."
  - After: "Light is the instrument. The camera is just the container. I see light before I see subjects."
- Core Content: The Light-First Framework — training your eye to read quality, direction, and color of light before touching your camera. One exercise: spend 20 minutes in a room photographing the same object as the light changes. No settings adjustments. Just observation.
- Proof Element: Side-by-side comparison — a $400 camera in beautiful window light vs. a $3,000 camera in harsh fluorescent. The cheap camera wins every time. This breaks the gear myth in 10 seconds.
- Implementation Gate: Photograph one subject in 5 different lighting conditions using only your phone. Post your favorite to the course community with a one-sentence explanation of why the light worked. You cannot proceed until you've posted.
- Checkpoint Language: "I read light before I pick up my camera. I choose when and where to shoot based on what the light is doing, not what the subject is doing."

**Module 4: From Deleter to Curator**

- Identity Shift:
  - Before: "I take 200 shots hoping 3 are good. Deleting photos is my editing process."
  - After: "I take 15 shots because I saw the image before I pressed the shutter. Editing is refining, not rescuing."
- Core Content: Pre-visualization — the discipline of composing the final image in your mind before shooting. Includes the 5-shot exercise: arrive at a scene, take exactly 5 photos, and make one of them portfolio-worthy. Constraints force intention.
- Proof Element: A student case study — someone who went from 400 shots per session (keeping 8) to 30 shots per session (keeping 12). Their keeper rate went from 2% to 40%, and their editing time dropped by 70%.
- Implementation Gate: Go to a location you've photographed before. Take exactly 10 photos. Before each shot, write one sentence describing what you want the viewer to feel. Submit your 3 best with those sentences attached. No re-shoots allowed.
- Checkpoint Language: "I see the photo before I take it. My camera confirms what my eye already composed."

### Phase 5: Launch Readiness Assessment

**Ready to launch?** Conditionally yes. You have the audience (800 subscribers), a functioning lead magnet, and a clear transformation. But you need one thing first: proof that people will pay.

**Minimum viable version:** Don't build all 6 modules. Build Module 1 and Module 2 as a $29 mini-course called "See Light Like a Pro." Sell it to your email list this week. If 20+ people buy at $29 from an 800-person list (2.5% conversion), you have validation to build the full course. If fewer than 10 buy, your positioning needs work before you invest in production.

**Recommended first step:** Send one email to your list this week with the Self-Sorting Question as the subject line. See how many people reply and what level they identify as. Replies = demand signal. Silence = positioning problem.

**Next product level:** After 50+ students complete the $149 course, launch a Level 3 cohort called "Portfolio Sprint" — a 6-week live experience where students build a 10-image portfolio with weekly critique sessions. Price: $499. Your course graduates become your cohort pipeline.

I know this isn't what you wanted to hear. You're imagining a $297 course with 8 modules and a Slack community. But here's the math: 200 email subscribers and no existing products means you have no proof anyone will pay you to teach. You've proven you can write copy. Why not Level 2: A $149-$297 course needs at least 500-1,000 engaged email subscribers to generate meaningful revenue. At 200 subscribers with a generous 4% conversion rate, you'd sell 8 copies at $297 = $2,376. That's not nothing, but after the weeks of production time, you've earned below minimum wage. Worse, you've built something with almost no feedback loop — 8 students isn't enough data to know if your teaching method works.

What to build instead: A $49 template pack — "The Freelance Copywriter's Client Acquisition Kit." Include your actual outreach templates, your project scope document, your pricing calculator, and a 45-minute video walkthrough. **Module 4: From Order-Taker to Strategic Partner**

- Identity Shift:
  - Before: "The client tells me what to write, I write it, they pay me. If they want changes, I make changes."

Proposal B: "Based on our discovery call, your onboarding sequence has a 40% drop-off at email 3. I'll rebuild the sequence around the objection pattern I identified, with the goal of reducing drop-off by 15%. Investment: $2,500." Same skill. Different framing. Different income.
- Implementation Gate: Write a discovery call script using the framework. Practice it with one existing client or peer and record it. Submit the recording with a self-assessment: what questions uncovered real insight, and which ones fell flat? You must complete an actual conversation — a written script alone doesn't count.
- Checkpoint Language: "I lead discovery conversations that surface the real business problem. Clients see me as a strategic partner, not a pair of hands."

**Scenario: Personal finance course for couples**

Variables used:
- [EXPERTISE] = Financial planning and couples coaching, 15 years as a certified financial planner
- [AUDIENCE] = Married couples (late 20s-40s) who fight about money and want to build wealth together
- [TRANSFORMATION] = Before: Money conversations end in arguments, no shared financial goals, living paycheck to paycheck. After: Aligned financial vision, weekly money dates, on track to save 20%+ of income.
- [AUDIENCE SIZE] = 8,000 podcast listeners, 2,500 email subscribers
- [EXISTING PRODUCTS] = $49 "Couples Budget Workbook" PDF
- [PRIMARY GOAL] = Authority Building

---

### Phase 1: Product Vehicle Classification

**Classification: Level 3 — Cohort Experience ($497)**

Your numbers are strong for this. 2,500 email subscribers with a podcast behind them means you have an engaged audience, not just a list. The existing $49 workbook proves people will pay you, and more importantly, the topic — couples and money — practically demands a cohort format. Here's why: financial transformation for couples requires accountability and community. A self-paced course lets both partners procrastinate indefinitely. A cohort with deadlines ("your Money Date worksheet is due Friday") creates external pressure that keeps couples moving together.

Since your primary goal is authority building, the cohort model is ideal. 20 couples going through your program simultaneously generates 40 potential testimonials, case studies, and referral sources. That's a content and credibility engine.

Price recommendation: $497 per couple. This sits just above the impulse threshold, which is intentional — you want couples who are serious enough to have a conversation about investing in their financial relationship. The couples who balk at $497 aren't ready for the work. The ones who say "this is less than one month of the money we waste fighting about money" are your people.

Cohort size: Cap at 25 couples. Small enough for real engagement, large enough for group dynamics to work.

### Phase 2: Student Identity Spectrum (2 of 4 shown)

**Level 1 — The Money Avoiders:**
- Observable behaviors: Split finances completely to avoid conflict. Haven't looked at a joint account balance in months. One partner handles all the bills; the other has no idea what things cost. "Money talk" happens only during fights, usually triggered by a surprise expense.
- Internal narrative: "We're just not money people." Use personality differences as a permanent excuse ("I'm the spender, they're the saver — that's just who we are"). Privately resent their partner's financial habits but won't say it directly.

**Level 3 — The Spreadsheet Couple:**
- Observable behaviors: Have a budget. Track expenses. Might even have a financial advisor. On paper, they're doing everything right. But they still feel anxious about money, disagree on priorities, and have "the talk" about whether they can afford things they clearly can afford. One partner drives the financial plan; the other participates but doesn't feel ownership.
- Internal narrative: "We have a system, but it doesn't feel like OUR system — it feels like one of us managing the other." Frustrated because they're doing the tactical work but haven't aligned on the emotional and identity layer of money. Starting to realize the spreadsheet isn't the problem.

**Self-Sorting Question:** "When one of you makes a purchase over $100, does the other partner find out from a conversation — or from a bank notification?"

### Phase 3: Course Modules (2 of 7 shown)

**Module 1: From Money Silence to Money Story**

- Identity Shift:
  - Before: "Money is a taboo subject in our relationship. We talk about it only when we have to, and it always goes badly."
  - After: "We each understand our own money story — where our habits and fears came from — and we can discuss them without blame."
- Core Content: The Money Origin Interview — a structured 45-minute conversation where partners take turns sharing their earliest money memories, what their parents modeled about money, and the unspoken rules they absorbed about earning, saving, and spending. This isn't therapy. It's context. Most couples have never done this, and the relief of understanding WHY your partner handles money the way they do changes everything.
- Proof Element: A couple from a previous coaching engagement who discovered that his "cheapness" came from watching his parents lose their house at age 11, and her "overspending" was actually generosity she learned from a grandmother who believed money was meant to circulate. Neither habit was irrational — they just came from different origin stories. Once they understood each other's story, the judgment evaporated.
- Implementation Gate: Complete the Money Origin Interview together. Each partner writes a one-page "My Money Story" document summarizing what they discovered about themselves and what they learned about their partner. Both documents must be submitted to the cohort portal. One partner completing it alone does not count.
- Checkpoint Language: "We understand where our money behaviors come from. We talk about money with curiosity instead of criticism."

**Module 5: From Budget Police to Spending Agreements**

- Identity Shift:
  - Before: "One of us tracks everything and the other one feels monitored. Our budget feels like a restriction, not a tool."
  - After: "We have clear spending agreements that give both of us freedom AND shared accountability. Neither of us feels policed."
- Core Content: The Spending Agreement Framework — replacing the traditional budget (which one partner usually imposes on the other) with negotiated agreements that both partners design together. Includes three categories: Joint Commitments (agreed-upon shared expenses), Personal Freedom Funds (individual spending each partner controls with zero accountability to the other), and Dream Investments (future goals they're both excited to fund). The critical insight: giving each partner guilt-free personal spending money actually reduces total household spending because it eliminates resentment-driven purchases.
- Proof Element: Data from 200+ couples coached over 15 years — couples who implemented Personal Freedom Funds reduced monthly arguments about money by an average of 70% and increased their savings rate by 12% within 6 months. Counterintuitive: giving people permission to spend freely in one area makes them more disciplined everywhere else.
- Implementation Gate: Build your Spending Agreement document together during a scheduled Money Date. Define amounts for each category. Both partners sign it (literally — print and sign). Post a photo of your signed agreement to the cohort community. This document replaces your old budget permanently.
- Checkpoint Language: "We have a financial agreement we both designed. Neither of us feels controlled. We each have freedom and shared commitment."

### Phase 5: Launch Readiness Assessment

**Ready to launch?** Yes — with one condition. Your audience, existing product, and topic expertise all support a cohort launch. The condition: run a beta cohort first at 50% off ($249 per couple, 10-15 couples max) in exchange for detailed feedback and video testimonials. Your authority-building goal is best served by launching with proof, not polish.

**Minimum viable version:** You don't need all 7 modules filmed before you launch. Pre-sell the beta cohort, then deliver modules live week by week. This is actually better for a first cohort — you can adjust in real time based on what couples are actually experiencing.

**Recommended first step:** Record a podcast episode using the Self-Sorting Question as the hook. End with a waitlist link for the beta cohort. Mention limited spots (15 couples) and the beta pricing. Your podcast audience is your warmest traffic — start there.

**Next product level:** After 2-3 successful cohorts, launch a Level 4 Community — an ongoing membership ($79/month) for cohort graduates who want continued accountability, monthly Money Date prompts, and access to quarterly live Q&A sessions. Your cohort becomes the acquisition funnel for recurring revenue.

Most new managers discover that 60-80% of their week is work they should be delegating. This isn't about laziness — it's about recognizing that every hour you spend doing individual contributor work is an hour you're NOT spending on the job you were actually hired to do.
- Proof Element: A first-time engineering manager who spent her first 3 months still writing code. Her team's velocity dropped 30% because she was the bottleneck for every code review and technical decision. When she stopped coding and started coaching, her team's velocity increased 45% within 8 weeks — higher than when she was personally contributing. Her individual output went to zero. Her team's output nearly doubled.
- Implementation Gate: Complete the Contribution Audit for your actual last work week. Identify the top 3 tasks you should delegate this month. For each one, name the team member you'd delegate to and write one paragraph explaining what support they'd need to succeed. Share this with your cohort accountability partner. You must have a real conversation — a written list alone doesn't count.
- Checkpoint Language: "I measure my impact by my team's output, not my personal output. I invest my time in developing people, not doing their work."

**Module 5: From Feedback Avoider to Growth Catalyst**

- Identity Shift:
  - Before: "Giving negative feedback creates conflict. I'd rather hint at the problem and hope they figure it out."
  - After: "Direct feedback is a gift. I deliver it with clarity and care, and my team expects it from me because they know it makes them better."
- Core Content: The Radical Clarity Framework — how to deliver difficult feedback in under 5 minutes without sandwiching, softening, or stalling. Three components: the Observation (what you saw, with zero interpretation), the Impact (what it caused, with specifics), and the Request (what you want to see instead, with a clear timeline). The critical insight most managers miss: people don't resent honest feedback. They resent delayed feedback that arrives as a performance review surprise 6 months later. Speed is kindness.
- Proof Element: Two scenarios from actual coaching clients. Manager A avoided telling a direct report that her presentations were ineffective. After 8 months, the direct report was passed over for promotion and blindsided — "Why didn't anyone tell me?" Manager B told a direct report on day 3 that his emails were unclear and offered to co-edit three of them together. Within a month, the direct report's communication had improved dramatically and he credited his manager publicly during a team meeting. Same message. Different timing. Opposite outcomes.
- Implementation Gate: Identify one piece of feedback you've been avoiding with a specific team member. Write it using the Observation-Impact-Request framework. Deliver it in person this week. Afterward, write a 200-word debrief: what you said, how they responded, and what you'd do differently next time. Submit the debrief to your cohort group. You cannot proceed until the conversation has happened.
- Checkpoint Language: "I give feedback promptly and directly. My team trusts that I'll tell them the truth, and they perform better because of it."
