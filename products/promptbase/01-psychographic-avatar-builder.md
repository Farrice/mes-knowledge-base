## PROMPT TEMPLATE
(Paste everything below into the "Prompt" field — 8192 token max)

You are a consumer psychologist specializing in behavioral identity analysis. Your job is to build a deep psychographic profile of a single, specific individual — NOT a demographic composite, NOT a market segment, NOT a persona template.

## YOUR INPUTS

I will provide:
- **Brand/Product/Service:** [WHAT YOU SELL]
- **Category:** [THE MARKET YOU OPERATE IN]
- **Brand Essence:** [1-2 SENTENCES — WHAT YOU STAND FOR]
- **Any Known Signals:** [OPTIONAL — THINGS YOU ALREADY KNOW ABOUT YOUR BUYERS: BEHAVIORS, QUOTES, COMPLAINTS, PURCHASES, ETC.]

## YOUR EXECUTION

Follow these steps in exact order. Do not skip or combine steps.

### Step 1: Reject Demographic Framing
Do NOT start with age, income, gender, or location. These are lazy shortcuts that create fictional composites nobody recognizes. Instead, begin with a single behavioral observation: What is ONE thing this person DOES that signals they belong in this market?

### Step 2: Envision a Single Individual
From that behavioral signal, construct ONE specific person. Not "people like them." ONE person. Give them a name. Make them so vivid that if you described them at a dinner party, someone would say "Oh, I know exactly who you're talking about."

### Step 3: Map Their Occupation in the World
Not just their job title. How do they occupy space in the world?
- What role do they play in their family, friend group, workplace?
- What do people come to them for?
- What identity do they project vs. what they feel internally?
- What would they never want to be seen as?

### Step 4: Map Their Activity in the World
What are the rituals and behaviors that reinforce their identity?
- Morning routines, media consumption, purchasing patterns
- What apps/tools/brands do they use daily and WHY (identity-reinforcing, not just functional)?
- What do they do when no one is watching that reveals who they really are?
- What activities feel "like them" vs. "not like them"?

### Step 5: Map Their Internal Logic
How does this person process decisions?
- What feels authentic vs. inauthentic to them?
- What are they protecting (status, identity, self-image, worldview)?
- What would make them feel embarrassed, proud, or validated?
- What internal narrative do they tell themselves about why they haven't solved this problem yet?
- What would need to be true for them to act TODAY rather than "someday"?

### Step 6: Synthesize the Behavioral Portrait
Combine Steps 2-5 into a cohesive portrait that answers:
- Who is this person at their core?
- What tension exists between who they are and who they want to become?
- Where does your brand/product sit in that tension?
- What language do they use to describe their situation (their words, not expert words)?

### Step 7: Prediction Test
Generate 5 hypothetical scenarios this person has never encountered before. For each, predict:
- How they would respond
- What they would say (in their actual voice)
- Whether they would engage with your product/brand in that moment and why

If you can predict behavior in novel situations, the portrait is accurate. If predictions feel uncertain, the portrait needs more specificity.

## YOUR OUTPUT

Deliver the following sections:

**THE INDIVIDUAL**
Name, vivid 2-3 sentence description that makes them instantly recognizable

**OCCUPATION IN THE WORLD**
Role, identity projection, internal vs. external self, what they'd never want to be seen as

**ACTIVITY IN THE WORLD**
Daily rituals, identity-reinforcing behaviors, brands/tools they gravitate toward and why, what they do when no one's watching

**INTERNAL LOGIC**
Decision-making patterns, authenticity filters, what they're protecting, the internal story about why they haven't acted yet, the trigger that would make them act now

**THE BEHAVIORAL PORTRAIT**
Synthesized 3-4 paragraph profile that reads like you've been observing this person for months

**PURCHASE PSYCHOLOGY**
- What triggers purchase consideration
- What creates resistance/hesitation
- What language opens them up vs. shuts them down
- What they need to believe about THEMSELVES (not your product) to buy

**PREDICTION TEST**
5 novel scenarios with predicted responses, quoted language, and engagement likelihood

**LANGUAGE BANK**
10-15 phrases and expressions this person actually uses when talking about this problem area — written in their voice, not marketing language

**Scenario: Online fitness coach selling a 12-week transformation program to busy professionals**

Variables used:
- [WHAT YOU SELL] = 12-week body transformation program with app-based coaching
- [THE MARKET YOU OPERATE IN] = Online fitness coaching
- [WHAT YOU STAND FOR] = Real results for people with real schedules. No gym-bro culture, no extreme diets — sustainable strength for professionals who refuse to sacrifice their careers for their health.
- [OPTIONAL SIGNALS] = Buyers tend to have tried and quit 2-3 programs before. They google "workout plans for busy people" and buy fitness books they never finish.

---

**THE INDIVIDUAL**

Marcus Reeves. The guy who still has his college rugby team photo on his office shelf but hasn't run a mile in four years. He manages a product team at a mid-size tech company, wears Allbirds and Patagonia vests, and has a Whoop band he stopped charging three months ago. If you saw him at a weekend barbecue, he'd be the one holding a craft beer, telling a story about the half-marathon he "used to run," quietly aware that his belt is one notch looser than it was last year — in the wrong direction.

**OCCUPATION IN THE WORLD**

Marcus is the reliable operator. At work, he is the person leadership drops hard problems on because he ships. In his friend group, he is the planner — the one who organizes the ski trip, books the Airbnb, builds the spreadsheet. His wife considers him the steady one. He projects competence and control in every domain, which is exactly why his body frustrates him: it is the one area where his discipline has visibly lapsed. He would never want to be seen as the guy who "let himself go." That phrase makes his jaw tighten. He does not fear aging — he fears becoming someone who stopped trying.

**ACTIVITY IN THE WORLD**

Marcus checks Slack before his feet hit the floor. His morning is coffee (black, specific beans he orders from a roaster he found on Reddit), a scan of Hacker News or Morning Brew, then straight into meetings. He eats lunch at his desk or skips it. His gym bag has been in his trunk for two weeks. He owns a Peloton he rode consistently for five months, then sporadically, then not at all. He still pays for the subscription. On weekends he is active — hikes with his wife, throws a football with his neighbor's kid — and these moments remind him of the version of himself he misses. When no one is watching, he stands sideways in the bathroom mirror and exhales slowly, checking. He has googled "how to get back in shape at 36" more than once, always in incognito mode.

**INTERNAL LOGIC**

Marcus does not distrust fitness products — he distrusts himself around fitness products. His internal narrative is: "I know what to do, I just can't stay consistent." He has reframed each past failure as a timing problem ("that program was too rigid for my schedule") rather than a motivation problem, because admitting a motivation problem would threaten his identity as someone who executes. The trigger that would make him act today: seeing a peer who was in worse shape than him post a genuine transformation — not an influencer, a real person — combined with a program that explicitly addresses the "I've tried before" pattern rather than pretending he is starting from zero.

**PURCHASE PSYCHOLOGY**

- *Trigger:* A moment of physical self-awareness — getting winded playing with kids, seeing an unflattering photo, a doctor's visit where a number moved in the wrong direction
- *Resistance:* "I don't have time for another program that assumes I have two hours a day." Also: fear that buying another program is proof he cannot do it alone
- *Language that opens him up:* "Built for people who've already tried the other stuff." "Thirty minutes. No ego. Just results."
- *Language that shuts him down:* "Transform your life!" "No excuses!" "Beast mode." Anything that sounds like a 22-year-old wrote it
- *What he needs to believe about himself:* That starting again is not a failure — it is what people who actually care do. That consistency does not require perfection.

**PREDICTION TEST**

1. *Marcus sees a gym buddy post a shirtless before/after on LinkedIn.* He cringes, scrolls past, then privately thinks: "I'd never post that, but... he does look good." He screenshots it and texts his wife, "Should I do something like this?" He is half-joking and fully serious. *Engagement likelihood: Medium — he would research the program privately but not buy from a LinkedIn post.*

2. *He receives a targeted ad showing a 35-year-old professional doing a 25-minute kitchen workout with dumbbells.* He watches the full video. He notices the guy looks like him — same build, same setting. He clicks through. If the landing page says "No gym required" and shows a time commitment under 30 minutes, he bookmarks it. *Engagement likelihood: High — this mirrors his identity.*

3. *His company launches a wellness stipend covering fitness apps.* He immediately searches "best fitness apps for busy professionals," reads three comparison articles, and picks the one with the shortest daily time commitment. He tells his wife about it that evening to create external accountability. *Engagement likelihood: Very high — removes the cost objection and gives him a reason to restart.*

**LANGUAGE BANK**

- "I know what I should be doing, I just haven't been doing it."
- "I don't need a trainer, I just need something that fits my schedule."
- "I used to be really active."
- "It's not that I don't care, it's that work has been insane."
- "I'm not trying to get huge, I just want to feel good again."
- "I'll get back into it once things calm down." (Things never calm down.)
- "I did a program last year and it worked, I just fell off."
- "I don't want some influencer yelling at me."
- "Thirty minutes I can do. An hour, forget it."
- "My wife keeps saying I should do something."
- "I already know how to eat, I just need the structure."
- "I'm not starting from scratch — I just need a reset."

**Scenario: Handmade jewelry seller on Etsy targeting women buying meaningful gifts**

Variables used:
- [WHAT YOU SELL] = Custom birthstone jewelry and personalized name necklaces
- [THE MARKET YOU OPERATE IN] = Handmade/artisan jewelry on Etsy
- [WHAT YOU STAND FOR] = Jewelry that carries meaning, not just style. Every piece is a story someone wears.
- [OPTIONAL SIGNALS] = Buyers often mention "I wanted something that felt personal" in reviews.

---

**THE INDIVIDUAL**

Danielle Hwang. The friend who gives gifts that make people cry. She is a school counselor in Portland who keeps a running Notes app list of things people mention wanting throughout the year. She buys her sister a birthday present in February for a July birthday because she spotted it and "it was just so her." Her own jewelry is minimal — a thin gold chain, small studs — but every piece she wears has a story. If you complimented her bracelet, she would tell you where she got it, who gave it to her, and why it matters. She does not shop for jewelry. She hunts for meaning.

**OCCUPATION IN THE WORLD**

Danielle is the emotional architect of her social circle. She is the friend people call after a breakup, the coworker who remembers your kid's name, the daughter who sends a card that arrives on the actual anniversary of her dad's passing — not a day late. She takes pride in being the person who notices. At work, she holds space for teenagers processing hard things. At home, she holds space for everyone else. What she projects: warmth, thoughtfulness, effortless emotional intelligence. What she would never want to be seen as: performative. She has a visceral reaction to people who give expensive gifts with no thought behind them. To Danielle, a $300 gift card is lazier than a $30 necklace chosen with intention.

**ACTIVITY IN THE WORLD**

Danielle drinks her coffee slowly on Saturday mornings while browsing Etsy. Not shopping — curating. She saves items to collections labeled by person: "Mom — Mother's Day," "Jess — wedding." She follows four or five jewelry makers on Instagram — small shops, not brands — and watches their behind-the-scenes reels. She likes knowing who made something. Her daily carry includes a tote bag from a local bookstore, her Hydro Flask, and a planner she writes in by hand. She reads poetry — not as a personality trait, but because she genuinely finds it settling. When no one is watching, she re-reads old text threads to find the exact moment someone mentioned something they loved, so she can surprise them later.

**INTERNAL LOGIC**

Danielle filters every purchase through one question: "Will this make them feel seen?" Price is secondary. She will happily spend $80 on a necklace from a solo artisan and feel good about it, but she will not spend $80 at a chain jewelry store because "anyone could buy that." She needs the object to carry a story she can tell while handing it over. Her resistance is not about cost — it is about inauthenticity. If a shop feels mass-produced or if the "handmade" label feels like a stretch, she exits immediately. She does not need to be convinced that personalized jewelry is a good gift. She needs to be convinced that *this maker* understands what "personal" actually means.

**THE BEHAVIORAL PORTRAIT**

Danielle does not buy products. She commissions moments. When she browses your Etsy shop, she is not evaluating craftsmanship in isolation — she is mentally placing each piece into a specific scene: her best friend opening a box at brunch, her mother touching the pendant and going quiet with recognition. She is already scripting the emotional payoff before she clicks "add to cart."

What separates Danielle from a casual gift buyer is that she has a deeply internalized standard for thoughtfulness, and she measures herself against it. A generic gift is not just a missed opportunity — it feels like a small betrayal of who she is. She takes her reputation as "the one who gives the best gifts" seriously, not out of ego, but because gift-giving is how she speaks her most honest emotional language.

She will spend twenty minutes reading your shop's About section. She will notice if your product descriptions sound like templates. She will click away from anything that feels like it was written for SEO rather than for someone who cares. But if your listing tells a story — if it makes her feel something — she will save it, return to it three times, and buy it at 11pm on a Tuesday when the gift occasion is still six weeks away.

**PURCHASE PSYCHOLOGY**

- *Trigger:* An upcoming occasion for someone she loves, combined with stumbling across a piece that feels "exactly right" — the intersection of event and serendipity
- *Resistance:* Shops that look mass-produced with handmade pricing. Shipping timelines that threaten her planning window. Product photos that look too polished (stock-photo energy = instant exit)
- *Language that opens her up:* "Each piece is made to order." "Tell me the story behind the name." "Designed to be kept, not just worn."
- *Language that shuts her down:* "Trending now!" "Best seller!" "Limited stock!" — anything that centers popularity over meaning
- *What she needs to believe about herself:* That choosing this piece proves she is as thoughtful as she aspires to be — that this purchase is evidence of her attention, not just her spending.

**LANGUAGE BANK**

- "I wanted to get her something that actually means something."
- "I don't do gift cards. That's giving up."
- "It's not about the price, it's about whether it feels right."
- "I've been sitting on this idea for her birthday since March."
- "Do you do custom? I have a really specific idea."
- "I saw this and immediately thought of her."
- "I like knowing there's a real person making it."
- "The packaging matters — it's part of the experience."
- "She's going to cry when she opens this."
- "I'd rather give one perfect thing than five okay things."
- "If I can't find the right thing, I'd rather wait."
- "I keep a list. I'm always collecting ideas."

**Scenario: SaaS founder selling a project management tool to freelance designers**

Variables used:
- [WHAT YOU SELL] = Project management and client portal app for creative freelancers
- [THE MARKET YOU OPERATE IN] = SaaS tools for freelancers
- [WHAT YOU STAND FOR] = Creative work deserves creative tools. We built the project management app that designers actually want to use — not another spreadsheet wearing a UI.
- [OPTIONAL SIGNALS] = Users often say "I just need something simple that doesn't feel corporate"

---

**THE INDIVIDUAL**

Nico Salazar. A freelance brand designer who works from a converted garage studio in Austin, has a portfolio site on Cargo, and keeps his client list intentionally small — eight to twelve projects a year, all referrals. He wears the same rotation of five black t-shirts, has strong opinions about font licensing, and once turned down a project because the client's existing logo used Papyrus. If you asked him what tools he uses, he would list Figma, Arena, Notion (reluctantly), and "a bunch of sticky notes." He is allergic to software that looks like it was designed for project managers instead of people who actually make things.

**OCCUPATION IN THE WORLD**

Nico occupies a specific niche in the creative ecosystem: he is the designer other designers respect. His Instagram is sparse — maybe fifteen posts, all finished work, no carousels titled "5 Tips for Better Branding." He does not call himself a "creative entrepreneur" or a "solopreneur." He calls himself a designer. In his client relationships, he is the calm authority. Clients trust him because he pushes back. In his friend group — mostly other freelancers, a few agency people — he is the one who went independent early and made it work. He would never want to be seen as disorganized, even though his backend systems are held together with duct tape. He would also never want to be seen as someone who cares about productivity content. The phrase "optimize your workflow" makes him physically uncomfortable.

**ACTIVITY IN THE WORLD**

Nico starts his day by not starting his day. He does not have a "morning routine" — he has coffee, opens Arena to look at references, and eases into whatever project has the most creative momentum. He checks email twice a day, intentionally. He uses Notion for project tracking but resents it — the databases feel bureaucratic, and he has rebuilt his workspace template four times without being satisfied. He invoices through Stripe, sends contracts via HelloSign, and stores files in a Dropbox structure only he understands. When no one is watching, he spends forty-five minutes adjusting the kerning on a personal project that will never make money. His browser bookmarks include brutalist web design galleries, a Japanese stationery shop, and a Hacker News thread about people who quit tech to make furniture.

**INTERNAL LOGIC**

Nico's decision-making operates on a single axis: does this feel like something made for me, or something made for everyone? If a tool looks like it was designed for a sales team and then slapped with a "creative" skin, he can tell instantly, and he is gone. He is not anti-tool. He is anti-bloat. He wants to manage his projects without feeling like he is performing project management. His internal narrative: "I should be more organized, but every tool I've tried makes me feel like I'm filling out forms instead of doing my job." He has considered building his own system in Airtable at least twice. The trigger that would make him switch: seeing another designer he respects — not an influencer, a working designer — casually mention they use the tool, the way you mention a good restaurant.

**THE BEHAVIORAL PORTRAIT**

Nico does not evaluate software features. He evaluates identity fit. When he lands on your website, he is making a snap judgment in the first three seconds: Is this for me, or is this for LinkedIn people? The color palette, the typography, the screenshot UI, the tone of the copy — all of it either signals "we understand creative work" or "we understand project management." He cannot articulate this filter consciously, but it governs every decision.

His deepest frustration is not disorganization — it is the cognitive dissonance of being a designer who uses ugly, bloated tools to manage his design work. Every time he opens Notion and drags a card across a Kanban board that looks identical to every other Kanban board on earth, a small part of him dies. He does not want a "powerful" tool. He wants a tool that respects the way he already works — loosely structured, visually-driven, minimal admin overhead.

The irony is that Nico will spend thirty minutes evaluating a tool's typography and never read the feature comparison page. He will sign up for a free trial based entirely on how the onboarding feels. If the first screen asks him to "set up his team" or "define his workflow," he will close the tab. If the first screen shows him a clean, empty canvas and says "start a project," he will stay.

**PURCHASE PSYCHOLOGY**

- *Trigger:* A specific moment of tool frustration — losing a file, sending the wrong version to a client, realizing he forgot to invoice someone for three weeks — combined with seeing the alternative exist
- *Resistance:* "I'll just end up migrating everything and then stop using it in two months." Also: a deep suspicion that any tool with a free trial will eventually become bloated with enterprise features that ruin it
- *Language that opens him up:* "Built for independent creatives, not teams of 50." "No Gantt charts. No sprints. Just your projects." "Designed by designers."
- *Language that shuts him down:* "Scale your freelance business." "Boost productivity by 40%." "Enterprise-ready." Any sentence containing the word "stakeholders"
- *What he needs to believe about himself:* That using a tool is not a concession to corporate thinking — it is a design decision, the same way choosing the right typeface is a design decision.

**LANGUAGE BANK**

- "I just need something that doesn't get in my way."
- "If it looks like Jira, I'm out."
- "I'd rather use a paper notebook than another ugly dashboard."
- "I don't need project management. I need project awareness."
- "Every tool eventually adds a feature that ruins it."
- "I tried Asana once. It felt like I was filing my taxes."
- "I don't have a team. I just have clients and deadlines."
- "Why does every app assume I want a Kanban board?"
- "Show me the design. If it's good, I'll try it."
- "I'd pay more for something that stays simple."
- "The best tool is the one I actually open."
- "I'm not disorganized — my system is just... informal."

**Scenario: Career transition coach helping mid-career professionals pivot into tech**

Variables used:
- [WHAT YOU SELL] = 6-month career pivot coaching program with job placement support
- [THE MARKET YOU OPERATE IN] = Career coaching / career transition
- [WHAT YOU STAND FOR] = Your career isn't a ladder — it's a portfolio. We help experienced professionals translate their existing skills into tech careers without starting over.
- [OPTIONAL SIGNALS] = Clients often say "I feel like I'm too old to switch" or "I don't want to be an intern again"

---

**THE INDIVIDUAL**

Priya Chandrasekaran. A 38-year-old operations director at a regional healthcare system who has been quietly bookmarking product management job listings for eleven months. She has a MBA, manages a team of twelve, and is very good at a job she no longer wants. At her last performance review, her boss said "You're the backbone of this department" and she smiled and felt trapped in the same breath. She listens to Lenny's Podcast during her commute, has completed two free product management courses on Coursera, and has a half-finished LinkedIn Learning path she has not opened in six weeks. If you met her at a networking event, she would describe herself as "in healthcare operations" and immediately gauge your reaction to decide whether to add "but I'm exploring a transition into tech."

**OCCUPATION IN THE WORLD**

Priya is the competent one. She has been the competent one her entire career — the person who gets promoted because she makes hard things look effortless. Her parents reference her title at family gatherings. Her husband considers her the financial anchor of the household. Her direct reports rely on her institutional knowledge. She occupies a position of quiet authority that everyone around her values and no one questions, which is exactly the problem: questioning it feels like ingratitude. She would never want to be seen as reckless. The idea of someone saying "She threw away a great career to chase some tech fantasy" keeps her up at night. She also cannot stomach being seen as someone who settled — who had the ability to make a bold move and chose comfort instead.

**ACTIVITY IN THE WORLD**

Priya's morning starts at 5:30am — not because she is a productivity optimizer, but because it is the only time that belongs entirely to her. She reads product management case studies, skims job boards, and occasionally writes in a journal she would be mortified if anyone found. During the workday, she is fully engaged — running operations meetings, solving staffing crises, managing vendor relationships. But she notices patterns: every problem she solves well is a product problem in disguise. She has started reframing her work internally ("That was really stakeholder management. That was prioritization under constraints.") She follows three career coaches on Instagram, has saved dozens of "how I pivoted" posts, but has not DM'd or commented on any of them. On weekends she studies — but she hides it. She does her Coursera modules at the kitchen table and switches tabs when her husband walks in, like she is doing something illicit.

**INTERNAL LOGIC**

Priya's internal logic runs on two competing operating systems. System One says: You have sixteen years of experience, financial stability, people who depend on you, and a clear trajectory. Do not blow this up. System Two says: You are going to be doing this for twenty-five more years. Is this really it? The tension between these systems produces a specific behavior pattern: she researches extensively, builds elaborate plans, and then does not execute them — not out of laziness, but because every plan forces her to confront what she would have to give up. Her internal story: "I'll make the move when the timing is right." The timing, she suspects, will never feel right. What she is protecting: her identity as someone who makes smart, measured decisions. A career pivot feels impulsive to her, even if she has been thinking about it for years. The trigger that would make her act: hearing a specific, credible person say "Your experience is not a liability — it is exactly what tech companies need" and believing they mean it. Not motivational fluff. A concrete, evidence-backed argument that her skills transfer.

**THE BEHAVIORAL PORTRAIT**

Priya is not paralyzed by fear. She is paralyzed by competence. She is so good at her current job that leaving it feels irrational by every metric she was taught to value — stability, progression, expertise. The career she wants requires her to voluntarily become a beginner again, and she has not been a beginner at anything in fifteen years. That gap between "seasoned director" and "junior product manager" is not just a title change — it is an identity earthquake.

What makes Priya different from someone casually browsing career content is that she has already done the intellectual work. She understands what product management is. She can articulate why her skills transfer. She has mapped the salary implications. The missing piece is not information — it is permission. She needs someone with credibility to look at her specific situation and say "This is not a risk — this is a logical next step, and here is exactly how to execute it."

She will not respond to aspirational messaging. "Follow your passion" makes her eyes roll — she has a mortgage and two kids, and her passion is not the problem. She will respond to structural messaging: frameworks, timelines, case studies of people with similar backgrounds who made the transition and ended up better off. She needs the pivot to feel engineered, not emotional. She needs it to feel like the smart move, not the brave one.

**PURCHASE PSYCHOLOGY**

- *Trigger:* A convergence event — a bad week at work (political, not performance-based) combined with seeing a job listing that perfectly describes what she wants to do, producing a visceral "That should be me" response
- *Resistance:* "I can figure this out on my own — I don't need a coach." Also: fear that spending money on coaching is an admission she cannot navigate this herself, which conflicts with her self-image as someone who figures things out
- *Language that opens her up:* "You don't need to start over. You need a translation layer." "We've helped 40+ operations leaders move into product roles." "This is a strategy engagement, not a feelings exercise."
- *Language that shuts her down:* "Leap and the net will appear." "Life's too short for a job you hate." "Believe in yourself!" — anything that frames the decision as emotional rather than analytical
- *What she needs to believe about herself:* That she is not running away from something — she is running toward the best deployment of her skills. That this is not a midlife crisis. It is a midlife correction.

**PREDICTION TEST**

1. *Priya attends a tech meetup for the first time.* She stands near the edge, listens more than she speaks, and asks one sharp question during Q&A that reveals her operational depth. Afterward, two people approach her and say "You should be in product." She drives home buzzing but tells no one. *Engagement likelihood: Low for immediate purchase, but she saves the coaching program URL on her phone.*

2. *A former colleague — same level, same background — announces a PM role at a startup on LinkedIn.* Priya likes the post, then reads every comment. She DMs her colleague that evening with a casual "Congrats! Would love to hear how you made the switch." This conversation becomes the most important data point in her decision-making. *Engagement likelihood: High — if the colleague mentions coaching or a structured program, Priya will investigate seriously.*

3. *She gets passed over for a VP promotion she did not want but expected to receive.* The rejection does not hurt because of the lost role. It hurts because it proves the organization does not see her the way she sees herself. Within 48 hours, she reopens every career pivot resource she has bookmarked. This time, she fills out the intake form. *Engagement likelihood: Very high — the identity disruption lowers the switching cost.*

4. *Her husband finds her Coursera dashboard and asks "Are you thinking about switching careers?"* Depending on his tone, she either opens up or deflects. If he is supportive: she tells him everything, cries a little, and feels relief that the secret is out. Within a week, she takes a concrete step. If he is skeptical: she says "Just keeping my options open" and the timeline extends by months. *Engagement likelihood: Dependent on spousal reaction — supportive spouse is a major accelerant.*

5. *She sees a targeted ad for the coaching program featuring a testimonial from a healthcare director who became a PM at Stripe.* She reads the testimonial three times. She checks the person's LinkedIn to verify they are real. She calculates the program cost as a percentage of the salary increase she would get in a PM role. She bookmarks the page and returns to it four times over two weeks before purchasing. *Engagement likelihood: Very high — the specificity and social proof match her evidence threshold.*
