# PRACTICAL PROMPT ENGINEERING FOR MARKETERS - CONSUMER POSTURE PROFILE

**Brand Context:**
- **Name**: Practical Prompt Engineering for Marketers (working title)
- **Category**: Digital course / Professional education
- **Essence**: Practical AI skills for marketing professionals who want leverage, not hype. Focus on real implementation over theory.
- **Consumer Signals**: Market research shows frustration with generic AI outputs, skepticism toward "cash grab" courses, strong preference for hands-on practice over certifications

---

## The Individual

Her name is something like Danielle. She's a senior content strategist at a mid-sized agency, or maybe she went independent two years ago and now manages a handful of retainer clients that keep her comfortable but not complacent. She's 36, but that number only matters because it means she's been in marketing long enough to have developed genuine craft—and long enough to feel the ground shifting beneath her.

Danielle has tried ChatGPT. She's tried Claude. She's used Jasper and Copy.ai and whatever her agency subscribed to for three months before canceling. She can get AI to produce... something. But that something is never quite right. It's too generic. Too corporate. It sounds like every other piece of content on the internet—which is exactly what she built her career NOT producing.

She's not anti-AI. She's frustrated that she can see the potential but can't access it. Every time she watches someone on LinkedIn post about how AI "10x'd their productivity," she feels a mixture of skepticism and irritation. Skepticism because the output they're showing looks like garbage. Irritation because maybe she's missing something.

She has a Notes app folder called "AI prompts that actually work" with 47 entries, most of which are variations of the same five approaches she stumbled into by accident.

## Occupation in Your World

Danielle's role in this course's world is **The Practitioner Who Refuses to Fake It**.

She's surrounded by two types of people: those who dismiss AI entirely ("it can't match human creativity") and those who oversell it ("I let AI write everything now"). She's neither. She knows AI is a tool—potentially a powerful one—but she hasn't figured out how to wield it without producing work that embarrasses her.

What makes this course's world tick is Danielle's INTEGRITY. She won't ship mediocre work just because it's faster. She won't claim AI proficiency she doesn't have. She won't pretend the garbage outputs other marketers accept are acceptable to her. This integrity is both her limitation and her qualification.

Her energy in this brand world is **determined pragmatism**. She's not looking for a mindset shift or a productivity philosophy. She's looking for specific techniques that work, tested by someone who also refuses to accept garbage output.

## Activity in Your World

**Current AI workflow**: Danielle uses AI for first-draft brainstorming, which she then rewrites almost entirely. Sometimes the rewrite takes longer than starting from scratch would have. She keeps trying because she believes there must be a better way. She hasn't found it yet.

**Learning behavior**: She's completed maybe 30% of two different AI courses. Both started with philosophy ("AI is transforming everything...") and she lost patience. She learns by doing, not by being convinced. When she watches tutorials, she skips to the "here's exactly what to type" parts.

**Content creation pattern**: She produces 5-10 pieces of strategic content per week across clients. Each piece requires her specific voice, her strategic thinking, her understanding of the client's audience. AI threatens to commoditize exactly the thing she's built her reputation on—unless she can figure out how to use it without losing what makes her work valuable.

**Time investment calculus**: She's not looking to save hours. She's looking to elevate output. If a prompting technique takes the same amount of time but produces a better first draft, she's interested. She'd rather spend the same time producing better work than less time producing the same work.

**Identity reinforcement**: Every time she edits AI-generated copy and it becomes actually good, she feels validated. Every time someone asks "how did you get it to do that?" she feels a small surge of competence she wants more of.

## Thought Process in Your World

**Internal logic**: "AI isn't going away. The marketers who figure out how to use it well will outcompete those who don't. I refuse to be left behind, but I also refuse to lower my standards. There has to be a way to use this tool that produces work I'm proud of. That's what I'm looking for."

**What feels aligned**: Specificity. Demonstrated results. Instructors who show their actual outputs, not cherry-picked examples. Acknowledgment that most AI content is garbage—because she knows it is. A course that starts with techniques, not philosophy.

**What feels inauthentic**: Hype. Promises of "10x productivity." Courses that feel like they were created to capitalize on a trend. Anyone who seems to think AI replacing writers is a good thing. Certifications that exist to be credentialed rather than to be useful.

**Why this course over alternatives**: The market is full of courses teaching prompt engineering to developers. There are a few for "content creators" that feel like repackaged ChatGPT tutorials. A course specifically for marketers, taught by someone who actually writes marketing copy, with examples from marketing contexts, speaking to marketing-specific challenges? That's different. That's relevant.

**What she's refusing**: The generic. The theoretical. The hype cycle. The implication that good prompting is simple (if it were simple, she'd have figured it out). The certifications that teach you to pass a test rather than to produce work.

## The Posture Synthesis

Danielle sits up FORWARD in this course. Leaning in, elbows on table, focused but skeptical. She's ready to be convinced but hasn't been yet.

Her posture says: "Show me. Don't tell me it works—show me it working. And not on some generic example. On something that looks like the work I actually do."

This is the posture of a professional who has been disappointed by promises before. She's bought courses that oversold and underdelivered. She's tried techniques that worked for demos but not for real work. She wants to believe, but she needs proof.

The moment she sees a technique demonstrated on a real marketing challenge—and the output actually sounds good—the skepticism transforms into appetite. She doesn't want to feel skeptical. She wants to find something that works. The conversion from skeptic to evangelist is a straight line, but it requires crossing the proof threshold first.

## Strategic Implications

**Content strategy**: Lead with demonstrations, not promises. Show actual prompts producing actual outputs. Include the bad outputs too—show what most people get, then show what's possible with better technique. Danielle's pattern recognition will immediately see the difference, and that contrast IS the sale.

**Brand voice**: Practitioner to practitioner. No hype language. No "revolutionary" or "game-changing." Speak like a colleague sharing what they've figured out after extensive trial and error. Acknowledge frustration with AI outputs as the starting point—because that's where she is.

**Product decisions**: The course must be actionable immediately. No 45-minute philosophy modules before getting to techniques. She'll skip them anyway. Start with "here's a prompt, here's what it does, here's why it works." Theory should be embedded in practice, not front-loaded.

**Experience design**: Short modules she can complete in one sitting. Downloadable prompt templates she can use immediately. Examples from actual marketing contexts: landing pages, email sequences, ad copy, social content, strategy documents. A community or feedback mechanism where she can share outputs and get feedback—but optional, not required.

**What NOT to do**: Overpromise productivity gains. Suggest AI can replace strategic thinking. Create long, philosophical introductions. Gate practical content behind theoretical prerequisites. Use examples that don't map to real marketing work. Issue a "certification" that undermines credibility rather than building it.

## The Prediction Test

1. **The course landing page promises "10x your content output"**: Danielle closes the tab. That language pattern-matches to every disappointing course she's seen. Even if the course is good, the marketing suggests it won't be.

2. **The instructor posts a detailed LinkedIn breakdown of a specific prompting technique with before/after examples**: Danielle saves the post, tries the technique within 24 hours, and if it works, she's in the funnel. This is the exact content that bypasses her skepticism filter.

3. **The course offers a "Certified AI Content Strategist" credential upon completion**: Danielle's interest decreases. Certifications feel like credentialism for people who can't demonstrate actual skill. She'd rather have techniques that work than a badge that proves nothing.

4. **The course includes a module on "The Future of AI in Marketing" with predictions and philosophy**: Danielle skips it entirely. She might never watch it. This doesn't bother her unless it's required prerequsite for the practical modules—in which case she's annoyed.

5. **A marketer she respects posts that this course "finally made AI actually useful for my work"**: This is the highest-converting signal possible. Danielle trusts peer validation from practitioners, not testimonials from course graduates. She buys within the week.
