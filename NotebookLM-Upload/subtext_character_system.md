# SUBTEXT CHARACTER SYSTEM
## AI Brain Build Sprint — Who You're Talking To In Every Post
### Built Using Sam Goddard's Subtext Character Builder

> **The Rule**: You never write to "coaches." You write to ONE of these people. Every hook, every example, every story passes through: *"Would [Name] feel seen right now?"* That's how your content feels like it was made for one person while resonating with thousands.

> **Source Data**: Bullseye Research (9-query deep research swarm), Avatar Bible (8-agent synthesis), FARRICE.md personal context

## CHARACTER 1: "RACHEL"

| Element | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Archetype** | The Invisible Expert |
| **Age** | 42 |
| **Niche** | Executive leadership coach |
| **Revenue** | $165K/year |
| **Exact Situation** | 14 years deep. Certified in everything. ICF credential on her wall. She's the one people call when a VP is about to get fired and needs to be "coached up" in 90 days. She saves companies $200K in wrongful termination costs and gets paid $8K per engagement. She's brilliant on calls. Then she opens LinkedIn to write a post and types "Leadership isn't about authority, it's about..." and deletes it for the third time this week. |
| **This Week** | She watched a coach with 2 years of experience and a TikTok following post a 30-day content sequence that got 47 comments. Rachel has posted twice this month. Both got 3 likes. One was from her mom. |
| **Inner Monologue** | *"I've saved two executives' careers this quarter. I can't write a LinkedIn post. There's a 28-year-old coach with a ring light who has 10x my following and 1/10th of my depth. What is wrong with me?"* |
| **What She's Tried** | ChatGPT Plus ($20/month, used 4 times). A "LinkedIn for coaches" course ($497, completed Module 1). Canva templates for posts. Hired a VA to "help with content" but couldn't explain her methodology well enough for the VA to write anything that sounded like her. Fired the VA after 6 weeks. |
| **The Aha She Needs** | *"You don't have a content problem. You have a translation problem. Everything you say effortlessly on coaching calls is locked in a format AI can't access yet. The second we extract it and build your AI Brain, the blank screen disappears. Permanently."* |
| **Superfan Trigger** | Show her the exact moment when a coach opens their AI Brain, types "write me a post about the difference between authority and leadership," and the system responds using HER framework, HER examples, HER voice. Not generic leadership content. Her methodology, deployed on command. When she sees that's possible, she's done deliberating. |

### Aha Moments for Rachel:

1. **"You're not bad at content. You're bad at translating yourself."** She's world-class in conversation and invisible in writing. That's not a skill gap. It's a format mismatch. Her expertise lives in real-time dialogue, not static text. The AI Brain translates.

2. **"The 28-year-old isn't beating you. She's just systematized before you did."** Rachel thinks the gap is talent or age. It's not. It's infrastructure. That younger coach has a content system. Rachel has a methodology that's 10x deeper but lives entirely in her head. The depth wins once it's deployed.

3. **"Your VA failed because she didn't have your brain. She had your instructions."** Instructions are generic. A brain is specific. Rachel couldn't hand-off content because there was nothing to hand off. No document. No framework library. No voice guide. The AI Brain IS the hand-off.

### Wide Net Analysis:
Writing for Rachel captures: every experienced coach frustrated by the content game, every consultant who's brilliant in rooms but invisible online, every expert who watches less-qualified people outproduce them, every professional who tried hiring content help and it "never sounded right." The "2 years vs 14 years" tension is universal. Anyone who's ever felt outpaced by someone who simply systemized first will feel this character in their chest.

A $6,000 mastermind he left after 4 months. Three Kajabi subscriptions (two of which he forgot to cancel). He can TEACH every framework he's ever learned. He just can't find any of them when he needs to. His Google Drive has 47 documents. His Notion has 12 databases. Three of them are called "Content Ideas." None of them are current. |

| **Inner Monologue** | *"I keep buying the premium version of myself and never becoming him. $47K in courses and I'm still manually rebuilding my frameworks from memory every time I need them. What was the point of all of it?"* |
| **What He's Tried** | Built a "knowledge base" in Notion three separate times (abandoned each one within a month). Tried recording himself on Loom and transcribing it (too time-consuming). Used ChatGPT to "organize my frameworks" and it spit back a generic coaching model that looked nothing like his. Considered hiring a copywriter to document his methodology ($3K quote, couldn't stomach the cost). |
| **The Aha He Needs** | *"Your $47K in courses isn't wasted. It's undeployed. Every framework you've ever learned is still in your head. You just don't have a retrieval system. The AI Brain IS the retrieval system. We're not starting from scratch. We're organizing what you already know."* |

Not a generic summary. HIS remix. HIS voice. That's when the $47K stops feeling like waste and starts feeling like investment. |

### Aha Moments for Devon:

1. **"Your course graveyard isn't wasted money. It's an unorganized vault."** Devon mourns the spending. But the knowledge didn't disappear. It's scattered, not lost. The AI Brain turns $47K of scattered learning into a searchable, deployable system that makes him more valuable, not less.

2. **"You can teach your methodology in 60 minutes on a call. You can't find it in 60 seconds when you need it."** The problem isn't knowledge. It's retrieval. Devon's brain is the database but it has no search function. The AI Brain is the search function.

3. **"You've been rebuilding from memory because nobody told you there was another option."** Devon assumed the only way to access his frameworks was to remember them in real-time. That's like owning a library but carrying every book in your head instead of using the shelves. The AI Brain is the shelving system.

### Wide Net Analysis:
Writing for Devon captures: every coach who's invested heavily in their own development, every entrepreneur with a "course graveyard" they're ashamed of, every expert who can articulate brilliantly in conversation but freezes in writing, every person who's built knowledge bases that became graveyards. The "$47K" number hits because it's specific. Anyone who's spent even $5K on courses they didn't finish will wince and keep reading.

## CHARACTER 3: "KEISHA"

| Element | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Archetype** | The "I Should Be Able to Figure This Out" Perfectionist |
| **Age** | 45 |
| **Niche** | Business strategy consulting for women entrepreneurs |
| **Revenue** | $210K/year |
| **Exact Situation** | Keisha is the coach other coaches hire. She's genuinely successful. $210K, waitlist for 1:1 clients, speaks at conferences. She teaches systems. She teaches strategy. She teaches organization. Her clients' businesses are more systematized than hers. Her own content workflow is: stare at screen, write something, hate it, rewrite it, post something mediocre, feel embarrassed forever. She signed up for ChatGPT Plus two months ago and has used it exactly three times. Each time, it produced something she described as "what a college intern who Googled 'business coaching' would write." |
| **This Week** | A friend in her mastermind casually mentioned she's been using AI to write all her client proposals. "It takes me 15 minutes now. It used to take 3 hours." Keisha smiled and said "That's amazing, I need to do that." She went home and didn't do it. She doesn't know why. Actually, she does. She feels like she SHOULD already know how to do this. Asking for help feels like admitting she's not the expert she positions herself as. |
| **Inner Monologue** | *"I literally teach women how to build systems. My own content system is 'wing it and hope for the best.' If my clients could see my Google Drive, they'd fire me. I should be able to figure this out myself. The fact that I can't makes me question everything."* |
| **What She's Tried** | Nothing, publicly. That's the point. She hasn't tried because trying and failing would confirm the fear. She's watched from the sidelines while less-successful coaches figure out AI. She tells herself she's "being strategic" and "waiting for the right tool." She's not. She's paralyzed by the shame of not already knowing. |
| **The Aha She Needs** | *"Teaching systems doesn't mean you have to build every system yourself. Surgeons don't sterilize their own instruments. You're the strategist. Let someone else build the infrastructure. That's not weakness. That's what you literally tell your clients to do."* |
| **Superfan Trigger** | The framing. If Farrice's content ever implies coaches are dumb for not figuring out AI, she's gone forever. If it says "you're not behind, you're just trying to do something yourself that should be done-with-you," she exhales for the first time in months. The positioning of the offer as "done-with-you" (not "done-for-you" or "learn-it-yourself") is what converts Keisha. |

### Aha Moments for Keisha:

1. **"You teach your clients to delegate. You won't delegate this."** The cognitive dissonance is the sale. Keisha advises every client to "stop trying to do everything yourself." Then she goes home and tries to do everything herself. Naming this gently, without judgment, makes her feel understood instead of exposed.

2. **"The shame of not knowing isn't about AI. It's about identity."** Keisha doesn't have a technology problem. She has an identity problem. Her self-concept is "the expert who has systems for everything." Admitting she doesn't have an AI system threatens that identity. The reframe: adding AI doesn't mean she was deficient. It means she's evolving.

3. **"Your clients' businesses are more organized than yours. That's not ironic. It's normal."** The cobbler's children have no shoes. Every consultant's backend is messier than their clients'. Normalizing this removes the shame that's keeping her frozen.

### Wide Net Analysis:
Writing for Keisha captures: every successful coach/consultant with imposter syndrome, every "expert" whose own business doesn't reflect the advice they give, every high-performer who won't ask for help because it threatens their identity, every woman entrepreneur who feels she has to be self-sufficient in everything. The "I teach systems but don't have my own" tension is devastatingly universal.

## CHARACTER 4: "MARCUS"

| Element | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Archetype** | The Existential Threat Processor |
| **Age** | 51 |
| **Niche** | Transformation and life coaching (specializes in men's work and purpose discovery) |
| **Revenue** | $130K/year |
| **Exact Situation** | Marcus has been coaching for 17 years. He's the real deal. Deep work. Shadow integration. Men who come to him in crisis leave with clarity they've never had. He charges $12K for a 6-month engagement and has never had a refund request. But last month, a friend showed him a ChatGPT conversation where the AI did a credible job walking through a purpose exercise. It wasn't great. But it was... not bad. And it was free. Marcus hasn't slept well since. |
| **This Week** | He read an article that said AI coaching tools could handle "up to 90% of day-to-day coaching functions." He knows the statistic is misleading. He knows depth work requires human presence. But the number 90% echoes in his head at 2 AM. He's started quietly questioning whether 17 years of mastery can compete with a tool that costs $20/month. He hasn't told anyone about this fear. |
| **Inner Monologue** | *"What if the thing I've spent my entire career building can be replicated by a well-prompted chatbot? What if I'm just a glorified cheerleader and AI finally proves it? I know that's not true. But I don't have proof it's not true. And that's what's killing me."* |
| **What He's Tried** | He opened ChatGPT once. He asked it something vague about purpose. The answer was mediocre. He closed it. He hasn't gone back. He also hasn't gone back because part of him is afraid of what happens if he asks it a better question and gets a better answer. |
| **The Aha He Needs** | *"That ChatGPT conversation your friend showed you? It was generic because it wasn't trained on anything real. Your 17 years of methodology, your specific framework, your stories, your language are what make YOUR coaching irreplaceable. An AI Brain trained on your work doesn't threaten you. It PROVES you. The fact that we NEED your frameworks to build it is the proof that no chatbot can replace you."* |
| **Superfan Trigger** | The positioning. If the AI Brain Build is framed as "here's how to use AI to replace the parts of coaching that are commoditizable," Marcus will never buy. If it's framed as "this is how you prove your 17 years of depth are irreplaceable by building a system that literally can't function without YOUR methodology," he's not only buying. He's telling every coach he knows. |

### Aha Moments for Marcus:

1. **"The 90% statistic scared you because you don't have proof of the 10% that can't be automated."** Marcus KNOWS his work can't be replaced. But he can't point to evidence. His methodology lives in conversations, not documents. The AI Brain gives him the artifact. The tangible, organized, deployed PROOF that his 17 years created something no chatbot can replicate.

2. **"A custom AI Brain trained on YOUR work is the ultimate proof you're irreplaceable."** The paradox: the purchase itself resolves the fear. If his methodology were generic, we couldn't build a custom AI from it. The fact that his AI Brain works BECAUSE of his unique frameworks is the proof he's been looking for.

3. **"You avoided ChatGPT because you were afraid of what a better answer would mean. You should have been afraid of what NOT having your answer out there means."** Marcus's fear made him avoid AI entirely. But the real risk isn't that AI replaces him. It's that AI replaces the coaches who DO systematize while Marcus sits on his irreplaceable methodology without deploying it.

### Wide Net Analysis:
Writing for Marcus captures: every veteran coach questioning their relevance, every expert haunted by the "will AI replace me?" fear, every experienced professional who saw a headline about AI capabilities and couldn't shake it, every person who avoids technology not because they can't learn it but because they're afraid of what it reveals. The "90% statistic" fear is universal among established coaches. The reframe (your AI Brain is PROOF of irreplaceability) is the most powerful sale in the entire offer.

# "WOULD [NAME] FEEL SEEN?" CHECKLIST

Run this on EVERY post before publishing:

- [ ] **Which character am I writing to?** (Pick ONE. Always.)
- [ ] Does the hook address their EXACT inner monologue?
- [ ] Does at least one example mirror their specific situation?
- [ ] Is there an aha moment that reframes something they've been doing wrong?
- [ ] Would this content make them DM you saying "it's like you're in my head"?
- [ ] Does the CTA match where THEY are in the journey (not where you want them to be)?
- [ ] Am I creating tension they feel (not just information they agree with)?

# HOW TO USE THIS SYSTEM

### Before Writing Any Content:
1. Open this document
2. Pick ONE character
3. Write the entire post through their lens
4. Run the "Would [Name] feel seen?" checklist
5. If any answer is no, rewrite that section

### When Choosing Which Character:
- **Bucket 1 (Education)**: Rachel or Devon (they need the problem named)
- **Bucket 2 (Stories)**: Keisha or Marcus (they need proof and normalization)
- **Bucket 3 (Contrarian)**: Marcus or Devon (they need reframes that challenge their assumptions)
- **Bucket 4 (Promotion)**: Rachel or Keisha (they're closest to buying)

### The Paradox That Makes This Work:
The more specifically you write for ONE character, the wider the net catches. Writing for "Rachel's 2-likes-and-one-was-from-her-mom" moment captures every expert who's ever felt invisible online. Writing for "Devon's $47K course graveyard" captures everyone who's ever bought something they didn't finish. Specificity IS the wide net. Generic content captures no one.
