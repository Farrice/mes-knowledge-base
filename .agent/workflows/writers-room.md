# Writers' Room Treatment Workflow

> **Invocation**: `/writers-room` | `@writers-room` | "run writers-room"
> **Purpose**: Take a drafted post from first-draft quality to publication-ready through three layers: structural compression, emotional/psychological engineering, and platform optimization + pull-through + voice authenticity.
> **When**: After ANY content draft exists and needs tightening, sharpening, tension, wit, emotional depth, engagement architecture, or voice verification.

---

## Prerequisites

- A completed first draft (any format — LinkedIn post, article, email, script)
- User feedback on what's working and what isn't (or your own honest assessment)

## Phase 1: Load the Writers' Room (3 layers, 9 sources)

### Layer 1: Structure & Compression

These three compress, cut, and tighten the architecture:

#### Mitch Albom — The Structural Editor
**Load**: `skills/mitch-albom-writing-mastery/genius.md`
**Lenses**:
- **Theme-First Architecture**: State the piece's theme in under 10 words. If you can't, the piece doesn't know what it's about.
- **The Tight Cut**: After reading every sentence, ask: "Does this earn its place?" Remove anything that can be removed without losing meaning.
- **Uncle vs. Aunt**: Is the piece being the uncle (momentum, action, forward motion) or the aunt (nitpicky details, over-explaining, throat-clearing)? Cut the aunt passages.
- **First/Last Marriage**: Do the opening and closing work together as a frame? The last beat should feel like the inevitable destination of the first.
- **98% for Readers**: Before committing to any element — "Am I adding this for ME or for READERS?"

#### Jonathan Franzen — The Compression Editor
**Load**: `skills/jonathan-franzen-storytelling/genius.md`
**Lenses**:
- **Minimal Detail Maximum Impact**: For any section, identify the 2-3 details that do the most work. Cut everything else. Trust the reader.
- **Want-Collision**: Every section should have tension. If a section is just... stating something... it's dead weight.

#### Nicolas Cole — The Sentence-Level Editor
**Load**: `skills/nicolas-cole-sentence-craft/genius.md`
**Lenses**:
- **Terminal Word Power**: The last word of every sentence/line carries the most weight. Place the strongest word there.
- **Opening Sentence Power**: First sentence of the piece should be under 10 words and create immediate tension or curiosity.
- **Three-Pass Compression**: Read through three times. Each pass, find 10-15% more to cut. If it reads the same without it, it goes.
- **4th Grade Vocabulary Anchoring**: Complex ideas in simple words. Never use a big word when a small one hits harder.

### Layer 2: Emotion, Psychology & Entertainment

These three inject the heartbeat — tension, wit, belief, and "damn this person can write":

#### Erica Mallet — The Belief & Identity Editor
**Load**: `skills/erica-mallet-brand-magnetism/genius.md`
**Lenses**:
- **Enemy Effect**: Every magnetic piece has an enemy — not a person, but a force, system, or status quo. Name what you stand against. Readers who share that enemy become tribe.
- **Belief Architecture**: What belief does this piece plant? Readers should walk away believing something they didn't fully articulate before.
- **Vegetable-in-Cake Packaging**: Hide the belief inside an entertaining story. Don't preach — package. The reader should absorb the belief without realizing they're being taught.
- **"Finally Someone Said It" Test**: Would readers screenshot this and send it to a friend? Does it name something they've felt but never heard articulated?
- **Through-Line Discovery**: Does this piece connect to the larger body of work? Is there a thread running through everything you write?

#### Joanna Wiebe — The Authority & Persuasion Editor
**Load**: `skills/joanna-wiebe-persuasion-mastery/genius.md`
**Lenses**:
- **Authority Craft**: Does the WAY this is written demonstrate mastery? The writing itself should prove you're a master writer. Not by claiming it — by showing it in every sentence. Readers should think "damn, I need this person writing for me."
- **Stealing Thunder**: Volunteer disqualification before the reader can object. "Not templates. Not 'post more.'" is stealing thunder — you named what you're NOT before they could dismiss you.
- **Anti-Hype Engine**: Hype signals desperation. Anti-hype signals confidence. Understatement > overstatement. "We just stopped doing what everyone else was doing" beats "game-changing breakthrough."
- **Bucket Brigades**: 1-5 word transitional phrases that create micro-curiosity and carry the reader across section breaks. Deploy after dense ideas, before belief shifts, at attention drop-off points.
- **Catchy Phrasing (Meme Test)**: Are there lines memorable enough to screenshot? Familiar syntax + distinctive word + compression + portability. Could this phrase work on a t-shirt?

#### Robert Mack — The Comedy & Tension Editor
**Load**: `skills/robert-mack-comedy-writing/genius.md`
**Lenses**:
- **Tension-Relief Architecture**: Build tension through discomfort or stakes → hold it → release with insight or reframe. The audience needs to feel the tension BEFORE the payoff lands.
- **Skewed Perspective Engine**: Take one familiar truth and view it from an off-center angle. "What's the OTHER valid interpretation?" The unexpected angle is where humor and insight live.
- **Vivid Specificity Principle**: Replace generic with maximally vivid, picture-generating language. Never "she's struggling" → paint what struggling LOOKS like. Close your eyes — can you picture it?
- **Naming Mastery**: When you NAME an unnamed experience, you own that concept. "The translation problem." "Silence isn't neutral." Give readers language for things they've felt but couldn't articulate.
- **Universal-to-Specific Pipeline**: Find the universal truth → make it painfully specific → the specificity is what makes it universal. Paradox: the more specific, the more relatable.
- **Recognition Mining**: Target the "damn, that's true" response. The silent nod. The exhale. Comedy isn't just laughs — it's the feeling of being SEEN.

### Layer 3: Platform, Pull-Through & Voice Authenticity

These three ensure the post works on LinkedIn specifically, keeps readers locked in from first line to last, and sounds like a real human — not a writer performing:

#### Lara Acosta — The Platform & Engagement Editor
**Load**: `skills/lara-acosta-linkedin-mastery/genius.md`
**Lenses**:
- **Algorithm Optimization**: LinkedIn rewards dwell time (longer reads that hold attention), early engagement (first hour = 80% of reach), native content, authentic voice, and conversation starters. Every post must be designed for these signals.
- **8-Word Rehook Architecture**: The hook must work in ~8 words on mobile before the "see more" cutoff. First-Principle Hook Test: remove your name and avatar — does the hook still stop scrolling?
- **SLAY Framework**: Story → Lesson → Actionable → Your Turn. The "Your Turn" is the engagement mechanism — not engagement bait ("comment YES"), but a genuine question or invitation that drives real conversation.
- **F-Shape Visual Formatting**: Mobile readers scan in an F-pattern. Front-load key words. Use line breaks and white space for visual pacing. Dense paragraphs kill dwell time.
- **Post & Ghost Kill Switch**: Don't post if you can't engage for 30 minutes after. Reply to every comment in the first hour. Posting without engaging is wasting good content.

#### Pull-Through Principles — The Compulsion & Open Loop Editor
**Load**: `skills/kallaway-content-psychology/genius.md` + `skills/fresh-voice-system/genius.md`
**Lenses**:
- **Open Loop Architecture** (Fresh Voice): Five loop types — Promise ("I'll show you tomorrow"), Implication ("But this is where it gets tricky"), Story ("She called me three days later"), Pattern ("I saw the same thing happen again — with a twist"), Question (leaves a genuine question unanswered). Plant 1-2 loops per post. Close at least 1 from the previous post.
- **Compulsion Test** (Fresh Voice): "Would YOU come back tomorrow to read the next chapter?" If the post feels complete and closed, the loop isn't open enough. The reader should need the next post like the next episode.
- **Anticipation Engineering** (Kallaway): Head-fakes that lead toward an obvious answer then subvert. Partial reveals that let readers guess (then surprise). Tension ratcheting that increases stakes toward the payoff. At any point, if the reader can predict what's next, you've lost them.
- **Curiosity Resets**: Where does attention risk flagging mid-post? Add micro-curiosity mechanisms at those points — a question, a surprising detail, a tension spike. Don't let the reader coast.

#### Voice Authenticity — The "Would He Say This?" Editor
**Load**: `skills/ghostwriting-voice-engine/genius.md` + `FARRICE.md` + voice captures
**Lenses**:
- **The Friend Test**: Read every line aloud. Would Farrice say this to a smart friend over coffee? Any line that sounds like "writing" instead of "talking" gets rewritten. If it sounds impressive but nobody actually talks that way — it fails.
- **Show Don't Tell Enforcement**: Key claims must be demonstrated through story, dialogue, or example — never stated directly. "I help experts become visible" = TELL. Showing the consultant's prospect saying "I feel like I already know you" = SHOW.
- **Voice Lives in Imperfections** (GVE): Real voice has fragments, rhythm breaks, personality quirks. Don't polish these out. A sentence that's technically imperfect but sounds like a real person > a perfect sentence that sounds like a copywriter.
- **Jargon Kill**: Any phrase that sounds impressive but means nothing to a normal person gets rewritten in plain language. "Making expertise visible to strangers" → "getting your name out there so the right people find you." Test: would a non-expert understand this instantly?

## Phase 2: Diagnose the Draft

Before editing, articulate:

### Layer 1 Diagnostics (Structure):
1. **Theme** (under 10 words): What is this piece actually about?
2. **Opening power**: Does the first line stop a scroll? Rate 1-10.
3. **Structural flow**: Map each section's purpose in 3-5 words. Any section that can't be summarized in 5 words is bloated.
4. **Dead weight**: Which sections are "aunt" passages? Where does momentum die?
5. **First/Last marriage**: Do opening and closing connect?

### Layer 2 Diagnostics (Emotion):
6. **Enemy** (Erica): What force does this piece fight? Is the enemy clear enough that readers instantly know what side they're on?
7. **Belief** (Erica): What belief does this plant? Would readers say "finally someone said it"?
8. **Authority demonstration** (Joanna): Does the WRITING prove mastery, or just claim it? Where could the craft itself become the evidence?
9. **Tension map** (Robert): Where does the piece build tension? Where does it release? If neither — where SHOULD it?
10. **Comedy/wit check** (Robert): Any moment of humor, surprise, charm? If not, where's the natural opening for it?
11. **Recognition moments** (Robert): Any "damn that's true" beats? Any naming of unnamed experiences?

### Layer 3 Diagnostics (Platform, Pull-Through & Voice):
12. **Hook test** (Lara): Does the first line stop the scroll in ~8 words? Remove your name — does it still work?
13. **Dwell time structure** (Lara): Is the post structured for slow, engaged reading? Line breaks at tension points? F-shape visual pacing? Or are there dense paragraphs that kill attention?
14. **Engagement invitation** (Lara): Does the closing invite genuine interaction? Not "comment YES" — a real question that makes readers want to share their own experience. SLAY "Your Turn."
15. **Open loops** (Fresh Voice): Is there at least one open loop? Promise, implication, story, or pattern type? Does the reader NEED the next post?
16. **Compulsion test** (Fresh Voice): Would you come back tomorrow to read the next chapter? If the post feels complete and closed, the loop isn't open enough.
17. **Pull-through audit** (Kallaway): Where does attention risk flagging mid-post? Is there a curiosity reset at each danger point? Can the reader predict what's next at any point? (If yes, you've lost them.)
18. **Voice authenticity** (GVE): Read every line aloud. Would Farrice say this to a friend? Any line that sounds like "writing" instead of "talking"? Any jargon that sounds impressive but means nothing?
19. **Show don't tell**: Are key claims demonstrated through story/dialogue/example? Or stated directly? Every TELL is a missed opportunity to SHOW.
20. **User feedback**: What specific problems did the user flag?

## Phase 3: Apply the Treatment

### Step 1: Layer 1 — Compress (run first)

Work through the draft section by section:

**For each section, ask:**
- **Mitch**: Does every sentence earn its place? Is this uncle or aunt?
- **Franzen**: What are the 2-3 details doing the most work? Can I cut the rest?
- **Cole**: Is the terminal word the strongest word? Can I compress this sentence?

**Structural moves:**
- If 3+ examples are saying the same thing → compress to 1-2 (pick the ones that signal the right audience)
- If a metaphor is followed by an explanation → cut the explanation (trust the metaphor)
- If a transition feels forced → cut the transition and let the section break do the work
- If the ending is passive → add a genuine question or emotional stake

**Target compression:**
- First pass: 30-40% cut (remove dead weight, aunt passages, redundant examples)
- Second pass: 10-15% more (sentence-level compression, terminal word optimization)

### Step 2: Layer 2 — Inject (run second, on the compressed draft)

Now add the emotional, psychological, and entertainment layers:

**Erica pass:**
- Sharpen the enemy. Is it named or just implied? Make readers feel which side they're on.
- Check belief packaging. Is the belief hidden in the story (vegetable-in-cake) or stated directly? If stated directly, can you embed it instead?
- Test for tribal belonging. Would someone share this and say "THIS is exactly what I've been saying"?

**Joanna pass:**
- Audit for authority demonstration. Find 1-2 lines where the CRAFT can become the proof. Where can the writing itself make readers think "I need this person"?
- Add bucket brigades where attention might flag — especially between sections or after dense ideas.
- Check for stealing thunder opportunities. Where could preemptive disqualification build credibility?
- Apply anti-hype filter. Any line that sounds like marketing → rewrite as confident understatement.

**Robert pass:**
- Engineer tension-release. Identify the natural story tension. Build it — hold it longer than feels comfortable — then release with insight or unexpected angle.
- Add skewed perspective. Find at least one moment where a familiar truth gets reframed from an unexpected angle.
- Inject vivid specificity. Replace any generic description with picture-generating language.
- Check for naming opportunities. Any unnamed experience that could get a name?
- Find the comedy beat. Not forced jokes — Ryan Reynolds-style: self-aware, wry, charming. One beat minimum. Could be dark humor, self-deprecation, absurd specificity, or observational wit.
- Mine for recognition. At least one moment designed to make readers silently nod or exhale.

### Step 3: Layer 3 — Platform, Pull-Through & Voice (run third, on the emotionally-charged draft)

Now ensure the post works on LinkedIn, keeps readers locked in, and sounds like a real human:

**Lara Acosta pass:**
- Test the hook: Does it work in ~8 words? Strip your name and avatar — does it still stop scrolling? If not, rewrite the hook.
- Check visual formatting: F-shape for mobile. Line breaks at tension points. No dense paragraphs. White space is a tool, not decoration.
- Add engagement invitation: SLAY "Your Turn." Not engagement bait — a genuine question that invites specific responses. "What's YOUR version of this?" or "I'd love to hear that story."
- Dwell time optimization: Structure the post for slow reading. Tension holds, reveals, section breaks that make readers pause. Dense = death.

**Pull-Through pass (Kallaway + Fresh Voice):**
- Plant open loops: 1-2 per post. Choose from: Promise ("I'll show you what happened next"), Implication ("But this is where it gets interesting"), Story ("She called me back three days later"), Pattern ("I saw the same thing happen — with a twist"). **Critical: "More on this soon" is NOT an open loop. It's a dead end. The loop must create genuine curiosity about something specific.**
- Run the Compulsion Test: Would the reader feel compelled to finish this post? Would they need the next one? If the ending feels closed and complete, the loop isn't open enough.
- Check for curiosity resets: Where does attention risk flagging mid-post? Add micro-curiosity at those points — a surprising detail, a question, a tension spike.
- Anticipation engineering: Can the reader predict what's next at any point? If yes, add a head-fake or subvert the expectation.

**Voice Authenticity pass:**
- The Friend Test: Read every single line aloud. Would Farrice say this to a smart friend over coffee? Mark any line that sounds like "writing" instead of "talking." Rewrite those lines in the way a person would actually say it.
- Show Don't Tell enforcement: Find every line that TELLS (states a claim directly) and convert to SHOW (demonstrates through story, dialogue, example, or result). "I help experts become visible" → show the expert's prospect saying "I feel like I already know you."
- Jargon kill: Any phrase that sounds impressive but no normal person would actually say → rewrite in plain language that creates a picture in the reader's mind.
- Imperfection check: Is the post too polished? Real people have fragments, unfinished thoughts, self-corrections. If every sentence is a gem, it reads as AI.

### Step 4: Anti-Slop Filter (run last)

Mandatory check against the Anti-AI-Slop Protocol (full spec in `/content-sprint` workflow). Quick checklist:
- [ ] No banned phrases ("let's dive in," "game-changer," "moreover," etc.)
- [ ] No banned structural patterns (checkmark lists, repeated "it's not X it's Y," etc.)
- [ ] At least one detail so specific it couldn't be fabricated
- [ ] An opinion that could alienate someone
- [ ] Vocabulary matches Farrice's actual speaking voice
- [ ] Screenshot test: If someone said "this is AI" — could they make a case?

## Phase 4: Validate

Run ALL checks on the final version:

### Layer 1 Checks (Structure):
- [ ] **Theme test**: Can you state the theme in under 10 words? Does every section serve it?
- [ ] **Tight Cut test**: Read every sentence. Would the piece lose meaning without it?
- [ ] **Uncle test**: Read aloud. Does it build momentum or stop to explain?
- [ ] **First/Last marriage**: Read only the first and last sections. Do they form a complete arc?
- [ ] **Three-year-old test**: Where would a restless listener zone out?
- [ ] **Terminal word audit**: Check the last word of each key line. Is it the strongest possible?
- [ ] **Scroll-stop test**: Does the first line work in under 8 words on mobile?

### Layer 2 Checks (Emotion):
- [ ] **Enemy test**: Is there a clear force we stand against? Do readers know what side they're on?
- [ ] **Belief test**: What belief does the reader walk away with? Was it packaged in story, not stated?
- [ ] **Authority demonstration**: Does the WRITING prove mastery, not just claim it? Would a reader think "damn, I need this person writing for me"?
- [ ] **Tension-release**: Does the piece build and release tension at least once?
- [ ] **Comedy/wit beat**: At least one moment of humor, surprise, or Ryan Reynolds-style charm?
- [ ] **Recognition test**: At least one "damn that's true" moment? A silent nod or exhale?
- [ ] **Body response test**: Does reading this create a physical reaction? (nod, laugh, exhale, stomach drop, goosebumps)

### Layer 3 Checks (Platform, Pull-Through & Voice):
- [ ] **Scroll-stop test**: Would someone stop scrolling for this hook? Works in ~8 words? Works without knowing who you are?
- [ ] **Pull-through test**: At least one open loop that creates genuine curiosity about something specific. NOT "more on this soon" — a real unresolved question.
- [ ] **Compulsion test**: Would the reader feel compelled to finish? Would they need the next post? If the ending feels complete and closed — fail.
- [ ] **Voice authenticity test**: Every line passes "would Farrice say this to a friend?" No line sounds like "writing" instead of "talking."
- [ ] **Show-don't-tell test**: Key claims demonstrated through story, dialogue, or example. No important claim is only stated.
- [ ] **Engagement test**: Closing invites genuine interaction. Not "comment YES" — a real question driving real conversation.
- [ ] **Dwell time test**: Post structured for slow, engaged reading. Line breaks at tension points. No dense paragraphs. Reveals create pauses.
- [ ] **Jargon-free test**: No phrase that sounds impressive but nobody actually says. Plain language that creates pictures, not abstractions.
- [ ] **Imperfection test**: Post has human texture — not every sentence is a polished gem. Fragments, rhythm breaks, personality quirks present.

## Phase 5: Present Changes

When presenting the rewrite to the user:

1. **List what was cut and why** (map each cut to a specific Layer 1 expert principle)
2. **List what was injected and why** (map each addition to a specific Layer 2 expert principle)
3. **List what was optimized for platform and voice** (map each change to Layer 3 principles)
4. **Show the compression ratio** (original lines → new lines)
5. **Flag any remaining weak spots** you see but chose to keep
6. **Highlight the emotional engineering** — where tension builds, where the comedy lands, where authority demonstrates itself
7. **Call out the open loops** — what unresolved questions pull the reader to the next post?
8. **Voice authenticity report** — any lines that were rewritten because they sounded like "writing" instead of "talking"

## Content Type: Profile & About Sections (Special Protocol)

Profile copy (headline, About, Featured) requires ADDITIONAL diagnostics beyond the standard 3-layer treatment because:
1. It's **permanent** (always visible), not temporal like posts
2. It must **complement** post content without cannibalizing language
3. The reader should be the **protagonist**, not the author
4. The writing itself must **demonstrate** the service being sold

### Pre-Treatment: Content Ecosystem Check
Before ANY draft, read ALL active content in `_active/linkedin-launch/arcs/` and map off-limits language:
- Distinctive phrases from upcoming/recent posts
- Metaphors, proof stories, dialogue that posts own
- Any language that would overlap and flatten impact

### Additional Diagnostics (Add to Phase 2)
- **21. Reader-as-Protagonist check**: Is the reader the main character? Or is the About section a monologue? If you removed every "I" statement and the piece still works through "you" — you're on track. If the piece is mostly "I" — rewrite.
- **22. Recognition beat count**: Are there at least 4 moments where the reader thinks "that's me"? Space them evenly. An About section without recognition moments is a resume.
- **23. Writing-as-demonstration**: Does the WAY this is written prove the service? A ghostwriter's About section that reads like generic copy disproves its own claim.
- **24. Cannibalization check**: Grep the active arc folder. Any phrase overlap with published or queued posts → rewrite the profile version.
- **25. Soft close test**: Does the ending feel like an invitation or a sales pitch? "I'd like to hear about it" vs "Book a call today."

### Treatment Priority
For About sections, Layer 2 (Emotion) is disproportionately important. The #1 failure mode is "structurally sound but flat" — all the right points covered, zero tension or recognition. Spend extra treatment time on:
- Robert Mack's tension-release architecture (build anticipation before reveals)
- Robert Mack's recognition mining (target the "damn, that's true" response)
- Erica Mallet's belief architecture (what does the reader believe about themselves after reading?)

## Anti-Patterns

- **Don't compress personality out of the piece.** Tighten structure, not voice.
- **Don't cut the valley.** Emotional lows are earned — they make the highs hit harder.
- **Don't add where you should subtract in Layer 1.** Compression is removal.
- **Don't force comedy.** If the humor doesn't serve the piece, don't add it. Wit should feel effortless.
- **Don't template the process.** Each piece has its own structural and emotional problems. Diagnose first, then treat.
- **Don't let Layer 2 undo Layer 1.** Injecting emotion doesn't mean adding words back. The best emotional beats are compressed too.
- **Don't fake open loops.** "More on this soon" is not an open loop — it's a dead end. A real loop creates genuine curiosity about something specific and unresolved.
- **Don't write lines nobody would say.** If a phrase sounds impressive on paper but nobody would say it in conversation, it fails the voice test. Rewrite in the way a person would actually say it.
- **Don't TELL when you can SHOW.** Never state a claim directly when you could demonstrate it through story, dialogue, or result. "I help experts become visible" is telling. Showing the prospect who said "I feel like I already know you" is showing.
- **Don't ignore the algorithm.** Beautiful writing that gets zero reach helps nobody. Design for dwell time, engagement, and the first-hour window. Work WITH the platform, not against it.
