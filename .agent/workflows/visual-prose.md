### Step 1: Scene Intelligence Gathering
Understand the scene's purpose:

- What is the thematic function of this scene?
- What time of day, season, weather serves the mood?

### Step 2: Five-Layer Sensory Composition

**Layer 1 — The Visual Anchor**: The dominant image — what the eye lands on first. Not a wide establishing shot but a specific, telling detail.

**Layer 2 — The Ambient Sound**: The soundscape that creates atmosphere. Not "birds chirping" but "the irregular tick of a sprinkler hitting a car nobody has driven in months."

**Layer 3 — The Tactile/Kinesthetic**: Temperature, texture, physical sensation. "The kind of heat that makes cotton stick to your back."

**Layer 4 — The Temporal Marker**: Something that tells you when you are — not a title card, but a detail. "A newspaper with a headline nobody's read yet."

**Layer 5 — The Emotional Undertow**: The one detail that carries the scene's emotional weight without stating it. The camera lingers here even if no one's watching.

### Step 3: Write the Visual Prose
Compose the scene description as Roth would — a miniature novel that makes the reader SEE and FEEL the world. This is prose that:
- Reads like literature, not stage directions
- Guides the director without constraining them
- Creates a mood that infects every line of dialogue that follows

### Step 4: Rhythm Check
Read the prose aloud. It should have a cadence — not poetry, but musical. Roth's descriptions breathe.
