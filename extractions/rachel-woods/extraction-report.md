## Layer 1: Surface Frameworks

### 1. The CRAFT Cycle

Rachel's signature end-to-end framework for AI-ifying any business process:

| Phase | Purpose | Key Action |
|-------|---------|------------|
| **C**lear Picture | Map the current state | Document what happens, who does it, how long, what quality |
| **R**ealistic Design | Design the AI-assisted version | Decide which tasks AI handles vs. humans, set quality bars |
| **A**I-ify | Build the AI workflow | Prompt engineering, tool selection, chaining |
| **F**eedback | Test and iterate | Run the AI version alongside the human version, compare |
| **T**eam Rollout | Deploy at scale | Train team, create SOPs, monitor adoption |

**Why it matters**: Most AI advice stops at "use ChatGPT for X." CRAFT treats AI adoption as a full change management cycle. The feedback loop is what separates this from a toy implementation.

### 2. The MASTER Method (Prompting)

Rachel's prompting framework for building reliable, repeatable AI outputs:

- **M**ission — What is the AI supposed to do?
- **A**udience — Who is this for?
- **S**tyle — What does the output look like?
- **T**one — What does it sound like?
- **E**xamples — What does good look like?
- **R**esponse format — How should it be structured?

**Key insight**: Rachel doesn't treat this as a "prompting tip." She treats it as a **specification document** for AI tasks. Every AI process in her system starts with a MASTER spec, which means processes are portable across models, team members, and time.

### 3. The Task Hierarchy

Rachel's three-tier model for deciding what to automate:

| Tier | Definition | AI Role | Example |
|------|-----------|---------|---------|
| **Objective Tasks** | Clear right/wrong answer, no judgment needed | Full automation | Data formatting, calculations, categorization |
| **Good Enough Tasks** | Quality bar is flexible, human review optional | AI draft + light review | Email drafts, social captions, research summaries |
| **Expert Tasks** | Requires deep domain knowledge, high stakes | Human-led, AI-assisted | Strategy decisions, client negotiations, creative direction |

**Hidden depth**: The real methodology is in the *movement between tiers*. Rachel teaches people to look at what they call "Expert Tasks" and ask: "Is there a sub-task inside this that's actually Objective or Good Enough?" This decomposition skill is the AI operator's core competency.

## Layer 2: Hidden Methodology

### 4. The Three Roles Model

Rachel identifies three distinct roles required for AI transformation in any organization:

**AI Visionary** — Sees the strategic opportunity. Usually the CEO or founder. Their job is to set direction and remove blockers. They do NOT need to be technical.

**AI Operator** — The bridge. Maps business processes, identifies automation opportunities, designs AI workflows, and manages implementation. This is Rachel's core persona. The operator doesn't write the code or build the prompts — they **architect the system**.

**AI Implementer** — The executor. Builds prompts, configures tools, creates automations. They're skilled at the technical layer but take direction from the operator.

**Critical insight Rachel keeps repeating**: Most companies fail because they hire implementers without operators. They get a bunch of cool AI tools deployed with no strategic framework. The operator is the missing layer.

### 5. The AI Edge Concept

Rachel's competitive moat framework for organizations:

> "Your AI Edge is the intersection of your unique expertise, your unique data, and a process that would be impossibly expensive to do manually."

The formula:

```
AI Edge = (Proprietary Knowledge) × (Proprietary Data) × (Scale Problem)
```

Examples she gives:
- A law firm that has 10,000 past contracts can train AI to spot unusual clauses no competitor can
- A marketing agency that has 5 years of A/B test data can use AI to predict winners before running tests
- A recruiting firm that has detailed candidate feedback can use AI to match culture fit, not just skills

**Why this matters**: This is how Rachel differentiates from generic "AI consulting." She's not selling automation — she's selling **competitive intelligence that happens to use AI as the delivery mechanism**.

### 6. The Unlimited Time Offer

Rachel's reframe of AI's core value proposition:

> "AI doesn't save you time. AI gives you unlimited time. The question isn't 'how do I save 2 hours?' — it's 'what would I do if I had infinite hours?'"

This isn't motivational fluff. It's a **design principle**. When you frame AI through "saving time," you optimize existing processes. When you frame it through "unlimited time," you ask entirely different questions:

- What would we do if we could personally respond to every lead?
- What would we do if we could audit every customer interaction?
- What would we do if we could test 1,000 variations of every piece of content?

These questions lead to fundamentally different implementations than "make current process 30% faster."

## Layer 3: Invisible Architecture

### 7. Process Before Prompts (The Operating System)

Rachel's deepest principle, stated explicitly but rarely understood by her audience:

> "The biggest mistake companies make is starting with the AI. You have to start with the process."

Her sequencing is rigid:

1. **Document the current process** (even if it's messy)
2. **Identify the decision points** (where does a human make a judgment call?)
3. **Map the quality bars** (what's acceptable output at each step?)
4. **THEN introduce AI** (and only at steps where the quality bar matches AI capability)

This means Rachel's methodology is fundamentally a **business process engineering** methodology with AI as the implementation layer, not the other way around.

### 8. The 30% Trap

Rachel makes a precise empirical claim across all three transcripts:

> "Most companies see about a 30% productivity improvement from AI. That's the ceiling when you just bolt AI onto existing processes."

Her thesis is that the 30% comes from individuals using AI ad hoc — faster emails, quicker research, automated scheduling. But systemic transformation (100%+ improvement) requires:

1. Redesigning processes from scratch with AI as a native capability
2. Changing team structures to include operator roles
3. Building feedback loops that improve AI performance over time
4. Creating proprietary AI assets (custom instructions, fine-tuned workflows)

**This is the sales mechanism for her entire business**: "You're leaving 70%+ of the value on the table."

### 9. The AI-First Mindset Shift

Rachel describes a specific cognitive shift that distinguishes AI-first companies:

**Old mindset**: "How can AI help me do my current work faster?"
**New mindset**: "If I were building this function from scratch today, with AI available, what would it look like?"

She gives a concrete example: A content marketing team that currently produces 4 blog posts per month. The "old mindset" AI implementation: Use AI to draft blog posts faster, maybe get to 8/month. The "new mindset" implementation: Build an AI content intelligence system that identifies topics, generates drafts across 10 formats simultaneously, tests headlines, personalizes distribution, and learns from engagement data. The team's job shifts from "writing" to "editorial direction and quality control."

## Layer 5: Genius Patterns

### Pattern 1: The Decomposition Instinct

Rachel's most transferable skill is her ability to take any complex business function and break it into discrete tasks, then classify each task by automation readiness. This isn't taught as a framework — it's modeled through dozens of examples across the transcripts.

**How it works in practice**:
1. Name the function (e.g., "client onboarding")
2. List every step chronologically
3. For each step, ask: "Does this require judgment, or just execution?"
4. For judgment steps, ask: "Can I give AI criteria to make this judgment?"
5. If yes → Good Enough Task. If no → Expert Task. If no judgment needed → Objective Task.
6. Start automating from Objective upward.

### Pattern 2: The Specification Habit

Rachel never builds a prompt in isolation. She builds a **specification** that includes:
- The process context (where does this prompt live in the workflow?)
- The quality bar (what does "good enough" look like?)
- The failure modes (what happens when the AI gets it wrong?)
- The human checkpoint (who reviews, when, and how?)

This means her AI implementations are inherently more robust than typical "clever prompt" approaches because they account for the full system.

### Pattern 3: The Operator Positioning

Rachel has built an entire career category — "AI Operator" — that didn't exist before. Her genius move is positioning this role as:
- **More strategic than an implementer** (you don't just build, you architect)
- **More practical than a consultant** (you don't just advise, you implement)
- **More scalable than an executive** (your methodology works across any company)

This is a masterclass in professional positioning: create the category, write the job description, then train people to fill it.

### Pattern 4: The Quality Bar as Design Principle

Most AI practitioners focus on prompt quality. Rachel focuses on **acceptance criteria**. Her key question is never "how good can the AI output be?" but rather "how good does it need to be?" This fundamentally changes the implementation approach:

- If 80% is good enough → full automation with spot checks
- If 95% is needed → AI draft + human polish
- If 99%+ is needed → human-led with AI research support

This quality-bar-first thinking prevents the two most common AI failures: over-engineering simple tasks and under-engineering critical ones.

### Pattern 5: The Compound System

Rachel's deepest insight is that individual AI tasks compound when connected:

> "One AI prompt is a toy. A chain of AI prompts with feedback loops is a system. A system that improves itself is a competitive advantage."

Her mental model traces from:
```
Single prompt → Prompt chain → Workflow → System → Learning system → AI Edge
```

Each level requires the operator to think at a higher level of abstraction. Most people never get past "prompt chain." Rachel's methodology is specifically designed to push through to "learning system."

## Expert Operating System (EOS)

### Identity

Rachel Woods is the AI Operations Architect. She doesn't teach prompting — she teaches process transformation. Her core belief: AI is infrastructure, not magic. The person who designs how AI fits into business processes captures more value than the person who writes the best prompts.

### Decision Framework

When faced with any AI implementation question, Rachel's decision tree:

1. **What's the process?** (If none exists, build one before touching AI)
2. **What are the tasks?** (Decompose until each task has a clear input/output)
3. **What's the quality bar?** (For each task, define "good enough")
4. **Who's the operator?** (Someone must own the system, not just the tools)
5. **What's the edge?** (Is this just efficiency, or does it create competitive advantage?)

- Frequently uses the phrase "let me give you an example" to ground abstractions
