## Executive Summary
- **Core Genius**: Proof isn't persuasion — it's the *elimination of doubt*. Iha treats skepticism as a physical force that must be neutralized at specific anatomical points in the sales argument.
- **What Makes Him Different**: He doesn't stack proof randomly. He taxonomizes 22 distinct proof types across 5 psychological categories and matches each to specific doubt types. This is surgical, not shotgun.
- **Deployable Skills**: Complete proof arsenal selection for any sales asset. Ability to diagnose which proof type neutralizes which doubt. Experiential proof design.
- **Hidden Knowledge Captured**: The hierarchy of proof credibility scales with fabrication difficulty. Experiential proof (letting them try it) defeats all other categories.

## Genius Patterns

### 1. The Proof Taxonomy (22 Types × 5 Categories)
- **What They Do Unconsciously**: Categorize all possible proof into psychological, experiential, empirical, credible, and social buckets — then select from each category based on the specific doubt being addressed.
- **Executable Behavior**: Before writing any sales asset, audit which proof categories are represented. If any category has zero entries, that's a vulnerability. Minimum 3 categories per major claim.
- **Deployment Context**: Sales pages, VSLs, email sequences, landing pages — any conversion asset.
- **Success Metric**: Every "Big Claim" has proof from at least 2 non-overlapping categories.

### 2. The Promise-Proof Match
- **What They Do Unconsciously**: Different promise types require different proof categories. A speed promise needs empirical data. A transformation promise needs case studies. A mechanism promise needs demonstrations.
- **Executable Behavior**: Map each promise to its optimal proof category: Speed → Empirical | Transformation → Social/Case Study | Mechanism → Experiential/Visual | Authority → Credible/Third-party | Safety → Psychological/Guarantee.
- **Deployment Context**: Claim construction phase of any sales asset.
- **Success Metric**: No promise stands unsupported by its *matching* proof type.

### 3. The Hard-to-Fake Hierarchy
- **What They Do Unconsciously**: Rank proof by how difficult it is to fabricate. User-generated video > written testimonial > star rating. Published study > internal data > anecdote.
- **Executable Behavior**: For high-stakes claims, always use the hardest-to-fake proof available. If you only have easy-to-fake proof, acknowledge the gap or upgrade the proof.
- **Deployment Context**: CTA proximity, pricing reveals, mechanism claims — anywhere doubt is highest.
- **Success Metric**: The proof at the highest-doubt moments is also the hardest to fabricate.

## Hidden Knowledge

- **Experiential Proof Supremacy**: "Let them experience it" beats all other proof categories. Free trials, live demos, interactive calculators — proof the prospect can *feel* is neurologically different from proof they *read*. This is why SaaS companies with free tiers convert better than those with case studies alone.
- **The 22-Type Arsenal**: Having a taxonomy prevents proof gaps. Most copywriters use 3-4 proof types max. Iha deploys from a menu of 22. The taxonomy itself is a competitive advantage — it forces you to find proof others miss.
- **Proof is Safety (existing)**: The function of proof isn't to convince — it's to make the prospect feel *safe* to act. This reframes the entire copywriting task from "persuasion" to "anxiety elimination."
- **Emotion of Truth (existing)**: The best proof doesn't prove a fact — it produces a feeling. The prospect should feel "god, this is obviously true" before they can articulate why.
- **Visuals > Data (existing)**: A screenshot of a dashboard showing results beats a paragraph describing those same results by 3-5x in conversion impact.
