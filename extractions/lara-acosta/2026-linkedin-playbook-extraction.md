## Genius Patterns

### Pattern 1: Attention Arbitrage Economics
**What She Does Unconsciously**: Frames every platform decision through supply/demand economics, not features.
**Executable Behavior**: Before investing in any platform, calculate: supply of creators vs. demand of consumers. LinkedIn = 1B users consuming, almost none creating. Massive arbitrage.
**Deployment Context**: Platform selection, client conversations about "which platform", content strategy pivots
**Success Metric**: Can articulate the exact economic reason a platform is worth time investment (not just "it works")

### Pattern 2: Three Growth Shape Diagnosis
**What She Does Unconsciously**: Instantly categorizes a creator's trajectory into one of three archetypal shapes — steady, exponential, or identity crisis.
**Executable Behavior**: 
- **Steady**: Consistent, predictable, slow — reliable but won't break through
- **Exponential**: Hockey stick — happens when content-market fit is found
- **Identity Crisis**: Grow → pivot → lose momentum → restart — the slowest because you reset every time you change direction
**Deployment Context**: Auditing any creator's growth trajectory, diagnosing why someone is stuck
**Success Metric**: Correctly diagnose which shape someone is in within 60 seconds of reviewing their profile

### Pattern 3: Inflection Point Hunting
**What She Does Unconsciously**: Identifies that fast growth becomes *predictable* at specific moments — and these moments come from *focus*, not volume.
**Executable Behavior**: "Hitting inflection points isn't about posting more. It happens when you decide to focus on one thing rather than many and make it your purpose to make it work in your niche."
**Deployment Context**: When a creator plateaus and thinks they need more output. The answer is usually more focus, not more content.
**Success Metric**: Growth rate increases after narrowing scope (not expanding it)

### Pattern 4: Profile-as-Algorithm-Signal (2026 Update)
**What She Does Unconsciously**: Treats profile optimization not just as a human trust signal, but as an *algorithm input* — LinkedIn engineering now uses domain expertise signals to decide distribution.
**Executable Behavior**: 
1. Headline = pain you solve + for whom + proof (not title/role)
2. Banner = social proof (clients, logos, reviews) — NOT generic branding
3. Featured section = proof of work
4. **All three must align to a single niche** so LinkedIn's algorithm can categorize you
**Deployment Context**: Profile audits, new client onboarding, quarterly profile refreshes
**Success Metric**: LinkedIn recommends your content to people in your niche (check post analytics for "recommended" distribution)

> **Net-new insight**: LinkedIn's engineering blog now explicitly confirms domain expertise affects algorithmic distribution. Prior extractions treated profile as human-facing only.

### Pattern 5: The "Mental Folder" Positioning Test
**What She Does Unconsciously**: Uses cognitive science — the brain holds ~7 pieces of information at once. If your profile sends mixed signals, people can't categorize you, so they ignore you.
**Executable Behavior**: Run the Mental Folder Test — "If someone forwarded my profile to a friend, could that friend describe what I do in one sentence?" If no → positioning is broken.
**Deployment Context**: Every profile audit, every positioning workshop
**Success Metric**: Pass the "what do you do?" test — if people ask after seeing your content, positioning has failed

### Pattern 6: The Anti-Niche-Trap (Big Creator Nuance)
**What She Does Unconsciously**: Preempts the "but big creators aren't niched!" objection by showing they ARE niched — just broadly within one lane.
**Executable Behavior**: Even broad creators stay in one cognitive lane: Steven Bartlett = entrepreneurship. Justin Welsh = solopreneurship. Lara = making content work. The lane can be wide, but it's ONE lane.
**Deployment Context**: When a creator resists niching because they think it limits them
**Success Metric**: Audience can complete the sentence "I follow [name] because they're the person for ___"

### Pattern 7: The 1+3 Comment Formula (Credited to Jasmin Alic)
**What She Does Unconsciously**: Systematizes commenting as a growth engine, not just "being social."
**Executable Behavior**: 
1. Leave a substantive comment on one post
2. Then leave 3 replies to OTHER people's comments on that same post
3. Result: 4x more profile views daily
**Why it works**: When you reply to someone's comment, *their entire network* sees you — not just the original poster's audience.
**Deployment Context**: Daily engagement routine (30 min/day minimum)
**Success Metric**: Track daily profile views — should increase 2-4x within 30 days

### Pattern 8: The "Post & Ghost" Kill Switch
**What She Does Unconsciously**: Identifies the most destructive behavior on LinkedIn — posting and disappearing.
**Executable Behavior**: "If you can't be there to engage, don't post at all during that day." LinkedIn distributes based on network engagement. No engagement = content dies.
**Protocol**: 
- 20 min engagement BEFORE your post goes live
- Reply to every comment in first 30 minutes after posting
- Engage with industry peers' posts
- Reply to comments on YOUR previous day's post
**Deployment Context**: Content scheduling, daily routine design
**Success Metric**: Average comments per post increases within 2 weeks

### Pattern 9: The Revenue Bridge Architecture
**What She Does Unconsciously**: Separates the funnel into distinct stages where each has a specific job.
**Executable Behavior**: 
```
Content → Profile → Lead Magnet → Email List → Sales
  (attract)  (convert to follower)  (capture email)  (nurture)  (sell)
```
- Content's job: attract the right people. NOT to sell.
- Profile's job: convert visitors to followers.
- Lead magnet's job: capture email addresses.
- Email's job: nurture and sell. **70-80% of revenue comes from email, not LinkedIn.**
**Deployment Context**: Building a monetization system, explaining the path from content to revenue
**Success Metric**: Email list growing weekly from LinkedIn-driven lead magnets

## Hidden Knowledge

1. **The 70-80% Email Rule**: Lara's revenue comes overwhelmingly from email, not LinkedIn directly. LinkedIn is top-of-funnel only. This contradicts the common belief that "LinkedIn converts directly" — it doesn't. It feeds the engine that does.

2. **Comments > Posts for Revenue**: Lara made $10K passively from comments (linking to a Gumroad product in replies). She got speaking invites from comments asking "what conferences should I speak at?" Comments are an under-exploited revenue channel, not just engagement.

3. **The Lead Magnet Simplicity Signal**: "Some took days, some took hours, some under 30 minutes. It doesn't need to be a hyperdesigned thing." The conversion driver is solving one key problem for one key person well — not production quality.

4. **The Audience Segmentation Trap**: Being TOO niched in content alienates followers who could refer you. "My business gets 10 leads every week without outbound" outperforms "If you're a B2B business owner making over $10M a year, here's how..." — the first is accessible, the second segments ruthlessly and kills shareability.

5. **The Network Effect Layer**: LinkedIn works on a network basis — your network must engage for algorithmic distribution. This is fundamentally different from TikTok/Instagram where the algorithm distributes to strangers. On LinkedIn, you must actively *activate* your network.
