**Pattern 1: Experience-Purchase Decoupling**
In every "experiential" store, experience and purchase were separate activities conducted by separate visitors. Photography visitors did not become purchasing visitors. The basketball court selfie-takers left without entering the shoe department.

**Pattern 2: Phone-First Behavior**
Across all locations, customers making purchases consulted phones during transaction. They were confirming prices, checking reviews, comparing to Amazon. The store visit was validation of a decision already made elsewhere, not the decision-making venue.

**Pattern 3: Staff Deployment Mismatch**
"Experience" areas were heavily staffed (DJ booth, customization bars, styling stations). Transaction areas were understaffed. The staff was deployed to where the photography happens, not where the money moves.

**Pattern 4: The Return Motivation**
The only repeat visitors I observed across multiple locations were conducting returns. The physical store's irreplaceable function appeared to be unwinding online transactions‚Äîseeing, touching, and rejecting products that looked different on screen. This is operationally necessary but narratively embarrassing, so it goes unmentioned.

**Actual Customer Journey**: Research online ‚Üí possibly visit store to confirm ‚Üí purchase online or in-store based on shipping speed, not experience. The store is a fitting room for decisions made elsewhere.

**Actual Store Function**: Three purposes emerged that don't appear in retail conference presentations:
1. Returns processing (high-touch, staff-intensive, uninstagrammable)
2. Inventory verification (customer wants to see/touch before buying)
3. Immediate gratification (customer wants item now, not in 2 days)

None of these are "experiences." All of them are logistics.

**Actual Staff Expertise**: The most effective staff interactions I observed were transactional‚Äîhelping customers find sizes, checking back inventory, processing returns efficiently. The "brand ambassador" interactions (storytelling, styling consultations) went longer but produced fewer transactions.

**The Photography Economy**: Store design now optimizes for photography, which generates social media content, which theoretically generates future customers. ### STRATEGIC IMPLICATIONS

**Experiential Retail Assessment**: The "experience" investment is producing content, not commerce. Stores optimized for Instagram generate Instagram content for visitors, not customers. The ROI math only works if photography converts to future purchase‚Äîa connection no one I observed was able to demonstrate.

- Stores that minimize friction to known-item purchase
- Stores that enable efficient returns processing
- Stores that maintain deep inventory for immediate availability
- Stores where staff expertise is product-functional, not narrative

**Strategic Recommendation**: Retail transformation should optimize for the jobs customers actually hire physical stores to do: touch products before committing, get items immediately, resolve problems with previous purchases. The "community hub" and "experience destination" positioning is generating press coverage and photography, not comparable store sales.

**The Honest Repositioning**: Physical retail's sustainable advantage is logistics‚Äîthe irreducible physicality of products meeting bodies. Everything else can happen online. Stores that embrace their logistics function (fitting room, return center, immediate availability) will outperform stores that pretend to be community centers.

**Medium Confidence**: Phone-first behavior is category-wide, not store-specific. Staff deployment is optimized for metrics that don't correlate with revenue.

**Low Confidence**: Long-term impact of photography/content generation on brand value. Whether any retailer has successfully connected experiential investment to same-store sales growth.

**Recommended Intelligence Gathering**: Access to retailer same-store sales data correlated with experiential investment. Photography-to-purchase conversion tracking if any retailer has implemented it.

# Letter from San Francisco, 2024

The best meal I had this year was in Taipei, at a basement restaurant where the chef served seventeen courses over four hours to twelve people who didn't speak each other's languages. The dishes arrived without explanation‚Äîif you didn't know what you were eating, the chef wasn't going to help you. Somewhere around course eleven, a broth so clear it looked like water but tasted like the distilled essence of decades of practice, I realized this was the opposite of what I'd been seeing all year in technology.

In AI, everything is explained. Every product launches with a narrative, a positioning document, a series of tweets explaining what it means and why it matters. The technology is real‚ÄîI've seen it transform workflows that used to take hours into tasks that take minutes‚Äîbut it arrives pre-interpreted. The demos come with the interpretation built in: this changes everything, this is the future, this is why you should care.

The chef's broth required nothing from me except presence. It either worked on my palate or it didn't. The experience was unmediated by narrative. I've been thinking about that broth all year, because almost nothing else I encountered operated that way.

---

**I. The Year of Performed Conviction**

The question I heard most often in 2024‚Äîat dinners, in meetings, in the particular tone of voice people use when they want to sound casual about something they're deeply anxious about‚Äîwas some variant of: "Is this all real?"

The "this" was AI, but the question was about more than technology. It was about whether the conviction everyone was performing was actually conviction, or whether we were all looking at each other to figure out how to feel.

I spent time this year with founders who've raised hundreds of millions of dollars for AI companies. Many of them, in private moments, express uncertainty about whether their products will matter in two years. The technology is moving too fast to build durable moats. The thing they launched in March was obsolete by September. They're raising money to survive long enough to figure out what to build.

This is not the same as the public narrative. The public narrative is about transformation, inevitability, the obvious trajectory toward intelligence that surpasses human capability. The private narrative is about tactical survival in conditions of profound uncertainty.

I don't think the founders are lying. I think they hold both truths simultaneously‚Äîthe conviction required to raise money and recruit talent, and the uncertainty that keeps them up at night. The performance of conviction is necessary for the enterprise; it's not incompatible with genuine doubt.

But the gap between public performance and private uncertainty is wider this year than I've seen it in a decade of observing technology companies. The hype cycle is not news; what's new is how many people inside the hype privately believe they're inside a hype cycle.

---

The official story goes something like this: AI capabilities are improving exponentially. Models are getting smarter at an accelerating rate. What was impossible in 2022 is trivial in 2024; what's trivial in 2024 will look primitive by 2026. The trajectory is clear.

The ground truth I observed was messier. Yes, the capabilities are improving. The models can do things that genuinely surprise me. But the improvement curve is less smooth than the narrative suggests. Benchmarks are plateauing. The easy gains have been captured. The models are becoming more expensive to train, not less. The "scaling hypothesis"‚Äîthe belief that more data and more compute automatically produce better intelligence‚Äîis meeting real-world resistance.

More interesting to me than the capability question is the deployment question. How are these models actually being used?

I visited a dozen companies this year that had integrated AI into their workflows. The pattern was consistent: the technology worked better than expected for structured, repeatable tasks. It worked worse than expected for anything requiring judgment about ambiguous situations. The gap between demo and deployment remained significant.

The demos show the model at its best, on problems selected to showcase its strengths. The deployment environments expose its failure modes‚Äîthe hallucinations, the confident wrongness, the brittleness when inputs vary from training distribution. The people using these tools daily are more skeptical than the people selling them.

---

**II. What's Actually Changing**

If I had to summarize what I observed actually changing in 2024‚Äînot in press releases, but in the lived experience of people doing work‚ÄîI'd highlight three things:

The first is the death of the first draft. For many categories of writing‚Äîemails, reports, documentation, initial analyses‚Äîthe human role has shifted from creator to editor. People generate drafts with AI assistance and then revise. This is not trivial. The psychological experience of starting with something rather than nothing transforms the emotional character of work. The blank page problem is solved, even if what's on the page requires substantial revision.

This sounds like productivity gain, and it is, but it's also a loss. The first draft is where you discover what you think. When the machine generates the first draft, you're responding to its frame rather than creating your own. I've watched people adopt AI-generated structures that they would never have chosen independently, simply because the structure was there and it was easier to edit than to reimagine.

The second change is in search behavior. People are asking AI systems questions they used to type into Google‚Äîand the AI answers directly rather than providing links to sources. This is faster, but it removes the serendipity of search. When you Google a question, you encounter adjacent information; you fall into rabbit holes; you find sources that reframe your question. When AI answers directly, the exploration collapses. You get the answer (or "answer") without the journey.

The third change is in how people think about their skills. I talked to many people this year who are questioning which of their capabilities will remain valuable. Lawyers, writers, analysts, programmers‚Äîthey're all running the same calculation: which parts of what I do can be automated, and which parts can't? This is not new‚Äîevery technology shift triggers this questioning‚Äîbut the speed and breadth of AI capability is creating widespread existential uncertainty in knowledge professions that previously felt secure.

---

**III. The Reading Year**

My reading this year was shaped by trying to understand intelligence‚Äîboth the artificial kind and the human kind that's supposedly being replicated.

I returned to Michael Polanyi's *The Tacit Dimension*, written in 1966, which argues that human knowledge contains an irreducible component that cannot be articulated. "We know more than we can tell," Polanyi wrote. His example: we recognize faces instantly, but we cannot describe what features we're detecting that enable recognition. The knowledge is embodied, implicit, resistant to specification.

The AI systems we're building operate on the opposite principle‚Äîeverything must be explicit, tokenized, represented in a form the model can process. They're extraordinarily good at pattern matching over explicit representations. They struggle with the tacit.

This explains, I think, why AI performs better at chess than at cooking dinner for friends. Chess is fully specified; the rules are complete; nothing is tacit. Cooking dinner for friends involves reading the room, adjusting to unexpected preferences, improvising when ingredients aren't available, knowing when to keep the meal going and when to transition to dessert. The tacit dimension is large.

I also read Albert Hirschman's *Exit, Voice, and Loyalty*, a 1970 book about how people respond to declining quality in organizations and products. They can exit (leave), voice (complain), or stay loyal despite decline. Hirschman argued that voice‚Äîthe attempt to improve a situation from within‚Äîis underrated relative to exit.

I thought about this in the context of AI companies. The engineers who are most skeptical of their employers' public narratives are not exiting. They're staying, often quietly, collecting equity, hoping things work out. Voice is muted because the cost of public skepticism is high. The selection pressure keeps internal critics silent, which means leadership receives less accurate information about what's actually true.

---

**IV. The Food Interlude**

The Taipei meal was the highlight, but the year in food was broader.

In Hong Kong in March, I ate at a dai pai dong‚Äîan open-air street food stall‚Äîthat served claypot rice with Chinese sausage. The owner, who must have been eighty, worked the charcoal braziers with a precision that reminded me of a conductor working an orchestra. Each claypot required different timing; she managed twelve simultaneously, adjusting heat by moving coals with bare fingers that had clearly stopped feeling heat decades ago.

She was performing a skill that took a lifetime to develop. I watched customers photograph her, post to social media, consume the aesthetic of the experience. I wondered how many of them understood what they were looking at‚Äînot the food, but the embodied expertise. The decades compressed into movements so practiced they appeared effortless.

This is what I want to preserve in a world of AI-generated content: the recognition that some things cannot be shortcut. The chef's expertise, the writer's voice, the judgment that emerges from years of confronting ambiguous situations‚Äîthese are not computational problems. They are accumulations of experience that resist compression.

---

**V. What Builders Should Focus On**

If I were advising someone building in AI today, I'd suggest three orientations:

First, solve problems where the tacit dimension is small. The opportunities are in structured, specifiable tasks where AI's pattern matching can operate without human judgment. Workflow automation, code generation for well-defined problems, document processing‚Äîthese are domains where the technology's strengths align with the task requirements.

Second, build for the skeptical user. The people who've been using AI tools for a year are less impressed than they were when they started. They've encountered the failure modes. They've learned where to trust and where to verify. The products that will last are the ones that earn trust from experienced users, not the ones that wow naive users with demos.

Third, maintain optionality. The technology is changing faster than business models can stabilize. The companies that survive will be the ones that can pivot quickly when the next capability shift arrives. Conviction about the long-term direction of AI should not translate into rigidity about specific product bets.

---

The year ended as it began, with more questions than answers. The broth in Taipei keeps returning to mind‚Äîsomething that worked without explanation, that required no interpretation, that simply was what it was.

I'm hopeful that we can build technology that earns that quality of presence. But most of what I saw this year was technology that came pre-packaged with narrative, seeking to convince before it demonstrated. The convincing and the demonstrating are not the same thing, and the gap between them is where the most honest work remains to be done.
