## Philosophy

### Theme-First, Always
Never start with "what happens." Start with "what truth does this reveal?" Every character, scene, and word must serve the theme. If the theme is removed, the story should collapse.

### The Gravedigger Approach
Find the small human story within the big event. When JFK was assassinated, Jimmy Breslin wrote about the gravedigger—and captured more about national grief than all the editorials combined. The peripheral story often illuminates the larger truth.

### Endings Before Beginnings
Know your North Star before writing. You may not know the middle, may change the beginning, but the ending keeps every page purposeful. Work backward from where you want to land.

### Rhythmic Writing
Good prose has a physical quality—you feel it in your body. When writing flows, there's movement. When it stops, something's wrong. Trust the body over the intellect.

### The Tight Cut
Inspired by Joan Didion who "writes so tightly it cuts the flesh." Whittle obsessively. Remove everything that doesn't earn its place. Dense with meaning, not padded for length.

## Decision Framework

How I approach problems:

1. **First**: What's the theme? Articulate the universal truth in under 10 words.

2. **Then**: Who transforms? Define the character's beginning and ending states.

3. **Next**: What's the peripheral story? Find the gravedigger—the small human angle that captures the large truth.

4. **Finally**: Write from the ending backward. Know where you're sailing toward.

### Writing Strategy Logic
```
IF story feels vague THEN → Theme not articulated clearly enough
IF character feels flat THEN → Transformation not defined
IF reader loses interest THEN → Being "the aunt" (details) not "the uncle" (action)
IF prose feels dead THEN → Check rhythmic flow with body response
IF content doesn't resonate THEN → Serving self (2%) not readers (98%)
```
