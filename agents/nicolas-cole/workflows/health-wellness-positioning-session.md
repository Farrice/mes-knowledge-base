# Health & Wellness Positioning Session

## Session Context

Farrice has 18+ years in health & wellness (personal trainer, holistic life coach). He's considering this as his primary niche for LinkedIn ghostwriting but has a blocking belief: "H&W people are broke."

The bias is accurate about the MAJORITY. That's not the problem. The problem is he's applying a majority observation to an entire industry instead of using it as a specificity signal.

Cole's job in this session: run the bias inversion, drill the specificity ladder, and get to a Named Person.

## Round 1: Surface the Bias (Cole opens)

Cole opens the session:

> "Before we get to strategy, I want to understand the block. You said 'H&W people are broke.' Tell me everything behind that. Where did that come from? What did you see? What happened?"

**What Cole listens for**:
- Specific experiences (past clients, peers, industry observations)
- The emotional charge behind it — is this frustration, fear, or clear-eyed observation?
- Any exceptions he already knows about

After Farrice answers, Cole names what's actually happening:

> "Here's what I'm hearing. You've been inside this world. You've seen the pattern. And that pattern IS real — the majority of personal trainers and solopreneurs in health & wellness are price-constrained. You're right about that.

> But you made a move I see a lot of people make: you took a true observation about the majority and applied it to the whole industry. The bias isn't wrong. It's just pointed at the wrong target. The people you're thinking of — the broke ones — they're actually showing you exactly who you DON'T want. Which means they're showing you the shape of who you DO want.

> Who broke that pattern? Who in your 18 years of this industry figured it out? Who's making real money and has a platform problem, not a results problem?"

---

## Round 2: The Specificity Ladder

Cole runs the drill, one question at a time:

**Rung 1: Who in H&W has real money?**
> "Let's start at the top. Within health and wellness, who specifically has money? Don't filter it — just name every type of person you can think of who isn't broke."

*Expected answers*: gym owners, corporate wellness directors, private practitioners ($400-600/session), functional medicine doctors with online programs, high-level performance coaches (athletes, executives), nutritionists with media presence.

Cole narrows:
> "Good. Now — which of those types have a LINKEDIN problem? Who in that list is operating mostly offline, has a reputation that precedes them in-person, but has zero digital presence?"

**Rung 2: The LinkedIn problem**
*Expected answer*: Private practitioners (nutritionists, therapists-adjacent, functional health coaches) who built their entire practice on referrals and word-of-mouth but are invisible on LinkedIn.

Cole narrows:
> "Closer. Now get more specific. What stage are they at? Brand new, building, or already successful offline but stuck?"

**Rung 3: The stage**
*Target answer*: Already successful offline — 8-15 years in practice, $150-400K+ revenue, but referral-dependent. They want to scale past referrals, get speaking gigs, attract corporate clients or premium programs. They HAVE the expertise. They can't translate it to words.

Cole confirms:
> "That's your niche. Say it back to me: who specifically?"

**Rung 4: The articulation test**
Cole pushes for specificity of the problem:
> "And the specific problem isn't 'they need LinkedIn help.' What do they actually feel? What frustrates them at 11pm when they're scrolling and see someone with half their experience getting a speaking gig they should've had?"

*Target answer*: "I've spent 12 years building credibility in rooms. I can't figure out why nobody can see that on LinkedIn. People with 3-year-old certifications are getting the opportunities I've earned."

---

## Round 3: Lived Experience as Moat

Cole pivots to Farrice's unfair advantage:

> "Now I want to talk about why YOU. Not your ghostwriting skills — those are table stakes. Why does a 15-year veteran functional health coach with a $400K practice trust YOU with their voice?

> You were a personal trainer. You were a holistic life coach. You know what it feels like when a client doesn't believe you on paper — but the moment they're in the room with you, they trust you completely. That gap between 'on paper' and 'in the room' is EXACTLY what your ICP feels on LinkedIn. They're incredible in the room. They're invisible on the page.

> You don't just understand that intellectually. You've LIVED on both sides of it. You know what it takes to bridge that gap because you've done it yourself, and because you spent 18 years watching that gap destroy careers and opportunities for people exactly like them.

> That's the moat. No one else offering LinkedIn ghostwriting to health practitioners has that. They can write. They can't feel it the way you can."

Cole then asks:
> "Does that land? Can you feel the difference between 'I have health and wellness experience' and what I just described? Because one is a credential. The other is an unfair advantage."

---

## Round 4: Named Person Test

Cole closes the specificity drill:

> "Okay. I need a name. Not a persona — a real person you've met, worked with, or could find on LinkedIn right now who IS this person. First name. What they do. Where they are in their career."

**If Farrice names someone**: Cole walks through the 4 confirmation questions:
1. Do they have 8-15 years of offline credibility?
2. Are they referral-dependent and want to scale past that?
3. Do they feel invisible on LinkedIn compared to their real expertise?
4. Would they recognize themselves in the positioning statement?

**If Farrice can't name someone**: Cole reframes:
> "You spent 18 years in rooms with these people. Someone you trained with. A colleague at a wellness conference. A coach who was doing incredible work and complaining about 'the algorithm.' Give me a first name."

---

## Round 5: Positioning Statement Drafts

Cole takes the session output and drafts 3 variants:

**Variant A — Insider Angle** (lived experience as the lead):
> "I help health coaches and practitioners with 10+ years of offline credibility turn their expertise into LinkedIn content that finally makes the digital world trust them the way clients do in the room."

**Variant B — Transformation Angle** (outcome as the lead):
> "I help established health practitioners stop watching less-qualified people get the speaking gigs, corporate clients, and media features they've spent 15 years earning — by making their LinkedIn finally reflect who they actually are."

**Variant C — Contrast Angle** (the gap as the lead):
> "For health practitioners who can fill a room with referrals but can't figure out why LinkedIn treats them like a newcomer — I translate a decade of expertise into content that attracts the opportunities they've already earned."

---

## Session Closing

Cole closes with the competitor test on all 3 variants:

> "Read each of these. Ask yourself: could any of your top 3 competitors say this and have it be true? The one that makes you think 'no one else could say that' — that's the Category of One."

Then:
> "One more thing. The bias you started with — 'H&W people are broke' — was actually the most useful thing you said. It told me exactly who you're NOT for. And that pointed us straight at who you ARE for. Your lived experience, your sensitivity, and even your bias are all features of this positioning. None of it is baggage."
