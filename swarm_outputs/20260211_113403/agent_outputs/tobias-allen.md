**SUMMARY**
The shift from "The Rebuild" to "The Resurrection" moves the brand from a commodity "fix-it" service to a premium "optimization" category. By framing the transition as the reactivation of dormant power rather than the repair of a broken man, we align the marketing with the psychology of a $10K executive buyer who views himself as elite but currently under-leveraged.

**KEY FINDINGS**
- **The Power of "Latent Potential":** High-performers are more likely to invest in reclaiming a "lost edge" than in fixing a "weakness." Shame-based marketing (e.g., "coward") creates defensive friction; diagnostic marketing (e.g., "corrupted code") creates curiosity and a desire for a solution.
- **Identity Arbitrage:** "The Resurrection" implies that the greatness already exists within the client—it just needs the right environment and protocol to resurface. This lowers the perceived effort of the transformation while increasing its value.
- **Biological Hardware vs. Strategic Software:** By framing the "body-first" approach as hardware maintenance, we bypass the "I’m not an athlete" objection and position physical optimization as a non-negotiable prerequisite for executive performance.

**DISSENT**
While "Resurrection" is a powerful, premium term, it carries heavy religious and "back from the dead" connotations. To ensure it doesn't default back to "crisis" language, every piece of content must heavily anchor the word to **"Reactivation"** and **"Optimization."** If the audience perceives "Resurrection" as "I am currently dead/failing," the $10K executive lead will bounce. We must maintain the frame that they are *already successful*, just *under-powered*.
