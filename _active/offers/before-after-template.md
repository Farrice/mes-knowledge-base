# Before/After Post Template

> Use this format to turn demos into content for YOUR LinkedIn.
> Each demo you run should produce at least 1 post for your feed.
>
> **Source workflow**: `skills/ghostwriting-voice-engine/workflows/03-unsolicited-demo.md`

## Template A: The Transformation Post

> **Use after EVERY demo.** This is the most concrete and compelling format.
> Shows the transformation with real examples.

Copy-paste this and fill in the blanks:

---

A [niche] coach wrote this on LinkedIn:

"[2-3 lines from their BEFORE post -- the weakest part]"

Good insight. But it sounds like every other [niche] coach on the platform.

Here's the same idea, rewritten to sound like THEM:

"[2-3 lines from your AFTER rewrite -- the strongest part]"

Same message. Same person.

The difference?

The first one came from staring at a blank screen.
The second came from a 30-minute call where I captured
how they actually think and teach.

Most coaches don't have a content problem.
They have a translation problem.

The expertise is there. The ideas are there.
The gap is between what they know
and how it shows up on LinkedIn.

DM me "POSTS" if you're a coach who's tired of
sounding generic online.

---

### Template A -- Formatting Notes

- The before/after quotes should be SHORT (2-3 lines max). Pick the most dramatic contrast.
- Put the before quote first. Let the reader feel the "meh." Then hit them with the after.
- The "Same message. Same person." line creates the aha moment. Don't rush past it.
- Keep the CTA to "POSTS" -- one word, easy to type.

## Template B: The Process Post

> **Use when you want to show the system working.**
> Good for demonstrating your methodology without revealing every step.

---

I just rewrote 3 LinkedIn posts for a [niche] coach.

They didn't ask me to. I just did it.

Here's what I noticed about their content:

Their ideas were sharp.
Their frameworks were original.
Their posts could have been written by anyone.

The problem isn't talent. It's translation.

When I rewrote their posts using patterns
from their OWN best content, the difference
was night and day.

[1-2 sentences about what specifically changed.
Example: "Their strongest insight was always buried
in paragraph 3. I moved it to line 1 and restructured
the rest as proof."]

This is what ghostwriting actually is:

Not writing FOR someone.
Capturing how they think,
then expressing it better than they would
on a random Tuesday afternoon.

If you're a coach posting content that doesn't
reflect how good you actually are --

DM me "POSTS."

---

### Template B -- Formatting Notes

- Keep it short. This post works because of the rhythm, not the length.
- The specific detail (what you changed) is critical -- without it, the post is all claim and no proof.
- "Not writing FOR someone" reframes ghostwriting. This handles the biggest objection (inauthenticity) before it's raised.

## Template C: The Data Post

> **Use after doing 3+ demos.** You need enough pattern data to generalize.
> This positions you as someone who studies the space, not just sells a service.

---

I analyzed [X] LinkedIn posts from [number] [niche] coaches.

Here's what I found:

The coaching was world-class.
The content was interchangeable.

3 patterns showed up in almost every profile:

1. [Specific observation about a common pattern.
   Example: "Their best insight was never in the first line.
   It was buried in paragraph 3 or 4."]

2. [Specific observation about a missed opportunity.
   Example: "They all had signature frameworks
   they use with clients -- none of them showed up
   in their LinkedIn content."]

3. [Specific observation about the voice gap.
   Example: "Their comments on other people's posts
   sounded like a completely different person
   than the one writing their own posts.
   More natural. More direct. More them."]

That gap has a name: The Voice Gap.

You sound brilliant on calls.
Your LinkedIn sounds like everyone else.

The fix isn't "write more."
The fix is capturing how you actually think
and building content from THAT.

DM me "POSTS" to see what your content sounds like
when it captures how you actually think.

---

### Template C -- Formatting Notes

- The numbered list must contain SPECIFIC, concrete observations. Not generic claims.
- "The Voice Gap" is a concept worth repeating across multiple posts. It becomes your language.
- This post works best when the observations surprise the reader -- things they wouldn't have noticed themselves.
- Adjust the number of coaches/posts analyzed based on your actual data. Don't inflate.

## Usage Rules

1. **Template A after EVERY demo** -- it's the most concrete and compelling.
2. **Template B** when you want to show the process and position ghostwriting correctly.
3. **Template C after 3+ demos** -- you need real data to generalize.
4. **Anonymize** unless the coach gives explicit permission to name them.
5. **Always end with the same CTA**: DM me "POSTS" -- consistency builds recognition.
6. **Post 2-3x per week** from demos alongside your regular content calendar.
7. **Never stack two before/after posts back to back** -- alternate with other content types.
8. **The before quote must be real** -- do not exaggerate or cherry-pick the worst possible line to make the coach look bad. Pick representative weakness, not worst-case.

---

## Adaptation Notes

**If you have permission to name the coach:**
- Replace "A [niche] coach" with their name and tag them.
- They'll likely engage with the post, which boosts reach.
- Only do this if the framing is generous (showing their potential, not their problems).

**If the rewrite is for a specific niche:**
- Add niche-specific language to the CTA.
- Example: "DM me 'POSTS' if you're an executive coach whose LinkedIn doesn't match your boardroom presence."

**If engagement on before/after posts is strong:**
- Create a series: "Voice Gap #1," "Voice Gap #2," etc.
- The series format trains your audience to expect and look for these posts.
