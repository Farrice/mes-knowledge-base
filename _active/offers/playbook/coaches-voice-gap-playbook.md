## It's Not Laziness. It's Neuroscience.

Here's something you need to hear before we go any further:

The reason your LinkedIn content doesn't sound like you has nothing to do with discipline, motivation, or being "a content person."

It's cognitive. Your brain is literally working against you.

Researchers at MIT Sloan have documented a phenomenon called the **Curse of Knowledge**: once you deeply understand something, you become neurologically incapable of seeing it from a beginner's perspective. Your brain has compressed years of expertise into intuitive shortcuts. When you try to explain those shortcuts in writing, you either oversimplify into something generic or overcomplicate into something your audience can't follow.

This is why the coach with 14 years of experience writes posts that sound less compelling than the coach with 3 years. The 3-year coach still remembers what it felt like to NOT understand. The 14-year coach has forgotten what confusion feels like.

You can't undo this by trying harder. It's a cognitive bias, not a character flaw.

What you CAN do is build a system that captures how you communicate when you're NOT writing -- when you're coaching, when you're explaining your methodology to a friend, when you're fired up about what your industry gets wrong.

That's what the rest of this playbook is for.

But first, let's name the three specific ways the Curse of Knowledge breaks your content.

---

## Translation Failure #1: Abstraction Drift

**What it is:** You talk about your frameworks at the level of a peer, not a prospect. Your LinkedIn audience isn't your mastermind group.

**What it sounds like:**

> *"My integrative leadership model uses a three-phase approach to help executives align their internal values framework with their external behavioral patterns through systematic self-observation and iterative feedback loops."*

That's how you'd explain it to another coach who already speaks your language. It makes perfect sense to you. It makes your mastermind group nod.

It makes a prospect's eyes glaze over in 0.4 seconds.

**What it should sound like:**

> *"Most executives know exactly what kind of leader they want to be. They just can't figure out why they keep acting like a different one when the pressure hits. I help them close that gap -- usually in about 3 months."*

Same methodology. Same framework. But the second version starts where the prospect IS, not where you are.

**The fix:** When you write about your methodology, pretend you're explaining it to a smart friend who's never been in your industry. Not a dumb person. A smart person who lacks YOUR specific context.

The Voice Dump in Part 3 forces this naturally -- because when you talk into a voice memo, you instinctively adjust your language down from peer-level to human-level.

---

## Translation Failure #2: Voice Flattening

**What it is:** When you sit down to write, you strip out everything that makes your coaching voice compelling -- the rhythm, the emphasis, the pauses, the storytelling, the "let me reframe that" moments. What's left is flat, safe, and forgettable.

**What it sounds like:**

> *"Here are 5 tips for better leadership communication:*
> *1. Listen actively*
> *2. Be present in conversations*
> *3. Show empathy to your team*
> *4. Communicate your vision clearly*
> *5. Follow up consistently"*

Now here's the thing -- you would NEVER coach like that. On a call, if a client said "I'm struggling with my team," you wouldn't respond with "5 tips for better leadership communication." You'd lean forward. You'd ask a question. You'd tell a story about a client who had the exact same problem. You'd reframe their situation in a way that made them see it differently. You'd use a metaphor that stuck.

THAT is your voice. And it disappears the second you open a LinkedIn text box.

**What it should sound like:**

> *"A client told me last week: 'My team doesn't listen to me.'*
>
> *I asked her one question: 'When was the last time you listened to them? Not waiting-for-your-turn-to-talk listening. Actually-changed-your-mind listening.'*
>
> *Silence.*
>
> *That's where leadership communication starts. Not with a framework. With a mirror."*

Same coach. Same expertise. But the second version has rhythm, tension, a specific moment, and a reframe. That's what you sound like on your best day. That's what's missing from your posts.

**The fix:** Stop writing. Start talking. Record yourself answering the prompts in Part 3, then extract the moments where you got animated, specific, and real. THOSE are your posts. Not the flattened, "content" version of your thinking.

---

## Translation Failure #3: Template Trapping

**What it is:** You default to formats you've seen other coaches use -- "5 tips for..." / "3 mistakes you're making..." / "The secret to..." -- instead of formats that match how you actually think and teach.

**What it sounds like:**

> *"3 Signs You Need Better Boundaries:*
> *1. You feel exhausted after conversations*
> *2. You say yes when you mean no*
> *3. You avoid difficult people instead of engaging them*
>
> *Boundaries aren't walls. They're bridges to better relationships.*
>
> *Like this post if you agree! Follow for more."*

There are 47,000 versions of this post on LinkedIn right now. Different coaches, same template. You could swap the byline to any of them and nobody would notice.

This happens because when you sit down to write, you unconsciously pattern-match to what you've seen. And what you've seen is a lot of templated content from coaches who are also stuck in templates. It's circular.

**What it should sound like:**

> *"I had a client fire her biggest referral source last month.*
>
> *Not because the referrals were bad. Because every time that person called, my client needed two hours to recover from the conversation.*
>
> *We did the math: 12 referrals a year at $5K each = $60,000.*
> *48 recovery hours a year at her effective rate = $72,000 in lost productivity.*
>
> *She was paying $12,000 a year to keep a relationship that was 'good for business.'*
>
> *Boundaries aren't about saying no to bad people. They're about doing the math on expensive relationships.*
>
> *Sometimes the most profitable thing you can do is subtract."*

Same topic -- boundaries. But the second version is a STORY that only this coach could tell, with specific numbers, a real scenario, and an insight that makes you think. Nobody else would write this post because nobody else has this story.

**The fix:** Your unique format isn't "5 tips." Your unique format is however you naturally teach -- through stories, through reframes, through provocative questions, through client scenarios. The templates in Part 4 are built around these natural teaching patterns, not around LinkedIn cliches.

---

## The Core Problem (And The Way Out)

All three translation failures trace back to one root issue:

**You're trying to create content by writing. You should be creating content by talking.**

Your expertise lives in conversation. That's where you're brilliant -- in the dynamic, responsive, alive space of a coaching call where you can feel the other person's energy and adjust in real time.

LinkedIn requires text. But text doesn't have to start as text.

The most authentic content you'll ever create starts as YOUR VOICE -- recorded, transcribed, and shaped into posts that preserve how you actually think.

That's what Part 3 gives you.

# PART 3: THE 30-MINUTE VOICE DUMP FRAMEWORK

### Pages 13-18

---

## What You're About to Do

You're going to record yourself talking for 30 minutes. No writing. No editing. No overthinking. Just your voice, a phone, and 7 prompts designed to surface your best thinking.

This is a simplified version of the voice extraction process used by professional ghostwriters. It works because it captures you TALKING -- the format where you're already brilliant -- instead of asking you to write, which is where the translation breaks down.

---

## Setup

**What you need:**
- Your phone's voice memo app (or any recording tool)
- 30 uninterrupted minutes
- A quiet space where you won't self-censor

**Ground rules:**
- Don't prep. Don't rehearse. Don't write bullet points first.
- Talk like you're explaining to a smart friend at dinner -- not presenting at a conference.
- If you get stuck on a prompt, skip it and come back.
- If you go on a tangent, LET IT RUN. Tangents are where the gold lives.
- Aim for about 4 minutes per prompt. Some will be 2 minutes, some will be 6. That's fine.

**Hit record. Then read the first prompt.**

---

## The 7 Voice Dump Prompts

### Prompt 1: The Dinner Party Explanation
*~4 minutes*

**"Explain your core coaching methodology to someone who's never heard of you -- the way you'd explain it to a friend at dinner."**

Don't explain what coaching IS. Explain what YOU do that's different. What's your thing? What's the framework, the approach, the angle that makes your clients say "this is different from every other coach I've worked with"?

Talk about it the way you'd talk about it after a glass of wine with someone who genuinely wants to understand.

*What you're capturing: Your core message in its most natural, unfiltered form. This is often the seed of your "About" section and your go-to introductory post.*

---

### Prompt 2: The Almost-Didn't Client
*~4 minutes*

**"Tell me about a client who almost didn't work with you -- but then had a breakthrough."**

What was their objection or hesitation? What were they struggling with before they started? What happened during your work together? What was the moment it shifted?

Tell it like a story. Beginning, middle, turning point.

*What you're capturing: A narrative arc that demonstrates your value without you ever having to say "I'm good at what I do." These stories become your most powerful posts.*

---

### Prompt 3: The Industry Rant
*~4 minutes*

**"What does everyone in your industry get wrong?"**

Not a polite disagreement. What genuinely frustrates you about how other people in your space think, teach, or sell? What's the accepted wisdom that you know is incomplete, outdated, or flat-out wrong?

Get specific. Name the belief. Explain why it's wrong. Say what's true instead.

*What you're capturing: Your contrarian perspective. This is LinkedIn gold -- posts that challenge conventional thinking consistently outperform posts that repeat it. And your contrarian take is uniquely yours because it's born from YOUR experience.*

---

### Prompt 4: The Opening Move
*~4 minutes*

**"What's the first thing you'd tell a new client who comes to you with [your most common client problem]?"**

Think about the problem your clients show up with most often. The thing they say in the first 10 minutes of a discovery call. Now -- what do you actually say back? Not the polished version. The real one.

What's the reframe you give them? The question you ask that shifts how they see the problem? The thing you say before you even start coaching them?

*What you're capturing: Your signature opening insight -- the thing that makes prospects lean forward. This becomes a "Let Me Show You How I Think" post (Template 5 in Part 4).*

---

### Prompt 5: The Signature Insight
*~4 minutes*

**"What's the thing you say to clients that makes them go 'nobody's ever explained it like that before'?"**

Every coach has at least one. The metaphor, the reframe, the way of explaining something that makes clients stop mid-sentence. The thing your clients quote back to you weeks later.

What is it? And why does it land?

*What you're capturing: Your most powerful intellectual property. This is the kind of content that gets shared, saved, and screenshot-DM'd to friends. It's the reason people will follow you on LinkedIn.*

---

### Prompt 6: The Honest Failure
*~4 minutes*

**"Tell me about a time your framework didn't work -- and what you learned."**

Not a disguised humble-brag. A real moment where your approach fell short. A client it didn't serve well. A situation that forced you to evolve your methodology.

What happened? What did you change? What do you know now that you didn't know then?

*What you're capturing: Vulnerability that builds trust. Coaches who only share wins sound like infomercials. Coaches who share what they learned from failure sound like people worth trusting with your career.*

---

### Prompt 7: The One-Concept Legacy
*~4 minutes*

**"If you could only teach one concept for the rest of your career, what would it be?"**

Not a list. Not a framework with 7 pillars. ONE idea. The thing that, if every person you ever worked with truly understood it, would make the biggest difference.

What is it? Why that one? What does the world look like when people actually get it?

*What you're capturing: Your deepest conviction. This becomes the kind of post that defines your entire presence on LinkedIn -- the one people screenshot and share because it cuts through everything else.*

## How to Extract Content From Your Voice Dump

### Step 1: Listen Back (30 minutes)

Play your recording. As you listen, flag every moment where you:

- **Got animated** -- your energy shifted, your pace increased, your voice got more intense
- **Used a specific example** -- a client story, a number, a concrete scenario
- **Said something surprising** -- even to yourself. A phrase you didn't plan.
- **Made a reframe** -- took a common assumption and flipped it
- **Used a metaphor or analogy** -- compared something complex to something simple

Mark the timestamp for each flagged moment. You'll have 8-15 of these.

### Step 2: Transcribe the Flagged Sections

You don't need to transcribe the whole 30 minutes. Just the flagged moments. Use:
- Otter.ai (free tier works)
- Your phone's built-in transcription
- Or just type out the key phrases while listening

You want the EXACT WORDS you used. Not a cleaned-up version. Your raw phrasing is the whole point.

### Step 3: Sort Into Content Pillars

Take your transcribed snippets and sort them into these categories:

| Content Pillar | What Goes Here | Example From Your Dump |
|---------------|---------------|----------------------|
| **Frameworks** | Your methodology, your process, your approach to solving problems | Prompt 1 and 4 material |

| **Contrarian Takes** | What you disagree with, what you'd change about your industry | Prompt 3 material |
| **Signature Insights** | Your unique way of explaining things, your "aha moment" generators | Prompt 5 and 7 material |

## TEMPLATE 1: THE FRUSTRATION MIRROR

**What it does:** Starts with a specific frustration your audience is living in. Reframes it. Shows the real problem underneath. Makes them feel seen, not lectured.

**Why it works:** Coaches scroll LinkedIn feeling their frustrations alone. When someone names the frustration with precision -- in the language they'd use in their own head -- they stop scrolling. That's the "how did you know?" effect.

**Word count:** 150-250 words

---

### The Structure

```
[HOOK: A specific frustration in their inner monologue voice -- 1-2 lines]

That's not a [common label for the problem].
That's a [your reframe of the problem].

Here's the difference:

[Common label] means you need [common solution they've tried].
[Your reframe] means you need [what actually solves it -- stated as a principle, not a pitch].

The proof:

[A concrete example, data point, or client scenario that shows the reframe is correct]

[One sentence naming the real cost of staying stuck]

---

[Soft CTA: question that invites engagement, or "DM me [keyword]" if you have an offer]
```

---

### 3 Example Hooks

1. *"You spent 45 minutes staring at a blank post trying to explain what you do. Gave up and scheduled another free discovery call instead. That's not writer's block."*

2. *"You hired a ghostwriter for $800 a month. She wrote about 'unlocking your potential' and 'finding your why.' You don't talk like that. Nobody you coach talks like that."*

3. *"Your referral pipeline is the only reason you're still in business. You haven't said that out loud yet, but you've thought it."*

---

### What NOT to Do

- **Don't generalize the frustration.** "Many coaches struggle with content" is invisible. "You spent 45 minutes staring at a blank post" is a gut punch. Specificity is the mechanism.
- **Don't offer the solution too early.** Sit in the frustration for at least half the post. If you rush to the fix, they don't feel seen -- they feel sold to.
- **Don't use "we" language.** "We all struggle with this" dilutes the impact. Use "you." Speak directly to ONE person.
- **Don't end with "Agree?"** as your CTA. Ask a real question: "When was the last time this happened to you?" or "What did you do instead of posting?"

---

## TEMPLATE 2: THE FRAMEWORK REVEAL

**What it does:** Takes one of your proprietary frameworks and walks through it the way you would on a coaching call. Not a listicle. A narrative breakdown with a specific client scenario woven through it.

**Why it works:** Your frameworks are your intellectual property. They're what make you different from every other coach in your niche. When you reveal HOW you think -- not just what you know -- you build trust faster than any testimonial.

**Word count:** 200-300 words

---

### The Structure

```
[HOOK: A client scenario or common problem that the framework addresses]

Most [coaches/leaders/professionals] try to solve this by [common wrong approach].

Here's what I do instead:

[Framework name, if you have one]

Step 1: [First element -- explained through the client scenario, not abstractly]

Step 2: [Second element -- show what shifts when they do this]

Step 3: [Third element -- the part that surprises people]

The result for [this client]:

[Specific outcome -- numbers, emotions, concrete change]

The thing most people miss about [this framework]:

[Your deepest insight about why it works -- the part only you would say]

---

[CTA: "I use this with every client in the first [X weeks]. If you're curious how it would apply to [your audience's situation], [action]."]
```

---

### 3 Example Hooks

1. *"A client came to me last month and said: 'I know exactly what I should be doing. I just can't make myself do it.' I hear some version of this every week. Here's the framework I use when motivation isn't the problem."*

2. *"Every leadership framework I've seen focuses on what to do. None of them explain what to stop doing. That's why I built [framework name]."*

3. *"My clients don't pay me for advice. They pay me for the sequence. Because the right actions in the wrong order produce zero results."*

---

### What NOT to Do

- **Don't list the steps without a story.** "Step 1: Identify your values" is forgettable. "Step 1: I asked her to name the last decision she made that she was proud of -- and the last one she regretted" is alive.
- **Don't include more than 3-4 steps.** LinkedIn posts aren't courses. Give them enough to understand your thinking, not enough to implement without you.
- **Don't use jargon without translating it.** If your framework uses terms you invented, define them in human language first.

## TEMPLATE 3: THE CONTRARIAN TAKE

**What it does:** Names something your industry accepts as truth and dismantles it. Not for shock value -- because you genuinely see a better way and you've watched the conventional approach fail.

**Why it works:** LinkedIn is drowning in recycled advice. ## Algorithm-Aligned Formatting Tips

These aren't hacks. They're how the platform rewards content that's easy to read and keeps people engaged.

**Posting Schedule:**
- Best days: Tuesday, Wednesday, Thursday
- Best time: 8-11 AM in your audience's timezone
- Minimum for traction: 2 posts per week
- Sweet spot: 3 posts per week
- Consistency matters more than frequency. 2 posts every week beats 5 posts one week and zero the next.

**Formatting for Readability:**
- **Short lines.** 1-2 sentences per line, max. LinkedIn rewards dwell time, and white space keeps people reading.
- **Break after every 2-3 lines.** The more scrollable your post, the longer people stay on it.
- **First 3 lines are everything.** These are what appear before "...see more." Your hook lives here. If those 3 lines don't create a reason to click, the rest of the post doesn't exist.
- **Use bold sparingly** for emphasis on key phrases. Don't bold entire sentences.
- **No hashtags in the body.** If you use them, put 3-5 at the very bottom. They don't drive discovery the way they used to.

**Engagement Tactics:**
- Reply to every comment within the first 60 minutes. The algorithm treats your replies as additional engagement signals.
- Ask a question at the end of your post. Not "Agree?" -- a real question that invites a real answer.
- When someone comments something substantive, reply with a follow-up question. Turn comments into conversations.
