# Post 6: "Imposter Syndrome Is a Deployment Problem"
**CTA Keyword**: VERSION | **Format**: Text post | **Post time**: Tue-Thu, 8-10am EST
**Prompt Kit**: `prompt-kits/06-unique-version-finder.md` | **First comment**: Link to hosted prompt kit

---

She told me:

"I was about to post about resilience.
Then I saw Adam Grant posted about resilience.
So I deleted mine."

I asked when that was.

"March."

It was November.

Eight months of silence
because Adam Grant said the word "resilience"
on a Tuesday.

---

This happens more than you think.

A coach spends 45 minutes writing a post
about self-sabotage.

She's proud of it.
She knows it's true
because she watched it happen
in a client's eyes last Thursday.

Then she opens LinkedIn.

Brene Brown posted about vulnerability.
Simon Sinek posted about leadership.
Some guy with 400K followers
posted the exact same TOPIC
with a graphic and 12,000 likes.

She closes her laptop.

"Someone already said it.
Someone said it better.
Someone said it first."

Delete.

And the post never goes up.

---

Here's what nobody tells you
about imposter syndrome and content:

It's not a confidence problem.

It's a deployment problem.

The coach I'm describing?
She doesn't doubt herself in sessions.
She doesn't freeze up on client calls.
She doesn't think she's a fraud
when a client has a breakthrough
and texts her at 10pm saying
"that changed everything."

She knows she's good.

The doubt only shows up
when she tries to say it IN PUBLIC.

Because "in public" is where the comparison lives.

In a coaching session,
there's no one to compare yourself to.
It's just you and the client
and the work.

On LinkedIn,
you're standing next to every author,
every TED speaker,
every guru with a bestseller
and a podcast and 200K followers.

And your brain does the math:
"They said it. They said it bigger.
Why would anyone need MY version?"

---

Here's why.

Brene Brown doesn't coach your clients.

Simon Sinek doesn't know their names.

Adam Grant has never sat across from
a 42-year-old operations VP
who built a $3M division
and still can't accept a compliment
without deflecting.

The metaphor you invented on a Tuesday call --
the one your client still quotes 2 years later --
that's content no bestselling author
will ever produce.

Not because they're not smart enough.

Because they weren't in the room.

Your VERSION is the value.

Not the topic. Not the concept.
Not the "take."

The version.

The way YOU explain self-sabotage
using the story of the client
who kept firing employees
right before they got promoted
because success felt like a setup for loss.

The way YOU talk about boundaries
by describing the moment a client realized
she wasn't afraid of saying no --
she was afraid of being unnecessary.

That's not a recycled idea.
That's an idea that passed through
your methodology,
your client work,
your specific lens on the world.

Nobody can produce that version.
Because nobody has your hours.

---

The coaches who post consistently
don't post because they think
their ideas are original.

They post because they understand
something most coaches miss:

The topic is never the point.

LinkedIn is not a race
to say "resilience" first.

It's a window into how you think.

And how you think
is what makes a prospect say:
"I don't know why,
but I trust this person."

That trust doesn't come from
covering a topic no one has covered.

It comes from covering a topic
through a lens no one else has.

YOUR lens.

---

Every week you don't post
because "someone already said it"
is a week your unique version
doesn't reach the one person
who needed to hear it YOUR way.

Not Adam Grant's way.
Not Brene Brown's way.

Yours.

And that person isn't comparing you
to a bestselling author.

They're scrolling LinkedIn at 8am
looking for someone who gets it.

Someone who describes their problem
so specifically
that it feels like a private conversation
in a public feed.

Your version does that.

If you deploy it.
