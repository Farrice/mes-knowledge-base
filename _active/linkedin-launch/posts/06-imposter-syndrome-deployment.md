---

Adam Grant posted about resilience on a Tuesday.

She deleted her draft and went silent for eight months.

Eight months.

Because a famous person used the same word she was going to use.

This is the content version of imposter syndrome.

And it's not a confidence problem.

It's a deployment problem.

She doesn't freeze on client calls. She doesn't doubt herself when a client texts at 10pm saying "that changed everything." She knows she's good.

The doubt only shows up in public. Because public is where the comparison lives.

In a session, it's just you and the client and the work. On LinkedIn, you're standing next to every TED speaker, every guru with a bestseller and 200K followers.

And your brain does the math: "They said it bigger. Why would anyone need MY version?"

---

Here's why.

Brene Brown doesn't coach your clients. Adam Grant has never sat across from a 42-year-old operations VP who built a $3M division and still can't accept a compliment without deflecting.

The metaphor you invented on a Tuesday call -- the one your client still quotes 2 years later -- that's content no bestselling author will ever produce.

Not because they're not smart enough. Because they weren't in the room.

The way YOU explain self-sabotage using the story of the client who kept firing employees right before they got promoted -- because success felt like a setup for loss.

That's not a recycled idea. That's an idea that passed through your methodology, your client work, your specific hours in the room.

Nobody else can produce that version.

---

Every week you don't post because "someone already said it" is a week your version doesn't reach the person who needed to hear it YOUR way.

They're not comparing you to a bestselling author. They're scrolling at 8am looking for someone who describes their problem so specifically it feels like a private conversation in a public feed.

Your version does that. If you deploy it.
