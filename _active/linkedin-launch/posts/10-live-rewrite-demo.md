---

A leadership coach told me:

"Resilience is the ability to bounce back
from adversity."

I said: "That's not a LinkedIn post.
That's a dictionary entry."

She went quiet.

Not offended.
Embarrassed.

Because she knew.

She'd been posting variations of that sentence
for 6 months.
Getting 14 likes on a good day.
All from other coaches.

Not a single DM from a prospect.
Not a single "I needed to hear this."
Not a single stranger saying
"How do I work with you?"

Just polite engagement
from people who'd never hire her.

---

So I asked her one question:

"What do you actually say to a client
when they tell you they need to be
more resilient?"

She paused.

Then she said:

"I usually tell them --
you're not burnt out.
You're carrying work that was never yours
to carry.
And your body figured that out
before your brain did."

I stared at her.

"THAT is a LinkedIn post."

---

Read both versions again.

Version A:
"Resilience is the ability to bounce back
from adversity."

Version B:
"You're not burnt out.
You're carrying work that was never yours
to carry."

Same coach.
Same expertise.
Same methodology.

One sounds like a textbook.
The other sounds like someone who's sat
across from 500 executives
and heard what they're actually afraid of.

The difference isn't talent.
It isn't copywriting skill.
It isn't a course on LinkedIn hooks.

The difference is ONE question:

"What do you actually say
when you're in the room?"

---

This is what I mean
when I talk about translation.

Every coach I've worked with has a Version B
living inside their practice.

It's the thing they say in session 3
that makes the client lean forward.

It's the reframe they've delivered
200 times
that still gets a sharp inhale.

It's the question they ask
that nobody else in their field thinks to ask.

That material exists.
It exists in YOUR sessions right now.

But it's not in your content.

Because when you sit down to write,
you don't write like you coach.
You write like you think LinkedIn
wants you to sound.

Formal. Authoritative. Safe.

And safe content
is invisible content.

---

Here's what I've learned
doing this work:

The translation from "how you coach"
to "what you post"
doesn't require more creativity.

It requires a different lens.

LENS 1 -- THE METHODOLOGY LENS
Take your generic statement.
Replace it with the specific process
you actually use.
Not "building resilience" --
but the exact conversation you have
in session 1 when the client says
"I think I'm burning out."

LENS 2 -- THE STORY LENS
Find the moment.
Not the lesson. The MOMENT.
The client's face. The silence.
The sentence they said
that changed the direction of the session.
That moment is the post.

LENS 3 -- THE RECOGNITION LENS
Write the thing that makes your ideal client
stop scrolling and think:
"How does this person know
what I'm going through?"
Not advice. Recognition.
The feeling of being seen
by someone who actually understands.
