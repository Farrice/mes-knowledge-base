# Post 8: "Content That Pre-Sells (vs. Content That Gets Likes)"
**CTA Keyword**: AUDIT | **Format**: Text post | **Post time**: Tue-Thu, 8-10am EST
**Prompt Kit**: `prompt-kits/08-content-to-client-audit.md` | **First comment**: Link to hosted prompt kit

---

I studied 50 coaches' LinkedIn accounts.

The ones making $30K+/month from content
had something in common:

Their posts weren't "educational."

The coaches getting polite likes,
nice comments,
"great advice!" replies,
and zero DMs?

Their posts were textbooks.

---

Let me explain.

A coach DM'd me 6 months ago.

"I'm posting 3x a week.
Getting good engagement.
70-100 likes per post.
People are saving my content.
Sharing it."

I asked: "How many DMs
have turned into discovery calls this quarter?"

Silence.

Then: "Two. Maybe three."

Three calls from 48 posts.

She was doing the math in real time.
I could feel it.

"That can't be right," she said.

It was right.

I asked her to send me her last 10 posts.

Here's what I saw:

"5 signs you're a people-pleaser
and what to do about it."

"The 3 biggest mistakes leaders make
when giving feedback."

"Why emotional intelligence
is the #1 skill for 2026."

Every post was a mini-lesson.
Clean. Structured. Valuable.

The kind of content
that makes people nod and say
"Great stuff" and keep scrolling.

---

Here's what was missing.

Not a single post
made you feel like you KNEW her.

Not one post revealed
how SHE thinks about the problem.

Not one post described
the specific moment in a coaching session
where her approach produces a result
no other coach's approach produces.

She was teaching her audience.

She was not letting them
inside her methodology.

There's a massive difference.

---

Value content creates students.

Pre-selling content creates clients.

Here's the difference:

Value content says:
"Here are 5 ways to manage conflict at work."

Pre-selling content says:
"A client came to me last month
convinced her team hated her.
In session 2, I asked one question
that changed the entire narrative.
Turned out the team didn't hate her.
They were afraid of her.
Not because she was harsh --
because she was so competent
they felt like they couldn't keep up."

The first post teaches WHAT to do.

The second post teaches HOW YOU THINK
about the problem.

A reader finishes the first post
and thinks: "Good tips. I'll try those."

A reader finishes the second post
and thinks: "This person sees things
I didn't know could be seen.
I want to talk to them."

One creates a follower.
The other creates a prospect.

---

The coaches making $30K+/month from content
aren't better writers.

They post differently.

Their content follows a pattern:

1. It opens with a specific situation.
Not a tip. A moment.
A client's face. A conversation.
Something that happened.

2. It reveals their THINKING.
Not "here's what you should do" --
but "here's how I diagnosed this."
Here's the question I asked.
Here's what I noticed.
Here's what most coaches miss.

3. It creates a feeling of proximity.
After reading 3 posts,
you feel like you've already
had a coaching conversation with them.

That's not engagement.
That's pre-selling.

The reader isn't learning from you.
They're EXPERIENCING your methodology
in miniature.

And when they're ready to buy,
they already know who to call.

---

Here's the uncomfortable truth:

If your content gets consistent likes
but no DMs,

you haven't failed at content.

You've succeeded at the WRONG content.

You've built an audience of students
who learn from you for free
and will never buy.

Not because they don't value you.
Because your content trained them
to see you as a teacher.

Not as their coach.

---

I built an audit for this.

It's called The Content-to-Client Audit.

You paste your last 10 LinkedIn posts
and it categorizes each one:

-> "Student content" (teaches what to do)
-> "Client content" (reveals how you think)

Then it scores your ratio
and shows you exactly which posts
could be rewritten to pre-sell
instead of educate.

Most coaches who run it discover
80-90% of their content is student content.

That's not a failure.
That's a diagnosis.

And once you see the pattern,
you can't unsee it.
