**Type**: Text post | **Format**: Narrative origin story | **Purpose**: Introduce new positioning
**Post time**: ASAP — transition post, not arc chapter
**Series**: Transition (0 of 0 — standalone bridge into serial narrative world)

## How to Use These Variants

These 3 variants share the same origin (kitchen scene, stay-at-home dad, 18 years of coaching) and the same core insight (translation problem, not writing problem). They diverge in how they CLOSE — what the ending makes the reader feel and do.

| Variant | Voice | Ending Strategy | Open Loop Type |
|---------|-------|-----------------|----------------|
| **A — "The First Client"** | Grounded, specific | Shows the translation in action with a real result | Story loop — "what she said changed how I think about all of this" |
| **B — "The Coaching Lens"** | Cross-domain, insightful | Connects fitness career to content — the pattern recognition superpower | Question loop — "what's actually stopping you?" |
| **C — "The Confession"** | Vulnerable, personal | Farrice was his own first invisible expert | Implication loop — "it's not the reason you think" |

## VARIANT A — "The First Client"

My wife left for work at 7:15am.

I was standing in the kitchen
with a bottle, a toddler,
and a to-do list that started with "survive today."

Stay-at-home dad.
Best decision I ever made.
But a part of me was going quiet —
the part I'd built everything on.

---

That quiet part started paying attention.

For 18 years as a coach and trainer,
I'd watched the same pattern:

The most brilliant people in any room
were the ones nobody could find online.

A consultant who'd solved the problem
a hundred times —
losing deals to firms with better websites
and worse answers.

A strategist charging $15K a quarter
with a LinkedIn bio that said
"Helping businesses grow."

Brilliant in a room.
Ghost online.

---

I used to think they just couldn't write.

Eighteen years taught me otherwise.

They could explain a $200K strategy
in a conversation and make it feel obvious.
But ask them to write 200 words
for strangers on LinkedIn?

Silence.

It's not a writing problem.
It's a translation problem.

The skill that makes you extraordinary in a room
has nothing to do with the skill
that makes a stranger stop scrolling.

Different language entirely.
One most experts never needed —
until the room moved online.

---

So I built something for it.

Not templates. Not "post more."
Not "just be authentic" —
as if the problem was honesty
and not language.

The first person I tried it with
was a business strategist.

Fifteen years of experience.
Could walk into any boardroom
and own it within ten minutes.

I asked her one question:
"What do you say to a client
in that first meeting
that makes them trust you?"

She talked for forty minutes.

I turned twenty seconds of what she said
into a LinkedIn post.

Her DMs opened up within a week.

Not because I wrote something clever.
Because I wrote something
that sounded exactly like her
on her sharpest day.

That's the work.
Not writing. Translating.

If you've ever lost a client to someone less qualified
who just showed up better online —
I'd love to hear what happened.

Because what I learned from that strategist
changed how I think about all of this.

I'll tell you what she said tomorrow.

Not templates. Not "post more."

Here's what 18 years of coaching taught me:

The person who can explain their process
always beats the person who just does the work.

In fitness, the trainers who lasted
weren't the strongest.
They were the ones who could look at a client
and say: "Here's why your body does that.
Here's what we're going to do about it."

Same thing online.

The experts who win
aren't the smartest in the room.
They're the ones who figured out
how to let a stranger see
the way they think.

That's the hard part.
That's the part nobody teaches.

If you're an expert who's been watching
less experienced people
build audiences while you stay quiet —

what's actually stopping you?

I've been asking that question a lot lately.
And the answer keeps surprising me.

I'll tell you what I keep hearing —
but I want to know if it matches your experience first.

## VARIANT C — "The Confession"

My wife left for work at 7:15am.

I was standing in the kitchen
with a bottle, a toddler,
and a to-do list that started with "survive today."

Stay-at-home dad.
Best decision I ever made.
But a part of me was going quiet —
the part I'd built everything on.

I used to walk into rooms
and know exactly what to say.
Eighteen years of coaching.
Thousands of clients.
I could read a room before I sat down.

Then the room disappeared.

And I realized I had no idea
how to show up anywhere else.

---

That quiet part started paying attention.

I'd watched the same pattern
for years:

The most brilliant people in any room
were the ones nobody could find online.

A consultant who'd solved the problem
a hundred times —
losing deals to firms with better websites
and worse answers.

A strategist charging $15K a quarter
with a LinkedIn bio that said
"Helping businesses grow."

Brilliant in a room.
Ghost online.

---

I used to think they just couldn't write.

Eighteen years taught me otherwise.

They could explain a $200K strategy
in a conversation and make it feel obvious.
But ask them to write 200 words
for strangers on LinkedIn?

Silence.

It's not a writing problem.
It's a translation problem.

Different language entirely.
One most experts never needed —
until the room moved online.

---

I know because I lived it.

Standing in that kitchen,
I had eighteen years of insight
and zero presence online.

I was my own first case study.

So I started with the only question
that had ever worked in coaching:

Not "what do you know?"
But "how do you actually think?"

How do you connect the dots
when you're sitting with a client
and the real work begins?

I found the answer.
Then I found out the answer
was different for every single expert.

That's what made it interesting.

If you've ever lost a client
to someone less qualified
who just showed up better online —
tell me about it.

Because I think I know why it happened.
And it's not the reason you think.

## Mixing Guide

**Strongest opening**: Variant C's expanded kitchen scene adds "I used to walk into rooms and know exactly what to say / Then the room disappeared" — turns the origin from backstory into a gut-punch. A and B's openings are clean but more standard.

**Strongest middle**: All three share the same body through "until the room moved online" — the $200K/$200 words contrast and "Brilliant in a room / Ghost online" land equally well across all variants.

**Strongest ending**:
- **A** has the most specific proof — "She talked for forty minutes. I turned twenty seconds into a LinkedIn post." Shows the work, doesn't describe it. Best for establishing credibility.
- **B** has the strongest cross-domain insight — connecting fitness coaching to content expertise is Farrice's unique lens. "The person who can explain their process always beats the person who just does the work" is t-shirt worthy. Best for positioning.
- **C** has the deepest emotional truth — "I was my own first case study" makes the reader trust Farrice because he's not just selling, he's confessing. "I think I know why it happened / And it's not the reason you think" is the strongest open loop. Best for building reader investment.

**Recommended Frankenstein**: C's opening (expanded kitchen scene + "Then the room disappeared") → shared body → C's confession ending. The personal vulnerability makes the "translation problem" feel earned, not theoretical. A's "first client" story could replace the more abstract ending if Farrice wants proof over vulnerability.
