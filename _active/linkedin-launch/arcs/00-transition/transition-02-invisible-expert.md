## How to Use These Variants

These 3 variants share the same core story (consultant, 4 vs 14 years, silence isn't neutral, the feed became the room) but diverge in their closing approach. Read all three, then:

1. Pick the ending that hits hardest
2. Grab any body sections you prefer from other variants
3. Frankenstein your final version

| Variant | Voice | Ending Strategy | Open Loop Type |
|---------|-------|-----------------|----------------|
| **A — "The Result"** | Observational, coach's eye | Shows transformation through specific dialogue | Story loop — "it wasn't the posts that changed her business..." |
| **B — "The Pattern"** | Personal, connected to Post #1 | Names the translation problem, asks a genuinely curious question | Question loop — invites real engagement |
| **C — "The Emotional Twist"** | Provocative, gets under the skin | Subverts — the real danger is what invisibility does to self-worth | Implication loop — "I'll explain what we changed" |

## VARIANT A — "The Result"

A consultant I know lost a deal last year.

Not because she wasn't qualified.
Not because her price was wrong.

The prospect told her:
"We went with someone who felt more established."

The other firm had 4 years of experience.

She had 14.

They charged more.
She would have delivered more.

But their LinkedIn?
Every post taught something.
Every post made you feel like you knew them.

Her profile?
Two posts in six months.
A headline that said "Helping businesses grow" —
the LinkedIn equivalent of a blank stare.

The prospect never saw her depth.
They only saw silence.

---

Silence isn't neutral.

When someone lands on your profile
and sees nothing — no perspective, no depth,
no evidence of the thing
you've spent a decade mastering —

they don't think
"probably just busy."

They fill the gap with a story.
And it's never the one you'd write.

"If they were that good,
wouldn't they be talking about it?"

---

The experts who built credibility in rooms —
behind closed doors, through referrals,
reputation passed quietly
from one client to the next —

they didn't need to be visible.
The room was enough.

That worked when the room
was the first place people checked.

It stopped working
when the feed became the room.

---

I've been building a practice around this.

Not templates. Not "post more."

I sit with an expert until I hear it —
the way they explain things to a client
when the walls come down
and the real insight lands.

The story they keep telling
because it always works.

The question they ask
that makes the whole conversation shift.

That's the stuff that's invisible online.
That's what I help translate.

The consultant I mentioned?
We started working together two months ago.

Last week a prospect told her:
"I feel like I already know you."

She hasn't lost a deal since.

But here's what surprised me —
it wasn't the posts that changed her business.
It was something else entirely.

I'll tell you what it was tomorrow.

## VARIANT B — "The Pattern"

A consultant I know lost a deal last year.

Not because she wasn't qualified.
Not because her price was wrong.

The prospect said:
"We went with someone who felt more established."

The other firm had 4 years of experience.
She had 14.

They charged more.
She would have delivered more.

But they showed up on LinkedIn
like they owned the category.
Every post taught something.
Every post made you feel
like you were already their client.

Her profile?
Two posts in six months.
A headline that said "Helping businesses grow."

The prospect never saw her depth.
They saw silence.

---

Silence isn't neutral.

When a prospect lands on your page
and sees nothing —
no perspective, no depth,
no evidence of the thing
you've spent a decade mastering —

their brain doesn't think
"they're probably just busy."

It fills the gap with doubt.

"If they're that good,
why aren't they talking about it?"

Every week that goes by,
someone with half your experience
is building the authority you earned.

Not because they're better.
Because they showed up.

---

The experts who built reputations in rooms —
behind closed doors, through results,
word of mouth from one client to the next —

they never needed visibility.
The room was the whole game.

That worked when the room
was the first place people checked.

It stopped working
when the feed became the room.

---

I've been working on this problem for a while now.

Not with templates. Not with "just post more."

With something harder —
sitting across from an expert
and listening until I hear the thing
they say to a client
that makes everything click.

Then figuring out how to give that moment
to someone who's never been in the room.

I call it the translation problem.
And it's everywhere.

If you've ever watched someone
with half your experience
build the career you earned —

what did that feel like?
I'm genuinely curious.

Because I keep hearing the same answer,
and it's not the one I expected.

## VARIANT C — "The Emotional Twist"

A consultant I know lost a deal last year.

Not because she wasn't qualified.
Not because her price was wrong.

The prospect told her:
"We went with someone who felt more established."

The other firm had 4 years of experience.

She had 14.

They charged more.
She would have delivered more.

But their feed?
Every post made you feel like you knew them.
Like you'd already worked together.

Her feed?
Two posts in six months.
A headline that said "Helping businesses grow."

The prospect never saw her depth.
They saw silence.

---

Silence isn't neutral.

When someone lands on your profile
and finds nothing — no perspective,
no depth, no evidence of the thing
you've spent a decade mastering —

they don't give you the benefit of the doubt.

Their brain writes a story for you.
And that story sounds like:
"If they were that good,
wouldn't we have heard of them?"

---

The experts who built credibility in rooms —
behind closed doors, through real results,
reputation passed word-of-mouth
from one client to the next —

they didn't need to be visible.

That worked when the room
was the first place people checked.

It stopped working
when the feed became the room.

---

I started working with that consultant
two months ago.

Not on her skills.
Not on her pricing.
Not on her pitch.

On the gap between
what she sounds like in a room
and what a stranger sees on a screen.

She hasn't lost a deal since.

But here's the part that keeps me up at night:
she almost didn't reach out.

She told me she'd convinced herself
the problem was the market.
That clients just didn't value experience anymore.
That the game had changed and she'd missed it.

That's the real danger of being invisible.

Not just the lost deals.
Not just the money left on the table.

You start believing the silence
is telling you something about your worth.

It's not.

If that hit close to home,
I'll explain exactly what we changed
in her next post.

## Mixing Guide

**Strongest hook**: All three open identically. Variant B adds "like you were already their client" — slight edge.

**Strongest middle**: Variant C's "Their brain writes a story for you / And that story sounds like..." has the most visceral show-don't-tell. Variant B's "Not because they're better. Because they showed up." lands hard.

**Strongest ending**:
- **A** has the tightest story loop — the "it wasn't the posts" reveal is a genuine cliffhanger. Best for pulling readers into tomorrow's post.
- **B** has the most genuine engagement invitation — "what did that feel like?" is a real question. Best for driving comments.
- **C** has the most emotional gut-punch — "you start believing the silence is telling you something about your worth" hits a nerve nobody else is touching. Best for making people screenshot and share.

**Recommended Frankenstein**: C's ending on A or B's body. The emotional twist is the strongest differentiator — it goes where no other LinkedIn content goes.
