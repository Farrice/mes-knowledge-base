# Transition Post #2: "The Invisible Expert Epidemic"
**Type**: Text post | **Format**: Observational narrative | **Purpose**: Establish the problem Farrice solves
**Post time**: Next publishing day (Tue-Thu, 8-10am EST)
**Series**: Transition (builds on Post #1 — references "the translation problem")

---

A consultant I know lost a deal last year.

Not because she wasn't qualified.
Not because her price was wrong.

The prospect told her:
"We went with someone who felt more... established."

The "established" firm had 4 years of experience.
She had 14.

They had a polished framework
they'd licensed from a consulting playbook.
She had a methodology refined
through 3,000+ hours of real client work.

They charged more.
She would have delivered more.

But they showed up on LinkedIn
like they owned the category.
Every post taught something.
Every post made you feel like you knew them.

Her profile?
Two posts in six months.
A headline that said "Helping businesses grow."
A headshot from 2020.

The prospect never saw her depth.
They only saw silence.

---

I call this the Invisible Expert Epidemic.

And it's not a rare condition. It's the default.

The experts who built their credibility in rooms —
behind closed doors, through real results,
reputation passed quietly from one client to the next —

They didn't build it in public.

That worked for a long time.

It stopped working when the feed
became the first room people check.

Now your first impression isn't your handshake.
It's not the thing you said
that made a client go "wow, they get me."

It's your LinkedIn profile.
And for most experts,
that profile is a ghost town.

---

Here's the part that gets me.

The silence isn't neutral.

When a prospect lands on your page
and sees nothing — no perspective, no depth,
no evidence of the thing
you've spent a decade mastering —

Their brain doesn't think
"oh, they're probably just busy."

It fills the gap with doubt.

"If they're that good,
why aren't they talking about it?"

Every day your expertise stays invisible,
someone less qualified is building the presence
you should have.

They learned the watered-down version
of YOUR insight at a weekend workshop.
And they're getting the client.

---

I mentioned the translation problem the other day.

This is the cost of leaving it unsolved.

It's not just that great experts
don't know how to write posts.

It's a mismatch between how they BUILT their credibility
and how the market now EVALUATES it.

It's like a personal trainer
who can transform someone's body in 12 weeks
but can't explain what they do
in a way that makes a stranger walk through the door.

Different skill entirely.
And one most experts never had to develop —
until the rules changed.

---

I've been building a methodology around this.

Not "how to write better posts."
Not templates.

Something deeper:
capturing the way an expert actually thinks.
Not just what they know —
how they connect the dots
when they're sitting with a client
and the real work begins.

If any of this sounds familiar,
there's an 8-minute diagnostic
in the Featured section of my profile.
It won't pitch you anything.
It'll just show you where the gap is.

The bigger question — how to actually close it —
that's what I've been working on.

More on that soon.
