# Post 12: "The Voice Test"
**CTA Keyword**: RECOGNIZE | **Format**: Text post | **Post time**: Tue-Thu, 8-10am EST
**Prompt Kit**: `prompt-kits/12-recognizability-test.md` | **First comment**: Link to hosted prompt kit

---

Your last LinkedIn post.

Could anyone tell it was yours without seeing your name?

Go look. Seriously. Pull it up right now. Strip your name and photo off it mentally. Drop it into a stack with 4 other coaches in your niche.

Would anyone pick yours out?

I ran this experiment with a leadership coach. 15 years of experience, 1,200 client sessions, a methodology sharp enough to rewire how you think about executive performance.

I stripped her name off 5 posts. Mixed them with posts from 4 other leadership coaches. Showed all 25 to someone who'd sat across from her every other week for six months.

"Which ones are Sarah's?"

One out of five.

A person who trusted this coach with her career couldn't find her in her own content.

---

That's not a writing problem. That's a disappearing act.

When Sarah coaches, she's specific, sharp, unmistakable. When she writes for LinkedIn, she vanishes. There's a filter between "what I know" and "what I post" -- and it whispers the same thing to every coach: be professional, be broadly applicable, don't be too specific, don't alienate anyone.

I lived behind that filter for years. I could read a client's tension patterns in the first five minutes, hear the real question hiding under the one they asked, build a program that fit their body like a second skin. Eighteen years of watching people move and knowing what they needed before they could name it. But my content? Generic. Safe. You could have swapped my name for any other coach's and nobody would have blinked.

The filter doesn't just sand the edges. It strips the fingerprint.

Sarah's stories become "a client I worked with." Her frameworks dissolve into "consider these three principles." Her hardest-won insights flatten to "leadership requires empathy and self-awareness."

Smooth. Safe. Gone.

---

Recognizability beats engagement. Every time.

A thousand likes from people who will never hire you is applause in an empty theater. But the prospect who reads your post in a feed of 50 coaches and thinks "THIS is the one who gets it" -- that's a business.

What makes content recognizable isn't style or hooks. It's three things:

**Your methodology.** Not tips. The thing you do in session one that nobody else does. The question you ask. The framework you built from the wreckage of watching something fail enough times. In 18 years of training, I could strip the name off a coach's programming and tell you who wrote it -- by the exercise selection, the progression logic, the philosophy embedded in every rep. Your content should work the same way. If another coach could claim it, it's not yours yet.

**Your stories.** Not "a client was struggling." The specific moment. The exhale. The dropped shoulders. The sentence that split the session in half. Stories only you have because only you were in the room -- and only you knew what to look for.

**Your beliefs.** Not safe opinions everyone nods along to. The hill you'd die on. The conventional wisdom you've watched fail so many times you can't stay quiet about it anymore. The thing you know in your bones that the rest of your industry is still getting wrong.

Methodology + stories + beliefs = a fingerprint no one can copy.

If your content is missing any of the three, you're writing in someone else's voice.
