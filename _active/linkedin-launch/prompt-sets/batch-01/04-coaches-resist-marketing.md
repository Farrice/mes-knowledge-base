# Topic: Why the Coaches Who Need Marketing Help Most Are the Ones Who Resist It Most

**Psychological core**: Psychological reactance — the pressure to market triggers the same autonomy defense coaches help clients work through. Plus the merit-based fantasy: "good work should speak for itself." They're using clinical sophistication to build better rationalizations for their own avoidance.

**Why now**: The market is demanding better marketing (algorithm changes, saturation, AI competition). The coaches with the most to offer are the most resistant. "Why good coaches stay broke" is a conversation getting massive engagement.

---

## Talking Points — Talk Through These

*Don't write. Just talk. Skip any that don't land. Lean into the ones that light you up.*

### 1. The resistance you've seen
Think about coaches you know — talented, experienced, transformative — who actively resist marketing. What do they SAY about why they don't market? And what do you think is actually going on underneath those reasons? What's the real reason behind the stated reason?

### 2. The irony
Coaches spend all day helping clients move through resistance — the excuses, the deflections, the "I know I should, but..." And then they do the EXACT same thing with their own marketing. Have you noticed this pattern in yourself or peers? Walk me through a specific example where a coach's reasons for not marketing sounded EXACTLY like what their clients say about avoiding change.

### 3. The merit fantasy
"Good work should speak for itself." Where does this belief come from? Is it true? Was it ever true? Have you personally held this belief, and what happened? What would you say to a coach who genuinely believes that excellence alone should be enough to attract clients?

### 4. Reactance theory
From your psychology lens — there's a concept where the MORE you pressure someone to do something, the MORE they resist (even if it's good for them). Is that what's happening when coaches hear "you need to market yourself"? The recommendation itself creates the resistance? How does that work psychologically?

### 5. Your own resistance
Be honest: have YOU resisted marketing? When someone told you "you need to be on LinkedIn" or "you need to build a funnel" — what was your internal response? What did it feel like? And looking back, was the resistance about the marketing itself, or about something deeper?

### 6. The clinical sophistication trap
Here's the graduate-level question: Do experienced coaches use their psychological training to build BETTER rationalizations for not marketing? Like — they can name their avoidance as "setting boundaries" or "protecting the therapeutic frame" because they know the language. They're too sophisticated to just say "I'm scared" — instead they construct clinical justifications. Have you seen this?

### 7. What actually works
For a coach who is resistant to marketing — not just procrastinating, but genuinely resistant at an identity level — what's the approach that works? Not tips. Not "just start posting." What's the psychological intervention that helps them see their own pattern?
