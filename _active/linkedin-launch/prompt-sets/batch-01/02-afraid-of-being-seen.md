# Topic: You're Not Afraid of Content — You're Afraid of Being Seen

**Psychological core**: The helper's bind — coaches hold space for others' vulnerability all day, then refuse to practice their own. Visibility fear in coaches is a projection of their client work. Public content violates the psychological contract they made with themselves about what "being a coach" means.

**Why now**: 84.9% of marketers experience imposter feelings. LinkedIn now requires personal profiles over company pages. The platform is forcing visibility on people whose identity was built in private rooms.

---

## Talking Points — Talk Through These

*Don't write. Just talk. Skip any that don't land. Lean into the ones that light you up.*

### 1. The private room
Describe what it feels like to be in a coaching session — the intimacy, the safety, the contained space. Now describe what it feels like to open LinkedIn and think about posting. What's different? Not logistically — emotionally. What shifts in your body between those two contexts?

### 2. The helper's bind
You spend your days holding space for other people's vulnerability. When's the last time YOU were vulnerable — not in a session where you're the container, but in public where you're exposed? What does that feel like? Is there a part of you that chose coaching BECAUSE you could help from behind the scenes?

### 3. A specific client
Think of a client who was brilliant privately — transformed in sessions, crushing it in their actual work — but absolutely frozen when it came to being visible. What did you notice about them? What was the defense mechanism? What did you help them see about themselves that they couldn't see on their own?

### 4. The projection
Here's the deeper question: Do you think coaches PROJECT their own visibility fear onto their content avoidance? Like — they rationalize it as "the algorithm," "I don't have time," "I need a better strategy" — but the real issue is they're afraid of being SEEN the way they see their clients? As a psychologist, what pattern would you name this?

### 5. Your own story
Have you experienced this yourself — being the expert who helps everyone else show up, while hiding your own light? Not the inspirational version. The honest version. What was the moment you realized you were doing the thing you help clients stop doing?

### 6. What changes it
What actually breaks this pattern? Not "feel the fear and do it anyway" platitudes. From your coaching experience — what's the intervention that works? What do you say to the coach (or client) who can hold space for anyone except themselves?

### 7. The identity question
Is "being a coach" fundamentally in tension with "being visible"? Or is that a story coaches tell themselves to avoid discomfort? What would you say to a colleague who said "I became a coach to help people, not to market myself"?
