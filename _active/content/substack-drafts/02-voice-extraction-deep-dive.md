## The Problem With "Capturing Your Voice"

Every ghostwriting service promises to capture your voice. Here's why that promise almost always fails.

Voice is surface. It's word choice, sentence rhythm, whether you say "journey" or "process." A decent writer can mimic your voice after reading ten of your posts.

But mimicry isn't the problem coaches have.

The problem is that nobody captures how you *think*.

Your thinking is the layer underneath your voice. It's why you ask the questions you ask. It's the pattern you see across 500 clients that nobody else in your niche sees. It's the moment in a session when you say something and the client goes quiet because you just articulated something they've felt for years but never had words for.

That's not voice. That's methodology. And methodology is what makes your content *yours* instead of interchangeable.

When a ghostwriter interviews you for 60 minutes and writes posts based on what they *think* you sound like, they're capturing the skin. Not the skeleton.

The result: content that's technically correct and emotionally hollow. Posts the coach reads and thinks, "This isn't wrong. But it's not me."

## The 3 Translation Failures

After studying how coaches try to turn their expertise into content, I've identified three failure modes. Every coach I've talked to falls into at least one. Most fall into all three.

**1. Abstraction Drift**

You know your material so well that you've compressed it into abstractions. "I help leaders develop executive presence." "I facilitate transformational breakthroughs." "I guide clients toward authentic alignment."

These phrases mean something to you. They contain 10 years of experience and hundreds of client stories. But to a prospect scrolling LinkedIn, they mean nothing. They're so abstracted from the original experience that the emotional charge is gone.

The Curse of Knowledge, as MIT researchers call it: the more you know about something, the harder it becomes to explain it simply. You literally can't see your own frameworks through a beginner's eyes anymore.

**Fix**: You don't need to "simplify." You need to *re-ground* the abstraction in a specific story. "Executive presence" becomes "the moment my client realized she'd been shrinking in every meeting because she thought being quiet was the same as being strategic." Same concept. Completely different impact.

**2. Voice Flattening**

You sit down to write a LinkedIn post. Something happens between your brain and the screen. The way you'd explain this to a client -- the stories, the analogies, the specific language -- flattens into Corporate LinkedIn Speak.

Suddenly you're writing "In my experience, authenticity is the cornerstone of effective leadership" when what you actually said to your client was "You keep performing confidence instead of saying the thing you actually think. That performance is what's making your team not trust you."

Voice Flattening happens because writing feels formal and coaching feels conversational. The medium changes the message. And the message loses its teeth.

**Fix**: The content shouldn't be *written*. It should be *extracted*. Spoken, captured, and then shaped. The raw material has to come from the conversational brain, not the writing brain. They produce different outputs.

**3. Template Trapping**

You downloaded the "LinkedIn Content Calendar for Coaches." You bought the "50 Post Templates for Service Providers." You followed the framework that says "Hook, Problem, Story, Solution, CTA."

The templates work -- in the sense that they produce posts. But they produce posts that sound like everyone else who's using the same templates. The structure is borrowed. The thinking is borrowed. The only thing that's yours is the name at the top.

And your audience can feel it. 54% of long-form LinkedIn posts are now AI-generated, and they get 45% less engagement. Not because AI is bad at writing. Because templated content -- whether human or AI -- has a sameness that the human nervous system detects and skips.

**Fix**: Templates should structure your thinking, not replace it. The content that performs -- the posts that make people DM you -- starts with a genuine insight from your practice and uses structure to deliver it clearly. Not the other way around.

## What Voice Extraction Actually Looks Like

I won't give away the full process -- that's the product -- but here's enough to understand why it's different.

**The input** is a 30-minute conversation. Not an interview. Not a questionnaire. A conversation where I ask questions designed to access your *teaching brain* -- the mode you're in when you're with a client and something clicks.

Questions like:
- "Tell me about a client who came to you with one problem but the real issue was something completely different."
- "What's the thing you find yourself saying to almost every client, even across different niches?"
- "What's a piece of advice your industry gives that you privately disagree with?"

These aren't random. They're designed to bypass Abstraction Drift and access the specific stories, frameworks, and counterintuitive insights that live underneath your polished elevator pitch.

**The extraction** isn't transcription. It's translation. I'm listening for the moments when your voice changes -- when you lean in, when you tell a story instead of explaining a concept, when you contradict yourself and then correct the contradiction in real-time.

Those inflection points are where the best content lives.

**The output** is content that sounds like you wrote it on your sharpest day. Not "like you" in a mimicry sense. Like you in a *thinking* sense. Your frameworks, your stories, your specific way of seeing the problem -- structured into content that a prospect can feel.

Here's the before/after from the career coach I mentioned at the top:

**Before** (from her LinkedIn): "I help professionals navigate career transitions with clarity and confidence."

**After** (from the extraction): "She didn't need a career change. She needed to stop building someone else's vision and start building her own. Most 'career pivots' aren't about finding a new direction. They're about remembering the direction you abandoned."

Same coach. Same expertise. Completely different impact.

## The Question I Keep Getting

"Why can't I just do this myself?"

You can. In theory. The same way a therapist can theoretically therapize themselves.

But the Curse of Knowledge makes it nearly impossible to see your own methodology from the outside. You've been inside your frameworks for so long that you've lost the ability to hear what's remarkable about them.

You need someone standing outside, listening not just to what you say -- but to what you don't realize you're saying.

That's the job. Not writing. Not ghostwriting. Extraction.
