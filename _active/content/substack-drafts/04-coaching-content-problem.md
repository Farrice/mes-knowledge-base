Here's a number that should make you angry if you're a coach:

71%.

That's the percentage of coaches who say their number one business challenge is getting clients. Not delivering results. Not building frameworks. Not the actual work of coaching.

*Finding the people who need them.*

The International Coach Federation surveyed thousands of coaches globally. And seven out of ten said the same thing: the hardest part of this job isn't the job. It's getting someone to walk through the door.

Now hold that number in your head and consider this one:

The coaching industry generates $16 billion annually. It's growing 15% year-over-year. There are 232,000 coaches in the US alone. Demand for coaching -- especially executive coaching, leadership coaching, and business coaching -- has never been higher.

So the market is massive. The demand is real. And 71% of practitioners still can't find enough clients.

Something doesn't add up.

---

## The Content Vacuum

Here's what I think is happening.

The coaches who are best at coaching are worst at *talking about* coaching.

Not because they lack intelligence. Not because they lack writing ability. Because of a specific psychological phenomenon that gets worse the more expertise you accumulate.

MIT Sloan researchers call it the Curse of Knowledge. Once you've deeply internalized something -- once you've spent 4,000 hours in sessions watching real transformation happen in real time -- you literally lose the ability to see your own expertise through a beginner's eyes.

Your frameworks, which feel obvious to you, are invisible to your prospects.

Your methodology, which you can deploy instinctively in a session, refuses to translate into a LinkedIn post.

Your best client stories -- the ones that would stop a stranger mid-scroll -- sit in your memory filed under "just another Tuesday."

So the coaches with the deepest expertise produce the least content. The ones who've been in the field for 15 years have LinkedIn profiles that look abandoned. And the ones who post consistently are often the newest, the least experienced, the ones whose frameworks come from a weekend seminar rather than a decade of practice.

The invisible experts lose clients to the visible amateurs.

Not because the market doesn't value expertise. Because the market can't *see* expertise that only exists in private.

---

## The LinkedIn Factor

80% of B2B social media leads come from LinkedIn. Not Instagram. Not TikTok. Not email marketing. LinkedIn.

For coaches -- especially those serving professionals, executives, and business owners -- LinkedIn is where your clients are making decisions before they ever talk to you.

A prospect researches a coach online. They find your LinkedIn. They see one of three things:

**Scenario 1: The Ghost.** Last post: 8 months ago. Bio: "Helping leaders lead." About section: a wall of text about certifications. The prospect's brain does what all brains do -- it fills the silence with doubt. *If she's that good, why isn't she talking about it?*

**Scenario 2: The Template.** Posts every week. But every post sounds like it was generated by "LinkedIn Content Templates for Coaches." "5 Tips for Better Leadership." "3 Ways to Build Executive Presence." Technically competent. Emotionally forgettable. The prospect skims, doesn't feel anything, moves on.

**Scenario 3: The Extraction.** Posts that sound like a real human with specific expertise and genuine perspective. A client story that makes the prospect think "that's exactly what I'm dealing with." A framework that makes them stop scrolling because they've never heard anyone articulate it that way. The prospect doesn't just read -- they *feel* something. They check the profile. They follow. They eventually DM.

Scenario 3 is rare. And that's the opportunity.

---

## The AI Acceleration Problem

Here's where it gets worse.

54% of long-form LinkedIn posts are now AI-generated. And those posts get 45% less engagement than original content.

The market for generic, templated, AI-assisted content is already saturated. Every coach who opened ChatGPT and typed "write me a LinkedIn post about leadership" is now contributing to a feed that feels increasingly hollow.

And prospects can feel it. Not consciously. They can't point to the AI-generated post and say "that's fake." But their nervous system does what nervous systems do -- it skips content that doesn't register as emotionally real.

The result is a paradox:

**It has never been easier to create content. And it has never been harder to create content that matters.**

Coaches who use AI templates sound like everyone else. Coaches who don't use AI don't post at all. And the gap between "real voice" and "manufactured content" gets wider every month.

The coaches who will win in this environment are the ones whose content sounds undeniably *human*. Not anti-AI -- AI is a tool. But content that carries the weight of real experience, specific methodology, and genuine perspective that no template can generate.

---

## The Translation Gap

So we have:

- An industry where 71% of practitioners struggle to find clients
- A platform (LinkedIn) where 80% of B2B leads originate
- A content landscape polluted by 54% AI-generated posts
- And the most experienced coaches producing the least content

The problem isn't that coaches need to "post more." It's not a discipline problem or a motivation problem or a time management problem.

It's a translation problem.

The expertise exists. The stories exist. The frameworks exist. The unique perspectives exist. All of it is inside the coach's head, deployed expertly in sessions, invisible everywhere else.

The translation layer between "how you think" and "what the world sees" is missing.

And nobody's building it.

Ghostwriters try, but they capture voice -- not thinking. The content comes back sounding like it was written *for* a coach, not *by* that coach.

AI tools try, but they generate from patterns -- not from your specific methodology. The output is grammatically perfect and experientially empty.

Content templates try, but they structure someone else's thinking -- not yours. The posts are publishable but indistinguishable from 10,000 other coaches using the same template.

The gap is not "how to write." The gap is "how to extract what you already know and translate it into language your ideal clients already use."

That's a different skill entirely. And it's the one I'm building my practice around.

## What This Means If You're a Coach

You're not failing at content. You're experiencing a well-documented cognitive phenomenon that gets worse as you get better at your craft.

The Curse of Knowledge isn't a flaw. It's a tax on expertise. And like any tax, it doesn't mean you're doing something wrong -- it means you need a different approach to solve it.

If you've tried ghostwriters and they missed the mark -- it's probably not because they were bad writers. It's because they were trying to capture your voice when they should have been capturing your thinking.

If you've tried AI tools and the output felt generic -- it's because AI generates from statistical patterns, not from your 4,000 hours of session experience.

If you've tried templates and you felt like a fraud posting them -- good. That feeling is your expertise rejecting content that doesn't carry your actual methodology.

The fix isn't writing more. It's not "being more consistent." It's not another content calendar.

The fix is having someone extract how you actually think and teach -- and translate it into content that a prospect can feel.
