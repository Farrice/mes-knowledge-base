## 1. POSITIONING: LinkedIn vs. Substack

**LinkedIn** = the hook. Short-form, scroll-stopping, frustration-mirroring content that makes coaches stop and think "this person is inside my head." Visibility engine. Reach machine.

**Substack** = the depth. Long-form essays that prove you're not just a good writer -- you're a *thinker*. The place where a coach who found you on LinkedIn goes to decide: "Can I trust this person with my voice?"

**The distinction matters because:**
- LinkedIn rewards hooks. Substack rewards follow-through.
- LinkedIn builds awareness. Substack builds relationship.
- LinkedIn gets you noticed. Substack gets you *chosen*.
- LinkedIn posts disappear in 48 hours. Substack editions compound in an archive.

**Positioning statement**: "The weekly newsletter for coaches who are brilliant on calls but invisible online -- exploring the psychology of why expertise doesn't translate to content, and what to do about it."

## 3. CONTENT PILLARS

Mirroring LinkedIn's pillars but with depth and longer-form treatment:

| Pillar | LinkedIn Version | Substack Version | Frequency |
|--------|-----------------|------------------|-----------|
| **Frustration Deep Dives** | Short frustration mirrors (200-400 words) | Full psychology behind the frustration -- why it exists, what maintains it, and the specific fix (800-1200 words) | 2x/month |
| **Methodology Reveals** | Before/after extraction demos | Detailed walkthrough of the process, the thinking behind it, and what makes it different from traditional ghostwriting | 2x/month |
| **Build-in-Public Updates** | Quick transparency reports (numbers, wins, losses) | Full behind-the-scenes essays -- what happened this week, what I learned, real revenue numbers, real failures | 2x/month |
| **Industry Analysis** | Contrarian one-liners with data | Deep essays backed by ICF data, LinkedIn algorithm research, ghostwriting market analysis. The pieces that make coaches say "I've never seen anyone break this down like this." | 1x/month |

**Rotation**: Each edition leads with one pillar but can blend elements from others. The welcome edition and special editions (case studies, reader Q&A) don't need to fit a pillar.

## 4. FREQUENCY & TIMING

**Cadence**: Weekly. Every Monday at 8:00 AM EST.

**Why Monday:**
- Primes the week before Tue-Thu LinkedIn posts
- Coaches are planning their week Monday morning -- your newsletter lands when they're thinking about visibility
- Creates a rhythm: Monday = depth (Substack), Tue-Thu = visibility (LinkedIn)
- Monday is LinkedIn's engagement-only day (no post), so Substack fills the content gap

**Why weekly, not more:**
- You're building a service business, not a media company
- Quality > frequency at this stage -- one excellent edition beats three mediocre ones
- Weekly is sustainable within the 1-3 hour/day work constraint
- Gives you 7 days of LinkedIn content to reference and deepen in each edition

**Production time target**: 60-90 minutes per edition (draft + edit + schedule). Write on Sunday evening, schedule for Monday 8 AM.

## 5. GROWTH TACTICS

### Primary: LinkedIn Cross-Promotion
- **Bio link**: Substack URL in LinkedIn profile (Featured section + contact info)
- **Post comments**: Drop Substack link in first comment of relevant posts: "I wrote a deeper version of this in my newsletter -- link in first comment"
- **Weekly teaser**: One LinkedIn post per week that's explicitly a teaser for the newsletter: "In tomorrow's newsletter, I break down [topic]. Subscribe link in my profile."
- **DM conversations**: When a coach expresses interest in voice extraction methodology, send the relevant newsletter edition: "I wrote about this in detail -- want me to send the link?"

### Secondary: Product Integration
- **Voice Gap Playbook**: Include newsletter signup link inside the PDF (page 2 and final page)
- **Proof Run onboarding**: Add new Proof Run clients to the newsletter automatically (with permission)
- **Proof Run debrief**: Share the case study newsletter with the featured coach so they share it with their network

### Tertiary: Organic Substack Growth
- **Substack recommendations**: Recommend 3-5 complementary newsletters (coaching, LinkedIn strategy, solopreneurship) and request reciprocal recommendations
- **Substack Notes**: Cross-post key insights from newsletters as Substack Notes (Substack's social feed)
- **Reader replies**: Encourage replies ("Hit reply and tell me...") -- replies boost deliverability and create 1:1 conversations that convert

### Growth targets:

| Timeframe | Subscriber Target | Source |
|-----------|------------------|--------|
| Week 1-4 | 50-100 | LinkedIn bio + DM conversations + friends/network |
| Week 5-8 | 100-250 | LinkedIn post teasers + Playbook buyers + Substack recommendations |
| Week 9-12 | 250-500 | Compounding LinkedIn growth + Proof Run case studies shared by featured coaches |
| Month 4-6 | 500-1,000 | All channels compounding + word-of-mouth + archive SEO |

## 6. NEWSLETTER STRUCTURE (Standard Template)

Every edition follows this skeleton. Deviation is fine for special editions (case studies, Q&A), but the default is:

```
SUBJECT LINE
- 6-10 words, curiosity-driven, no clickbait
- Format: Statement or question that creates a gap
- Examples: "The $0 content problem in a $16B industry"
           "What happened when I extracted a coach's voice in 30 minutes"
           "Why your ghostwriter failed you (it wasn't their fault)"

PREVIEW TEXT
- 1 sentence that complements (not repeats) the subject line
- Shows up in inbox preview -- this is the second hook

---

OPENING (100-200 words)
- Start with a story, observation, or provocative claim
- No throat-clearing. No "Hey friend!" No "Happy Monday!"
- First sentence should stop a coach mid-scroll in their inbox
- Establish the tension or question the essay will resolve

BODY (500-900 words)
- The meat. One core idea explored with depth.
- Use single-line paragraphs (mirror LinkedIn voice)
- Include at least one of:
  - A specific data point or research finding
  - A real story or example (client, personal, observed)
  - A framework or mental model
  - A before/after transformation
- Bold key phrases for scanners
- Break into 2-3 sections with clear progression

BRIDGE (100-150 words)
- Connect the essay's insight to the reader's situation
- "Here's why this matters for you specifically..."
- This is where the essay shifts from "interesting" to "useful"

CTA (50-100 words)
- Soft, not salesy. One of these depending on the edition:
  - Reply CTA: "Hit reply and tell me [specific question]"
  - Playbook CTA: "I put the full framework into the Voice Gap Playbook -- $37, link below"
  - Proof Run CTA: "If you want to see what your content sounds like when someone captures how you think -- DM me POSTS on LinkedIn"
  - Share CTA: "Know a coach who's dealing with this? Forward this to them."
- Rotate CTAs. Never use the same one twice in a row.
- Maximum ONE CTA per edition. Pick the most natural fit.

---

FOOTER
- "Written by Farrice Cain"
- LinkedIn profile link
- One-line: "I help coaches stop being invisible on LinkedIn by extracting how they already think."
```

## 9. CTA STRATEGY

**Principle**: Every newsletter should feel like a gift, not a pitch. The CTA is the natural next step for someone who found the essay valuable -- not a commercial interruption.

### CTA Rotation

| CTA Type | When to Use | Example |
|----------|------------|---------|
| **Reply** | Build-in-public editions, frustration deep dives | "Hit reply and tell me: what's the one thing about LinkedIn that drives you crazy?" |
| **Playbook** | Methodology reveals, industry analysis | "I put the full framework into the Voice Gap Playbook. It's $37 and it walks you through identifying your own voice gaps. [Link]" |
| **Proof Run** | Case studies, frustration deep dives | "If this resonated and you want to see what your content sounds like when someone captures how you think -- DM me 'POSTS' on LinkedIn." |
| **Share** | Welcome edition, case studies, contrarian essays | "Know a coach who's dealing with this? Forward this edition to them. It might be the push they need." |

### CTA Rules
1. **One CTA per edition. Maximum.** Multiple CTAs dilute all of them.
2. **Never repeat the same CTA type two weeks in a row.** Rotate to keep it fresh.
3. **Match the CTA to the content.** A frustration deep-dive naturally leads to Proof Run. A methodology reveal naturally leads to Playbook.
4. **The CTA should feel like generosity.** "I built this thing that might help" > "Buy my thing."
5. **Track which CTAs get clicks.** Double down on what converts, retire what doesn't.
