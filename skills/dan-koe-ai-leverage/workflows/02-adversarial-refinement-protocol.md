## PHASE 1: TARGET INTAKE

Ask the user for:
1. **The Draft**: Content, strategy, idea, argument, framework, or plan to stress-test
2. **Stakes Level**: 
   - **Standard** — blog post, tweet, casual content → moderate pressure
   - **High** — newsletter, client deliverable, strategic decision → aggressive pressure 
   - **Critical** — public positioning, product launch, irreversible decision → adversarial maximum
3. **Known Vulnerabilities**: "Is there anything you already suspect might be weak?" (Pre-seeding accelerates the process)
4. **Audience Context**: Who will see this? What would make THEM skeptical?

---

## PHASE 2: STRUCTURAL ANALYSIS

Before attacking, understand the architecture of what you're stress-testing:

### 2A: Claim Mapping
Identify every claim — explicit and implicit — in the draft:
- **Explicit claims**: What the user directly states
- **Implicit claims**: What the user assumes their audience will accept without evidence
- **Structural claims**: What has to be true for the overall argument to hold

### 2B: Dependency Mapping
Map which claims depend on which. Find the **load-bearing walls** — if one claim falls, what collapses with it?

### 2C: Assumptions Inventory
List every assumption the user is making:
- About their audience's knowledge
- About market conditions
- About cause-and-effect relationships
- About their own expertise/authority

Present this analysis: "Here's the structural map of your argument. These are the load-bearing claims and hidden assumptions I've identified."

---

## PHASE 3: ADVERSARIAL ATTACK (5 Vectors)

Run the draft through each attack vector. Calibrate intensity to the stakes level.

### Vector 1: The Blind Spot Sweep
"What are you NOT seeing? What are the perspectives you haven't considered?"
- Identify positions or counter-arguments the user hasn't addressed
- Surface the audience members who would disagree and WHY
- Find the obvious objection a skeptic would raise immediately

### Vector 2: The Assumption Crusher
"Which of your assumptions are weakest?"
- For each assumption from Phase 2C, rate fragility (low/medium/high)
- Challenge the highest-fragility assumptions directly
- Propose scenarios where the assumption fails

### Vector 3: The Devil's Advocate
"Let me argue the OPPOSITE position."
- Construct the strongest possible counter-argument
- Not a straw man — a steel man. The best version of the opposing view.
- Identify what's TRUE in the opposing view that the user should acknowledge

### Vector 4: The Expert Critic
"What would a domain expert flag?"
- Read the draft as someone with 20 years of experience in this domain
- Flag oversimplifications, nuances, and complexity the user is skipping
- Identify where the user is right but for the wrong reasons

### Vector 5: The Audience Proxy
"How will your SPECIFIC audience receive this?"
- Read as the target audience, not as a neutral party
- Identify confusing points, trust gaps, and "I would stop reading here" moments
- Flag the gap between what the user intends and what the audience will perceive

---

## PHASE 4: REFINEMENT PRESCRIPTIONS

For every vulnerability found, provide:

```
🔴 [VULNERABILITY NAME]
What's weak: [Specific description]
Why it matters: [What happens if you don't fix it]
Prescription: [Exact fix — specific language changes, structural moves, additions]
Effort: [Quick fix / Moderate rework / Structural rebuild]
```

Organize prescriptions by priority:
1. **Must Fix** — Load-bearing failures, factual errors, audience trust-breakers
2. **Should Fix** — Strength amplifiers, nuance additions, persuasion upgrades
3. **Could Fix** — Polish items, style improvements, optional enhancements

---

## PHASE 5: REBUILT OUTPUT

If the user requests it, produce a refined version that incorporates all "Must Fix" and "Should Fix" prescriptions while preserving the user's voice and original intent.

Then run a **verification pass**: "Here's what's stronger now, and here's what's still worth watching."

## QUALITY GATE (Dan Koe Standard)

Ask yourself before delivering:
1. **The Honest Friend Test**: Did you find REAL weaknesses, or did you manufacture trivial ones to seem thorough? If you found zero high-priority issues, say so — don't invent problems.
2. **The Steel Man Test**: Is your strongest counter-argument genuinely strong, or a straw man? Could the opposing side read your devil's advocate and say "yes, that's our actual position"?
3. **The Learning Test**: Will the user's NEXT piece of content be better because of this adversarial pass? Did you surface patterns, not just one-off fixes?
