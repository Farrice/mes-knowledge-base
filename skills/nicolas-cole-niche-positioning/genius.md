# Nicolas Cole — Niche Positioning Genius Patterns

These are Cole's actual mental models, not labels. Each pattern is a way of thinking that unlocks a specific type of positioning problem.

---

## Pattern 1: The Specificity Ladder

**The insight**: Every positioning problem is a specificity problem. "Health & wellness" is not a niche. "Health coaches" is not a niche. "Female health coaches over 40 with a successful offline practice who can't translate their expertise to LinkedIn" is getting close. "Jessica — former hospital nutritionist, 8 years in private practice, 12K Instagram, zero LinkedIn presence, frustrated that people with less experience are getting the speaking gigs she deserves" — that's a niche.

**The drill**:
1. Start broad: What industry?
2. Narrow to category: What type of person in that industry?
3. Narrow to niche: What specific problem do they have?
4. Narrow to micro-niche: What specific stage or context are they in?
5. Named Person Test: Can you name a real human being you've met or could find who IS this person?

**The signal**: Discomfort at step 4-5 = you've hit something real. If it still feels comfortable, you're not specific enough.

---

## Pattern 2: Lived Experience as Moat

**The insight**: The most defensible positioning comes from experience you can't buy or fake. Years inside an industry, personal failure in a domain, a specific wound or transformation — these create an unfair advantage that no competitor can replicate by reading the same books.

**The application**: The thing you're embarrassed about, the experience you think disqualifies you, the background you've been ignoring — that's usually the moat. It's the thing that makes "who else could say this?" impossible to answer.

**The test**: Could someone who never lived your experience write this positioning statement? If yes, strip it down until the answer is no.

---

## Pattern 3: The Bias Inversion

**The insight**: What you believe about the people you DON'T want to serve is a map to the people you DO. "H&W people are broke" is information — it means you've been inside that world, seen the majority, and know where the pattern breaks. The people who escaped that pattern are your actual ICP. Your bias is pointing at the arrow.

**The application**:
1. State the bias explicitly: "I think X people are Y"
2. Ask: Who in that group is NOT Y?
3. Ask: What did they do differently to escape that pattern?
4. Ask: Could your help accelerate that escape?
5. That person = your ICP

**The reframe**: You don't have a bias problem. You have an incomplete specificity drill.

---

## Pattern 4: Category of One

**The insight**: You can't be positioned if you're competing in someone else's category. The goal is to name an intersection so specific that you ARE the category. When someone asks "who does X for Y through Z?" and the answer is only you — that's a Category of One.

**The formula**: "I help [X — the Named Person] achieve [Y — the specific transformation] through [Z — the mechanism only you have because of your lived experience]."

**The test**: Could any of your competitors say this exact sentence? If yes, Z is wrong — you haven't named your mechanism yet.

---

## Pattern 5: "Who Specifically" Before Everything

**The insight**: Platform, offer, content strategy, pricing, voice — all of these become clear automatically once "who specifically" is locked. Trying to figure out content strategy before niche = trying to build a house before picking the lot. The order is non-negotiable.

**The application**: Every time someone wants to jump to "what should I post" or "what should I charge" — stop. Run the specificity drill first. The rest answers itself.

**The tell**: If you're arguing about tactics (format, cadence, platform), you've skipped the positioning question.

---

## Pattern 6: The Named Person Test

**The insight**: A persona is a fiction. A Named Person is a real human being you could call right now. Until you can pass the Named Person Test, you don't have a niche — you have a demographic description.

**The test**:
1. Can you name this person? (First name, last name or LinkedIn URL)
2. Do you know what they do specifically?
3. Do you know what problem they have that you solve?
4. Would they recognize themselves if they read your positioning statement?
5. Could you find 100 more people exactly like them?

**The unlock**: Once you pass this test, your content writes itself. You're literally writing to one person — everything lands because it's specific enough to feel personal at scale.
