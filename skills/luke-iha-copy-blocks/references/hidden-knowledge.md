## 1. Hooks Are 80% of Performance
Most copywriters spend 80% of their time on body copy. But the hook determines whether anyone reads the body. Luke's time allocation is inverted: 80% of effort on hooks, 20% on body. The best body copy in the world is worthless if no one reads past the first line. A mediocre body with a killer hook will always outperform a killer body with a mediocre hook. Test hooks independently before committing to body copy development.

## 2. Style Changes Rhythm, Not Content
In the CASH Method, "Style" (UGC vs editorial vs talking head vs meme) doesn't change WHAT you say — it changes the RHYTHM of copy block deployment. UGC has rapid, informal block cycling with short sentences and conversational transitions. Editorial has measured, evidence-heavy cycling with longer proof sections. Talking head has intimate, story-driven blocks with personal anecdotes. Understanding this means you can take the same Concept × Angle and deploy it across multiple styles WITHOUT rethinking the message.

## 3. Concept ≠ Product
The most common CASH failure is treating the Concept as the product description. The concept is the EMOTIONAL TERRITORY the ad operates in — the paradigm, worldview, or big idea that frames everything downstream. "Your skin is aging because of inflammation" is a concept. "Our serum contains niacinamide" is a product feature. The concept determines the conversation the audience enters; the product is introduced later within that conversation. Getting the concept wrong makes every downstream angle and hook misaligned.

## 4. Copy Blocks Aren't Sequential
Beginners use blocks in order: Pain → Promise → Proof → CTA. Experts interleave them: Pain → Curiosity → Promise → Pain (new angle) → Proof → Curiosity → Constraint → Promise (new angle) → Proof → CTA. The variable speed creates engagement through unpredictability. The reader never knows what's coming next, but each block feels inevitable after it appears. Deliberate velocity variation — fast in the opening, measured in the middle, slow before the CTA — creates psychological momentum that carries the reader to the close.
