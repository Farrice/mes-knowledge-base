## EXAMPLE OUTPUT 1: LinkedIn Thread on Remote Work Productivity

**TOPIC**: How remote workers actually get things done
**CORE INSIGHT**: The most productive remote workers have eliminated the concept of "work hours"—they work in energy cycles, not time blocks
**PLATFORM**: LinkedIn
**AUDIENCE**: Remote workers, managers, HR leaders
**FORMAT**: Thread/carousel post

---

**THE CONTENT:**

**[POST 1 - HOOK]**

I've been working remotely for 5 years.

My most productive day last month was a Tuesday where I worked 4 hours.

My least productive day was a Thursday where I worked 11 hours.

The difference wasn't discipline. It was something most productivity advice gets completely wrong.

🧵 Here's what actually works (after 5 years of experimenting on myself):

---

**[POST 2 - RECOGNITION + INSIGHT]**

First, the lie we all believe:

"More hours = more output."

This would be true if humans were printers. We're not. We're more like WiFi routers—sometimes we're running at full speed, sometimes we're buffering for no apparent reason, and occasionally we need to be unplugged and plugged back in.

The solution isn't working harder during buffer mode. It's recognizing buffer mode and doing something else.

---

**[POST 3 - FRAMEWORK]**

I track my energy in 3 states:

🟢 **CREATE MODE** — Deep work possible. Protect this time like it owes you money.

🟡 **CONNECT MODE** — Good for meetings, emails, collaboration. Not great for thinking.

🔴 **CONSUME MODE** — Your brain is done creating. Read, learn, or log off. Fighting this is how you get an 11-hour day with 2 hours of output.

Most people try to CREATE in CONSUME mode. That's like trying to make espresso with an empty water tank.

---

**[POST 4 - SPECIFIC TACTIC]**

The tactical shift:

I stopped scheduling my day by TIME.
I started scheduling by ENERGY.

Old way: "I'll write that proposal from 2-4pm."
New way: "I'll write that proposal in my next CREATE window, whenever that hits."

Sometimes that's 6am. Sometimes that's 9pm. Sometimes it's 2pm on a random Tuesday.

The work doesn't care when it gets done. It cares that it gets done WELL.

---

**[POST 5 - PAYOFF + CTA]**

The irony:

Since I stopped trying to work "normal hours," I work fewer hours total AND produce better work.

My manager sees output, not online status. If yours doesn't—that's a conversation worth having.

Remote work is a trust test, and the ones who pass it aren't working 9-5. They're working 4 focused hours and living the rest.

---

📌 Save this if you needed permission to stop forcing productivity during buffer mode.

♻️ Share if you know someone who needs to hear "you're not lazy, you're just in consume mode."

## EXAMPLE OUTPUT 2: Twitter/X Thread on Startup Mistakes

**TOPIC**: Mistakes first-time founders make
**CORE INSIGHT**: The biggest mistakes aren't strategic—they're emotional. First-time founders confuse motion with progress.
**PLATFORM**: Twitter/X
**AUDIENCE**: Founders, aspiring founders, startup employees
**FORMAT**: Thread

---

**THE CONTENT:**

**1/**
I've watched 50+ startups die.

Not from bad ideas.
Not from competition.
Not from lack of funding.

From founders who couldn't tell the difference between "being busy" and "making progress."

Here are 7 mistakes that kill companies while founders feel productive:

🧵

---

**2/**
Mistake 1: Building features nobody asked for.

"But users WILL want this when they see it."

No. They won't. Because they didn't ask. You're not building a product—you're building a monument to your own assumptions.

The graveyard of startups is full of "visionary" features with zero users.

---

**3/**
Mistake 2: Taking meetings that should be emails.

Every "quick sync" costs:
→ The meeting time
→ The context-switch time
→ The "recovering from human interaction" time

First-time founders take every meeting because it FEELS like progress.

Experienced founders know: most meetings are productivity theater.

---

**4/**
Mistake 3: Optimizing things that don't matter yet.

You have 12 users. You don't need to "scale the architecture."

You have $0 revenue. You don't need a "brand refresh."

You have no product-market fit. You don't need a VP of Marketing.

First, find the fire. THEN worry about the fire extinguisher.

---

**5/**
Mistake 4: Fundraising as a full-time job.

I've seen founders spend 6 months raising money for a product they could have launched in 6 weeks.

Investors want to see traction. Traction comes from building. Building happens while you're not in VC meetings.

The pitch deck won't save you. The product might.

---

**6/**
Mistake 5: Hiring to feel like a "real company."

You don't need:
- A COO (you're 3 people)
- An HR director (you're 5 people)
- A "Head of Culture" (you ARE the culture)

Every premature hire is salary you're burning and complexity you're adding.

Stay small until small doesn't work. THEN hire.

---

**7/**
Mistake 6: Copying what worked for someone else.

"But Airbnb did [X] and it worked."

Airbnb also had:
- Different market conditions
- Different timing
- Different founders
- Different luck

You're not Airbnb. What worked for them is a data point, not a playbook.

---

**8/**
Mistake 7: Confusing motion with progress.

The deadliest one.

Motion: Busy calendars, full Slack channels, long hours
Progress: More users, more revenue, clearer product-market fit

One feels productive. One IS productive.

Learn to tell the difference or die comfortable, exhausted, and confused about what went wrong.

---

**9/**
The common thread:

All 7 mistakes FEEL like you're building a company.
None of them actually build a company.

The founders who survive aren't smarter. They're just better at asking:

"Is this motion or progress?"

Answer honestly. Act accordingly.

---

**10/**
If you're a first-time founder reading this:

You're not behind. You're not failing. You're just learning what everyone learns the hard way.

But you don't have to.

📌 Save this list. Check it monthly.
♻️ Share it with a founder who needs the reminder.
