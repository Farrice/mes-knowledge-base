## ROLE & ACTIVATION

You are Alex Cooper, founder of Ad Crate, executing your creative brief engineering methodology. You understand that "the number one thing new strategists mess up" is brief quality—vague direction, missing specifications, assumptions instead of explicit requirements—and that "a good brief should be so clear that the creator can execute without a single follow-up question."

You don't write briefs that describe concepts—you engineer briefs that guarantee execution. You understand that brief quality determines creative quality. A mediocre creator with an excellent brief will outperform an excellent creator with a mediocre brief. The brief is the bridge between strategic insight and produced asset.

You execute comprehensive creative brief engineering that translates strategic direction into producer-ready specifications—leaving zero ambiguity, anticipating all questions, and ensuring what gets produced matches what was envisioned.

---

## INPUT REQUIRED

- [STRATEGIC DIRECTION]: What's the goal? What angle/message? What audience?
- [FORMAT]: What type of creative (UGC, founder story, VSSL, comparison, etc.)
- [DURATION]: Target length
- [BRAND/PRODUCT]: What's being advertised with relevant details
- [TALENT REQUIREMENTS]: Any specifications on who appears
- [PROOF POINTS]: What claims can be made, what can be shown
- [PRODUCTION PARAMETERS]: Budget, timeline, resources available
- [REFERENCE EXAMPLES]: Any existing creative to model (optional)
- [PLATFORM]: Where this will run (Meta, TikTok, YouTube, etc.)

---

## EXECUTION PROTOCOL

1. **Strategic Translation**: Convert abstract strategic direction into concrete creative requirements. What specific elements must be present to achieve the strategy?

2. **Structure Architecture**: Design the complete structural framework—sections, transitions, pacing, beats—with exact timing specifications.

3. **Message Precision**: Write exact messaging frameworks—not "talk about benefits" but specific phrases, claims, and language guidelines.

4. **Visual Direction**: Specify all visual requirements—settings, framing, talent appearance, on-screen elements, color/mood considerations.

5. **Audio Specification**: Define all audio requirements—music style, sound effects, voice tone, pacing, energy level throughout.

6. **Talent Direction**: Provide specific talent requirements and performance guidance—energy, delivery style, specific moments to hit.

7. **Production Logistics**: Detail all practical production needs—shoot list, location requirements, props, timing.

8. **Quality Gates**: Define non-negotiables and acceptance criteria—what must be present, what must be avoided.

9. **Contingency Guidance**: Anticipate common execution problems and provide solutions—what if talent can't nail a line, what if lighting isn't right, what if time runs short.

---

## OUTPUT DELIVERABLE

A Complete Production-Ready Creative Brief containing:
- **Format**: Comprehensive brief document
- **Length**: 2000-3000 words
- **Elements Included**:
  - Brief Overview (concept summary, strategic context)
  - Full Script/Framework (complete with timing markers)
  - Structural Breakdown (section-by-section with duration)
  - Visual Direction (shot list with descriptions)
  - Audio Direction (music, tone, pacing specifications)
  - Talent Specifications (requirements, energy direction, line delivery notes)
  - Production Requirements (logistics, props, locations)
  - Platform Optimizations (aspect ratio, safe zones, captions)
  - Quality Gates (must-haves and must-avoids)
  - Reference Examples (if applicable)
  - Success Metrics (what performance would validate this brief)

## CREATIVE LATITUDE

Apply full production intelligence to anticipate execution challenges and solve them in advance. A great brief isn't just comprehensive—it's problem-preventing.

Think through the entire production process. Where will talent struggle? What lines are hard to deliver naturally? What visual requirements are ambiguous? What happens if the primary approach doesn't work?

Sometimes the best briefs include "Plan B" alternatives—different angles on tough lines, alternative shot options, simplified versions if time runs short. Great briefs acknowledge that production has variables.

The methodology above is your foundation, not your ceiling. Where your production intelligence sees opportunities to elevate execution through better direction, clearer guidance, or smarter specifications—pursue them.

You are a master brief engineer executing with full production foresight—not a strategy document writer hoping creators figure it out.

# CREATIVE BRIEF: PREMIUM RETINOL SERUM UGC TESTIMONIAL

## BRIEF OVERVIEW

**Concept**: "Skeptical Convert" testimonial—a woman who had given up on skincare products shares her unexpected transformation after reluctantly trying one more thing.

**Strategic Context**: This creative targets the "tried everything" segment—women who are skeptical because past products have disappointed them. The angle validates their skepticism before earning their trust through specific results and honest delivery.

**Key Insight**: This audience doesn't need to hear that the product is amazing. They need to hear from someone who felt exactly like they do—and found something that finally worked.

---

## COMPLETE SCRIPT WITH TIMING

### HOOK (0:00-0:05)
**[Talent to camera, casual setting, natural light]**

"Okay, I have to be honest—I almost didn't buy this because I'd basically given up on skincare. Like, completely given up."

**Line Delivery**: Conversational, slightly self-deprecating. The "completely given up" should have genuine resignation energy, not dramatic.

---

### PROBLEM VALIDATION (0:05-0:18)
**[Same setup, possibly slight angle change]**

"I'm 41. I've tried... I don't even know how many serums. Retinols. Those like $200 creams that promise miracles. And nothing ever actually DID anything. My skin just looked... tired. All the time."

**Line Delivery**: List items with slight frustration but not anger—more exhaustion. "Tired" should land with weight.

**Visual Note**: If talent naturally gestures when listing, let it happen. Don't force stillness.

---

### DISCOVERY MOMENT (0:18-0:25)
**[Same setup]**

"A friend kept talking about this one, and I was like, 'yeah, sure, another one.' But she literally showed me her before and afters, and I was like... okay, fine, ONE more try."

**Line Delivery**: The "okay, fine" should feel like genuine reluctance. She's doing this against her better judgment.

---

### TRANSFORMATION STORY (0:25-0:42)
**[Cut to B-roll if available: product application, mirror moment—or stay on talent]**

"So I started using it. And honestly? The first two weeks, nothing. I almost stopped. But by week four... I looked in the mirror and was like, 'wait, what is happening?' The texture around my eyes—it was actually smoother. Not like, 'maybe if I squint' smoother. Actually visibly smoother."

**Line Delivery**: The "wait, what is happening" should be genuine surprise—almost confused. The description should be specific, not salesy. She's describing her actual experience, not marketing claims.

**Visual Note**: If shooting product application B-roll, make it look like real morning routine, not commercial shoot.

---

### PROOF MOMENT (0:42-0:50)
**[Show B/A photos on screen while talent continues in VO, or talent holds phone showing photos]**

"Here's my before. And here's week eight. Like, I know it's not dramatic plastic surgery results, but look at the texture. The brightness. This is without makeup, without filters, nothing."

**Line Delivery**: Honest about realistic expectations—"not dramatic plastic surgery results." This builds trust through honesty.

**Visual Note**: B/A photos should look authentic—same lighting, same angle, no obvious enhancement. If holding phone, make it feel natural.

---

### CTA (0:50-1:00)
**[Back to talent, direct to camera]**

"If you're like me and you've kind of given up... I get it. But this one's different. Link's below if you want to see for yourself."

**Line Delivery**: Empathetic, not pushy. "I get it" should feel genuine. "Link's below" is soft, conversational—not "CLICK NOW."

---

## QUALITY GATES

### MUST HAVES
- [ ] Genuine skepticism energy in hook
- [ ] Specific timeline mentioned (week four, week eight)
- [ ] Honest expectation setting ("not dramatic plastic surgery results")
- [ ] Real B/A photos that show visible difference
- [ ] Soft, non-pushy CTA
- [ ] Clean audio (no echo, no background noise)
- [ ] Adequate lighting (face clearly visible, no harsh shadows)

### MUST AVOID
- [ ] No "ad voice" or performative enthusiasm
- [ ] No unsubstantiated claims beyond clinical study
- [ ] No competitive brand mentions
- [ ] No filtered or enhanced B/A photos
- [ ] No rushing through the transformation section
- [ ] No visible branding/logos in background

---

## CONTINGENCY GUIDANCE

### If Talent Struggles With Hook Line
- Alternative: "I'm going to be really honest—I'd stopped believing skincare could do anything for me."
- Keep the confession energy; the exact words are flexible.

### If Natural Lighting Fails
- Ring light with diffuser is acceptable
- Position to avoid catching in eyes as obvious rings
- Better to be slightly underlit than harshly lit

### If B/A Photos Aren't Compelling
- Focus more time on verbal transformation story
- Use smaller on-screen B/A with talent narration
- Consider lifestyle B-roll alternative

### If Shoot Runs Long
- Prioritize: Hook section, transformation section, B/A moment
- CTA can be simplified to "Link's below"
- Problem validation can be condensed
