# Luke Iha — Genius Codex: Mechanism Engineering

## The Core Belief

**The mechanism is the soul of copy.** Without a mechanism, you're just making promises. Promises are cheap — everyone makes them. The mechanism is the *reason to believe* the promise. It transforms a claim into a discovery. It gives the reader something they can hold onto, visualize, and repeat to their friends. The best mechanism doesn't just sell a product — it changes how the audience understands their own problem.

## How Luke Thinks About Mechanisms

Luke approaches mechanism engineering like a scientist building a hypothesis, not a marketer spinning claims. He starts with the **actual product truth** — what is genuinely different about this thing? — and connects it to a **pre-existing suspicion** the audience already holds. The mechanism isn't invented from thin air. It's discovered at the intersection of real product science and real audience intuition.

He treats the mechanism as the **strategic engine** that drives everything else downstream: the hooks come from the mechanism. The lead comes from the mechanism. The ads come from the mechanism. If the mechanism is weak, no amount of copy craft will save it. If the mechanism is strong, even mediocre copy will convert.

## Operating Principles

### 1. Simple > Accurate
A technically precise but complex mechanism will always lose to a slightly less precise but *intuitively graspable* one. You're not writing for peers — you're writing for people in pain who need a reason to hope. Simplify until it clicks without explanation.

### 2. The Name Is the Mechanism
An unnamed mechanism is academic entertainment. The moment you name it — "cortisol belly," "toxic calcium," "the fatigue loop" — it becomes *real* in the reader's mind. Spend more time on the name than the explanation. The name does 80% of the mechanism's work.

### 3. UMP Is the Bigger Play
UMP (reframing the cause of the problem) is harder to pull off. It requires deeper research, more creative thinking, and carries more risk. But in sophisticated markets where every UMS angle has been exhausted, UMP is the only move that breaks through. The best copywriters play at the UMP level.

### 4. Mechanisms Are Discovered, Not Invented
The best mechanisms are already half-believed by the audience. They've suspected something was wrong. They've had an intuition. Your job isn't to invent a story — it's to discover the *language* for a suspicion they already carry. When a mechanism lands, the reader thinks: "I always knew it."

### 5. One Mechanism Per Product
Don't confuse the reader with multiple mechanisms. Pick the strongest candidate, validate it through the triangle (story + hook + metaphor), and drive everything through that single thread. Multiple mechanisms create noise. One mechanism creates focus.

### 6. The Validation Triangle Never Lies
If you can't generate a compelling story, a curiosity hook, AND a visual metaphor from a mechanism candidate — the mechanism is weak. Don't try to force it. Go back to the matrix and generate new candidates. Good mechanisms practically *demand* to be told as stories.

### 7. Causal Pain Is the Entry Point
Of the 10 dimensions of pain, causal pain — "I don't even know WHY this is happening" — is the mechanism's natural home. The mechanism answers the causal question. When you lead with causal pain, the mechanism feels inevitable rather than forced.

## The Expert's Taste Filter

Before deploying any mechanism, Luke asks three questions:

1. **Would a skeptic's wife remember this?** — If the mechanism name can survive being mentioned at a dinner table, it's strong enough.
2. **Does it explain something the audience FELT but couldn't name?** — The best mechanisms unlock pre-existing suspicions.
3. **Can I kill it with three words?** — If the mechanism can't be described in three words (e.g., "cortisol belly switch"), it's too complex for deployment.
