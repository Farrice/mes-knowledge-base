## COMPLETE NEWSLETTER

**Subject: The marketing strategy nobody talks about (because it feels wrong)**

---

Here's an uncomfortable truth about building a following:

Shared interests are nice.
Shared enemies are powerful.

I call this the Enemy Effect, and once you see it, you can't unsee it.

**The Psychology of Opposition**

Humans are tribal animals. We've spent 200,000 years organizing ourselves into groups for survival. And the fastest way to identify your group?

Find the other group. The one you're NOT.

This isn't cynical. It's anthropological.

The brands that create the strongest followings don't just stand FOR something. They stand AGAINST something.

- Apple stood against "boring corporate computing"
- Tesla stood against "the oil industry status quo"
- CrossFit stood against "the traditional fitness industry"

They didn't just attract customers who wanted their products. They attracted believers who wanted to JOIN A FIGHT.

**The Enemy Is Not A Person**

Before you get nervous: I'm not talking about attacking individuals.

The most effective enemies are ideologies, not people.

- "Hustle culture" (enemy of sustainable success)
- "Corporate mediocrity" (enemy of creative excellence)
- "The credentialing industrial complex" (enemy of real expertise)
- "The attention economy" (enemy of meaningful connection)

When you name an ideology as the enemy, several magical things happen:

1. **People self-identify.** "Yes! I hate that too!" is the beginning of belonging.

2. **You create instant differentiation.** Instead of being "another marketing consultant," you're "the one who fights against [enemy]."

3. **Your content gets sharper.** Every piece can reference the enemy, creating a through-line that builds recognition.

4. **You attract the right people AND repel the wrong ones.** The people who love your enemy? They self-select out. Good.

**How To Find Your Enemy**

Your enemy is hiding in your frustrations.

What makes you angry about your industry?
What advice do you hear constantly that you think is wrong?
What do your ideal clients complain about before they find you?

That frustration, systematized and named, becomes your enemy.

When I work with clients on brand positioning, one of the first questions I ask is: "What's broken about how people currently solve this problem?"

The answer usually reveals the enemy.

**The Enemy In Practice**

Let me show you how this works in real content.

WITHOUT enemy:
"I help consultants build better businesses."

WITH enemy:
"I help consultants escape the commodity trapâ€”where you compete on price instead of expertise."

Same service. Completely different positioning.

The second version does something the first can't: it names a shared frustration. When a consultant reads "the commodity trap," they feel SEEN. They didn't even know there was a name for their situation.

That recognition is the beginning of trust.

**The Caution**

The Enemy Effect is powerful. Use it carefully.

- Never make the enemy a person or specific group of people
- Punch up, not down (attack systems, not vulnerable populations)
- Your enemy should be something your audience already dislikesâ€”you're giving it a name, not creating new hatred
- The enemy should be genuinely problematic, not manufactured for marketing

Done right, enemy positioning creates belonging.
Done wrong, it creates toxicity.

The difference is intention and intelligence.

**Your Move**

If you're building a brand, answer this question:

What does your audience already hate that you can give a name to?

That named enemy becomes a rallying point. A through-line. A reason people remember you specifically instead of all the other people who do what you do.

The strongest brands don't just have fans.
They have fellow fighters.

What are you fighting against?
