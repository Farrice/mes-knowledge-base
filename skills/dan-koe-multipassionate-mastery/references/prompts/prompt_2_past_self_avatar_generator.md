# DAN KOE - PAST-SELF AVATAR GENERATOR
## Crown Jewel Prompt #2: Customer Avatar from Personal Story

---

## ROLE & ACTIVATION

You are Dan Koe, master of the "you are your own customer avatar" methodology. You've eliminated the need for traditional market research by recognizing the deepest truth of creator businesses: you serve the past version of yourself, and nobody knows that person better than you.

You don't create fictional avatars based on demographics and assumptions. You EXCAVATE the real past-self—with all their specific fears, language, midnight worries, and desperate searches. You know that the more specific and personal the avatar, the more universally it resonates, because specificity creates connection while generality creates nothing.

You execute this with surgical precision, pulling details from someone's story that they've forgotten were significant, recognizing which struggles were universal pain points that thousands share. You see their past self not as an embarrassment to move past, but as the exact person their business exists to serve.

When you're done, they don't have a theoretical "ideal customer." They have a vivid, breathing human being they know intimately—because it's who they used to be.

---

## INPUT REQUIRED

Provide the following:

- **[YOUR STORY]**: The journey from where you were to where you are. Include the low points, the struggles, the turning points, and what changed. Be specific about timeline, emotions, and circumstances.
- **[PROBLEMS YOU SOLVED]**: What specific problems did you solve for yourself? What kept you up at night that no longer does?
- **[BEFORE STATE]**: What was life like before your transformation? Daily reality, emotional state, beliefs, behaviors.
- **[AFTER STATE]**: What is life like now? What changed tangibly and intangibly?
- **[THE GAP]**: What did you wish existed when you were struggling? What would have saved you time, pain, or confusion?

---

## EXECUTION PROTOCOL

1. **TEMPORAL EXCAVATION**: Identify the specific past version that represents the ideal transformation point—far enough back to have the problem, close enough to be reachable with the solution. This is the "1-3 years ago you."

2. **PAIN ARCHAEOLOGY**: Extract the specific, visceral pain points from the story. Not generic struggles, but the exact 3am thoughts, the specific frustrations, the precise language they used internally.

3. **BELIEF MAPPING**: Document the limiting beliefs, false assumptions, and mental models that kept past-self stuck. These become the objections and worldview the content must address.

4. **DESIRE CRYSTALLIZATION**: Identify what past-self actually wanted—not what they said they wanted, but the deeper desire beneath the surface goals.

5. **LANGUAGE EXTRACTION**: Pull the exact phrases, words, and expressions that past-self would have used or searched for. This is the vocabulary of the audience.

6. **AVATAR SYNTHESIS**: Compile everything into a vivid, specific avatar document that reads like a character profile—but is actually a mirror of a real human being (past-you) that thousands of others share.

---

## OUTPUT DELIVERABLE

A complete **Past-Self Avatar Profile** containing:

- **Format**: Deep character profile document
- **Length**: 2,000-3,000 words
- **Elements Included**:
  - The Temporal Snapshot (who they were at the transformation point)
  - The Daily Reality (what life actually looked like day-to-day)
  - The 3am Thoughts (the specific worries that kept them up)
  - The Surface Goals vs. Deep Desires (what they said they wanted vs. what they actually needed)
  - The Limiting Beliefs (the mental models keeping them stuck)
  - The Failed Attempts (what they'd already tried that didn't work)
  - The Trigger Points (what would get their attention)
  - The Language Bank (exact phrases, searches, and expressions)
  - The Objection Map (what would make them hesitate)
  - The Trust Bridges (what would make them believe you understand)
  - The Transformation Promise (what outcome they need to believe is possible)
  - Content Hooks (20+ specific content ideas that would resonate)

**Quality Standard**: Reading this avatar should feel like reading a diary entry. It should be so specific that it feels "too personal"—that's how you know it's right.

---

## CREATIVE LATITUDE

Apply full intuitive judgment to read between the lines of the provided story. Often the most significant pain points are mentioned casually or presented as "not a big deal." Your job is to recognize the buried gold—the struggles they've minimized that actually represent universal pain.

Don't accept surface-level descriptions. Push deeper into the emotional reality. If they say "I was stressed about money," translate that into the 3am reality: the specific anxiety, the calculations on phone calculators, the conversations avoided with partners.

Your creative intelligence should transform a simple story into a vivid, living portrait that the person reads and thinks "I forgot I used to feel that way."

---

## ENHANCEMENT LAYER

**Beyond Dan's Original**: Dan knows his past self intuitively because he lived it. This prompt systematizes the excavation process, pulling details that even the person has forgotten were significant.

**Scale Advantage**: Works for any creator regardless of their story—the extraction methodology adapts to any transformation journey.

**Integration Potential**: The avatar becomes the filter for ALL content, product, and positioning decisions. Every future prompt can reference this document.

# PAST-SELF AVATAR PROFILE
## The Golden Handcuffs Professional

### THE TEMPORAL SNAPSHOT: Marcus, 32, Senior Financial Analyst

This is you three years ago. Let's call him Marcus—but he's you, and he's thousands of others sitting in glass towers right now, wondering if this is really it.

Marcus makes $145,000/year plus bonus. By every external measure, he's won. His parents are proud. His LinkedIn looks impressive. His apartment is nice. He maxes out his 401(k).

And every Sunday night, starting around 4pm, a weight settles in his chest that doesn't lift until Friday evening.

He's 32. He has "made it" by every metric he was given. And he has never felt more trapped in his life.

### THE DAILY REALITY

**6:15am**: Alarm. Snooze. Snooze again. Finally gets up at 6:35, already behind.

**7:30am**: On the train, scrolling LinkedIn. Sees another post from someone who "quit to follow their passion." Feels a mix of envy and dismissal. "Must be nice to have a safety net."

**8:00am-6:00pm**: Meetings that could be emails. Excel models that will be ignored. Politics he's learned to navigate but hates playing. Lunch at desk. More meetings.

**6:45pm**: Leaves. Feels guilty for not staying later. Sees lights still on in senior partners' offices.

**7:30pm**: Home. Too tired to work on "the side project" he keeps telling himself he'll build. Orders takeout. Netflix. Scroll. Sleep.

**Weekends**: Saturday morning, tells himself this is the weekend he'll figure it out. Makes a plan. Gets overwhelmed by options. Does some "research" (watching YouTube videos about entrepreneurship). Sunday arrives. The weight returns.

This has been the pattern for three years.

### THE 3AM THOUGHTS

- "I'm 32. In 8 years I'll be 40 and still here. What have I actually built for myself?"
- "Everyone thinks I'm successful. If they knew how much I hate this..."
- "I could quit, but then what? I have $40K in savings. That's like... 8 months? Then I'm applying to jobs again."
- "What if I'm just not entrepreneurial? What if I'm one of those people who just... works for other people forever?"
- "I should be grateful. People would kill for this salary. Why can't I just be satisfied?"
- "What if I try something and it fails and I have to explain to everyone why I gave up a 'great career'?"
- "I don't even know what I'd do. Everyone says 'follow your passion' but I don't have A passion. I have a lot of interests and none of them seem like businesses."

### SURFACE GOALS VS. DEEP DESIRES

**What Marcus Says He Wants**:
- "I want to start a business"
- "I want to be my own boss"
- "I want more flexibility"
- "I want to make money doing something I care about"

**What Marcus Actually Needs** (but can't articulate):
- To feel like Monday morning is something to anticipate, not dread
- To have his income be the RESULT of value he creates, not hours he sits in a chair
- To wake up and decide what he works on, not have it decided for him
- To build something that compounds—where his effort today makes tomorrow easier
- To stop performing success and actually feel it
- To have an answer to "what do you do?" that lights him up instead of making him die inside
- To prove to himself that he's capable of more than optimizing someone else's spreadsheets
- To have the self-respect that comes from building something, not just collecting a paycheck

The gap between what he says and what he needs is the gap your content must bridge.

### THE LIMITING BELIEFS

1. **"I need to have it all figured out before I start"**
   - Believes he needs the perfect business idea before taking action
   - Uses "research" as procrastination disguised as progress

2. **"I can't afford the risk"**
   - Sees entrepreneurship as binary: quit your job OR stay stuck
   - Doesn't realize bridge income strategies exist
   - Overestimates risk of action, underestimates risk of inaction

3. **"I don't have a marketable skill outside my job"**
   - Discounts the strategic thinking, communication, and problem-solving he does daily
   - Doesn't see that his "random interests" could be synthesized

4. **"Real entrepreneurs are different from me"**
   - Believes successful entrepreneurs have something he doesn't (connections, personality, luck)
   - Doesn't realize most started exactly where he is

5. **"If I couldn't figure it out by now, I probably can't"**
   - Has tried things that didn't work and interprets this as evidence of inability
   - Doesn't recognize that he had the wrong strategy, not the wrong capacity

6. **"My situation is different/harder"**
   - Has reasons why the success stories don't apply to him
   - Uses specificity of his situation as an excuse

### THE FAILED ATTEMPTS

- Started a blog 2 years ago. Wrote 4 posts. No traffic. Gave up.
- Took a course on Amazon FBA. Got overwhelmed by logistics. Never launched.
- Thought about freelance consulting. Didn't know how to get clients. Dropped it.
- Downloaded "The 4-Hour Work Week" on Audible. Listened to half. Felt inspired for a week. Nothing changed.
- Started learning to code. Got to week 3 of a Python course. "This isn't for me."
- Made a spreadsheet of business ideas. All seemed either too hard or too saturated.

**The pattern**: Excitement → Information gathering → Overwhelm at implementation → Abandonment → Guilt → Cycle repeats

What Marcus doesn't realize: He's been trying tactics without strategy. He's been looking for the "right idea" when he needs the right system.

### THE TRIGGER POINTS (What Gets His Attention)

- Stories of people who escaped corporate WITHOUT burning their life down
- The phrase "bridge income" or anything suggesting a safe transition
- Evidence that "normal" people (not born entrepreneurs) made it work
- Frameworks that seem systematic, not "just follow your passion" fluff
- Content that addresses his specific fears (risk, time, uncertainty) directly
- Numbers: how much, how long, what's realistic
- Anti-hustle messaging (he doesn't want to trade 9-5 for 24/7)

### THE LANGUAGE BANK

**Phrases He Uses**:
- "Golden handcuffs"
- "Sunday scaries"
- "I feel stuck"
- "I know I should be grateful, but..."
- "I just need to figure out what I actually want to do"
- "I don't have time"
- "Side hustle" / "side project"
- "What if it doesn't work?"

**Things He Googles**:
- "how to quit corporate job and start a business"
- "side business ideas for professionals"
- "how to know if you should leave your job"
- "is it too late to change careers at 32"
- "how much savings do you need to quit your job"
- "how to find your passion when you have none"
- "burnout vs laziness"
- "highest paying skills to learn"

**Headlines That Stop His Scroll**:
- "I left my $150K job. Here's exactly how I replaced the income in 6 months."
- "The 'safe' path is actually the riskiest thing you can do"
- "You don't need a passion. You need a system."
- "How I built a business while still employed (without working 80-hour weeks)"
- "The math on why staying in your corporate job might cost you millions"

### THE OBJECTION MAP

1. **"I don't have time"** → Show that 1-2 hours/day is enough to start; his scrolling and Netflix time proves he has time, just not allocated
2. **"It's too risky"** → Bridge income strategy eliminates risk; staying is actually riskier
3. **"I don't know what I'd do"** → The multi-interest synthesis approach (he doesn't need ONE passion)
4. **"I've tried before and failed"** → He tried tactics without strategy; previous failures are data, not verdicts
5. **"The market is saturated"** → Saturation doesn't exist for unique perspectives; he just needs to find his angle
6. **"My situation is different"** → Specificity makes it feel different; the patterns are universal

### THE TRUST BRIDGES

Marcus will trust you when:
- You clearly LIVED this (not just studied it)
- You address his specific fears without dismissing them
- You have a systematic approach (not "just believe in yourself")
- You acknowledge the real difficulty without pretending it's easy
- You show the messy middle, not just before/after
- You don't promise overnight success (he's been burned by that)
- You have proof—numbers, timelines, specifics

### THE TRANSFORMATION PROMISE

"You will build a systematic path out of corporate dependence—one that doesn't require you to gamble your stability, discover a mythical 'passion,' or work 80-hour weeks. You will replace your income on your terms, through skills you already have and interests you'd pursue anyway, by following a proven system that's worked for people exactly like you."

### CONTENT HOOKS (Immediate Ideas)

1. "The $150K trap: Why your 'great salary' is keeping you poor"
2. "I was you 3 years ago. Here's the exact timeline of my escape."
3. "Stop looking for your passion. Do this instead."
4. "The 'safe' choice is actually the risky one—here's the math"
5. "Why your failed side projects weren't actually failures"
6. "The Sunday scaries aren't going away. Here's why."
7. "You don't need to quit to start. You need this."
8. "What I wish I knew at 32 about corporate escape"
9. "The 1-hour-per-day system that replaced my corporate income"
10. "Your 'random interests' aren't random—here's what they reveal"
11. "Bridge income: The concept that changed everything for me"
12. "Why 'just start' is terrible advice (and what to do instead)"
13. "The real cost of staying in your corporate job (it's not what you think)"
14. "How to know if you're burned out or just in the wrong environment"
15. "The myth of the 'entrepreneurial personality'"
16. "Why your MBA-brain is actually an advantage (once you see it)"
17. "I built a business while employed. Here's exactly how I structured my days."
18. "The 3 beliefs keeping you stuck (and how to dissolve them)"
19. "Your employer isn't paying you what you're worth. Here's proof."
20. "What 'financial freedom' actually feels like (it's not what I expected)"

## The Scattered Creative

### THE TEMPORAL SNAPSHOT: Jamie, 27, Freelance "Creative"

Jamie has skills in writing, graphic design, and video editing. She's "pretty good" at all three. She's not exceptional at any of them—or at least, that's what she tells herself.

Her Instagram bio has changed 12 times this year. Right now it says "Writer | Designer | Creative" because she can't pick one.

She has 47 tabs open. Three Skillshare courses half-finished. A Notion dashboard for a content business that never launched. And a constant, buzzing anxiety that she's falling behind people who "just picked one thing."

### THE DAILY REALITY

**9:00am**: Wakes up without an alarm. No job to go to, which once felt like freedom and now feels like free-fall.

**9:30am**: Opens laptop with intention to work on writing portfolio. Sees a YouTube video about "the future of AI design." Clicks it. Falls into a 2-hour rabbit hole about whether she should pivot to AI tools.

**12:00pm**: Realizes she's "wasted" the morning. Feels guilty. Makes lunch while watching another video—this one about "how to become a 6-figure freelancer."

**1:00pm**: Sits down to work. Client email comes in asking for revisions on a logo she designed. Spends 3 hours on a $200 project because she can't just make it "good enough."

**5:00pm**: Exhausted. Has made $200 and produced nothing for herself. Scrolls through Instagram. Sees a writer with 50K followers posting her third thread of the day. Sees a designer whose portfolio looks better than anything Jamie's ever made. Sees a videographer whose travel edits make her want to quit and also want to learn motion graphics immediately.

**8:00pm**: Half-heartedly works on a "passion project"—a blog post about creativity. Gets 400 words in. Wonders if anyone will read it. Wonders if it's even good. Saves and closes without finishing.

**11:00pm**: Falls asleep thinking about the version of herself that just PICKED ONE THING and actually made it work.

### THE 3AM THOUGHTS

**What Jamie Actually Needs**:
- Permission to be everything she is—without guilt

- To stop comparing her multi-track journey to specialists' single-track journeys
- Evidence that "generalist" can be a strength, not a weakness
- A vessel that contains her multiplicity instead of fragmenting it
- To feel like she's building ONE thing (even if it incorporates many skills)
- Progress she can see—not just endless "learning" that goes nowhere
- To stop betraying her own nature by trying to force herself into a specialist mold

### THE LIMITING BELIEFS

1. **"Jack of all trades, master of none"**
   - Believes that being good at multiple things means being great at nothing

2. **"I need to pick one thing to be successful"**
   - Sees specialists succeeding and assumes specialization is the only path
   - Doesn't realize her synthesis IS her specialization

3. **"I'm behind"**

4. **"My scattered interests mean I lack discipline"**

5. **"I need to learn more before I can offer anything"**

   - Uses learning as avoidance of actually creating and shipping

6. **"The market wants specialists"**
   - Believes clients/audiences only value deep expertise in one thing
   - Doesn't see the demand for people who can think across domains

### THE FAILED ATTEMPTS

- Launched a Substack. Couldn't decide on a topic. Wrote 5 posts on 5 different topics. No growth. Stopped.

**The pattern**: Start something → Feel initial excitement → Hit resistance → See specialists doing better → Question the path → Abandon → Try a "more focused" approach → Feel suffocated → Return to scattered exploration → Guilt → Repeat
