# Self-Persuasion Question Designer

> "Who is more persuasive — you or them to themselves?" — Jeremy Miner

## When to Use

- Writing sales copy where you need the reader to reach a conclusion
- Designing discovery call questions
- Creating content that shifts perspective without being preachy
- Building landing pages that let the visitor sell themselves
- Designing email sequences that build toward a decision
- Any context where direct selling triggers resistance

## The Self-Persuasion Principle

**Don't tell them. Let them tell themselves.**

When YOU say "Your current solution isn't working," the brain resists — you're an outsider attacking their decision. When THEY say "Well, it's not really working because..." — they've just persuaded themselves. Same conclusion, 10x the power.

The design goal: **engineer questions where the only honest answer is your selling point.**

## Phase 1: Define the Target Conclusion

What do you need the prospect to believe by the end?

**Template:**
```
TARGET CONCLUSION: [The thing you want them to conclude]
Example: "My current approach to content creation isn't working 
and I need professional help."
```

**Rules:**
- Must be something THEY would say, not something YOU'd say
- Must be in their language, not marketing language
- Must feel like a natural realization, not a planted thought

## Phase 2: Reverse-Engineer the Question Chain

Work backwards from the target conclusion. Each question should make the next one inevitable.

### Self-Persuasion Question Types

**Type 1: Current State Evaluation Questions**
Force them to assess their own situation honestly.

```
"Are you 100% happy with [current approach]?"
"What's been the biggest challenge with [current method]?"
"If you could change ONE thing about how you're doing this, what would it be?"
```

**Type 2: Self-Diagnosis Questions**
Make them articulate WHY their current approach isn't working.

```
"What's caused you to feel like [current solution] might not 
eventually [get the result]?"
"What do you think is actually preventing you from [desired outcome]?"
"Why do you think [current approach] hasn't worked so far?"
```

**Type 3: Consequence Awareness Questions**
Make them describe the cost of NOT changing.

```
"If nothing changes in the next [timeframe]... where does that leave you?"
"What's this already cost you in terms of [time / money / opportunity]?"
"How is [problem] affecting your [life / business / relationships]?"
```

**Type 4: Desire Clarification Questions**
Make them articulate what they really want.

```
"In a perfect world, what would [topic] look like for you?"
"What would it mean for your [life/business] if you solved this?"
"If you could wake up tomorrow with [problem] gone, what would be different?"
```

**Type 5: Solution Direction Questions**
Make them describe what a solution should look like (which happens to match yours).

```
"Given everything you've described, what do you think needs to happen?"
"What kind of support do you think you'd need to get from [current state] to [desired state]?"
"What would the right solution look like for someone in your situation?"
```

## Phase 3: Chain Construction

Link questions into a sequence where each answer makes the next question natural.

**The Self-Persuasion Chain Template:**

```
Q1 [Evaluation]: "Are you 100% happy with [current approach]?"
→ They say no (nobody is 100% happy)

Q2 [What would change]: "What would you change if you could?"
→ They describe problems (now they're articulating gaps)

Q3 [Self-Diagnosis]: "What's caused you to feel like 
[current approach] might not work long-term?"
→ They list reasons (now they're selling themselves on change)

Q4 [Consequence]: "How has that been affecting you... personally?"
→ They add emotion to the reasons (urgency builds)

Q5 [Desire]: "What would it mean if you could solve all of that?"
→ They paint the picture (now they WANT it)

Q6 [Direction]: "What do you think needs to happen to get there?"
→ They describe your solution (they've just prescribed YOUR offer)
```

## Phase 4: Context Adaptation

### For Sales Calls
Use questions verbally. Add tonal annotations:
- Q1-Q2: 🔵 Curious tone
- Q3: 🟡 Confused tone (gets them to elaborate)
- Q4: 🟢 Concern tone + `[PAUSE]`
- Q5: 🔵 Curious + warm
- Q6: 🔵 Open, non-leading

### For Landing Pages
Convert to rhetorical questions with implied answers:
```
"How much longer can you afford to spend 10 hours a week 
on content that barely gets engagement?"

"What would you do with those 10 hours if someone else 
created content that actually converted?"

"What's really stopping you from getting the help you need?"
```

### For Email Sequences
One self-persuasion question per email:
- Email 1: Evaluation question (open the crack)
- Email 2: Self-diagnosis question (widen the crack)
- Email 3: Consequence question (deepen the urgency)
- Email 4: Desire question (paint the future)
- Email 5: Direction question (point to the solution)

### For Content (LinkedIn, YouTube, etc.)
Open with a self-persuasion question as the hook:
- "How long have you been telling yourself that [common excuse]?"
- "What would actually change if you stopped [common behavior]?"
- "Are you 100% satisfied with your [topic]? Or just... decent?"

## Output Format

For any given target conclusion, deliver:
1. **Target Conclusion** — what you want them to believe
2. **5-8 Question Chain** — the full sequence with annotations
3. **Adaptation Pack** — versions for sales calls, copy, email, and content
4. **Self-Persuasion Checkpoints** — moments where they should articulate YOUR conclusion in THEIR words
5. **Warning Flags** — where the chain might break and how to redirect

## Quality Gate

- [ ] Does the prospect/reader reach the conclusion WITHOUT you stating it?
- [ ] Could each question be answered honestly (no trick questions)?
- [ ] Does the chain feel like natural curiosity, not interrogation?
- [ ] Is the Target Conclusion something THEY would say, not marketing copy?
- [ ] Are tonal annotations appropriate for each question type?
