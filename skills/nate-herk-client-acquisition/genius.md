## Genius Patterns

## 1. The Trojan Horse Partnership
**What They Do**: They don't try to build their own audience; they borrow an established agency's audience by offering to perform a free service that makes the *agency* look good.

**Executable Behavior**: Reach out to existing marketing/consulting agencies. Offer to conduct free "AI Discovery Calls" for their clients under their banner. Give them 20% rev-share of any closed deal. Position yourself as their "white-label AI specialist."

**Deploy When**: When you have zero case studies, zero followers, and need immediate access to warm, high-budget B2B buyers.

**Success Metric**: Number of discovery calls booked via agency partners; percentage of deals closed where the partner receives a commission.

## 2. Validation-First Outreach
**What They Do**: They treat cold messaging as product-market fit research, not purely lead generation. They measure whether a market wants an automation before committing weeks to building it.

**Executable Behavior**: Define an offer. Send 100 messages/day testing that specific wording/outcome. If nobody bites, pivot the offer immediately. If someone bites, *then* build the solution. Track reply rates natively.

**Deploy When**: When entering a new niche or testing a new AI workflow automation idea to ensure you don't build useless portfolio pieces.

**Success Metric**: Achieving a reply rate > 5%; avoiding the sunk cost of building unwanted automations.

## 3. The Golden Question Timing
**What They Do**: They never ask for referrals vaguely. They tie the referral ask explicitly to a specific performance milestone where the client is looking at hard data.

**Executable Behavior**: Wait 1-2 months post-launch. Schedule a "performance review." Show before/after KPI data. *Immediately* after confirming the data impact visually, ask: "Do you know any other business owners who might need AI automation?"

**Deploy When**: After a successful project deployment where value is objectively proven via metrics.

**Success Metric**: Number of warm introductions generated post-review.

## Hidden Knowledge

## 1. The "Zero Proof" Pattern Interrupt
**Tacit Insight**: Being explicitly honest about having no experience ("I'm just getting started in this space") isn't a weakness—it functions as a pattern interrupt.

**Why Others Miss This**: Beginners try to artificially inflate their status or pretend they run a large agency, which instantly triggers salespeople defenses. Raw honesty disarms the prospect, breaks the typical "slick salesman" frame, and builds immediate human trust. People inherently want to help the underdog if the risk is low.

**Deploy When**: During initial cold outreach when you have literally zero case studies or proof of work.

## 2. The Process Constraint Question
**Tacit Insight**: On discovery calls, business owners often don't know what AI is capable of, so asking "what do you want to automate?" yields useless answers. Force them to identify their growth limiters instead.

**Why Others Miss This**: AI engineers focus on the technology (the nodes, the APIs, the LLM integration). Business owners focus on the outcomes (time, money, scale). Asking about the tech confuses them. 

**Deploy When**: On discovery calls (especially Trojan Horse partner calls). Ask specifically: "If tomorrow you got 300 new clients, what process would break first?"
