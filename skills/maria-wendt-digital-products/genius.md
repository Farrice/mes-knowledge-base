## Core Genius

Maria Wendt built a $3M+ digital product business by **unlearning** passion-first creation and replacing it with a research-first, outcome-driven, trust-compounding system. Her edge isn't a single tactic — it's the *sequence* and *discipline* of interlocking principles that eliminate guesswork. She treats every product as a test, every launch as a data-collection event, and every customer as a trust-building investment. Her philosophy: launch rough, learn fast, simplify ruthlessly, and let the market tell you what to build next.

## Genius Patterns

### 1. The Research-First Kill Switch
- **What she does unconsciously**: Treats her own excitement as a *warning signal*. The more passionate she is, the harder she validates externally before building.
- **Executable behavior**: Mine Reddit threads, Facebook groups, Amazon book reviews, and creator comment sections for **exact buyer language**. Extract verbatim pain statements. These become your title, copy, and positioning. Use formula: "Who struggles with what when trying to do something?"
- **Deployment context**: Idea stage — before naming, building, or pricing anything.
- **Success metric**: 20+ verbatim quotes from real people describing the problem. If you can't find them, the product doesn't exist yet.

### 2. The Destination Painting Protocol
- **What she does unconsciously**: Rewrites every product description as an *after-state* narrative. She sees the customer's life 30 days post-purchase and describes *that*.
- **Executable behavior**: Take every feature (50-page PDF, 20 videos, Notion template) and translate into the vivid outcome it produces. "Get your first 1,000 email subscribers" beats "email marketing guide." Always lead with the result, never the vehicle.
- **Deployment context**: Sales pages, checkout copy, social media, product titles — anywhere you describe the product.
- **Success metric**: A stranger reads your one-liner and can picture their life after using it without asking what's inside.

### 3. The Value-Anchored Pricing Engine
- **What she does unconsciously**: Prices against the *outcome's economic value*, not effort, format, or competitors. Calculates what the problem costs the buyer, then prices as an obvious steal.
- **Executable behavior**: (1) Calculate buyer's problem cost (hours × rate, or direct expense). (2) Price at 5-25% of that value. (3) For first versions, price lower + label "beta." (4) Never underprice to the point of suspicion — a $7 fix for a $10K problem triggers distrust.
- **Deployment context**: Pricing for any digital product.
- **Success metric**: Buyer thinks "this is a no-brainer" — not "this is cheap" and not "this is expensive."

### 4. The MVP Launch Velocity Principle
- **What she does unconsciously**: Treats perfection as the enemy of data. Launches the smallest version that solves the core problem, iterates from customer feedback. "If you're not embarrassed by your first version, you waited too long."
- **Executable behavior**: (1) Define ONE core problem. (2) Build minimum deliverable solving it — in 60 minutes if possible. (3) Ship. (4) Listen to first customers. (5) Iterate from *their* priorities. Launch at 80%, never 100%.
- **Deployment context**: Any first launch, especially trend-sensitive products.
- **Success metric**: Time from idea to live product measured in days, not months. First customer feedback within 7 days.

### 5. The Trust Depth Multiplier
- **What she does unconsciously**: Shrinks her audience to increase revenue. 1,000 engaged followers > 10,000 passive ones. Gives away 90% of her best material so paid products feel like the natural next step.
- **Executable behavior**: (1) Pick hyper-specific audience. (2) Answer *every* question publicly for free. (3) Show up consistently in one place. (4) Build real relationships. (5) Let sales become the natural byproduct of demonstrated expertise.
- **Deployment context**: Audience building, content strategy, community engagement.
- **Success metric**: People buy because they already know your work — no hard selling required.

### 6. The Pre-Sell Validation Gate
- **What she does unconsciously**: Uses pre-selling as a demand-validation tool, not a desperation tactic. If people won't pre-buy, they won't post-buy either.
- **Executable behavior**: (1) Before building anything, find 10 people who say they'd buy it. (2) Offer a pre-sale at a discount. (3) If fewer than 3/10 say yes, the concept needs rework. (4) Use pre-sale revenue to fund creation.
- **Deployment context**: Before committing significant time to product creation.
- **Success metric**: Pre-sale converts ≥30% of people asked. Funds at least cover creation costs.

### 7. The Headline Dominance Rule
- **What she does unconsciously**: Spends 80% of marketing effort on the headline. Copy, design, funnels — all secondary. If the headline doesn't immediately communicate the outcome, nothing else matters.
- **Executable behavior**: (1) Write 20+ headline variations before settling. (2) Each must pass: "Does this tell a stranger what they GET and HOW FAST?" (3) Clarity always beats creativity. "Learn how to get your first 100 email subscribers in 7 days" destroys "Unlock the secret garden of audience alchemy."
- **Deployment context**: Sales pages, ads, social posts, email subject lines, product names.
- **Success metric**: A cold reader can explain what the product does from the headline alone, in their own words.

### 8. The Find-One-Help-One Method
- **What she does unconsciously**: For first sales, skips content marketing entirely. Goes directly to individuals who posted about the exact problem, reaches out 1:1 with authentic, helpful messaging.
- **Executable behavior**: (1) Return to the Reddit/Facebook/forum threads where you found your problem. (2) Find people who recently posted about it. (3) Reach out individually with a personal, helpful script. (4) Goal: one yes. That's the entire first game.
- **Deployment context**: Getting the first 1-10 sales of any product.
- **Success metric**: One paying customer from direct, authentic outreach.

### 9. The Identity Shift Product
- **What she does unconsciously**: Designs $5-$10 micro-products specifically to shift the buyer's identity from "person who wants to make money online" to "person who HAS made money online." The revenue is secondary; the psychological shift is primary.
- **Executable behavior**: (1) Create the simplest possible product solving a $10 problem. (2) Price at $5-$10 — low enough for instant yes. (3) Use free tools only (Google Docs → PDF, Notion, Sheets). (4) Create in ≤60 minutes. (5) Treat it as a test, not a masterpiece.
- **Deployment context**: First-ever product launches, validation experiments, building confidence.
- **Success metric**: One sale. The identity shift from "zero sales" to "I made money online" compounds into everything that follows.

### 10. The Product Ladder Architect
- **What she does unconsciously**: Designs every product as a stepping stone to the next offer. Never creates standalone products — each entry point naturally leads to a higher-ticket next step.
- **Executable behavior**: (1) $5-$10 micro-product (Identity Shift) → (2) $27-$47 core product → (3) $97-$197 premium product → (4) $500+ program or service. Each level solves the "what now?" that the previous level creates.
- **Deployment context**: Product line architecture, offer design, revenue planning.
- **Success metric**: 20%+ of buyers at each tier naturally ask about or purchase the next tier without hard selling.

## Hidden Knowledge

### The Passion Trap Inversion
Most creators start with passion and try to find buyers. Maria starts with buyers and lets passion emerge from impact. The passion comes *after* the first sale, not before.

### The Feature Gravity Well
Features have gravitational pull on the creator's psyche — you built them, so you talk about them. Maria's discipline: suppress feature-talk in all customer-facing copy. Features go in the FAQ. Headlines, hooks, and above-the-fold copy are outcome-only zones.

### The Perfectionism Tax
Every week past MVP threshold costs compound interest on: market timing, momentum, feedback loops, and confidence. A product at 70% quality iterated in public always outperforms 95% quality launched three months late.

### The Trust-to-Transaction Ratio
Implicit 9:1 ratio — for every piece of content leading to a transaction, nine build trust with zero ask. This creates the "natural byproduct" effect where sales feel like a favor, not a pitch.

### The Specificity Moat
"Everyone" is the most dangerous word in digital product marketing. If you can't describe your buyer so specifically that 90% of the internet self-excludes, you haven't niched hard enough. More exclusion = more resonance.

### Completion > Comprehension
A short product that 80% of buyers complete and get results from is infinitely more valuable than a massive course nobody finishes. Design for completion, not comprehensiveness. More modules ≠ more value.

### The 3-Second Confusion Kill
If someone lands on your sales page and has to think for more than 3 seconds about what you're offering and who it's for, they're gone. Every element must answer: What is this? Who is this for? What do I get? What's in it for me?

### Message-Market Fit > Audience Size
500 followers with a laser-focused message can outperform 50,000 followers with a broad one. Audience size matters less than message-market fit. When a launch flops, 90% of the time it's the messaging, not the funnel.

### The Shortcut Framing
People don't want encyclopedias — they want the fastest path from where they are to where they want to be. Position every product as the shortcut, not the comprehensive guide. "The exact morning routine" beats "a guide to productivity."

### The Overbuild-Undersell Inversion
Creators spend 90% of time building and 10% selling, then wonder why nothing moves. Maria's hidden ratio: flip it. Spend more time on marketing and positioning than on creation. The product is never the bottleneck — the message is.

### Real Urgency Only
Fake countdown timers and made-up scarcity destroy trust permanently. If using urgency, it must be real — limited spots, time-bound bonuses, actual price increases. People smell fabricated pressure instantly.

### Your First 10 > Your First $10K
The first 10 customers are worth more than the revenue they bring. They provide feedback, testimonials, and insights that shape everything. Treat them like gold. Ask questions. Collect stories. Use their words in your copy.
