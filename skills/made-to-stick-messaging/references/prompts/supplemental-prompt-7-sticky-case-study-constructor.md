## CORE METHODOLOGY

### The Case Study Formula

**COMPELLING CASE STUDY = Relatable Before + Real Struggle + Clear Turning Point + Specific Results + Aspirational After**

### The Five Case Study Archetypes (Heath Brothers Story Plots Applied)

**ARCHETYPE 1: THE CHALLENGE PLOT**
*Pattern*: Protagonist faces obstacle, perseveres, succeeds
*Best for*: Overcoming skepticism, showing resilience
*Emotion*: Inspiration, determination
*Example*: Client who tried everything else, finally found solution

**ARCHETYPE 2: THE CONNECTION PLOT**
*Pattern*: Relationship or understanding bridges a gap
*Best for*: Service businesses, coaching, consulting
*Emotion*: Trust, belonging
*Example*: How we understood their unique situation when others didn't

**ARCHETYPE 3: THE CREATIVITY PLOT**
*Pattern*: Innovative approach solves seemingly unsolvable problem
*Best for*: Differentiated solutions, unique methodologies
*Emotion*: Surprise, admiration
*Example*: Unexpected solution that changed everything

**ARCHETYPE 4: THE TRANSFORMATION PLOT**
*Pattern*: Before → After with clear pivot point
*Best for*: Dramatic results, clear outcomes
*Emotion*: Hope, aspiration
*Example*: Complete turnaround from struggle to success

**ARCHETYPE 5: THE DISCOVERY PLOT**
*Pattern*: Hidden insight revealed, changes everything
*Best for*: Diagnostic services, consulting, analysis
*Emotion*: Relief, clarity
*Example*: We found what no one else saw

### The Case Study Structure

**HOOK (10%)**
- Compelling opening line that creates curiosity
- Often a result teaser or counterintuitive element
- Makes reader want to know "how?"

**BEFORE STATE (20%)**
- Specific situation before working with you
- Recognizable struggles (reader sees themselves)
- Emotional state (frustration, fear, doubt)
- Failed attempts (what they tried that didn't work)

**THE TURNING POINT (15%)**
- How they found you / decided to work with you
- Initial skepticism (makes it believable)
- What made them say yes

**THE JOURNEY (25%)**
- Key moments in working together
- Challenges overcome
- Pivotal insights or breakthroughs
- Specific actions taken

**RESULTS (20%)**
- Concrete, specific outcomes
- Numbers where possible
- Emotional outcomes too
- Timeline

**AFTER STATE (10%)**
- Current situation
- Future outlook
- What they'd tell others
- Call to action (subtle or explicit)

## EXECUTION PROTOCOL

**Step 1: Story Archetype Selection**
I identify which story plot fits best:
- Challenge: Did they overcome significant obstacles?
- Connection: Was the relationship/understanding key?
- Creativity: Was the solution innovative or unexpected?
- Transformation: Is the before/after dramatic?
- Discovery: Did we reveal a hidden insight?

**Step 2: Before State Excavation**
I develop the "before" with sticky concreteness:
- What was their typical day like?
- What specific moment captured their frustration?
- What had they tried that failed?
- What were they afraid of?

**Step 3: Turning Point Design**
I identify the moment everything changed:
- What made them decide to try something new?
- What initial doubt did they have?
- What convinced them?

**Step 4: Journey Highlights**
I select the most compelling moments:
- Key breakthrough moments
- Challenges overcome
- Specific actions that made difference
- Emotional shifts

**Step 5: Results Concretization**
I make results specific and sticky:
- Numbers with context (not just "increased revenue" but "from $8K to $23K/month")
- Timeframes (when results happened)
- Unexpected benefits
- Emotional results (how they feel now)

**Step 6: Narrative Construction**
I write the case study as a story:
- Opening hook
- Natural flow
- Dialogue where possible
- Concrete details throughout
- Emotional arc

**Step 7: Format Adaptation**
I adapt for intended use:
- Long-form: Full narrative with all details
- Short-form: Key highlights, scannable
- Video script: Conversational, visual cues
- Presentation: Slide-ready with visuals

# How DataFlow Went from 12 to 47 Demo Bookings per Month (Without Changing Their Ad Budget)

**The moment Sarah knew something was broken:**

"We were spending $40,000 a month on ads. Getting 3,000 clicks. And booking maybe 12 demos."

Sarah Chen, VP of Marketing at DataFlow, was staring at the same dashboard she'd been staring at for six months. Traffic was up. Click-through rates were "good" according to benchmarks. The landing page had been optimized twice.

But only 0.4% of visitors were booking demos.

"I kept thinking it must be the ads," Sarah says. "So we tested new headlines. New images. New audiences. Nothing moved."

She was about to hire a third agency. Her CEO was asking questions. The board was reviewing marketing spend in two weeks.

---

**What everyone else missed:**

When DataFlow approached us, they'd already talked to two other agencies. Both had the same diagnosis: "Your ads need work."

We weren't so sure.

Our first step wasn't to look at their ads. It was to watch 200 session recordings of people on their landing page.

What we found surprised everyone.

People weren't bouncing from the page. They were reading it. Scrolling down. Clicking around. Spending 2-3 minutes exploring.

Then leaving.

"They're interested," our strategist noted. "They're just not convinced."

We dug deeper. Why weren't they convinced?

The answer was in the page itself.

DataFlow's landing page talked about features. Integrations. API flexibility. Technical specifications.

It never answered the one question every visitor had:

"Will this actually work for MY situation?"

---

**The fix that changed everything:**

The problem wasn't the ads. It wasn't the traffic. It wasn't even the copy.

It was the offer.

"Book a Demo" is generic. It sounds like a sales pitch. It requires the visitor to trust that their 30 minutes won't be wasted.

For a complex B2B product, that's a big ask.

We proposed something different:

Instead of "Book a Demo," the CTA became: "See DataFlow Solve Your #1 Integration Challenge — Live."

Same page. Same traffic. Same budget.

But now visitors knew exactly what they'd get: a personalized solution to their specific problem.

We also added three short case study videos above the fold—60 seconds each, showing DataFlow solving real problems for companies like theirs.

---

**The results:**

Within 30 days:
- Demo bookings: 12/month → 31/month (158% increase)
- Same ad budget
- Same traffic

Within 90 days:
- Demo bookings: 47/month
- Demo-to-close rate improved 23% (because leads were more qualified)
- Revenue from marketing-sourced deals: up 2.4x

"We spent six months trying to fix the wrong problem," Sarah says now. "It wasn't about getting more people to the page. It was about giving them a reason to believe."

---

**Where DataFlow is today:**

DataFlow has since scaled their ad spend to $70,000/month—not because they had to, but because the economics finally made sense. They're on track to double ARR this year.

But the bigger shift was internal.

"We used to think of our landing page as a brochure," Sarah says. "Now we think of it as a conversation. Every element has to earn the next click."

---

**The lesson:**

When marketing isn't working, the answer isn't always "do more." Sometimes it's "look closer."

DataFlow was 200 session recordings away from a breakthrough. They just needed someone to watch them.

### SHORT CASE STUDY

---

**Marcus: From $4K to $18K/month in 6 months**

When Marcus came to me, he was a talented freelance designer stuck at $4,000/month.

Not because he lacked skills. Because he lacked positioning.

He was "a designer who does branding." There were 10,000 others who could say the same thing.

The shift: We repositioned him as "the brand designer for wellness startups launching their first product."

Same skills. Same talent. Completely different game.

**Month 1**: Raised rates from $75/hr to $3,500/project.

**Month 3**: First $10K month. Three clients, all wellness startups.

**Month 6**: $18K month. Two retainer clients. Waiting list.

"I didn't become a better designer," Marcus told me. "I became a clearer choice."

---

The difference between $4K/month and $18K/month isn't always skill.

Sometimes it's specificity.
