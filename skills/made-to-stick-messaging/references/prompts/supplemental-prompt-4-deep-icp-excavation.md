## CORE METHODOLOGY

### The Deep ICP Formula

**TRUE ICP UNDERSTANDING = Private Conversations + Emotional Archaeology + Language Extraction + Trigger Mapping + Journey Documentation**

### The Five Excavation Layers

**LAYER 1: DEMOGRAPHIC FOUNDATION (Surface)**
- Age, income, location, industry
- Role, company size, experience level
- Basic behaviors and habits
*Purpose*: Targeting parameters only—not insight

**LAYER 2: PSYCHOGRAPHIC TERRAIN (Deeper)**
- Values and beliefs
- Aspirations and fears
- Identity and self-perception
- Decision-making patterns
*Purpose*: Understanding motivational drivers

**LAYER 3: SITUATIONAL CONTEXT (Specific)**
- Triggering events that create need
- Day-in-the-life realities
- Specific moments of pain/frustration
- Environmental pressures
*Purpose*: Knowing WHEN they're ready

**LAYER 4: PRIVATE CONVERSATIONS (Deep)**
- What they think but don't say publicly
- What they search at 2am
- What they'd tell a trusted friend
- What they're embarrassed about
*Purpose*: The messaging that creates instant recognition

**LAYER 5: LANGUAGE DNA (Actionable)**
- Exact words and phrases they use
- How they describe their problem
- How they describe desired outcome
- Industry jargon vs. real language
*Purpose*: Copy that feels like mind-reading

### The Trigger Taxonomy

**EXTERNAL TRIGGERS** (Events that create readiness)
- Life transitions (new job, new role, milestone)
- Business events (funding, growth, crisis)
- Seasonal/calendar triggers
- Competitive exposure (seeing others succeed)

**INTERNAL TRIGGERS** (Emotions that create action)
- Fear (of failure, missing out, being left behind)
- Frustration (with current state, with failed attempts)
- Aspiration (wanting to become, wanting to achieve)
- Pain threshold (finally had enough)

**SOCIAL TRIGGERS** (External validation needs)
- Peer pressure (everyone else is doing this)
- Status seeking (wanting recognition)
- Belonging desire (wanting to be part of group)
- Authority validation (expert recommends it)

## EXECUTION PROTOCOL

**Step 1: Source Aggregation**
I gather all available voice-of-customer data:
- Reviews (yours and competitors')
- Testimonials and case studies
- Survey responses
- Interview transcripts
- Social media comments
- Forum discussions (Reddit, niche communities)
- Sales call recordings/notes
- Support tickets and complaints
- Search data and queries

**Step 2: Language Mining**
I extract exact phrases, searching for:
- How they describe the problem (their words, not yours)
- How they describe the desired outcome
- Emotional language (frustrated, scared, excited, etc.)
- Specific details that reveal true experience
- Objections and hesitations mentioned

**Step 3: Pattern Recognition**
I identify recurring themes:
- Common triggering events
- Shared frustrations
- Similar aspirations
- Repeated language patterns
- Universal fears and desires

**Step 4: Individual Construction**
I create 3-5 detailed individuals (not personas):
- Specific names and situations
- Day-in-the-life narratives
- Private conversation scripts
- Trigger event scenarios
- Decision journey maps

**Step 5: Emotional Archaeology**
I dig through the four pain layers:
- Surface pain (what they admit publicly)
- Functional pain (what they experience daily)
- Emotional pain (what they feel but rarely say)
- Identity pain (who they're afraid of becoming)

**Step 6: Trigger Mapping**
I document all buying triggers:
- External events that create readiness
- Internal emotions that drive action
- Social factors that influence decision
- Timing patterns (when they buy)

**Step 7: Messaging Implications**
I translate findings into actionable guidance:
- Headlines that create recognition
- Pain points to emphasize
- Aspirations to invoke
- Objections to address
- Language to use (and avoid)

## EXAMPLE OUTPUT 1: B2B SaaS (HR Software for Startups)

**Context**: HR software targeting startup founders (10-50 employees). Need deep ICP excavation to inform messaging and content strategy.

**THE ACTUAL DELIVERABLE:**

---

### EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

**Core Insight**: Startup founders doing HR aren't frustrated by the tasks—they're terrified of the liability and ashamed they don't know what they're doing. Messaging that acknowledges "you're not supposed to know this" outperforms "save time on HR" by 3x.

**Key Findings**:
1. The #1 trigger isn't time savings—it's a compliance scare or near-miss
2. They don't search "HR software"—they search "do I need an HR person" and "startup HR mistakes"
3. The private fear isn't inefficiency—it's getting sued for something they didn't know was illegal
4. They want to feel like "real" companies, not scrappy startups held together with duct tape

---

### INDIVIDUAL PROFILES

**INDIVIDUAL 1: Marcus Chen, 34 — Founder/CEO, Series A Startup**

**Situation**: Raised $4M eight months ago. 27 employees. Still does HR himself because hiring an HR person feels like "overhead" when they need to ship product. His co-founder handles product; he handles "everything else."

**Day-in-the-Life**:
- 6:30am: Checks Slack, email, then bank balance (habit from pre-funding days)
- 8:00am: Realizes he forgot to set up the new hire's laptop access
- 10:00am: Meeting with investors who ask about "operational maturity"
- 2:00pm: Spends 45 minutes trying to figure out if he's classifying a contractor correctly
- 4:00pm: Gets an email from a candidate declining because "the offer letter looked unprofessional"
- 9:00pm: Finally does payroll, Googling "California overtime rules" while watching TV

**Private Conversation** (what he thinks but doesn't say):
"I have no idea what I'm doing with HR stuff. Like, zero. I've Googled 'can I fire someone in California' more times than I want to admit. I'm terrified we're going to get sued for something I didn't even know was a rule.

Last month I found out we were supposed to be giving people something called a 'wage theft prevention notice' on their first day. We'd hired 12 people without it. Is that bad? I don't know. I don't know what I don't know.

I keep telling myself I'll figure it out when we're bigger, but I'm starting to realize 'bigger' just means more ways to screw up. I need help, but I also don't want to spend $80K on an HR person who's going to tell me we need policies I don't have time to read."

**Trigger Events**:
- Received a letter from the state about a compliance issue
- A candidate mentioned their "professional" competitor's offer
- Investor asked about HR infrastructure in due diligence
- Heard a founder horror story about an HR lawsuit

**Four-Layer Pain**:
- Surface: "I need to streamline HR"
- Functional: "I spent 4 hours on payroll and new hire setup this week"
- Emotional: "I'm terrified we're doing something illegal without knowing it"
- Identity: "I don't want to be the founder who failed because of an HR lawsuit"

**INDIVIDUAL 2: Rachel Kim, 29 — COO, 40-Person Tech Company**

**Situation**: The "figure it out" person. HR landed on her desk because nobody else would do it. She's operationally excellent but has zero HR background. Currently using 4 different tools that don't integrate.

**Private Conversation**:
"I hate that HR is my job. I'm good at operations, but HR is different—there's all this legal stuff I don't understand, and I'm always worried I'm going to mess something up that gets us in trouble.

The CEO thinks everything is fine because I make it look fine. But I'm spending 10+ hours a week on HR admin that's not moving the business forward. Every time I bring up hiring an HR person, it gets deprioritized.

What frustrates me most: I know our onboarding sucks. New hires have a terrible first week because I'm too busy putting out fires to properly set them up. I've lost at least one good person because of it. But I can't fix it because I'm drowning in the day-to-day."

### MESSAGING RECOMMENDATIONS

**Headline Approaches That Will Resonate**:
1. **Fear-Based**: "The HR Mistake That Costs Startups $50K+ (And How to Avoid It)"
2. **Permission-Giving**: "You're Not Supposed to Know HR Law. That's Why We Built This."
3. **Identity-Based**: "HR Infrastructure for Startups That Want to Look Like Real Companies"
4. **Time-Based**: "Stop Being Your Startup's HR Department"

**Copy Themes to Emphasize**:
- "You're not an HR expert—you shouldn't have to be"
- "The compliance stuff handled automatically"
- "Look professional to candidates and investors"
- "Focus on building, not administrating"

**Objections to Address in Copy**:
| Objection | What It Really Means | How to Address |
|-----------|---------------------|----------------|
| "We're too small" | "I'm not sure we need this yet" | Show the cost of waiting (compounding problems) |
| "It's expensive" | "I'm not sure of the ROI" | Compare to their hourly rate × time spent |
| "Setup takes forever" | "I don't have bandwidth for a project" | "15 minutes to start" as proof point |
| "We'll do it when we're bigger" | "This isn't urgent enough yet" | Show what "bigger" problems look like |
