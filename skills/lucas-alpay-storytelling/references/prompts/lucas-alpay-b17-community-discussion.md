## EXECUTION PROTOCOL

### Phase 1: Discussion Trigger Identification
Determine what makes people NEED to respond:
- Controversial but defensible positions
- Challenges to conventional wisdom
- Frameworks that demand self-categorization
- Questions without obvious answers
- Personal identification opportunities

### Phase 2: Stake Establishment
Make discussion feel important:
- Why does this position matter?
- What's at risk if people don't engage?
- How does this connect to audience identity?
- What makes silence feel like missing out?

### Phase 3: Entry Point Design
Create multiple ways to engage:
- Low-friction responses (agree/disagree, which category)
- Medium-friction responses (share experience, add nuance)
- High-friction responses (write counterargument, create content)
- All levels should feel valuable

### Phase 4: Ambiguity Engineering
Build in productive uncertainty:
- What can be interpreted multiple ways?
- What requires personal context to answer?
- What depends on values, not facts?
- What creates "it depends" responses?

### Phase 5: Share Architecture
Make sharing feel like self-expression:
- How does sharing this content say something about the sharer?
- What social capital does sharing create?
- How can people share with their own take attached?
- What makes NOT sharing feel like missing the conversation?

**Experience tells you what someone HAS done.**
**It tells you almost nothing about what they CAN do.**

- How they RESPOND when their first approach fails

Instead of:
- How many years they've repeated the same year
- How many logos they can drop
- How credentialed they look on paper

**The question I've started asking that reveals more than any resume:**

"Tell me about a time you were completely wrong about something important in your field. What changed your mind?"

If they can't answer it quickly and specifically, they're not learning. And someone who isn't learning is just accumulating years, not capability.

Agree? Disagree? I'd especially love to hear from people who've been on both sides of thisâ€”hired someone unexpected that worked out, or hired someone "perfect on paper" that didn't.
