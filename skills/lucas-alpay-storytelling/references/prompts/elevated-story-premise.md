## EXAMPLE OUTPUT

**Input**: Theme of inherited trauma, horror elements, rural setting

### ELEVATED PREMISE

**THEMATIC CORE**
How trauma passes through generations. Parents try to protect children from what hurt them, but in the protection, pass it on anyway. You can't outrun what's in your blood.

**GENRE CONTAINER**
Folk horror / Gothic family drama

**SPECIFIC LENS**
A house where each generation covers the walls with layers of new wallpaper. Each layer contains the nightmares of that generation—literally. The house makes you mad unless you keep adding layers. Each generation knows but pretends not to, thereby passing the same curse they're trying to hide.

**LOGLINE**

*Surface*: A woman inherits her grandmother's rural estate and discovers each layer of wallpaper contains the horrors her family has buried for generations.

*Deep*: A woman tries to break the cycle of family trauma only to discover that the act of hiding pain is itself the thing that transmits it.

**PREMISE DOCUMENT**

Title: LAYERS

Eleanor inherits the family homestead after her mother's death. Strange rules come with the house: Never strip the wallpaper. Never be alone when the wind blows from the east. Never mention the cellar.

Defiant and modern, Eleanor begins renovating. Behind the current wallpaper (1980s florals, her grandmother's era), she finds the layer beneath: 1940s patterns that seem to hold... images. Moving images. Her great-grandmother screaming without sound.

Each layer she peels goes further back. Each layer is worse. And someone—something—is noticing that she's looking.

The horror: What's in the walls is coming out.

The theme: To save herself and her daughter, Eleanor must choose: add another layer (perpetuate the secret) or expose everything (face what destroyed her mothers before her).

**WHY THIS ELEVATES**
- Horror = genre entertainment (scary, visceral)
- Wallpaper = metaphor (literal layers of hidden family pain)  
- Generational curse = real psychology (trauma transmission)
- Choice at end = moral complexity (no easy answers)
- Viewers leave thinking about their own families
