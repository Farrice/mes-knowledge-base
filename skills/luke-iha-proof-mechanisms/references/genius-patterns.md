## 1. The Jargon Flurry (Strategic Complexity)
**What They Do**: They write at a "caveman" level of simplicity for 95% of the copy, but intentionally drop a highly dense, jargon-rich sentence when explaining the mechanism of action.

**Executable Behavior**: Write 95% of the copy at a 5th-grade reading level. For the core mechanism, use 1-2 sentences of advanced, high-falutin terminology (e.g., "bioavailable curcuminoids with liposomal delivery"). Do not explain the jargon.

**Deploy When**: Introducing the "unique mechanism" of the product or service.

**Success Metric**: The prospect feels the creator is undeniably smart and authoritative, even if they don't understand the exact science described.

## 2. The Damaging Admission (Candid Communication)
**What They Do**: They highlight a genuine flaw, limitation, or negative aspect of their offer explicitly, before the prospect discovers it.

**Executable Behavior**: State one clear downside of the product (e.g., "Our customer support is not 24/7"). Immediately follow with the overwhelming positive result that makes the downside irrelevant ("...but our product generates 3x the revenue of competitors").

**Deploy When**: Early in the sales argument or pricing section to disarm skepticism.

**Success Metric**: The prospect's defense mechanisms drop, making all subsequent positive claims significantly more believable.

## 3. Proof Stacking at Doubt Nodes
**What They Do**: They don't cluster all proof at the end. They anticipate exactly when a reader will feel skeptical and insert a specific type of proof at that exact moment.

**Executable Behavior**: Map the sales argument. Identify the biggest, most unbelievable claims. Immediately attach a relevant empirical or credible proof point. Identify the call-to-action (where risk is highest) and attach experiential or social proof (guarantee or testimonial).

**Deploy When**: Structuring the layout of a VSL, sales page, or email.

**Success Metric**: Increased conversion rates and reduced bounce rates at traditional drop-off points.

## 4. Contextualized Authority
**What They Do**: They realize that prestige only works if the audience understands the prestige. They never assume the audience knows the source's credibility.

**Executable Behavior**: When citing a study or publication not globally recognized (like Harvard), add an explicit framing clause (e.g., "The Journal of Marketing, the most prestigious marketing journal").

**Deploy When**: Using empirical or credible proof from niche authorities.

**Success Metric**: The empirical proof lands with maximum psychological weight, eliminating exactly the "so what?" response.

## 5. The 22-Type Proof Arsenal
**What They Do**: They don't rely on one or two types of proof. They systematically select from 22 distinct proof types across 5 categories — Psychological (explanatory reasoning, objection pre-emption, story-based proof, pre-framing), Experiential (free trials, samples, demonstrations, case studies, certifications), Empirical (statistics, data, before/after, academic studies), Credible (expert endorsements, celebrity endorsements, credentials), and Social (testimonials, reviews, awards, user count, social media proof).

**Executable Behavior**: Before fortifying any asset, audit which proof CATEGORIES are represented. If any category is missing, the asset has a vulnerability. Select from the 22 types strategically — don't stack the same type. Variety across categories creates a "360-degree trust bubble."

**Deploy When**: Conducting proof audits, building sales assets, or planning content distribution strategies.

**Success Metric**: At least 3 of the 5 proof categories represented in every major sales asset.

## 6. Promise-to-Proof Match
**What They Do**: They match the SIZE of the promise to the SIZE of the proof. A big claim requires big proof. A small claim can get away with small proof. Mismatches — big claims with thin proof — are credibility killers.

**Executable Behavior**: Rank every claim in the copy on a 1-10 "boldness scale." For claims scoring 7+, attach heavyweight proof (academic study, large-scale case study, multiple testimonials). For claims scoring 4-6, attach medium proof (single testimonial, logical reasoning). Claims scoring 1-3 might not need explicit proof at all.

**Deploy When**: Editing any sales asset. The proof-promise ratio determines conversion.

**Success Metric**: No claim scoring 7+ should exist without attached proof that matches or exceeds its boldness.

## 7. Hard-to-Fake Proof
**What They Do**: They prioritize proof types that are difficult or impossible to fabricate. Demo videos, live screenshots, publicly verifiable certifications, named case studies with searchable details — these carry more weight than anonymous testimonials or vague claims.

**Executable Behavior**: For every proof element, ask: "Could a competitor fake this?" If yes, upgrade. Replace "Our customers love us" with "[Name], [Company], [LinkedIn profile]: 'Exact quote with specific numbers.'" The harder proof is to fake, the more trust it generates.

**Deploy When**: Any high-stakes conversion point — pricing reveals, CTA sections, guarantee pages.

**Success Metric**: 60%+ of proof elements should be verifiable or hard to fabricate.

## 8. The Experience-as-Proof Technique
**What They Do**: Instead of TELLING prospects the product works, they let them EXPERIENCE it. Free trials, sample chapters, live demonstrations, interactive tools — these eliminate all arguments because the prospect's own experience becomes the proof.

**Executable Behavior**: Identify one way the prospect can experience the value BEFORE buying. Design this experience as a proof mechanism. A free strategy session IS proof of expertise. A sample module IS proof of course quality. A free tool IS proof of capability.

**Deploy When**: High-price-point offers, sceptical audiences, new market entries where trust hasn't been established.

**Success Metric**: Conversion rate from "experience" to purchase exceeds 20%.
