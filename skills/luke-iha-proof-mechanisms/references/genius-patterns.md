## 1. The Jargon Flurry (Strategic Complexity)
**What They Do**: They write at a "caveman" level of simplicity for 95% of the copy, but intentionally drop a highly dense, jargon-rich sentence when explaining the mechanism of action.

**Executable Behavior**: Write 95% of the copy at a 5th-grade reading level. For the core mechanism, use 1-2 sentences of advanced, high-falutin terminology (e.g., "bioavailable curcuminoids with liposomal delivery"). Do not explain the jargon.

**Deploy When**: Introducing the "unique mechanism" of the product or service.

**Success Metric**: The prospect feels the creator is undeniably smart and authoritative, even if they don't understand the exact science described.

## 2. The Damaging Admission (Candid Communication)
**What They Do**: They highlight a genuine flaw, limitation, or negative aspect of their offer explicitly, before the prospect discovers it.

**Executable Behavior**: State one clear downside of the product (e.g., "Our customer support is not 24/7"). Immediately follow with the overwhelming positive result that makes the downside irrelevant ("...but our product generates 3x the revenue of competitors").

**Deploy When**: Early in the sales argument or pricing section to disarm skepticism.

**Success Metric**: The prospect's defense mechanisms drop, making all subsequent positive claims significantly more believable.

## 3. Proof Stacking at Doubt Nodes
**What They Do**: They don't cluster all proof at the end. They anticipate exactly when a reader will feel skeptical and insert a specific type of proof at that exact moment.

**Executable Behavior**: Map the sales argument. Identify the biggest, most unbelievable claims. Immediately attach a relevant empirical or credible proof point. Identify the call-to-action (where risk is highest) and attach experiential or social proof (guarantee or testimonial).

**Deploy When**: Structuring the layout of a VSL, sales page, or email.

**Success Metric**: Increased conversion rates and reduced bounce rates at traditional drop-off points.

## 4. Contextualized Authority
**What They Do**: They realize that prestige only works if the audience understands the prestige. They never assume the audience knows the source's credibility.

**Executable Behavior**: When citing a study or publication not globally recognized (like Harvard), add an explicit framing clause (e.g., "The Journal of Marketing, the most prestigious marketing journal").

**Deploy When**: Using empirical or credible proof from niche authorities.

**Success Metric**: The empirical proof lands with maximum psychological weight, eliminating exactly the "so what?" response.
