# Cross-Domain Genius Patterns

14 executable patterns from Eric Roth's Oscar-level craft — adapted for social media, content writing, copywriting, ghostwriting, and marketing.

> These patterns mirror the originals in `eric-roth-screenwriting-mastery/references/genius-patterns.md` but include cross-domain application guides and examples.

---

## Pattern 1: The Erosion Protocol

**Original**: Start every writing session from page one. Reread and refine everything before advancing.

**Cross-Domain Application**:
- **Blog/Newsletter**: Before writing new sections, reread from the opening. Shore up weak transitions. Let rhythm tighten naturally.
- **Ghostwriting**: Each editing pass begins at sentence one. By delivery, the voice has been refined 10+ times through the opening paragraphs.
- **Email Sequence**: Before writing email #3, reread emails #1 and #2. The arc tightens with each pass.

**Success Metric**: Does the opening paragraph of your content feel 5x more polished than the closing? If so, erosion is working.

## Pattern 2: Theme-First Compass

**Original**: Articulate the theme in one sentence before writing. Use it to navigate every scene.

**Cross-Domain Application**:
- **Content Series**: State the theme of the entire series before writing post #1. Every post answers: "How does this connect to [theme]?"
- **Marketing Campaign**: The campaign theme isn't the tagline — it's the gravitational field. Individual assets orbit it without repeating it.
- **Brand Story**: The theme of a brand's origin story governs which details to include and which to omit.

**Success Metric**: Can you read any piece in isolation and detect the theme without being told? If so, the compass is calibrated.

---

## Pattern 3: Beginning-End Architecture

**Original**: Know your first scene and last scene before writing the middle.

**Cross-Domain Application**:
- **LinkedIn Post**: Know your first line (hook) and last line (resonance beat) before writing the body.
- **Landing Page**: Lock the hero section and the final CTA before writing the middle sections.
- **Email Sequence**: Write the first email and the last email before filling the middle. The arc crystallizes.

**Success Metric**: Does reading only the first and last element give you a complete emotional journey?

## Pattern 4: Character Psychology Wallets

**Original**: Build a "wallet" for each character — neuroses, giddiness, anger triggers, specific possessions.

**Cross-Domain Application**:
- **ICP/Avatars**: Build a wallet for your ideal reader. Not demographics — *the crumpled receipt in their pocket, the podcast they listen to while doing dishes, the phrase that irritates them.*
- **Ghostwriting Voice Capture**: Build a wallet for the client's voice. What metaphors do they naturally reach for? What makes them laugh? What recurring stories do they tell?
- **Sales Copy**: The prospect isn't "a 35-year-old male marketer." They're someone with a specific crumpled phone number, a specific morning ritual, a specific thing they're afraid to admit.

**Success Metric**: Does your avatar feel like someone you could have dinner with, or a spreadsheet row?

---

## Pattern 5: Off-Center Subtext

**Original**: Convey emotion through displacement — dreams, unrelated stories, oblique references.

**Cross-Domain Application**:
- **Social Media**: Instead of "Here's what I learned about pricing," write about watching your kid negotiate for extra dessert. The pricing insight emerges through displacement.
- **Copywriting**: Instead of "Our software saves you 10 hours a week," tell the story of what 10 hours looks like — the recital you made it to, the walk you took.
- **Ghostwriting**: The client's expertise sounds richer when expressed through stories, not bullet points. Displace the insight into narrative.

**Success Metric**: Does the reader feel the truth before you state it? If the insight lands before the thesis, subtext is working.

## Pattern 6: The Fanciful Real

**Original**: Make characters psychologically true but circumstantially impossible. The emotional reality matters more than literal accuracy.

**Cross-Domain Application**:
- **Brand Stories**: A brand origin story doesn't need to be literally accurate — it needs to be *emotionally true*. The feeling of the founding moment matters more than the exact sequence of events.
- **Content**: Case studies and examples can be composites ("fanciful") if they're psychologically faithful. "A client I once worked with" can represent a pattern, not a single individual.
- **Marketing**: Product claims grounded in emotional reality ("this feels like having a second brain") outperform literal claims ("this saves 37 minutes per day").

**Success Metric**: Could someone be moved by this story while suspecting it's embellished? If yes, the fanciful real is working.

## Pattern 7: Visual Prose Cinematography

**Original**: Write what scenes "feel like and sound like" — weather, light, texture, time of day.

**Cross-Domain Application**:
- **Email Openings**: "It's 5:47 AM. The office is dark except for the blue glow of your monitor and the notification light on your phone, pulsing like a heartbeat."
- **Landing Page Heroes**: Sensory openings outperform pain-point openings. Make the reader feel *where they are* before telling them what to do.
- **LinkedIn Posts**: One sensory sentence at the top outperforms any hook formula. "The wind rattled the conference room windows as she said the words that changed everything."

**Success Metric**: Close your eyes after reading the opening. Are you inside a world, or reading words on a page?

## Pattern 8: Change the Weather

**Original**: When stuck on a scene, change the physical environment — make it rain, add wind, shift to dusk.

**Cross-Domain Application**:
- **Content**: A flat blog post often needs a scene shift, not a better argument. Move the setting of your example. Add a timestamp. Change the emotional weather.
- **Copywriting**: If a landing page section feels dead, shift the sensory context. Move from "office at noon" to "kitchen table at midnight."
- **Creative Block**: When a content calendar feels lifeless, the fix isn't discipline — it's changing the weather of the project. New format. New angle. New voice.

**Success Metric**: Does the weather shift unlock at least one new idea or emotion?

---

## Pattern 9: The Memorable Residue

**Original**: Engineer at least one moment per act that audiences will remember — a line, a visual, an ironic juxtaposition.

**Cross-Domain Application**:
- **Social Posts**: Every post needs one "residue moment" — a line the reader screenshots, a metaphor they'll reuse, a frame they'll reference.
- **Marketing**: Every campaign needs one quotable line. Not a tagline — a *truth* spoken so precisely that people adopt it as their own language.
- **Copywriting**: One memorable line per email or landing page section. The rest can be competent; the residue line must be unforgettable.

**Success Metric**: After reading, can someone name the one line they'll still remember tomorrow?

---

## Pattern 10: Time as Motif

**Original**: Use time as thematic architecture — span decades, age backwards, compress life.

**Cross-Domain Application**:
- **Content Series**: Use time as a structural device. "A year ago I believed X. Then..." Time compression creates narrative gravity in content.
- **Brand Stories**: Origin stories that span time periods (founding → struggle → now) feel more epic than single-moment stories.
- **Email Sequences**: Reference the passage of time within the sequence itself. "When you read email #1, you might have thought X. Now, three days later..."

**Success Metric**: Does the content's relationship with time make it feel more consequential?

---

## Pattern 11: The Third Rail

**Original**: In collaboration, find "the third way" — a solution neither partner would reach alone.

**Cross-Domain Application**:
- **Client Work**: When client feedback conflicts with your craft instinct, find the third rail. Don't capitulate or insist — synthesize.
- **Ghostwriting**: The best ghostwritten content is the third rail between the client's knowledge and the writer's craft.
- **Team Content**: When editor and writer disagree, the answer is almost never "your version" or "my version" — it's something neither would create alone.

**Success Metric**: Does the final version surprise both collaborators?

---

## Pattern 12: The Home Invitation

**Original**: Opening scenes must make audiences feel they've "found a home" worth staying in.

**Cross-Domain Application**:
- **Newsletter Openings**: The first paragraph should feel like settling into a favorite chair. Not a hook — an invitation.
- **Landing Pages**: The hero section should make visitors feel they've arrived somewhere worth exploring, not that they're being sold to.
- **Ghostwritten Pieces**: The opening should feel warm, specific, and lived-in. The reader should think "this person knows my world."

**Success Metric**: Would a reader stay for the second paragraph even if they didn't need what you're selling?

---

## Pattern 13: Ironic Mainstream

**Original**: Layer irony beneath accessible narrative — the naive with the tragic, the personal with the historic.

**Cross-Domain Application**:
- **Social Media**: Posts that work on two levels — surface wisdom for the scroller, buried irony for the careful reader — earn both engagement and respect.
- **Marketing**: Brand campaigns that are both sincere and self-aware feel contemporary. Nike does this. Patagonia does this.
- **Copywriting**: Sales pages that acknowledge the absurdity of their own medium (selling through words) while still selling effectively feel honest.

**Success Metric**: Can a sophisticated reader and a casual reader both enjoy the piece — for different reasons?

---

## Pattern 14: One-Word Outlining

**Original**: Outline with single words — "wedding, shootout, tension." No detailed plans. Know the next 5 steps, not 50.

**Cross-Domain Application**:
- **Content Planning**: Outline a content series with single words per piece: "origin, struggle, breakthrough, method, proof, invitation." No detailed briefs until you're two pieces in.
- **Email Sequences**: Map the sequence emotionally, not logically: "curiosity, fear, relief, proof, belonging, action."
- **Campaign Planning**: Plan marketing campaigns with feeling-words, not deliverable-words. The details emerge through momentum.

**Success Metric**: Does your plan feel like a creative launchpad or a corporate document?
