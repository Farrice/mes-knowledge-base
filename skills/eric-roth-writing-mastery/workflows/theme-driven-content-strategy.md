# Theme-Driven Content Strategy

Apply Eric Roth's Theme Compass to content strategy. Instead of planning by topic or format, plan by **theme** — a single gravitational field that holds diverse content together the way a film's theme holds diverse scenes together.

## Execution

### Phase 1: Theme Discovery

Guide the user through finding their theme. This is NOT a topic — it's the gravitational field.

**Topic**: "How to build an audience on LinkedIn"
**Theme**: *The courage to be visible before you're ready*

**Topic**: "AI tools for business"
**Theme**: *The line between leverage and dependence*

Discovery questions:
1. What keeps appearing in your best content — even when you're not trying?
2. What belief do you hold that most people in your industry would push back on?
3. If you could write about only one tension for the rest of your career, what would it be?
4. What's the question your clients ask that has no easy answer?

The theme should be expressible as a **tension**, not a conclusion. "Visibility vs. readiness." "Ambition vs. integrity." "Speed vs. depth."

### Phase 2: Beginning-End Architecture

Before planning the middle (individual pieces), lock the arc:

1. **Opening Piece**: What's the first truth you want to establish? This is the "home invitation" — the piece that makes readers feel they've found someone worth following.
2. **Closing Piece** (or the piece that would make the series feel "complete"): What's the final resonance beat? What truth do you arrive at?

**Example**:
- **Opening**: "I spent 6 months creating content nobody read. Here's what the silence taught me." (Establishes the tension: visibility vs. readiness)
- **Closing**: "You don't get ready and then show up. You show up and readiness finds you." (Resolves the tension)

### Phase 3: One-Word Outlining

Map the middle with single words — not titles, not outlines, not briefs.

**Example for a 10-piece series on "The courage to be visible before you're ready"**:
1. Silence
2. Imitation
3. Voice
4. Exposure
5. Feedback
6. Rejection
7. Persistence
8. Recognition
9. Identity
10. Freedom

Each word is a *feeling* or *moment* — not a topic. The specific content for each piece emerges when you sit down to write it, not when you plan it.

### Phase 4: Theme Calibration Across Pieces

For each piece in the series, verify theme alignment:

| Piece | Surface Topic | Theme Connection | Off-Center Angle |
|-------|-------------|-----------------|-----------------|
| #1 "Silence" | My first 6 months of dead content | The terror of being visible to no one | Tell it through the story of performing karaoke to an empty bar |
| #2 "Imitation" | How I copied other creators | The safety of someone else's voice vs. the risk of your own | Frame it through learning to cook by following recipes vs. tasting |

Every piece should answer: "How does this apply to [theme]?" — even obliquely.

### Phase 5: Temporal Architecture

Apply Pattern 10 (Time as Motif) to the series:
- Reference the passage of time between pieces: "Three weeks ago when I wrote about silence, I believed X..."
- Let the series compress or expand time: one piece might cover a single day; the next might span a year
- Use time as narrative texture, not just chronological order

## Output Requirements

**Deliver**:
1. **Theme statement** — one sentence, expressed as a tension
2. **Beginning-End architecture** — opening and closing pieces defined
3. **One-word outline** — the emotional map of the series
4. **Theme calibration table** — each piece with surface topic, theme connection, and off-center angle
5. **Temporal architecture notes** — how time operates across the series
6. **Series brief** — a single paragraph describing the arc, suitable for sharing with collaborators

**Quality Standard**: Someone should be able to read any piece in the series without having read the others — and still feel the theme. But reading them in order should create a cumulative effect greater than any individual piece.
