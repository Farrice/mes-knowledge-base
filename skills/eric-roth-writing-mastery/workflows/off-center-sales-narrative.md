# Off-Center Sales Narrative

Apply Eric Roth's off-center subtext to sales writing. The most powerful sales copy doesn't sell directly — it displaces the pitch into story, metaphor, and emotional truth. The reader persuades themselves.

## The Displacement Selling Principle

Direct sales copy says: "Here's your problem. Here's my solution. Here's why you should buy it."

Displacement selling says: "Let me tell you about something that happened. You'll recognize yourself in it. And by the time we're done, you'll know what to do — without me ever telling you."

Roth never has a character say "I'm upset with my mother." Instead, the character tells their brother about a dream. The upset is *conveyed* without being *stated*. The audience feels the truth.

The same principle applies to copy: the offer is *implied* through narrative displacement, not argued through logical persuasion.

## Execution

### Phase 1: Map the Direct Version

First, write the direct sales argument (you'll never use it, but you need to know it):
1. **The problem**: What pain does the prospect feel?
2. **The cause**: Why conventional solutions don't work?
3. **The mechanism**: How your solution works differently?
4. **The proof**: Why should they believe you?
5. **The offer**: What exactly are you selling?

### Phase 2: Find the Displacement

For each element of the direct version, find an off-center displacement:

| Direct Element | Displacement Technique |
|---------------|----------------------|
| **The problem** | A story from a different domain that creates the same feeling. Don't say "you're stuck" — describe a river that stops flowing. |
| **The cause** | An ironic observation about the conventional approach. Don't say "old methods don't work" — show someone doing the old method really well... and still failing. |
| **The mechanism** | A metaphor or analogy that makes the mechanism feel intuitive. Don't explain the technology — describe the experience of using it. |
| **The proof** | A telling detail, not a testimonial. Don't say "93% success rate" — describe the specific moment a single client realized it was working. |
| **The offer** | An invitation, not a pitch. Don't say "buy now" — extend the narrative: "if you're still reading, it's because this story is yours too." |

### Phase 3: Build the Narrative Arc

Structure the copy as a story with three movements:

**Movement 1: Recognition** (The reader recognizes themselves)
- Open with a scene or observation from a parallel world (displacement)
- The reader should think: "That's exactly how I feel, but I've never heard it described this way"
- Use visual prose to create immersion
- End with a turning point: something shifted

**Movement 2: Revelation** (The truth emerges through the story)
- The "mechanism" or "solution" enters the narrative organically
- It's described through its effects, not its features
- The reader understands what changed *because they felt it happen in the story*
- One telling detail replaces a paragraph of proof

**Movement 3: Invitation** (The reader completes the story through action)
- The CTA is the natural conclusion of the narrative
- It doesn't feel like "selling" — it feels like "continuing"
- The residue line precedes the action step

### Phase 4: Ironic Mainstream Check

Apply Pattern 13: the copy should work on two levels simultaneously.
- **Surface level**: An interesting story that any reader can enjoy
- **Sales level**: A precise persuasion architecture that a copywriter can reverse-engineer

If only one level works, the copy is either entertainment (no conversion) or manipulation (no trust). The sweet spot is both.

### Phase 5: The Non-CTA CTA

Engineer the closing action as a continuation, not a command:

**Direct**: "Start your free trial today."
**Displaced**: "The story doesn't end here. It ends [wherever the product takes them]. If you're ready for the next chapter..."

**Direct**: "Book a consultation."
**Displaced**: "I'm still writing this story for [specific people who match the ICP]. If this is your story, let's finish it together."

## Output Requirements

**Deliver**:
1. **Direct sales argument** — the conventional version (reference document)
2. **Displacement map** — each direct element → its off-center version
3. **Full narrative copy** — the displaced sales story in three movements
4. **Residue line** — the one line they'll remember
5. **Direct B-version** — a conventional copy version for A/B testing

**Quality Standard**: A reader should reach the CTA feeling like they decided on their own, not like they were persuaded. If the copy feels like "selling," the displacement isn't working.
