# Cinematic Email Sequences

Apply Eric Roth's filmmaking craft to email sequences. Each email is an "episode" in a serialized story. The sequence has a thematic arc, temporal structure, visual prose openings, and displacement-driven calls to action. The result: emails people *want* to open because they're continuing a story.

## The Cinematic Email Principle

Standard email sequences are structured as logical progressions: awareness → education → social proof → offer → urgency.

Cinematic email sequences are structured as **narrative arcs**: opening scene → complication → deepening → revelation → invitation. The logic is still there — but it's embedded in story, the way a film's "message" is embedded in plot.

## Execution

### Phase 1: Sequence Architecture

Apply Pattern 3 (Beginning-End Architecture):

1. **Write Email #1 (Opening)** — Know the emotional starting point. Where is the reader when they meet you?
2. **Write the Final Email** — Know the emotional ending. Where should the reader be when they're ready to act?
3. **Map the Middle** — Use Pattern 14 (One-Word Outlining). Single words for each middle email: "doubt, recognition, possibility, proof, belonging."

**Example** (7-email welcome sequence):

| # | One-Word | Emotional Function |
|---|---------|-------------------|
| 1 | **Arrival** | The reader has found a home worth staying in |
| 2 | **Recognition** | "You see me" — the reader feels deeply understood |
| 3 | **Doubt** | Honest acknowledgment of why change is hard |
| 4 | **Story** | A displaced narrative carrying the mechanism |
| 5 | **Proof** | A telling detail (not testimonial) that makes the result feel real |
| 6 | **Belonging** | The reader is already part of this; the question is whether to go deeper |
| 7 | **Invitation** | The natural next step, framed as continuation |

### Phase 2: Visual Prose Openings

Every email opens with a sensory scene. Not a hook — a **world**.

**Email #1 (Arrival)**:
> It's 6:14 AM and you're reading this in bed, phone held above your face, left thumb scrolling, right eye still half-closed. The coffee isn't made yet but you've already checked your inbox twice.

**Email #3 (Doubt)**:
> There's a folder on your desktop called "Someday." You created it in October. Inside it: a course you never started, a template you downloaded but never opened, and a screenshot of someone's results that made you feel equal parts inspired and defeated.

**Email #5 (Proof)**:
> Her Slack status said "Deep Work" but she was actually staring at the revenue dashboard, watching the number that hadn't moved in 11 days. Then it moved.

The sensory opening creates pattern recognition: the reader *wants* to open your emails because each one starts with a moment they recognize.

### Phase 3: Temporal Architecture

Apply Pattern 10 (Time as Motif) across the sequence:

- **Reference time between emails**: "When you read my first email three days ago, you might have thought..."
- **Compress time in stories**: "In the span of one coffee — from first sip to cold dregs — she rewrote her entire pricing page."
- **Expand time for impact**: "That decision in email #4? It took her two years to undo. Let me save you the time."
- **Use time of day as emotional control**: morning emails feel different from evening emails — lean into this

### Phase 4: The Displacement CTA

Every email's call-to-action is displaced, not direct:

| Direct CTA | Displaced CTA |
|-----------|--------------|
| "Click here to learn more" | "If you're still reading this at 6:14 AM with no coffee, we should probably talk." |
| "Buy now" | "The folder called 'Someday' is still on your desktop. This is the email where Someday becomes Tuesday." |
| "Book a call" | "I wrote this sequence for one specific person. You'll know if it's you." |
| "Join the program" | "The story continues on the other side of this link." |

### Phase 5: Residue Engineering Across the Arc

Each email needs one piece of memorable residue. But the *sequence* also needs a cumulative residue — a line, image, or motif that recurs and deepens:

**Recurring Motif Example**: "The cursor" — appears in email #1 (blinking on an empty doc), email #3 (blinking on a half-started draft), email #5 (moving confidently across a finished piece), email #7 (waiting for the reader to decide what to write next).

The motif creates narrative continuity without the reader consciously tracking it.

## Output Requirements

**Deliver**:
1. **Sequence architecture** — Beginning-End lock + one-word outline
2. **Full email drafts** — each with visual prose opening, displaced CTA, and residue line
3. **Temporal architecture map** — how time operates across the sequence
4. **Recurring motif** — the element that threads through all emails
5. **Subject line set** — subject lines that create curiosity through specificity, not mystery
6. **Conventional version** — a standard email sequence for A/B testing comparison

**Quality Standard**: A subscriber who reads all emails should feel like they experienced a story, not a funnel. If they can identify where the "selling" happens, the displacement isn't working.

## Sequence Type Adaptations

| Type | Modification |
|------|-------------|
| **Welcome Sequence** | 5-7 emails. Heavy on Home Invitation energy in #1. Delayed first offer until email #4+. |
| **Launch Sequence** | 7-10 emails. More urgency but still displaced. The story accelerates toward its climax. |
| **Nurture Sequence** | Ongoing. Each email is a standalone "episode." Thematic coherence without sequential dependency. |
| **Abandoned Cart** | 3 emails. Visual prose of the moment they almost bought. The product is a character in their story. |
| **Onboarding** | 5 emails. The reader is the protagonist. Each email is a chapter in their transformation. |
