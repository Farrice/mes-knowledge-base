# Fanciful Realism for Brands

Apply Eric Roth's core paradox — characters who are "fanciful" yet feel "more real than real people" — to brand storytelling. Brand stories don't need to be literally accurate. They need to be **emotionally true**. The fanciful real is what makes Forrest Gump more moving than a documentary.

## The Fanciful Realism Spectrum

| Level | What It Means | Brand Example |
|-------|-------------|---------------|
| **Documentary** | Everything literally happened | Most corporate "About" pages |
| **Curated Real** | Everything happened, but details were selected for narrative | Apple's garage origin story |
| **Fanciful Real** | Emotionally true, circumstantially elevated. The *feeling* of the founding moment, with details chosen for resonance over accuracy | Nike's waffle iron story — true, but told like myth |
| **Myth** | Disconnected from reality | To be avoided — loses trust |

The sweet spot is **Fanciful Real** — everything feels true, the emotions are genuine, the details are chosen for storytelling power. Nothing is fabricated, but the selection and framing elevate reality into resonance.

## Execution

### Phase 1: Extract the Emotional Truth

Before writing any brand story, identify the emotional core:

1. **The Founding Emotion**: What was the *feeling* that started the brand? Not the business plan — the feeling. (Frustration? Defiance? Love? Desperation?)
2. **The Core Tension**: What human tension does the brand live inside? (Control vs. freedom? Ambition vs. authenticity? Speed vs. depth?)
3. **The Moment of Commitment**: When did it stop being an idea and start being inevitable? (This moment is often the most powerful scene.)

### Phase 2: Build the Fanciful Real Version

For each brand story element, apply the fanciful real filter:

**Origin Story**:
- What literally happened → what it *felt* like it happened
- Strip away boring logistics (incorporation, market research, fundraising)
- Elevate the sensory details of the founding moment (where were they? what did it look like? what time of day?)
- Keep the emotional truth absolute — the *why* must be genuine

**Founder Story**:
- Apply Pattern 4 (Psychology Wallet) to the founder — not a bio, but a character
- Include telling details that reveal motivation without stating it
- The founder's flaws are as important as their vision — fanciful doesn't mean flattering

**Product/Service Story**:
- How the product was born → told as a narrative, not a feature list
- The "aha moment" written in visual prose — what did the environment look like when the idea clicked?
- The struggle between what the market wanted and what the product was trying to be

### Phase 3: Theme Compass Alignment

Lock the brand's thematic North Star and ensure every story element orbits it:

1. **State the theme** as a tension (not a slogan): "[Brand] lives at the intersection of _____ and _____"
2. **Test each story element**: "How does this detail relate to our theme?"
3. **Cut anything that's true but thematically irrelevant** — accuracy without resonance is noise
4. **Amplify anything that's thematically rich** — even if it was a minor detail in reality

### Phase 4: Visual Prose Treatment

Rewrite the brand story with Roth's visual prose technique:
- Open with a sensory scene (time of day, weather, physical environment)
- Use specific details instead of generalizations ("a cramped studio apartment in Oakland" not "humble beginnings")
- Include one sound, one sight, one texture in every paragraph
- Let the prose create a world the reader can inhabit

### Phase 5: Residue Engineering

Every brand story should leave one piece of memorable residue:
- **The Line**: One sentence people will quote when describing what the brand stands for
- **The Image**: One visual that people will reference ("the garage," "the waffle iron")
- **The Paradox**: One ironic truth that makes the brand feel both relatable and aspirational

## Output Requirements

**Deliver**:
1. **Emotional truth extraction** — founding emotion, core tension, commitment moment
2. **Fanciful real brand story** — the full narrative with visual prose treatment
3. **Theme compass** — the brand's thematic tension, stated clearly
4. **Residue elements** — the line, the image, the paradox
5. **Application guide** — how to deploy the story across website, social, pitch decks, and press
6. **Authenticity guardrails** — clear boundaries on what's elevated vs. what's fabricated (never cross into fabrication)

**Quality Standard**: A reader should feel like the brand is a character they want to root for. If the story reads like a press release, the fanciful real isn't working. If it feels dishonest, you've crossed into myth.
