# Genius Patterns

14 executable patterns from Eric Roth's 60 years of Oscar-level screenwriting mastery.

---

## Pattern 1: The Erosion Protocol
**Execute**: Start every writing session from page one. Reread and refine everything before advancing. Shore up what's "falling down." Make tired things "feel alive." Never begin where you left off — always re-enter from the beginning.

**Success Metric**: Does rereading from page one reveal improvements? If so, the erosion is working.

---

## Pattern 2: Theme-First Compass
**Execute**: Before writing any scene, articulate the theme in one sentence. When a scene feels inert, ask: "How does this apply to the theme of the movie?" Use theme as a navigation instrument, not a conclusion to prove.

**Success Metric**: Can every scene answer "What does this have to do with [theme]?" — even obliquely?

---

## Pattern 3: Beginning-End Architecture
**Execute**: Know your first scene and last scene before writing anything in the middle. Lock the emotional departure and arrival points. Treat the middle as a "great big blob" to be discovered scene by scene.

**Success Metric**: Does your ending feel earned by your beginning? Does reading only the opening and closing give you a complete emotional arc?

---

## Pattern 4: Character Psychology Wallets
**Execute**: For each character, build a "wallet" — neuroses, giddiness, anger triggers, crumpled phone number, picture of a supposed daughter, a saying they find interesting. The wallet creates completeness even when most details never surface.

**Success Metric**: Could you describe what's in this character's wallet? Does each voice sound distinct without attribution tags?

---

## Pattern 5: Off-Center Subtext
**Execute**: When you need to convey exposition or emotion, don't write it directly. Write "off-center" — use a dream, an unrelated story, an Athens judiciary, a fortune cookie. The subtext delivers the same payload through displacement.

**Success Metric**: Read your dialogue aloud. Are characters telling each other things they already know? If so, rewrite off-center.

---

## Pattern 6: The Fanciful Real
**Execute**: Accept that characters are "fanciful" — "the other side of the moon." Make them psychologically true but circumstantially impossible. Give them 2-3 things they love, clear motivations, and enough specificity that audiences think "this is somebody worth caring about."

**Success Metric**: Could someone cry for this character while knowing they're fictional?

---

## Pattern 7: Visual Prose Cinematography
**Execute**: Write screenplays with novelistic prose — what the scene "feels like and sounds like." Include weather, wind, rattle of windows, quality of light, time of day. Write for immersion, not efficiency.

**Success Metric**: Does a reader experience your script as a world, or as dialogue exchanges?

---

## Pattern 8: Change the Weather
**Execute**: When stuck on a scene, change the physical environment. Make it rain. Add wind. Move to dusk. The shift unlocks new behavior, new movement, new possibilities.

**Success Metric**: Does the weather shift unlock at least one new character behavior or beat?

---

## Pattern 9: The Memorable Residue
**Execute**: For every major scene, ask: "What will people quote from this? What will stay in their soul?" Engineer at least one element per act that audiences will remember — a line, a visual, an ironic juxtaposition.

**Success Metric**: After reading, can someone name 3 moments they'll never forget?

---

## Pattern 10: Time as Motif
**Execute**: Use time not just as a story element but as thematic architecture. Span decades. Age backwards. Show life compression. Make time itself a character — unstoppable, crushing, beautiful.

**Success Metric**: Does the story's relationship with time enhance its emotional impact?

---

## Pattern 11: The Third Rail
**Execute**: In collaboration, find "the third way" — a solution neither writer nor director would reach alone. Negotiate with the humility that "eventually it's the director who's going to make a decision."

**Success Metric**: After disagreement, does the final version incorporate genuine contributions from both?

---

## Pattern 12: The Home Invitation
**Execute**: Opening scenes must make audiences feel they've "found a home" worth living in for two hours. Achieve through sensory immersion, warmth, and environmental specificity — not through hooks or shock.

**Success Metric**: Would a reader want to keep reading past page 2?

---

## Pattern 13: Ironic Mainstream
**Execute**: Layer irony beneath accessible narrative. Juxtapose the naive with the tragic, the personal with the historic, the innocent with the violent. The irony is structural, not snarky.

**Success Metric**: Can a film professor and a 12-year-old both enjoy your work — for different reasons?

---

## Pattern 14: One-Word Outlining
**Execute**: Outline forward with single words — "wedding, shootout, tension." No detailed scene descriptions. Know the next 5 scenes at most. Always leave the day with a scene you've "licked" to start the next day with confidence.

**Success Metric**: Does your outline feel like a creative launchpad or a straitjacket?
