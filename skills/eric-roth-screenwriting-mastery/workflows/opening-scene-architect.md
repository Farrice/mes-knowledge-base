# Opening Scene Architect

Craft opening scenes that make audiences feel they've "found a home" — not through hooks or spectacle, but through sensory immersion and warmth that says *this world is worth two hours of your life.* The audience must feel invited, not challenged.

## Pre-Flight

Read these files before executing:
1. `skills/eric-roth-screenwriting-mastery/genius.md`
2. `skills/eric-roth-screenwriting-mastery/references/genius-patterns.md` (Patterns 7, 12)
3. `skills/eric-roth-screenwriting-mastery/references/hidden-knowledge.md` (HK-8)

## Input Required

- **Story context** — genre, theme, tone, era
- **Main characters** — who appears in (or is foreshadowed by) the opening
- **What the audience should feel** after the first 2 pages

---

## Workflow

### Step 1: The Home Test

Before writing anything, answer the core question:

> If your screenplay were a house, what kind of house is it?
> - A warm kitchen with coffee and morning light?
> - A rain-soaked porch with a creaking swing?
> - A vast dark room where you hear breathing before you see anything?

This metaphor sets your sensory palette.

**Roth's openings:**
- *Forrest Gump*: A feather drifts through a Southern sky — lightness, whimsy, fate
- *Benjamin Button*: "Everything begins in the dark" — mystery, birth, time
- *The Insider*: A blindfolded man in a car — danger, vulnerability, trust

---

### Step 2: Sensory World-Building

Using Visual Prose Cinematographer technique, establish:

1. **Time of day** — matching emotional register (HK-8)
2. **Weather/atmosphere** — what does the air feel like?
3. **Ambient sound** — what would you hear standing on this set?
4. **The first visual** — what does the eye land on before any character?
5. **The inciting texture** — one specific sensory detail that hooks curiosity

---

### Step 3: Character Introduction Architecture

How do we first meet the protagonist? Options:

| Method | Effect | Roth Example |
|--------|--------|-------------|
| **In medias res** | Immediate intimacy — audience joins mid-life | Gump on the bench, mid-story |
| **Sensory before identity** | Mystery, then reveal | The drifting feather before Gump's face |
| **Environment first** | The world is a character; the person is discovered within it | Button's maternity ward |
| **Retrospective voice** | Wisdom framing the narrative | Old Gump narrating to strangers |
| **Action that reveals** | A single behavior that tells you everything about them | Button's grandmother singing |

---

### Step 4: Theme Seed Planting

Plant a thematic seed in the opening that will bloom in the closing:

- What **image, line, or detail** in the opening will gain new meaning by the end?
- How can you introduce the theme without stating it?
- What **question** does the opening raise that the closing answers?

The opening and closing should form a marriage — reading only these two scenes gives you the complete emotional arc.

---

### Step 5: The Two-Page Test

Write your opening as 2 pages of visual prose. Then apply:

- [ ] **Home test**: Would someone want to stay here for two hours?
- [ ] **Anti-push test**: Is any element pushing the audience away through confusion, opacity, or coldness?
- [ ] **Curiosity test**: After 2 pages, would someone need to keep reading?
- [ ] **Theme test**: Is the theme present (even as a whisper)?
- [ ] **Sensory test**: Could someone describe what this scene *feels* like?

```
- Inciting texture: [the detail that hooks curiosity]

- Method: [in medias res / sensory first / environment first / etc.]

THEME SEED:
- Planted detail: [what will bloom in the closing]
- Question raised: [what the audience is curious about]

OPENING SCENE (FULL VISUAL PROSE):
[2 pages of immersive, novelistic scene description]
```
