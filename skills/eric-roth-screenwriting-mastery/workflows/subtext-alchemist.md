# Subtext Alchemist

Transform explicit, expository dialogue into layered subtext using Eric Roth's "off-center" displacement technique. Turn "I'm upset with my mother" into a dream sequence. Replace the Water Commissioner with a fortune cookie.

## Pre-Flight

Read these files before executing:
1. `skills/eric-roth-screenwriting-mastery/genius.md` (principle #3 — Off-Center Everything)
2. `skills/eric-roth-screenwriting-mastery/references/genius-patterns.md` (Pattern 5)
3. `skills/eric-roth-screenwriting-mastery/references/hidden-knowledge.md` (HK-2, HK-6)

## Input Required

- **Scene or dialogue excerpt** — the material to transform
- **What the scene needs to communicate** — the information/emotion the audience needs
- **Character context** — who's speaking, their relationship, what they know

---

## Workflow

### Step 1: Exposition Autopsy

Dissect the current dialogue. For each line, classify:

| Line | Classification | Verdict |
|------|---------------|---------|
| [line] | ⚠️ **Water Commissioner** — characters telling each other what they know | REWRITE |
| [line] | ⚠️ **Thesis Statement** — character stating the theme directly | REWRITE |
| [line] | ✅ **Off-center** — meaning arrives through displacement | KEEP |
| [line] | ✅ **Action-reveal** — behavior shows what words don't say | KEEP |

Every line tagged ⚠️ gets rewritten.

---

### Step 2: Displacement Mapping

For each ⚠️ line, choose a displacement method:

| Method | How It Works | Example |
|--------|-------------|---------|
| **Dream displacement** | Character tells a dream that metaphorically conveys the emotion | "I had this dream where all the clocks were running backwards" instead of "I feel like we're losing time" |
| **Unrelated story** | Character tells an apparently irrelevant story that carries the same payload | A story about Athens' government instead of saying "I feel judged" |
| **Object displacement** | A physical object carries the emotional weight | A fortune cookie that reads "An old love will come back to you" |
| **Environmental echo** | The setting mirrors the internal state | Rain starting as the conversation reaches its emotional peak |
| **Behavioral reveal** | Character does something that shows what they won't say | Rearranging items on a table obsessively during a calm conversation |
| **Historical/literary reference** | Character references something distant that maps to their situation | Quoting Longfellow about "the pause in the day's occupation" instead of saying "I'm tired" |

---

### Step 3: The Three-Layer Test

For each rewritten passage, verify three layers are present:

1. **Surface layer** — What the audience *hears/sees* (the dream, the story, the object)
2. **Subtext layer** — What the audience *understands* (the real emotion/information)
3. **Surprise layer** — What the audience *discovers* (something they didn't expect from the displacement)

If only layers 1 and 2 are present, the subtext is functional but not Roth-level. Push for layer 3.

---

### Step 4: Dialogue Reconstruction

Rewrite the full scene with all ⚠️ lines displaced. Rules:

- **No character should tell another character something they both already know**
- **Emotion should arrive through action, environment, or displacement — never through declaration**
- **Leave room for actors** — if a look could replace a line, note it (HK-4: Pacino's "one look")
- **Maintain character voice** — the displacement method should match the character's psychology

---

### Step 5: The Water Commissioner Test

Final validation — read the rewritten scene and ask:

- [ ] Would a director cringe at any line? (The Water Commissioner test)
- [ ] Are there 3 layers in every key exchange?
- [ ] Could an actor play this scene with minimal dialogue cuts?
- [ ] Does the scene communicate *more* than the original, not less?
- [ ] Is there at least one moment of surprise (layer 3)?

## Anti-Patterns

| ❌ Don't | ✅ Do |
|----------|------|
| Replace exposition with *different* exposition | Replace exposition with displacement |
| Make every line a metaphor (exhausting) | Mix off-center lines with natural, simple exchanges |
| Use displacement so oblique that meaning is lost | Ensure subtext layer is accessible to attentive viewers |
| Force literary references that don't match the character's voice | Use displacements that emerge from who the character IS |
