# Theme Compass Navigator

Find and maintain the thematic through-line that holds an entire screenplay together. "What's the theme of the movie? What's the movie literally about?" — not plot, not genre, but the single gravitational truth that every scene orbits.

## Pre-Flight

Read these files before executing:
1. `skills/eric-roth-screenwriting-mastery/genius.md` (principle #2 — Theme = Compass)
2. `skills/eric-roth-screenwriting-mastery/references/genius-patterns.md` (Pattern 2)

## Input Required

- **Project summary** — plot, characters, genre, setting
- **Current state** — is the theme already known, suspected, or completely unknown?
- **Problem** (if applicable) — "The script feels scattered" / "I can't articulate what it's about"

---

## Workflow

### Step 1: Theme Excavation

If the theme is unknown or vague, use these excavation methods:

**Method A: The "Literally About" Test**
> Complete this sentence: "This story is literally about ___."
> Not the plot. Not the character. The *truth* that makes this story worth two hours of a stranger's life.
>
> Examples:
> - NOT: "A man with an IQ of 75 who runs through American history" → That's plot
> - YES: "An innocent man witnesses the loss of American innocence" → That's theme

**Method B: The Character Contradiction Test**
> What does your protagonist believe at the start that they no longer believe at the end?
> The gap between those two beliefs IS the theme.

**Method C: The Audience Question Test**
> What question does the audience walk out asking themselves about their own life?
> That question, reversed into a statement, IS the theme.

---

### Step 2: Theme Articulation

Write the theme in three forms:

| Form | Purpose | Example (*Benjamin Button*) |
|------|---------|----------------------------|
| **One sentence** (under 12 words) | Your working compass | "Time destroys everything except the memory of love" |
| **One question** | The audience's takeaway | "Can love survive time's relentless forward march?" |
| **One paradox** | The creative tension that generates drama | "To grow young is to grow wise — but wisdom comes too late" |

---

### Step 3: Scene-Level Application

For every scene in the outline, answer:

| Scene | Theme Connection | Strength |
|-------|-----------------|----------|
| [scene] | How does this relate to the theme? | 🟢 Strong / 🟡 Weak / 🔴 Missing |

**Rules:**
- 🟢 scenes are fine — the theme is actively present
- 🟡 scenes need shoring up — find the angle where theme applies (even obliquely)
- 🔴 scenes must be reworked or justified — "a scene that feels inert" needs the theme injected

**Roth's method**: "When you have a scene that feels inert, you have to find a way to utilize it with the theme of the movie."

---

### Step 4: Theme Accumulation Check

Theme should build across the three acts, not just repeat:

| Act | Theme Function | What's Happening |
|-----|---------------|-----------------|
| **Act One** | **Introduction** — theme is present but unrecognized | Characters live within the theme without knowing it |
| **Act Two** | **Confrontation** — theme is challenged or complicated | The theme becomes a question, not an answer |
| **Act Three** | **Resolution** — theme is earned, deepened, or subverted | The audience feels the theme rather than hearing it |

If the theme is identical at the end as at the beginning, you haven't earned it. It must deepen.

---

### Step 5: The Inertia Audit

Final pass — identify every "inert" scene:
- Scenes that exist only to move plot from A to B
- Scenes that are exciting but thematically unconnected
- Scenes that repeat the same thematic note as a previous scene

For each inert scene:
1. **Can the theme be woven in?** → If yes, rework
2. **Is the scene essential for plot mechanics?** → If yes, add thematic texture through visual prose, subtext, or character behavior
3. **Is the scene replaceable?** → If yes, replace with a scene that does the same plot work while serving the theme
