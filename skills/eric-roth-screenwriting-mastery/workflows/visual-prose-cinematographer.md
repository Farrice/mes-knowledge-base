# Visual Prose Cinematographer

Write screenplay scene descriptions as a frustrated novelist would — not slug lines and minimal direction, but sensory prose that makes directors, actors, and readers *live* inside the scene. "I like to be very visual. My scripts are particularly human and very emotional."

## Pre-Flight

Read these files before executing:
1. `skills/eric-roth-screenwriting-mastery/genius.md` (principle #4 — Visual Prose)
2. `skills/eric-roth-screenwriting-mastery/references/genius-patterns.md` (Pattern 7)

## Input Required

- **Scene context** — what happens, who's present, where/when
- **Emotional register** — what should the audience *feel*?
- **Time of day** (if known) — or let the workflow determine it

---

## Workflow

### Step 1: Time of Day Selection (HK-8)

Choose the time of day based on the emotional register, not plot logistics:

| Time | Emotion | When to Use |
|------|---------|-------------|
| **Dawn** | Possibility, fragility, new beginning | Turning points, first meetings, moments of hope |
| **Morning light** | Clarity, energy, innocence | Exposition scenes, establishing normalcy |
| **High noon** | Confrontation, exposure, nowhere to hide | Conflict scenes, revelations, power plays |
| **Late afternoon** | Weariness, bittersweet warmth | Reflection, reconciliation, before-the-storm |
| **Dusk** | Melancholy, transition, endings | Loss scenes, goodbyes, the pause before change |
| **Night** | Intimacy, danger, isolation | Confessions, threats, secrets |
| **Full darkness** | Birth, origin, the unknown | Opening scenes, transformative moments |

---

### Step 2: Sensory Layering

For the scene, compose five sensory layers:

1. **SIGHT** — What does the eye land on first? What's in the background? What's the quality of light?
2. **SOUND** — Ambient noise, silence, the rattle of windows, distant music, a ticking clock
3. **TEXTURE** — The feel of surfaces — cracked leather, warm concrete, wet grass, cold metal
4. **WEATHER** — Not just "sunny" or "rainy" — the specific quality of the air, the wind direction, the heaviness of clouds
5. **MOVEMENT** — How does the world move around the characters? Leaves falling, traffic flowing, a curtain lifting

---

### Step 3: Prose Composition

Write the scene description as a mini-novel passage (3-8 sentences). Rules:

- **Lead with the most evocative image**, not the most informational
- **Use specific nouns** — not "a car" but "a rusted '72 Chevelle with one working headlight"
- **Use active verbs** — the wind doesn't "blow," it "snaps the clothesline" or "carries the smell of charcoal"
- **Include one sensory surprise** — something the reader doesn't expect in this setting
- **End with the emotional landing** — what does being here *feel like*?

Example (Roth-style):
> The afternoon has that heavy quality where even the shadows seem tired. Through the kitchen window, a line of white sheets hangs motionless in air that smells like cut grass and someone's charcoal grill two houses down. A ceiling fan overhead clicks on every third rotation — a sound so consistent it's become the room's heartbeat. The light catches dust motes above the table where SARAH sits with her hands around a coffee mug that went cold an hour ago. She hasn't moved. The house holds its breath with her.

---

### Step 4: Character Integration

Place characters within the sensory environment:

- How do they **interact with the space**? (Leaning against a doorframe, pacing, sitting unnaturally still)
- What does their **physical state** reveal? (Coat still buttoned indoors, sleeves rolled to elbows, shoes kicked off)
- What is the **distance** between characters? (Across a table, on opposite ends of a couch, close enough to touch but not touching)

---

### Step 5: Director's Possibilities

Add 1-2 "possibilities" — visual ideas that a director can use or ignore:

> *Perhaps: the camera finds the untouched coffee first, then drifts to Sarah's face. Or perhaps we hear the charcoal grill before we see anything — the sound of someone else's ordinary evening.*

These are Roth's gift to collaborators — not directives, but invitations.

---

## Output Format

```
SCENE: [Brief context]
TIME OF DAY: [Selected] — Emotional register: [rationale]

SENSORY LAYERS:
- Sight: [specific details]
- Sound: [specific details]
- Texture: [specific details]
- Weather: [specific details]
- Movement: [specific details]

VISUAL PROSE:
[3-8 sentence immersive scene description]

CHARACTER PLACEMENT:
[How characters exist within this sensory world]

DIRECTOR'S POSSIBILITIES:
[1-2 optional visual ideas]
```

## Quality Gate

- [ ] Time of day matches emotional register (not plot logistics)
- [ ] All 5 sensory layers are present
- [ ] At least one sensory surprise per scene
- [ ] Prose reads like a mini-novel (not slug lines)
- [ ] A director would find new ideas in this description
- [ ] The scene *feels* like something — not just describes something
