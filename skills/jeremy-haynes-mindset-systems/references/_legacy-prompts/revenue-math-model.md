# Revenue Math Model

Work backward from your revenue goal to deal structure — let the math show the path.

---

## Context

The pivotal decision in Jeremy Haynes' career: writing the math on a glass wall. Goal: $1M/month. Desired deals: 10. Required per deal: $100K/month. Mechanism: $10K base + 10% net profit share. This simple math invalidated his entire operating model (27 staff, 3 offices, dozens of services) and led to firing 23 staff, refunding $109K, closing all offices, and contracting to 4 people. "The best decision I ever made was doing the math."

**Source Pattern:** Genius Pattern #5 — Revenue Math Decision Framework

---

## Prompt

```
You are a revenue architect using Jeremy Haynes' Revenue Math Decision Framework.

I need to do what Jeremy did: write the math on the wall and let it show me the path. Here's my context:

**Current monthly revenue:** [Where you are now]
**Target monthly revenue:** [Where you want to be]
**Current business model:** [How you make money — services, products, pricing, number of clients]
**Current team size:** [Staff, contractors, overhead]
**Current pain points:** [What's not working — chaos, overwork, thin margins, too many clients, etc.]

---

Using Jeremy Haynes' Revenue Math Model, do the math:

### STEP 1: THE GOAL MATH
Start with the target and work backward:

**Target:** $[X]/month
**Desired number of deals/clients:** [Choose an audaciously small number — force concentration]
**Required revenue per deal:** $[X] ÷ [deals] = $[per deal]

Is this per-deal number achievable with your current model? If not, what model would make it possible?

**Jeremy's example:**
- $1M/month ÷ 10 deals = $100K/deal
- Mechanism: $10K base + 10% net profit share
- Client must net $1M for Jeremy to earn $100K
- Month-to-month contracts (competence signal)

### STEP 2: MODEL VIABILITY CHECK
Test your current model against the math:

| Factor | Current Model | Math-Required Model |
|--------|--------------|-------------------|
| Revenue per client | $ | $ |
| Clients needed for target | | |
| Team size needed | | |
| Overhead per client | $ | $ |
| Profit margin per client | % | % |
| Hours per client per month | | |
| Total hours required | | |

**The honest question:** Can your current model EVER reach the target? If the answer is "technically yes but requires 200 clients and 80 employees" — that's a NO.

### STEP 3: MODEL REDESIGN
Based on the math, design the model that actually works:

**Pricing structure:** [What you charge and how — flat fee, percentage, retainer, rev-share, hybrid]
**Ideal deal size:** [$X/month minimum]
**Client profile:** [Who can pay this and benefit from it — get specific]
**Delivery model:** [How you deliver value at this price point with minimal team]
**Contract terms:** [What signals competence and attracts the right clients]

Jeremy's insight: the rev-share model emerged directly from the math. He didn't copy industry pricing — he reverse-engineered from the goal.

### STEP 4: ELIMINATION AUDIT
What must be eliminated for the math to work?

**Staff to eliminate:** [Who doesn't serve the new model]
**Services to eliminate:** [What offerings don't justify themselves at the new price point]
**Clients to release:** [Who can't/won't pay at the required level]
**Overhead to cut:** [Offices, tools, subscriptions, anything that served the old model]
**Deals to refund:** [Jeremy refunded $109K. What active deals don't fit?]

This is the hardest step. Jeremy: "I fired 23 of 27 staff. I refunded $109K. I closed every office." The math requires ruthless elimination.

### STEP 5: TRANSITION PLAN
Week-by-week plan for moving from current model to math-required model:

**Week 1:** [Specific cuts/decisions]
**Week 2:** [Specific restructuring]
**Week 3:** [First outreach under new model]
**Week 4:** [Pipeline building at new price point]

**The safety net:** Jeremy contracted to 4 people and video-pitched from his apartment. He kept his ambition while shrinking his operation. What's your version of "apartment with a camera"?

### STEP 6: PROOF OF MODEL
How will you validate the new model before going all-in?

**First target client:** [Profile of the ideal first client at new pricing]
**The pitch:** [How to present the new model — reference Jeremy's "throw our balls on the table" approach]
**Validation milestone:** [What proves the model works? Jeremy: doubled Dan Luck's revenue in 2 weeks]
**Timeline to proof:** [Days/weeks to first validation]

Remember: "I call myself idiotic because I could have done this math well before I had 27 staff." The math is always available. Most people just don't do it. You're doing it now.
```

## Quality Check

Output passes if:
- [ ] The math is actually done — specific numbers, not ranges or approximations
- [ ] Current model is honestly assessed (most models can't reach the target — name it)
- [ ] Elimination audit names specific cuts (not "consider reducing overhead")
- [ ] Transition plan is week-by-week concrete (not "gradually shift")
- [ ] The user can literally write this math on a wall and see the path
