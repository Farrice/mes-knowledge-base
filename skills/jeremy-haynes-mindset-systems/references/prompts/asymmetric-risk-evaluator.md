# Asymmetric Risk Evaluator

Evaluate any decision through Jeremy Haynes' fire/water framework — because risk is rarely 50/50.

---

## Context

Jeremy Haynes uses a fire/water analogy: "If you're on fire and there's a body of what might be water or might be gas in front of you — are you jumping in?" Most entrepreneurial decisions have far more upside than downside (asymmetric upside), but most people never actually quantify the risk — they react emotionally. Three categories: high asymmetric upside (jump), unknown unknowns (intuition + faith), asymmetric downside (avoid unless forced).

**Source Pattern:** Genius Pattern #4 — Asymmetric Risk Assessment

---

## Prompt

```
You are a risk strategist using Jeremy Haynes' Asymmetric Risk Assessment framework.

I need to evaluate a decision I'm facing. Here's the context:

**The decision:** [Describe the choice — what you're considering doing]
**What I stand to gain:** [Best case outcomes]
**What I stand to lose:** [Worst case outcomes]
**Current situation without acting:** [What happens if I do nothing — the "staying on fire" scenario]
**My emotional read:** [How does this decision feel? Excited? Terrified? Confused?]
**Resources at risk:** [Money, time, reputation, relationships]

---

Using Jeremy Haynes' Asymmetric Risk framework, evaluate this decision:

### STEP 1: RISK CLASSIFICATION
Classify this decision into one of three categories:

**Category A — High Asymmetric Upside:**
Upside significantly outweighs downside. Worst case is recoverable. Best case is transformative. Most entrepreneurial decisions fall here. Verdict: JUMP.

**Category B — Unknown Unknowns:**
Genuinely can't quantify risk. Not enough information to calculate. Here, intuition + faith are the only tools. Verdict: TRUST YOUR GUT + FAITH.

**Category C — Asymmetric Downside:**
Downside significantly outweighs upside. Rare for entrepreneurial decisions, common for purely financial gambles. Verdict: AVOID UNLESS FORCED.

Show your reasoning for the classification.

### STEP 2: FIRE/WATER ANALYSIS
Apply the fire/water metaphor:

**What is the "fire" (current situation)?**
- How painful/costly/unsustainable is doing nothing?
- Is the fire getting worse over time?
- Is there a timeline before the fire consumes you?

**What is the "water" (the action)?**
- What's the probability this is actually water (upside)?
- What's the probability this is gas (downside)?
- If it IS gas — what happens? Is that survivable?

**The verdict:** Given that you're already on fire, does it make sense to stay burning because the water MIGHT be gas?

### STEP 3: RISK TYPE DECOMPOSITION
Break down what's actually at risk:

| Risk Type | Amount at Risk | Recoverability | Timeline to Recover |
|-----------|---------------|----------------|-------------------|
| Money | $ | Recoverable / Unrecoverable | |
| Time | months/years | Can't recover time, but skills compound | |
| Reputation | Scope | How fast can it be rebuilt? | |
| Relationships | Which ones | Are these relationships serving growth? | |
| Opportunity cost | What you'll miss | Is the alternative actually better? | |

Jeremy's insight: "When you can't risk capital, risk time and relationships instead. Time and effort based risks have longer cycles but still have financial upside."

### STEP 4: WORST CASE SURVIVAL TEST
Assume the WORST case happens. Walk through it:
1. **Day 1 after worst case:** What does life actually look like?
2. **Month 1:** What's the recovery path?
3. **Month 6:** Where are you relative to where you'd be if you never acted?
4. **The honest question:** Is worst case actually worse than staying on fire?

Jeremy's police story lesson: even when asymmetric risk goes wrong, the consequences are usually manageable — and the rage/energy from the setback can be recycled into fuel.

### STEP 5: DECISION PROTOCOL
Based on the analysis:

**JUMP** — Category A. The math favors action. Stop deliberating.
- First action within 24 hours: [specific]
- Commit publicly to: [specific]

**TRUST + LEAP** — Category B. Can't calculate. Trust gut + faith.
- What does your instinct say? Write it down.
- What would the next version of you do?
- Leap deadline: [date within 7 days]

**HOLD** — Category C. Genuine asymmetric downside. Don't be reckless.
- What information would change the classification?
- Can you restructure the deal to reduce downside?
- Is there a smaller version of this risk to test first?

Remember: Most people never even think in risk-assessment terms. They react emotionally. The fact that you're evaluating means you're already ahead. Now use the analysis to decide — and MOVE.
```

---

## Quality Check

Output passes if:
- [ ] Risk is classified with clear reasoning, not just assigned
- [ ] Fire/water analysis honestly assesses the "staying on fire" pain (not downplayed)
- [ ] Risk decomposition reveals that actual downside is less severe than emotional fear
- [ ] Worst case survival test is realistic (not either catastrophized or sugar-coated)
- [ ] Decision protocol includes a specific timeline for action, not open-ended
