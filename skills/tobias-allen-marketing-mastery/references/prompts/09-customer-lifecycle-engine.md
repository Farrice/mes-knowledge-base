## EXECUTION PROTOCOL

### Part A: Retention & Upsell Architecture

#### Phase 1: Churn Diagnostics
1. **MAP** the customer lifecycle from first purchase to churn event:
   - Key milestones (first use, first success, habit formation, renewal decision)
   - Churn risk moments (when do customers typically drop off?)
   - Success indicators (what behaviors predict long-term retention?)
2. **IDENTIFY** the "leakage points"—specific moments where customers disengage:
   - Post-purchase silence (no communication between purchase and next touchpoint)
   - Onboarding failures (customer doesn't achieve first win quickly enough)
   - Value perception decay (customer stops seeing the benefit)
   - Competitive offers (customer lured away by alternatives)
3. **CALCULATE** the financial impact of each leakage point (revenue lost per customer per month/year).

#### Phase 2: Retention Intervention Design
4. **DESIGN** interventions for each leakage point:
   - **Onboarding**: First-48-hour engagement sequence, quick-win tutorials, welcome call for high-ticket
   - **Early Stage**: Day 7/14/30 check-ins, usage prompts, success milestone celebrations
   - **Mid-Stage**: Exclusive content/community, loyalty recognition, personalized recommendations
   - **Risk Stage**: Re-engagement campaigns, special offers, feedback requests, personal outreach
   - **Win-Back**: Exit surveys, "we miss you" campaigns, re-entry incentives
5. **DESIGN** upsell/cross-sell architecture:
   - Natural next purchases (what does the customer need AFTER this product?)
   - Timing triggers (when is the customer most receptive to upgrade/expansion?)
   - Ascension path (entry → core → premium → enterprise/concierge)
   - "Because you bought X" recommendation engine

### Part B: Referral System Design

#### Phase 3: Referral Motivation Analysis
6. **IDENTIFY** what motivates customers to refer:
   - **Altruistic Referrers**: Want to help friends (reward: recognition, no monetary incentive needed)
   - **Self-Interest Referrers**: Want something in return (reward: discounts, credits, exclusive access)
   - **Status Referrers**: Want to be seen as connectors (reward: public recognition, insider access)
   - **Passive Referrers**: Won't actively refer but will share if friction is zero (reward: make sharing effortless)
7. **DESIGN** the referral program structure:
   - Incentive type (monetary, credits, exclusive access, or recognition)
   - Double-sided incentive (referrer + referred both benefit)
   - Referral mechanics (unique links, codes, in-product sharing)
   - Tracking and attribution system
   - Communication cadence (when and how to remind customers to refer)

#### Phase 4: Behavioral Residue Engineering
8. **ENGINEER** built-in visibility mechanisms:
   - Physical artifacts (branded items, certificates, badges visible to others)
   - Digital markers (shareable results, accomplishment graphics, social proof)
   - Community belonging signals (language, symbols, identity markers)
   - "Before & After" documentation that customers naturally want to share
9. **DESIGN** moments of delight that trigger organic sharing:
   - Unexpected bonuses or upgrades
   - Personalized celebrations of customer milestones
   - Community features that create screenshot-worthy moments

### Part C: Testimonial & Proof Extraction System

#### Phase 5: Systematic Proof Building
10. **DESIGN** the testimonial extraction protocol:
    - **Trigger Moments**: When to ask (after first win, after milestone, after renewal, after referral)
    - **Ask Framework**: How to ask to get SPECIFIC, compelling testimonials:
      - "What was the specific problem you were facing before?"
      - "What specific results have you seen?"
      - "What would you tell someone who's on the fence?"
      - "What surprised you most?"
    - **Format Variety**: Text, video, before/after photos, data screenshots
    - **Permission System**: Template for getting publishable permission
11. **CATEGORIZE** proof for strategic deployment:
    - **Outcome Proof**: Specific results with numbers
    - **Transformation Proof**: Before/after stories
    - **Skeptic Proof**: "I was skeptical but..." testimonials (most powerful for cold audiences)
    - **Similar-Person Proof**: Testimonials from people matching key objection demographics
    - **Speed Proof**: "Within [timeframe], I saw..."
12. **BUILD** the proof deployment strategy:
    - Which testimonials belong on the sales page (objection-specific placement)
    - Which become ad creative (social proof ads)
    - Which become case studies (longer-form nurture content)
    - Which belong in email sequences (segment-matched testimonials)

## CREATIVE LATITUDE

Retention is an experience design problem, not just a communications problem. Sometimes the highest-impact retention intervention isn't an email—it's improving the product experience at a critical moment.

Referral programs fail when they treat all customers the same. Segment your referral approach: power users need different incentives than casual users. And some of your best referral sources may not be customers at all—they may be partners, influencers, or community members.

The most powerful testimonials are the ones customers give you unsolicited. When your proof system captures these (monitoring social media mentions, support ticket compliments, NPS survey comments), you get the most authentic and persuasive content.
