## EXECUTION PROTOCOL

### PHASE 1: SHAREABILITY ANALYSIS

Before engineering, understand what makes content spread:

**The 6 Share Motivations (Why People Share):**

| Motivation | Description | Content That Triggers |
|------------|-------------|----------------------|
| **Social Currency** | Makes sharer look smart/informed | Counterintuitive insights, exclusive information, early access |
| **Practical Value** | Helps people the sharer knows | How-to content, useful frameworks, actionable advice |
| **Emotion** | Triggers strong feeling | Awe, anger, anxiety, humor, inspiration |
| **Identity Signal** | Shows who the sharer is | Values-aligned content, tribal markers, aspirational association |
| **Conversation Starter** | Gives something to talk about | Controversial takes, debate-worthy positions, surprising facts |
| **Story Completion** | Finishes a narrative people are following | Updates, resolutions, "what happened next" |

**Diagnose Your Content:**
- Which motivation(s) does this content trigger?
- Is the motivation strong enough to overcome sharing friction?
- Does sharing this make the SHARER look good?

### PHASE 2: VIRAL ELEMENT ENGINEERING

Build in the specific elements that drive sharing:

**Element 1: THE QUOTABLE**
Every viral piece needs a phrase that can stand alone—something people can copy, screenshot, or say aloud.

**Quotable Characteristics:**
- Fits in a tweet (under 280 characters, ideally under 140)
- Makes sense without context
- Sounds smart when repeated
- Has rhythm/memorability
- Says something true that people haven't heard said that way

**Examples:**
- "People don't remember what you said; they remember how you made them feel." (Angelou)
- "Move fast and break things." (Facebook)
- "The best time to plant a tree was 20 years ago. The second best time is now."

**Engineering Process:**
1. Identify your core insight
2. Compress it to one sentence
3. Make the sentence rhythmic
4. Test: Would someone repeat this at dinner?

**Element 2: THE COUNTERINTUITIVE**
Ideas spread when they challenge what people already believe:

| Expected | Counterintuitive |
|----------|------------------|
| "Work harder to succeed" | "Work less to achieve more" |
| "Listen to customer feedback" | "Customer feedback is lying to you" |
| "Hire for skills" | "Stop hiring for skills" |

**The Counterintuitive Formula:**
"Most people believe [common assumption]. Actually, [surprising truth]. Here's why."

**Element 3: THE SPECIFIC SURPRISING FACT**
Numbers and facts that violate expectations spread:

| Forgettable | Viral |
|-------------|-------|
| "Email marketing is effective" | "Email marketing has 4,400% ROI—higher than any other channel" |
| "Meetings waste time" | "The average employee loses 31 hours per month to unproductive meetings" |
| "Sleep matters" | "Sleeping 6 hours instead of 8 for two weeks impairs you like being legally drunk" |

**Fact Selection Criteria:**
- Surprising (violates expectation)
- Specific (exact number, not "many" or "significant")
- Consequential (has stakes)
- Verifiable (can point to source)

**Element 4: THE IDENTITY HOOK**
Content spreads within tribes. Engineer for tribal sharing:

**Identity Hook Types:**
- "This is for [identity group]"
- "If you believe [value], you'll appreciate this"
- "Real [identity] understand this"
- "The [industry] doesn't want you to know this"

**Example:**
A post about work-life balance spreads among parents because sharing it signals "I'm a parent who prioritizes family."

**Element 5: THE UTILITY ANCHOR**
Practical value that solves real problems spreads:

**Utility Formats:**
- "How to [achieve desired outcome]"
- "The [number] things you need to know about [topic]"
- "What I learned from [impressive experience]"
- "The framework for [valuable skill]"

**Utility Test:**
Would someone bookmark this to use later? Would they send it to a friend who needs it?

**Element 6: THE EMOTIONAL SPIKE**
Strong emotion drives immediate sharing:

**High-Arousal Emotions (Drive Sharing):**
- Awe (this is amazing)
- Anger (this is outrageous)
- Anxiety (this is alarming)
- Excitement (this is thrilling)
- Humor (this is hilarious)

**Low-Arousal Emotions (Don't Drive Sharing):**
- Sadness (without anger)
- Contentment (without awe)
- Relaxation

**Engineering Emotion:**
Don't just inform—make them FEEL something. Then channel that feeling toward sharing.

### PHASE 3: PLATFORM OPTIMIZATION

Different platforms have different viral mechanics:

**TWITTER/X:**
- Hook in first line (before the "see more" cut)
- Quotable stands alone
- Thread format for complex ideas
- Visual or no visual (both can work)
- Controversial takes spread faster
- QRT-worthy content (people want to add their take)

**LINKEDIN:**
- First line is everything (before fold)
- Professional identity signaling
- "I learned" and "I failed" formats work
- Practical value for career
- Engagement pods amplify early
- Post length: 150-300 words optimal

**EMAIL/NEWSLETTER:**
- Subject line = open rate
- First paragraph = read rate
- Quotable for forwarding
- Forward prompt: "Know someone who'd appreciate this?"
- Practical value for professional audience

**LONG-FORM (Blog, Medium, Substack):**
- Headline carries most weight
- Social image/preview matters
- Takeaway section for skimmers
- Quotable callouts throughout
- Clear point of view/argument

### PHASE 4: DISTRIBUTION ARCHITECTURE

Engineer the content structure for sharing:

**The Shareable Structure:**

```
HOOK (0-10%): Stops the scroll; creates curiosity gap
↓
PROMISE (10-15%): What reader will get; why this matters
↓
DELIVERY (15-85%): The actual value; the insight
↓
QUOTABLE (embedded): The phrase they'll repeat
↓
LANDING (85-95%): The implication; what this means
↓
SHARE TRIGGER (95-100%): Reason/prompt to share
```

**Share Triggers:**

| Type | Example |
|------|---------|
| **Direct Ask** | "If this resonated, share it with someone who needs it" |
| **Social Proof** | "10,000 people found this useful" |
| **Incomplete Loop** | "Part 2 drops next week—follow so you don't miss it" |
| **Identity Activation** | "Tag a founder who needs to hear this" |
| **Debate Invitation** | "Agree or disagree? I want to hear your take" |

## EXAMPLE OUTPUT 1: LinkedIn Post Viral Engineering

**Context**: Engineering a LinkedIn post about hiring for maximum shareability.

**THE ACTUAL DELIVERABLE:**

---

### VIRAL ELEMENT ENGINEERING:

**Share Motivation**: Social Currency (makes sharer look smart about hiring)

**Quotable**: "Stop hiring for skills. Skills can be taught. Judgment can't."

**Counterintuitive**: Challenges the default "hire for skills" assumption

**Specific Fact**: "73% of failed hires had the required skills. They lacked judgment."

**Identity Hook**: Managers and leaders who hire (large LinkedIn audience)

**Emotional Spike**: Recognition/validation ("I've seen this!")

---

### ENGINEERED POST:

**Hook (first line—before fold):**
I've made hundreds of hiring decisions. My worst hires had the best resumes.

**Body:**
Here's what I learned:

Stop hiring for skills. Start hiring for judgment.

Skills can be taught. Judgment can't.

73% of failed hires had the required skills. They lacked judgment—the ability to make good decisions when there's no playbook.

Think about your last bad hire. They probably COULD do the job technically. What they couldn't do:
• Know when to escalate vs. handle it themselves
• Read political dynamics
• Prioritize ruthlessly
• Admit what they didn't know

None of that shows up on a resume.

So I changed my interview process.

Now I spend 20% on skills (they can or can't—it's obvious fast).

I spend 80% on judgment:
• "Tell me about a time you had incomplete information but had to decide anyway"
• "When have you disagreed with your boss? What did you do?"
• "Describe a rule you broke at work—and why"

My hiring accuracy improved by 60%.

**Share Trigger:**
Hiring managers: What's your go-to question to test judgment?

## EXAMPLE OUTPUT 2: Newsletter/Article Viral Engineering

**Context**: Engineering an article about AI and writing for maximum forward rate.

**THE ACTUAL DELIVERABLE:**

---

### VIRAL ELEMENT ENGINEERING:

**Share Motivation**: 
- Social Currency (sends signal about AI sophistication)
- Practical Value (useful for anyone using AI)
- Conversation Starter (debate-worthy take)

**Quotable**: "AI won't replace writers. It will expose them."

**Counterintuitive**: Flips the "AI replacing writers" narrative

**Identity Hook**: Writers, content creators, knowledge workers

**Emotional Spike**: Anxiety (am I exposed?) + Relief (here's what to do)

---

### ENGINEERED STRUCTURE:

**Subject Line Options (A/B Test):**
A: "AI won't replace writers. It will expose them."
B: "The writers who should fear AI aren't who you think"
C: "What ChatGPT taught me about my own writing"
D: "I asked AI to write like me. Here's what I learned."

**Hook:**
"I asked ChatGPT to write in my voice. It did it perfectly.

That should have been flattering. Instead, it was terrifying.

If an AI can replicate my style, what exactly is my value?"

**Body with Quotables:**
[First quotable callout box:]
> "AI won't replace writers. It will expose them."

[Second quotable callout box:]
> "If AI can easily produce your argument, your argument isn't distinctive enough."

[Third quotable callout box:]
> "The parts of writing AI does well are the parts that were never valuable."

**Utility Section:**
"The AI-Proof Writing Test:
1. Take your last piece of content
2. Feed the same prompt to ChatGPT
3. Compare the outputs

If they're similar, you have a problem—but now you know what to fix.

If yours is obviously better, you know your edge.

The goal isn't to beat AI. It's to do what AI can't:
• Genuine insight from lived experience
• Counterintuitive connections only you can see
• Voice that can't be pattern-matched
• Judgment about what matters"

**Share Trigger:**
"Try the AI-Proof test. Hit reply and tell me what you learned.

If this reframed how you think about AI and writing, forward it to another writer who might need to hear it."

- Recipient will appreciate it (practical value)
