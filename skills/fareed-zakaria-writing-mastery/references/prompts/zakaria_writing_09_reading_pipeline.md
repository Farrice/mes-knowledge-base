## EXECUTION PROTOCOL

### PHASE 1: STRATEGIC READING SELECTION

Not all reading serves writing equally:

**Reading Type Hierarchy for Writers:**

| Type | Value for Writing | When to Prioritize |
|------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Primary sources | Highest | When originality matters; others haven't mined this |
| Expert interviews/conversations | Very High | Reveals thinking process, not just conclusions |
| Books by practitioners | High | Deep methodology and real-world application |
| Academic research | High | Evidence and rigor (but needs translation) |
| Quality journalism | Medium-High | Current events, well-reported stories |
| Industry publications | Medium | Staying current, finding angles |
| Social media/commentary | Low-Medium | Pulse-taking, not sourcing |
| Generic content | Low | Usually time-wasted |

**Selection Filter Questions:**
1. Could this become content? (If not, why am I reading it?)
2. Does this fill a knowledge gap I have?
3. Will this give me something to say that others can't?
4. Is this the best source for this information?

### PHASE 2: ACTIVE READING PROTOCOL

Passive reading produces nothing. Active reading produces content:

**The 4-Highlight System:**

| Color/Mark | Meaning | Future Use |
|------------|---------|------------|
| **Yellow** | Interesting fact or data | Evidence for arguments |
| **Blue** | Quotable passage | Direct citation |
| **Green** | Triggers my own idea | Original content seed |
| **Red** | Disagree/Counterpoint | Contrarian angle |

**Margin Annotation Codes:**
- **!** = Surprising (content fodder)
- **?** = Questionable (investigate further)
- **→** = Connects to [project name]
- **★** = Must use somewhere
- **⟲** = Reminds me of [other source]

**While Reading, Capture:**
1. Facts/data that support your arguments
2. Quotes that say something perfectly
3. Ideas that TRIGGER your own thinking
4. Disagreements that reveal your position
5. Connections to other things you've read
6. Questions the author doesn't answer

### PHASE 4: THE IDEA COMPOST SYSTEM

Not every extracted insight becomes content immediately. Build a system for ideas to develop:

**Idea Categories:**

| Category | Description | Action |
|----------|-------------|--------|
| **Ripe** | Ready to write now | Queue for this week |
| **Developing** | Needs more support/thinking | Add to research list |
| **Seeds** | Interesting but incomplete | File and revisit monthly |
| **Connections** | Links between ideas | Map relationships |

**The Compost Process:**
1. Extract ideas from reading → Add to Idea Bank
2. Weekly: Review Idea Bank, identify what's "ripened"
3. Monthly: Review Seeds, see what's grown
4. Quarterly: Prune dead ideas, harvest mature ones

**Idea Bank Structure:**
```
IDEA: [One-sentence capture]
SOURCE: [Where it came from]
STATUS: Ripe/Developing/Seed
CONNECTIONS: [Related ideas]
FORMAT FIT: [Where this could become content]
NEXT ACTION: [What would move this forward]
```

### PHASE 5: THE CONTENT CONVERSION

Transform extracted material into actual content:

**Conversion Pathways:**

| Input Type | Output Potential |
|------------|------------------|
| Single striking fact | Tweet, hook, opening line |
| Multiple facts on theme | Article section, thread |
| Expert quote | Authority backing, epigraph |
| Personal reaction | Opinion piece, contrarian take |
| Connection between sources | Original synthesis piece |
| Disagreement with source | Response article, counterargument |
| Question raised | Research piece, exploration |

**The 1-to-Many Principle:**
One piece of reading can generate multiple pieces of content:
- The core insight → Long-form article
- A single fact → Tweet or hook
- Your reaction → Newsletter take
- A quote → LinkedIn post with commentary
- A question → Research direction for future piece

**Conversion Template:**
```

1. TWEET/HOOK: 
"[Striking fact or insight in tweet form]"

2. THREAD POTENTIAL:

3. ARTICLE SEED:

What else I'd need: [additional research]

4. CONTRARIAN TAKE:
The common view: [what most people think]

```

### PHASE 6: THE INTEGRATION SYSTEM

Connect new reading to existing knowledge:

**Cross-Reference Protocol:**
When you finish reading something, ask:
1. What does this CONFIRM from other sources?
2. What does this CONTRADICT from other sources?
3. What does this ADD that I haven't seen elsewhere?
4. What THEMES does this connect to in my work?

**Theme Clustering:**
Organize ideas by theme, not just by source:
```
THEME: [e.g., "Remote work effectiveness"]

SOURCES:
- [Source 1]: Says X
- [Source 2]: Says Y
- [Source 3]: Contradicts with Z

SYNTHESIS:
[What I can say by combining these]

CONTENT POTENTIAL:
[What I can write that draws on multiple sources]
```

### PHASE 7: THE WEEKLY WRITING FUEL RITUAL

Systematize the reading-to-writing conversion:

**Weekly Ritual:**

**Monday (15 min): Triage**
- Review week's extractions
- Identify "ripe" ideas
- Assign ideas to content calendar

**Wednesday (15 min): Connect**
- Look for connections between recent reading
- Update theme clusters
- Identify synthesis opportunities

**Friday (15 min): Plan**
- What will I read next week?
- What writing does that reading support?
- What knowledge gaps need filling?

## EXAMPLE OUTPUT 1: Book Extraction

**Context**: Extracting writing fuel from a business book.

**THE ACTUAL DELIVERABLE:**

---

### SOURCE EXTRACTION

**SOURCE**: "The Mom Test" by Rob Fitzpatrick
**TYPE**: Business Book (Customer Research)
**TOPIC**: How to talk to customers without getting lied to

---

**KEY FACTS (Yellow):**
• Compliments are worthless data—people are polite, not honest (p. 12)
• The question "Would you buy this?" produces false positives 80%+ of the time (p. 24)
• Asking about the past ("What did you do last time this happened?") beats future ("Would you use X?") (p. 31)
• 15-minute conversations are often more valuable than hour-long interviews (p. 67)

**QUOTABLE PASSAGES (Blue):**
• "Opinions are worthless. You want facts and commitment." (p. 18)
• "The measure of a useful customer conversation is whether it gives you concrete facts about their lives and world views." (p. 44)
• "If they haven't looked for ways to solve it already, they're not going to look for yours." (p. 52)

**MY IDEAS TRIGGERED (Green):**
• This applies to ALL feedback, not just customer research—when asking for feedback on writing, people are polite, not honest. Need to extract behavior-based feedback.
• "Would you read this?" is the wrong question. "What did you do after reading the last newsletter you actually read all the way through?" is the right question.
• Content creators could use these techniques to understand audience without relying on surveys (which get the same false positives).

**DISAGREEMENTS (Red):**
• Author dismisses quantitative data too quickly—there's a place for surveys IF combined with behavioral data.
• The "15 minutes is enough" claim needs context—for complex B2B, probably not.

**CONNECTIONS:**
• Connects to consumer research prompt I built—same principle of revealed vs. stated preference
• Contradicts most "customer discovery" content that relies on "would you use this?"
• Reinforces Zakaria's map-territory principle—you have to go to the territory

---

### CONTENT CONVERSION

**TWEET/HOOK:**
"Stop asking 'Would you buy this?' Start asking 'What did you do last time you had this problem?' The first gets you lies. The second gets you truth."

**THREAD POTENTIAL:**
1. Why customer feedback lies to you (compliments are politeness)
2. The question that produces 80% false positives
3. How to ask about the past instead of the future
4. The behavioral signals that actually matter
5. Apply this beyond customers—to any feedback

**ARTICLE SEED:**
Headline: "Your Audience Research Is Wrong: What Customer Researchers Know That Content Creators Don't"
Core argument: The techniques that expose customer lies can expose audience lies—and most content creators rely on the same false signals.
Evidence from source: Mom Test principles
Additional research needed: Examples from content creators who've applied this

**NEWSLETTER TAKE:**
"I've been asking my readers the wrong questions. Here's what I learned from a book about customer research that has nothing to do with content..."

**Context**: Reading three articles on the same topic and synthesizing into original content.

**THE ACTUAL DELIVERABLE:**

---

### SOURCE CLUSTER: "AI and Writing"

**Source 1**: "Why AI Will Never Write Like a Human" (Atlantic)
- Key claim: AI lacks lived experience; writing requires suffering
- Evidence: Analysis of AI-generated vs. human prose
- My reaction: Partially agree, but misses that AI can write DIFFERENTLY, not worse

**Source 2**: "The Writers Using AI to Write More, Not Less" (Wired)
- Key claim: Professional writers use AI for drafts, not finished work
- Evidence: Interviews with 12 working writers
- My reaction: This matches my experience—AI as first draft, human as editor

**Source 3**: "AI Writing Tools Are Making Us Worse Writers" (New Yorker)
- Key claim: Reliance on AI atrophies writing skill
- Evidence: Anecdotal + speculation
- My reaction: Disagree—calculators didn't make us worse at math strategy

---

### SYNTHESIS CONTENT

**What No Single Source Says (My Original Take):**
The debate frames AI as replacement OR tool, but misses the third option: AI as MIRROR. AI-generated writing reveals what's generic about your thinking. When AI can easily produce your argument, your argument isn't distinctive enough. AI is a test of originality.

**ARTICLE:**
Headline: "AI Won't Replace Writers. It Will Expose Them."

Core argument: The writers who should be worried aren't the ones AI can replace—it's the ones AI reveals were never saying anything original.

Structure:
1. The replacement debate is wrong (synthesize sources 1-3)
2. What AI actually exposes (my original take)
3. The "could AI write this?" test for originality
4. How to ensure your writing is AI-proof

Evidence:
- From Source 1: AI can mimic style but not insight
- From Source 2: Professionals use AI for generic parts
- From Source 3: The skill atrophy fear (counter it)
- Original: The mirror principle
