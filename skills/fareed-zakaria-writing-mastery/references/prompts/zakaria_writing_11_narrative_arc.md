## Making Arguments Into Stories

## EXECUTION PROTOCOL

### PHASE 1: IDENTIFY THE UNDERLYING STORY

Every argument contains a hidden narrative:

**Narrative Archetypes in Non-Fiction:**

| Archetype | Description | Best For |
|-----------|-------------|----------|
| **The Discovery** | "We thought X. Then we learned Y." | Counterintuitive arguments |
| **The Journey** | "Here's where we started. Here's how we got here." | Historical/developmental pieces |
| **The Battle** | "There are two forces in conflict. Here's how it resolves." | Debates, competitive analysis |
| **The Transformation** | "This used to be. Now it's this." | Change pieces, case studies |
| **The Mystery** | "Something strange is happening. Let's figure out why." | Explanatory pieces |
| **The Quest** | "We're looking for something. Here's how we found it." | Research-based pieces |
| **The Warning** | "Things seem fine. But danger is coming." | Risk/threat analysis |
| **The Revelation** | "There's a hidden truth. I'm going to show you." | Exposé, insight pieces |

**Find Your Story:**
What archetype best fits your argument? The analytical point IS the plot; you're finding its narrative shape.

### PHASE 2: BUILD NARRATIVE TENSION

Stories need tension—a gap between what is and what could be (or what we know and what we need to know):

**Tension Types in Non-Fiction:**

| Type | How It Works | Example |
|------|--------------|---------|
| **Knowledge Gap** | Reader doesn't know something important | "There's one factor no one talks about..." |
| **Expectation Violation** | Reality differs from assumption | "Everything we assumed was wrong." |
| **Stakes Introduction** | Consequences if problem isn't solved | "If this continues, here's what happens..." |
| **Contradiction** | Two true things that seem incompatible | "Both can't be true. And yet..." |
| **Ticking Clock** | Time pressure on a situation | "We have 18 months before this becomes irreversible." |
| **Hidden Antagonist** | An unseen force causing problems | "The real enemy isn't who you think." |

**Tension Placement:**
- Introduce tension EARLY (first 10-20% of piece)
- Maintain tension THROUGHOUT (don't resolve too quickly)
- Resolve tension SATISFYINGLY (your argument IS the resolution)

### PHASE 3: CREATE NARRATIVE MOVEMENT

Stories move forward. So should non-fiction:

**Movement Techniques:**

**Technique 1: The Question Chain**
Each section ends with a question the next section answers—creating pull.
- Section 1: "The problem is clear. But why does it persist?"
- Section 2: "Because of X. But that raises another question..."
- Section 3: "Which leads us to the real insight..."

**Technique 2: The Progressive Reveal**
Each section reveals new information that reframes what came before.
- Section 1: Establish the situation
- Section 2: Add complication that changes understanding
- Section 3: Add twist that changes it again
- Section 4: Final revelation that synthesizes everything

**Technique 3: The Rising Stakes**
Each section increases consequences or importance.
- Section 1: "This affects your daily work"
- Section 2: "But it also affects your career trajectory"
- Section 3: "In fact, it affects the entire industry"
- Section 4: "And ultimately, it changes how we think about X entirely"

**Technique 4: The Zoom**
Start wide (or narrow) and progressively shift scale.
- Wide → Narrow: "The global economy is shifting. Here's how it affects your company. Here's how it affects your role. Here's what you should do Monday."
- Narrow → Wide: "One decision by one manager in 1987. How it changed an industry. How it changed business. How it changed how we think about innovation."

### PHASE 4: STRUCTURE FOR STORY

Translate argument into narrative structure:

**The Classic Three-Act Structure (Adapted):**

**ACT 1: SETUP (20-25%)**
- Hook: Create immediate interest
- Context: Establish the world/situation
- Tension: Introduce the problem/question/gap
- Stakes: Why this matters

**ACT 2: CONFRONTATION (50-60%)**
- Complication 1: First attempt to understand/solve
- Complication 2: Deeper level of the problem
- False Summit: What seems like the answer but isn't quite
- Dark Moment: The deepest complexity; hardest part to resolve

**ACT 3: RESOLUTION (20-25%)**
- Breakthrough: The real insight/solution
- Implications: What this means
- Resonance: What to carry forward

**Example Application:**

*Article: "Why Smart Companies Make Dumb Decisions"*

ACT 1: "Kodak invented digital photography. Then they ignored it. Why do intelligent organizations make obviously bad choices?" (Hook + Tension)

ACT 2: 
- Complication 1: "It's not stupidity—it's organizational structure."
- Complication 2: "But structure alone doesn't explain it—culture compounds the problem."
- False Summit: "So change the culture? Not so fast—there's a deeper issue."
- Dark Moment: "The real problem: success creates the blindness that causes failure."

ACT 3:
- Breakthrough: "Here's the specific mechanism, and here's what breaks it."
- Implications: "What this means for your company."
- Resonance: "The question isn't whether you're smart. It's whether you can see what your success is hiding."

### PHASE 5: CHARACTER AND SCENE

Even analytical writing can use character and scene:

**Introducing "Characters":**
- The example company/person that illustrates your point
- The researcher whose work you're citing
- The archetype reader you're addressing
- You, as a narrator with a perspective

**Using Scene:**
Don't just state facts; place readers IN a moment.

| Exposition | Scene |
|------------|-------|
| "Meetings are often unproductive." | "It's 2 PM on a Thursday. Twelve people sit around a table. Three are talking. Nine are checking email. Nothing will be decided." |
| "The CEO made a bold decision." | "She walked into the board room, looked at the projections everyone had approved, and said, 'We're not doing this.' Silence." |

**When to Use Scene:**
- Opening (hook through immersion)
- Key moments (illustrate pivotal points)
- Examples (make abstract concrete)
- Closing (leave readers in a moment)

### PHASE 6: NARRATIVE TRANSITIONS

Connect sections narratively, not just logically:

**Narrative Transition Types:**

| Type | Function | Example |
|------|----------|---------|
| **The "But"** | Introduces complication | "This seems promising. But there's a problem." |
| **The "And Then"** | Advances sequence | "That solved one issue. What happened next was surprising." |
| **The "Meanwhile"** | Shows parallel development | "While executives focused on X, something else was changing." |
| **The "Which Brings Us To"** | Connects to next point | "This pattern appears everywhere—which brings us to the real question." |
| **The "Turn"** | Signals shift in direction | "Everything changed in 2019." |
| **The "Zoom"** | Changes scale | "To understand why, we need to look at the bigger picture." |

## EXAMPLE OUTPUT 1: Turning Flat Article into Narrative

**Context**: Transforming a standard business article about pricing strategy into narrative structure.

**THE ACTUAL DELIVERABLE:**

---

### ORIGINAL STRUCTURE (Flat Exposition):

1. Introduction: Pricing is important
2. Point 1: Cost-plus pricing
3. Point 2: Value-based pricing
4. Point 3: Competitive pricing
5. Conclusion: Choose the right approach

**Problem**: No forward momentum; could be read in any order; no tension.

---

### NARRATIVE STRUCTURE (Story Shape):

**Archetype**: THE MYSTERY
"Companies are leaving money on the table. Let's figure out why."

**ACT 1: SETUP**

*Hook + Tension*:
"In 2018, a SaaS company raised their prices by 40%. Churn didn't increase. Revenue jumped 60%. Why? They hadn't improved the product. Hadn't added features. Hadn't changed their marketing.

They'd simply stopped being afraid of their own value.

Most companies underprice—not because they don't know better, but because of psychological barriers they don't even see. Here's how to see them."

**ACT 2: CONFRONTATION**

*Complication 1 (The Obvious Solution)*:
"The standard advice: calculate costs, add margin. Cost-plus pricing feels safe. It's also leaving you broke.

Here's why: Cost-plus answers 'What do I need to charge?' It never asks 'What are customers willing to pay?' And those are very different numbers."

*Complication 2 (Going Deeper)*:
"So you switch to value-based pricing. Calculate the value you create. Capture a portion.

But there's a catch: you have to believe your own value calculation. And most founders don't. They look at the number—$10,000/month instead of $500—and flinch.

The barrier isn't math. It's psychology."

*False Summit*:
"Maybe competitive pricing is the answer? Match the market. Don't overthink it.

Except: you're now letting your competitors—who are also afraid of their value—set your price. Fear of fair pricing is contagious. The market normalizes underpricing."

*Dark Moment*:
"Every pricing model has the same failure point: the moment you have to name a number to a real customer. That's where strategies die. Not in spreadsheets. In conversations."

**ACT 3: RESOLUTION**

*Breakthrough*:
"The companies that price well do something counterintuitive: they name a price that makes them uncomfortable, and then they watch what happens.

Most of the time? Nothing bad. Customers accept it. Revenue increases.

The discomfort was a signal—not of the price being wrong, but of the founder's relationship to their own value."

*Implications*:
"Your pricing problem isn't strategic. It's emotional. The spreadsheet already told you what to charge. You just didn't believe it."

*Resonance*:
"Raise your prices. Feel the discomfort. Then notice: the customers are still here. The only thing that changed was you."

## EXAMPLE OUTPUT 2: Narrative Structure for LinkedIn Post

**Context**: Turning a tip about delegation into a micro-narrative.

**THE ACTUAL DELIVERABLE:**

---

### FLAT VERSION:

"Delegation tip: Don't just assign tasks. Explain why they matter and how they fit into the bigger picture. This helps team members feel ownership and do better work."

---

### NARRATIVE VERSION:

"I used to delegate tasks. My team completed them.

Then one day, an employee asked: 'Why are we doing this?'

I realized I'd never told her. I knew the strategy. She knew the spreadsheet. The gap between them was making her work harder, not smarter.

So I changed how I delegated. Not 'Please do X by Friday.' But: 'Here's what we're trying to achieve. Here's why X matters to that. Here's how your piece connects.'

Same task. Same person. Different result.

Delegation isn't about handing off work. It's about handing off understanding.

When people know WHY, they figure out HOW."

**NARRATIVE ELEMENTS:**
- Setup: "I used to" (establishes before-state)
- Tension: The employee's question (gap revealed)
- Complication: The realization (what was wrong)
- Resolution: The new approach (what changed)
- Resonance: "When people know WHY" (the takeaway)

## NARRATIVE STRUCTURE QUICK REFERENCE

**The 5 Questions:**
1. What's the tension? (What gap/conflict/problem?)
2. What's the shape? (Which archetype?)
3. What's the movement? (How does it progress?)
4. What's the resolution? (How does the argument resolve the tension?)
5. What's the resonance? (What do readers carry away?)

**The Minimum Viable Narrative:**
- Before: [How things were / what we believed]
- But: [The complication / new information]
- Therefore: [What this means / what changes]

**Movement Words:**
- "But then..."
- "Which raises the question..."
- "What changed was..."
- "The real issue turned out to be..."
- "This leads us to..."
- "In the end..."
