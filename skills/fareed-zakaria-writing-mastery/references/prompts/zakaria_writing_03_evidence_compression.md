## EXECUTION PROTOCOL

### PHASE 1: EVIDENCE INVENTORY

Before selecting, see everything you have:

**Catalog All Available Evidence:**

| Evidence | Type | Source | What It Proves | Strength |
|----------|------|--------|----------------|----------|
| [Item 1] | Data/Example/Quote/Study | [Source] | [Which claim] | High/Med/Low |

**Evidence Types to Catalog:**
- **Statistics/Data**: Numbers, percentages, measurements
- **Research/Studies**: Academic findings, experiments
- **Examples**: Specific cases, illustrations
- **Quotes/Authority**: Expert statements, notable sources
- **Analogies**: Comparisons that illuminate
- **Anecdotes**: Stories that make it real
- **Logic**: Reasoning from principles

### PHASE 2: EVIDENCE QUALITY ASSESSMENT

Not all evidence is created equal:

**Quality Criteria:**

1. **Relevance**: Does it directly prove your claim?
   - High: Directly proves the point
   - Medium: Supports but requires interpretation
   - Low: Tangentially related

2. **Credibility**: Will readers believe this source?
   - High: Premier source, unimpeachable
   - Medium: Solid but could be questioned
   - Low: Questionable source, requires defense

3. **Recency**: Is this current enough?
   - High: Recent, up-to-date
   - Medium: Somewhat dated but still valid
   - Low: Old enough to question relevance

4. **Memorability**: Will this stick?
   - High: Vivid, surprising, concrete
   - Medium: Solid but forgettable
   - Low: Abstract, dry, generic

5. **Uniqueness**: Is this adding something new?
   - High: Evidence readers haven't seen
   - Medium: Familiar but well-deployed
   - Low: Cliché, overused, expected

**Score each piece of evidence. High-scoring evidence has priority.**

### PHASE 3: CLAIM-EVIDENCE MATCHING

Match evidence to claims strategically:

**The Minimum Viable Evidence Rule:**
For each claim, determine:
- What's the MINIMUM evidence needed for belief?
- What evidence is redundant (proves same thing already proven)?
- What evidence is insurance (backup if primary is challenged)?

**Match Table:**
| Claim | Primary Evidence | Backup Evidence | Evidence to Cut |
|-------|-----------------|-----------------|-----------------|
| Claim 1 | [Best proof] | [If challenged] | [Redundant] |
| Claim 2 | | | |

### PHASE 4: COMPRESSION DECISIONS

Make the hard choices:

**Evidence Elimination Framework:**

| Situation | Decision |
|-----------|----------|
| Two examples prove same point | Keep the more vivid/memorable one |
| Three statistics say similar things | Keep the most striking; mention pattern |
| Quote and data prove same thing | Usually keep data; quotes for unique perspective |
| Old study + new study same finding | Keep new unless old is more famous |
| Complex evidence requiring explanation | Cut if simpler evidence exists |
| Evidence that requires defensive caveats | Cut unless essential |

**The "And Also" Test:**
If you're using "and also" or "furthermore" to add more evidence for the same point, ask: Is the first evidence not sufficient? If it is sufficient, cut the addition.

**The "For Instance" Limit:**
Maximum 2-3 examples per point. More examples ≠ more persuasive. It equals tedious.

### PHASE 5: EVIDENCE DEPLOYMENT

Present evidence for maximum impact:

**Deployment Principles:**

1. **Lead with strongest**: Your best evidence comes first, not last.

2. **Cite concisely**: "A Stanford study found X" not "According to researchers at Stanford University who published their findings in the Journal of..."

3. **Interpret immediately**: Evidence → What it means, no gap.

4. **Vary evidence types**: Data, then example, then quote—not three statistics in a row.

5. **Compress ruthlessly**: "Three separate studies found the same thing" is often better than describing all three.

**Evidence Introduction Patterns:**

| Pattern | When to Use | Example |
|---------|-------------|---------|
| Direct statement | Strong, clear evidence | "Remote workers are 23% more productive." |
| Source attribution | When authority matters | "As Peter Drucker argued..." |
| Pattern observation | Synthesizing multiple sources | "Study after study finds the same result." |
| Contrast setup | Counterintuitive evidence | "The assumption was X. The data shows Y." |
| Specific example | Making abstract concrete | "Consider the case of..." |

### PHASE 6: COMPRESSION EDITING

Final pass to tighten:

**Compression Checklist:**
- [ ] No evidence introduced then never used
- [ ] No evidence that proves point already proven
- [ ] No evidence requiring more explanation than the point it proves
- [ ] No evidence from sources weaker than the argument needs
- [ ] No evidence that could be summarized rather than detailed
- [ ] No evidence that's interesting but doesn't advance the argument

**Compression Techniques:**

| Before | After | Technique |
|--------|-------|-----------|
| "According to a study published in Harvard Business Review in 2022 by researchers Smith and Jones, who surveyed 1,247 managers..." | "A Harvard Business Review study of 1,200+ managers found..." | Cut unnecessary attribution |
| "There's evidence from the auto industry. And also from tech. And also from retail." | "The pattern holds across industries—auto, tech, retail, and beyond." | Consolidate examples |
| [Three paragraphs explaining one study] | [One paragraph with key finding] | Summarize rather than detail |
| "This suggests that X might be true" | "This shows X is true" | Remove hedging if evidence is strong |

### PHASE 4: COMPRESSED EVIDENCE DEPLOYMENT

**DEPLOYED IN ARTICLE:**

*Mechanism evidence:*
"The explanation isn't intuitive. Katherine Phillips, a Columbia researcher, found that diverse teams feel more uncomfortable—and that's precisely why they perform better. Homogeneous teams mistake agreement for correctness. Diverse teams are forced to question assumptions, articulate reasoning, and consider alternatives. The discomfort is the feature, not the bug."

"The financial evidence is striking: Harvard researchers found that venture capital firms with diverse partners saw 26% higher returns than those with homogeneous teams. When the stakes are highest, diversity delivers."

*Example evidence:*
"Pixar's 'Braintrust'—a diverse group that critiques every film—institutionalized this principle. The group's explicit purpose is to surface problems the creators can't see. As Ed Catmull wrote, 'Early on, all of our movies suck. The Braintrust is how we fix them.' The tension is built in by design."

## THE COMPRESSION HIERARCHY

When you have too much evidence, apply this hierarchy:

1. **One perfect piece > Two good pieces**
2. **Data > Quote > Anecdote** (for proving facts)
3. **Anecdote > Data > Quote** (for creating emotion)
4. **Recent > Old** (unless old is more famous)
5. **Specific > General** ("23%" > "significant increase")
6. **Surprising > Expected** (counterintuitive evidence lands harder)
7. **Their source > Your source** (what the audience already trusts)
