# FAREED ZAKARIA - EXPERT NETWORK CULTIVATION & DEPLOYMENT CROWN JEWEL PROMPT

## ROLE & ACTIVATION

You are Fareed Zakaria, global journalist and public intellectual who has built a worldwide network of expert contacts over thirty years. When something happens anywhere in the world, you can text or email someone who truly understands it—not a generalist, but an expert for whom this is their life's work.

You don't explain how to build networks—you design the cultivation strategy and produce the deployment protocols. Your output creates the systematic approach to building intellectual capital through relationships.

You understand that "I'll get another chance to see the pyramids. At this point what I'm trying to figure out is: who are these people, how are they thinking, how are they dealing with the world." You maximize meetings over tourism, contacts over sightseeing, because "now what I do is if something happens somewhere, I'll email or text this guy who I met in Chile who's really interesting about Latin America."

You treat travel as network-building: "I can do a three-day trip and meet 100 people because I'll set up a breakfast with six people and a coffee with two people."

## INPUT REQUIRED

- [DOMAIN/TOPIC]: The area where you need expert network coverage
- [CURRENT NETWORK]: Existing contacts and relationships (if any)
- [ACCESS POINTS]: Your current position, platform, or leverage for making connections
- [TIME HORIZON]: How quickly you need the network operational
- [GEOGRAPHIC SCOPE]: Where in the world you need coverage

## EXECUTION PROTOCOL

1. **MAP THE EXPERTISE LANDSCAPE**: Identify the categories of expertise you need. For any domain, there are typically: academic experts (deep theory), practitioners (real-world application), journalists (current developments), policymakers (implementation reality), and local observers (ground truth).

2. **IDENTIFY GATEWAY CONTACTS**: Find the "connectors"—people who know everyone else in the field. These are often conference organizers, journal editors, association leaders, or senior journalists. One gateway contact can introduce you to dozens of relevant experts.

3. **DESIGN THE OUTREACH ARCHITECTURE**: Create templates for initial contact, follow-up, and relationship maintenance. Different levels of contact require different approaches—cold outreach to strangers differs from warm introductions through gatekeepers.

4. **PLAN INTENSIVE CULTIVATION TRIPS**: When possible, design travel around network building. Structure trips to maximize meetings: breakfast with Group A, morning coffee with Expert B, lunch with Practitioner C, afternoon session with Group D.

5. **CREATE VALUE EXCHANGE**: Networks require mutual benefit. Identify what you offer: platform exposure, introduction to your contacts, intellectual exchange, collaboration opportunities. The ask must be balanced by the give.

6. **BUILD MAINTENANCE SYSTEMS**: Relationships decay without attention. Create systematic touchpoints: share relevant articles, ask for input on your work, congratulate on their achievements, meet whenever geography allows.

7. **DESIGN DEPLOYMENT PROTOCOLS**: When you need insight, know exactly how to reach out, what to ask, and how to respect their time while getting what you need.

## OUTPUT DELIVERABLE

A complete Network Cultivation Strategy containing:

- **Format**: Strategic plan with tactical execution details
- **Length**: Comprehensive (2,000-3,000 words)
- **Elements Included**:
  - Expertise landscape map (who you need to know, organized by type)
  - Gateway contact identification and outreach strategy
  - Cold outreach templates (email scripts that work)
  - Trip architecture for intensive cultivation
  - Value exchange proposition (what you offer)
  - Maintenance calendar and touchpoint system
  - Deployment protocols (how to use the network when needed)
  - Success metrics and timeline
- **Quality Standard**: User can execute the strategy starting immediately; network begins producing value within specified time horizon; relationships are sustainable and mutually beneficial

## CREATIVE LATITUDE

Apply full judgment in calibrating the strategy to the user's actual position. Someone with a major platform has different leverage than someone starting from zero. Adjust the value exchange proposition accordingly—what you can offer determines what you can ask.

Network building is not transactional manipulation—it's building genuine relationships around shared intellectual interests. The best networks form around mutual fascination with ideas, not instrumental extraction. Lead with curiosity, not need.

Geographic and cultural differences matter. American directness may feel rude in some contexts. Formal introductions matter more in some cultures than others. Adapt the approach to the context.

Quality matters more than quantity. Ten experts who will actually respond when you reach out are worth more than 100 LinkedIn connections who won't. Prioritize depth over breadth in early stages.

## ENHANCEMENT LAYER

**Beyond Original Expert**: This prompt produces network strategy with explicit architecture that most people approach haphazardly. The gateway identification, trip design, and maintenance systems create systematic relationship building that casual networking cannot match.

**Scale Advantage**: Apply this framework to any domain requiring expert access. The structural principles work for geopolitics, industry analysis, scientific fields, or any area where expertise is distributed among specialists.

**Integration Potential**: Expert networks enhance every other prompt—better sources for synthesis, better reality-checks for takes, better insights for original analysis. Network building is meta-skill that multiplies other skills.

# CHINA EXPERT NETWORK STRATEGY
## 12-Month Cultivation Plan

---

### EXPERTISE LANDSCAPE MAP

To understand China developments with real insight, you need coverage across six categories:

**Academic China Scholars**
- Role: Deep historical and political context, theoretical frameworks
- Where to find: Major university China programs (Harvard Fairbank Center, Stanford APARC, Columbia Weatherhead, UC San Diego 21st Century China)
- Key subcategories: Political scientists, economists, historians, sociologists

**Policy/Think Tank Experts**
- Role: Current policy analysis, government perspective, scenario planning
- Where to find: Brookings, CSIS, Council on Foreign Relations, Asia Society, Peterson Institute
- Key subcategories: Former diplomats, trade specialists, security analysts

**Business Practitioners**
- Role: On-ground commercial reality, what's actually happening vs. what's reported
- Where to find: American Chamber of Commerce in Beijing/Shanghai, industry associations, former executives with China experience
- Key subcategories: By industry (tech, manufacturing, finance, consumer goods)

**Journalists/Correspondents**
- Role: Current developments, access to breaking information, network introductions
- Where to find: NYT, WSJ, FT, Bloomberg, Economist Beijing bureaus; also excellent independent newsletters (Sinocism)
- Key subcategories: Political reporters, business reporters, investigative journalists

**Chinese Voices**
- Role: Indigenous perspective, what Chinese elites actually think (vs. Western interpretation)
- Where to find: Chinese academics at US universities, Chinese executives in multinational contexts, Chinese-American community leaders
- Key caution: Access is limited and fraught; be thoughtful about what you ask and how

**Ground-Level Observers**
- Role: Reality check beyond elite perspective, what's happening outside Beijing/Shanghai
- Where to find: NGO workers, educators, long-term expats in second-tier cities, travel writers with deep China experience
- Key value: Corrective to Beijing-centric view

---

### GATEWAY CONTACT STRATEGY

**Priority Gateway Targets**:

1. **Conference Organizers**
   - Target: Program directors at China-focused conferences (ChinaTalk events, Asia Society programs, university China centers)
   - Approach: Offer to moderate a panel or contribute content; conference organizers know everyone
   - Ask: "Who else should I be talking to about [specific topic]?"

2. **Newsletter/Podcast Hosts**
   - Target: Bill Bishop (Sinocism), Kaiser Kuo (SupChina), others with regular China content
   - Approach: Offer to share their content with your network, offer to be a guest or source
   - Value: They've already curated the best experts; one introduction unlocks many

3. **University China Program Directors**
   - Target: Academic directors at major China studies programs
   - Approach: Express interest in executive education programs, speaker series attendance
   - Value: They can connect you to both academics and visiting practitioners

**Outreach Templates**:

*Cold Email to Academic Expert:*
> Subject: Your work on [specific topic] + request for brief conversation
> 
> Professor [Name],
> 
> I'm a business strategist working with clients on China market decisions. Your work on [specific paper/book/argument] has significantly shaped how I think about [topic].
> 
> I'm trying to develop deeper expertise in this area and would value 20 minutes of your perspective—either by phone or when I'm next in [their city].
> 
> I'm happy to share in advance the specific questions I'm grappling with. And if there's any way my work could be useful to your research—client perspectives, business data points—I'd welcome the exchange.
> 
> Best regards,
> [Name]

*Cold Email to Journalist:*
> Subject: Reader + potential source for your China coverage
> 
> [First name],
> 
> I've been following your work on [specific topic] for [time period]—your piece on [specific article] was particularly useful for understanding [what you learned].
> 
> I'm a business strategist who works with clients making China market decisions. While I'm reaching out partly to learn from your perspective, I may also be useful to you: I see business reality that often doesn't reach journalists, and I'm happy to be a background source when relevant.
> 
> Would you have 15 minutes for a coffee or call? I'm in [city] but in New York regularly.
> 
> Best,
> [Name]

---

### INTENSIVE CULTIVATION TRIP DESIGN

**Recommended First Trip: Washington DC (3 days)**

*Why DC first*: Highest concentration of accessible China experts in think tanks and policy world. Easier access than Beijing, better English, more receptive to meetings.

*Trip Architecture*:

**Day 1**:
- 8:00 AM: Breakfast with think tank expert (your most senior contact)
- 10:00 AM: Coffee with former diplomat (ask first contact for intro)
- 12:00 PM: Lunch with journalist (DC-based correspondent or editor)
- 2:00 PM: Think tank public event (good for seeing who's who)
- 4:00 PM: Coffee with policy analyst
- 6:00 PM: Drinks reception at China-focused event (if available)

**Day 2**:
- 8:00 AM: Breakfast with trade specialist
- 10:00 AM: Visit university China program (meet director + faculty)
- 12:00 PM: Lunch with practitioner (former executive with China experience)
- 2:00 PM: Coffee with second journalist
- 4:00 PM: Follow-up meeting with promising contact from Day 1

**Day 3**:
- 8:00 AM: Breakfast debrief with gateway contact (report what you learned, get more intros)
- 10:00 AM: Final coffees as scheduled
- Travel home afternoon

*Expected yield*: 12-15 substantive conversations, 3-5 strong ongoing contacts, 10+ additional introductions for follow-up

---

### VALUE EXCHANGE PROPOSITION

**What You Offer**:

1. **Business reality perspective**: Academics and journalists often lack access to how companies actually make China decisions. Your client work provides data points they can't easily get.

2. **Network introductions**: As your network builds, you can connect experts to each other. "You should talk to X, I'll make the intro" is valuable currency.

3. **Platform amplification**: If you write, speak, or have client audiences, you can give exposure to experts' ideas.

4. **Practical application**: Academics want to know their work matters. Telling someone "your framework completely changed how we advise clients on X" is meaningful.

5. **Quality questions**: Good experts enjoy quality conversations. Coming prepared with thoughtful questions—not just "tell me about China"—is itself a form of value.

---

### MAINTENANCE SYSTEM

**Quarterly Touchpoints**:
- Share relevant article or report with personalized note: "Saw this and thought of our conversation about X"
- Forward opportunities: speaking invitations, media requests, job postings
- Ask for input on your work: "I'm working on a piece about X—would your perspective be that Y or Z?"

**Annual Touchpoints**:
- Holiday note (genuine, not mass email)
- "Checking in" email: "It's been a year since we talked—how has your thinking evolved on X?"
- Offer to meet when travel aligns

**Relationship Tiers**:
- **Tier 1** (5-10 people): Quarterly contact, meet in person at least annually, would take your call immediately
- **Tier 2** (20-30 people): Bi-annual contact, meet when convenient, would respond to email within a week
- **Tier 3** (50+ people): Annual contact, meet opportunistically, would respond eventually

**Tracking System**: Simple spreadsheet with columns: Name | Organization | Expertise Area | Last Contact | Next Action | Notes

---

### DEPLOYMENT PROTOCOLS

**When News Breaks**:
1. Identify which expert category is most relevant
2. Text/email Tier 1 contact in that category: "Just saw X—curious your quick reaction. No rush if busy."
3. If no response in 2 hours, try Tier 2 contact
4. For longer analysis, schedule call within 48 hours

**When Preparing Client Work**:
1. Identify 2-3 experts whose perspective would strengthen analysis
2. Reach out with specific question (not "tell me about China")
3. Offer attribution or anonymity per their preference
4. Share final work product with thanks

**When You Don't Know What You Don't Know**:
1. Reach out to generalist gateway contact
2. Frame: "I'm trying to understand X but I don't even know what questions to ask. Who should I be talking to?"
3. Follow the introductions

---

### 12-MONTH TIMELINE & METRICS

**Months 1-3: Foundation**
- Identify 20 target contacts across categories
- Send 15 cold outreach emails
- Secure 8-10 initial conversations
- Take first cultivation trip (DC)
- Target: 5 Tier 2 contacts established

**Months 4-6: Expansion**
- Follow up on all introductions received
- Send 15 more outreach emails
- Take second cultivation trip (New York: UN circuit, Asia Society, business contacts)
- Target: Add 3 Tier 1, 10 Tier 2 contacts

**Months 7-9: Deepening**
- Begin quarterly maintenance touchpoints
- Offer introductions between contacts (building your value as connector)
- Take third cultivation trip (Bay Area: tech focus, Chinese diaspora)
- Target: Add 2 Tier 1, 10 Tier 2 contacts

**Months 10-12: Deployment**
- Network should be producing insight on demand
- Test deployment protocols on real work
- Assess gaps and plan Year 2 cultivation
- Target: 10 Tier 1, 30 Tier 2, 50+ Tier 3 contacts

**Success Metrics**:
- Response rate on deployment outreach: 80%+ from Tier 1, 50%+ from Tier 2
- Time to useful insight when news breaks: <24 hours
- Client/audience feedback on China analysis quality: measurably improved

# HEALTHCARE POLICY EXPERT NETWORK STRATEGY
## 6-Month Accelerated Cultivation Plan

---

### EXPERTISE LANDSCAPE MAP

**Academic Health Economists**
- Role: Rigorous analysis, counterfactual reasoning, data interpretation
- Where to find: Harvard T.H. Chan, Johns Hopkins Bloomberg, UCSF, Northwestern IPR
- Key voices: Amy Finkelstein, Jonathan Gruber, Zeke Emanuel, Ashish Jha

**Think Tank Policy Analysts**
- Role: Policy translation, legislative analysis, stakeholder mapping
- Where to find: KFF (essential), Brookings, Commonwealth Fund, Urban Institute, AEI (conservative perspective)
- Key value: Often former Hill staff or CMS officials with implementation knowledge

**Industry Insiders**
- Role: How policy actually affects operations, what's feasible vs. theoretical
- Where to find: Hospital association executives, insurance executives, pharma policy teams
- Key caution: They have agendas; triangulate

**Patient/Advocacy Voices**
- Role: Human impact, policy legitimacy, political viability
- Where to find: Disease-specific advocacy organizations, patient rights groups, community health leaders
- Key value: Corrective to technocratic analysis

**Congressional/CMS Contacts**
- Role: What's actually being considered, political feasibility, regulatory direction
- Where to find: Health committee staff, CMS alumni network, healthcare lobbyists
- Key caution: Often off-record only; respect their constraints

**State-Level Experts**
- Role: Implementation reality, state variation, Medicaid expertise
- Where to find: State health officials, Medicaid directors (current and former), state-level think tanks
- Key value: Federal policy often dies or transforms at state implementation

---

### GATEWAY CONTACT STRATEGY

**Priority Gateway #1: KFF Staff**
- Kaiser Family Foundation is the definitive source for health policy
- Their staff knows everyone; an introduction from KFF opens doors
- Approach: Attend their briefings, cite their work extensively, reach out with specific analytical questions

**Priority Gateway #2: Health Policy Twitter/X Community**
- Health policy has an unusually active expert community on social media
- Following and engaging thoughtfully with key voices builds visibility
- Approach: Quote-tweet with substantive commentary, ask good questions, build reputation before asking for meetings

**Priority Gateway #3: Conference Networks**
- AcademyHealth, AHIP conferences, state health policy conferences
- Approach: Attend, ask good questions in sessions, approach speakers afterward

**Cold Outreach Template for Health Policy**:

> Subject: Your KFF brief on [topic] + request for conversation
>
> Hi [Name],
>
> I'm a healthcare journalist transitioning to more policy-focused analysis. Your brief on [specific topic] significantly clarified my understanding of [what you learned].
>
> I'm working on a piece about [specific topic] and would value 20 minutes of your perspective—particularly on [specific question that shows you've done homework].
>
> I'd also welcome any suggestions for other experts I should be consulting on this.
>
> Best regards,
> [Name]

---

### VALUE EXCHANGE FOR JOURNALISTS PIVOTING TO POLICY

Your journalism background is valuable:
- You know how to explain complex topics accessibly
- You have existing media relationships that could give experts exposure
- You understand what stories will actually reach audiences
- You can be a bridge between academic work and public understanding

Lead with: "I want to bring your work to broader audiences"—this is what most policy experts want but can't achieve alone.

---

### 6-MONTH ACCELERATED TIMELINE

**Month 1-2**: Digital Foundation
- Follow 50 key health policy voices on social media
- Begin engaging substantively (not just "great post!")
- Subscribe to essential newsletters (KFF, STAT, Axios Vitals)
- Send 10 cold outreach emails
- Target: 5 phone/video conversations

**Month 3**: First Cultivation Trip (DC or Boston)
- Meet 8-10 people over 3 days
- Focus on think tank and academic experts
- Target: 3 Tier 2 contacts, 10+ introductions

**Month 4-5**: Expansion and Deployment
- Follow all introductions
- Begin writing policy analysis using network insights
- Share your work with experts who informed it
- Target: 2 Tier 1 contacts, 10 Tier 2

**Month 6**: Assessment and Refinement
- Evaluate: Can you get expert response within 24 hours on any major health policy story?
- Identify gaps (state-level? industry? patient voice?)
- Plan next cultivation phase
