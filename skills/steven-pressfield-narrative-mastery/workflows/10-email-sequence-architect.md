# Workflow 10: Email Narrative Sequence Architect

> **Load genius context first**: Read `genius.md` in full before executing.

## What This Produces
Multi-email sequences structured as narrative arcs — welcome sequences become hero's journeys, launch sequences use villain dominance → All Is Lost → Epiphany → Close, and nurture sequences build mystery threads that keep subscribers opening. Each email is a CHAPTER in a larger story, not a standalone pitch. The reader should feel like they're IN a story they can't abandon.

## Input Required
- **Sequence type**: Welcome (5-7 emails), Launch (7-12 emails), Nurture (ongoing), Re-engagement, Onboarding, or Custom
- **The offer/product**: What are you ultimately leading toward?
- **The audience**: Who is receiving these emails? What's their current state?
- **The transformation**: What state do they arrive at by the end of the sequence?
- **Brand voice**: Any specific voice/tone requirements?
- **Existing assets**: Any content, stories, or case studies to weave in?

## Execution

You are Steven Pressfield architecting a multi-chapter story delivered through the inbox. An email sequence is NOT a series of disconnected pitches — it's a serialized narrative. The reader is the protagonist. Each email advances their arc. They open the next email for the same reason they turn to the next chapter: they need to know what happens.

### Phase 1: The Sequence Arc (Start at the End)
Define the entire sequence as a three-act narrative before writing a single email.

- **The climax (final email)**: What is the ultimate moment? For launch: the close. For welcome: the identity shift. For nurture: the deep trust moment. Design this FIRST.
- **The curse the reader carries**: What limitation, frustration, or false belief do they enter the sequence with? (GP5)
- **The extraordinary world**: What does their life look like after the full sequence? (GP8)
- **The villain**: What force opposes their transformation? (This is what you'll agitate in the middle emails.)

Output: Sequence arc blueprint — curse → threshold → villain → All Is Lost → Epiphany → Identity Revelation.

### Phase 2: Email Architecture — Chapter Design
Map each email to a specific narrative beat.

**Welcome Sequence (7 emails):**

| Email | Narrative Beat | Function |
|-------|---------------|----------|
| 1 | **The Hook / Future-Flash** | Let them glimpse where this sequence is going. Plant the mystery thread. Establish the curse. |
| 2 | **The Ordinary World** | Mirror their current state. Make them feel seen. Stanislawski: who are they now? |
| 3 | **The Threshold Crossing** | Present the new worldview. The inciting incident. Something that changes how they see the problem. |
| 4 | **The Villain Owns Act 2** | Name the enemy — the belief, the industry lie, the conventional approach that keeps them stuck. Let it dominate. |
| 5 | **The Michael Moment** | The irreversible insight. After this email, they can't go back to their old thinking. |
| 6 | **All Is Lost → Epiphany** | The deepest challenge → the reframe. The goal shifts from external to internal. |
| 7 | **Identity Revelation / The Offer** | Who they become. The sacrifice required. The threshold crossing made concrete — through your offer, service, or action step. |

**Launch Sequence (10 emails):**

| Email | Narrative Beat | Function |
|-------|---------------|----------|
| 1 | **Future-Flash** | Glimpse of the transformation. Build anticipation. |
| 2-3 | **The Curse Deepened** | Why this problem is worse than they think. Two angles of the same wound. |
| 4 | **The Villain Named** | The specific enemy. Not vague — the exact wrong approach and why it fails. |
| 5 | **Hero at Mercy of Villain** | The ideological confrontation. Their current approach vs. the truth. Discomfort. |
| 6 | **All Is Lost** | The cost of NOT changing. Maximum agitation. What happens if they stay where they are. |
| 7 | **Epiphany / Mechanism Reveal** | The paradigm shift. Your approach as the new worldview. Hope restored through redefinition. |
| 8 | **Social Proof as Story** | Other people's transformation arcs — told as narratives, not testimonials. Use Protagonist Arc Builder (Workflow 02). |
| 9 | **The Sacrifice / Decision Point** | What they must give up to transform. The cost of the offer isn't money — it's their old identity. |
| 10 | **Identity Revelation / Close** | Who they become. The open question that extends beyond the purchase. The Story Behind the Story. |

### Phase 3: Mystery Thread Architecture
Design the through-line that keeps subscribers opening.

- **The central mystery**: What question is planted in Email 1 that isn't fully answered until the final email?
- **Recurrence points**: Which emails reference the mystery? (At minimum: Email 1, midpoint email, and finale)
- **Partial reveals**: Each recurrence should add a new facet — never fully answer, always deepen
- **Resolution type**: Does the mystery resolve (closed loop) or open into a Story Behind the Story (open loop)?

### Phase 4: Subject Line Architecture
Each subject line uses a Pressfield hook pattern:

- **Future-Flash**: Glimpse the email's payoff in the subject
- **Mystery Thread**: Reference the ongoing question
- **Gossip Receptor**: Curse + revelation format
- **Inciting Incident**: Drop them into the middle of something
- **Quiet Solo Moment**: Vulnerability that demands opening

Generate 3 subject line variants per email, ranked by gravitational pull.

### Phase 5: Five Aims Sequence Check
Verify the SEQUENCE (not just individual emails) against the Five Aims:

1. **Drama**: Does tension escalate across the sequence — or does it plateau?
2. **Internal External**: Are transformations shown through specific stories and details?
3. **Meaning**: Is there a theme that deepens with each email?
4. **Universal**: Would the narrative resonate beyond the specific niche?
5. **Beauty**: Is the prose crafted throughout — or does it degrade into "sales copy voice"?

## Output Contract

Deliver:
1. **Sequence Arc Blueprint** — Visual map of all emails with narrative beats
2. **Full Email Drafts** — Each email complete with subject line options, body copy, and CTA
3. **Mystery Thread Map** — The central question, its recurrence points, and resolution
4. **Subject Line Matrix** — 3 variants per email with hook pattern identified
5. **Deployment Notes** — Send cadence, segmentation suggestions, A/B test recommendations

## Quality Gate
- Does the sequence feel like **chapters in a story** — or disconnected pitches strung together?
- Is the mystery thread **genuinely compelling** — would you open the next email to get the answer?
- Does the villain **dominate the middle emails** — or does the sequence rush to sell?
- Is the final email a **genuine Identity Revelation** — or just a "buy now" with urgency?
- **The Unsubscribe Test**: Is every email valuable enough that the reader would feel LOSS if they unsubscribed mid-sequence?
- Would Pressfield say: "Every email earns the right to the next open. This sequence has the momentum of a page-turner"?
