# Workflow 13 — Manifesto & Declaration Engine

> **What this is**: Create manifestos, declarations, mission statements, and belief documents using Pressfield's narrative physics. Every manifesto is a compressed hero's journey — curse → threshold → sacrifice → identity revelation — delivered in the language of conviction. For brand manifestos, personal purpose statements, movement-building documents, "about" pages that convert, and founder origin stories.

---

## What This Produces

1. **Core Manifesto** — The primary document (500–2,000 words depending on context)
2. **Compressed Variants** — Tagline, one-liner, and 3-sentence version
3. **Structural Annotations** — Which narrative physics powers each section
4. **Deployment Guide** — How to use across about pages, social bios, pitch decks, email headers
5. **Evolution Notes** — How the manifesto grows as the brand/person evolves

---

## Input Required

- **Who is this for?** — Personal brand, company, movement, product, community
- **What is the curse?** — The injustice, the broken thing, the inherited limitation that drives everything
- **What was sacrificed?** — What was given up to pursue this path?
- **Who is the audience?** — Who needs to hear this? What curse do THEY carry?
- **Format context** — Where will this manifesto live? (Website, deck, social, print, spoken)

## Execution

### Phase 1 — The Curse Diagnosis

Before writing a word, answer:
- **What is fundamentally broken?** — Not a minor inconvenience; the SYSTEMIC failure that drives everything
- **Why does this matter to the author personally?** — The curse must be CARRIED, not observed
- **How does the audience share this curse?** — Same pain, different expression
- **What has been tried before and failed?** — Status quo solutions that don't work

The curse establishes moral authority. Without it, a manifesto is a mission statement. Mission statements are forgettable. Manifestos are not.

### Phase 2 — The Threshold Crossing

Every manifesto documents a moment of irreversible commitment:
- **What was the ordinary world?** — The comfortable, compromised existence before
- **What triggered the crossing?** — The inciting incident that made staying impossible
- **What was the sacrifice?** — Comfort, approval, money, relationships — what was LEFT BEHIND
- **What was gained?** — Not success — CLARITY. The identity that emerged on the other side

Structure this as a compressed narrative — 2-3 paragraphs max. The audience should FEEL the threshold crossing. Not explained, SHOWN.

### Phase 3 — The Belief Architecture

The core manifesto structure:

```
I. THE CURSE (Opening — what is broken)
   ↓ Establish the injustice, the systemic failure, the inherited limitation
   ↓ Let the audience recognize their own version of this curse
   
II. THE ORDINARY WORLD (What most people accept)
   ↓ Describe the status quo and why it's insufficient
   ↓ Name the compromises others make — without judgment, with empathy
   
III. THE THRESHOLD (The moment of no return)
   ↓ The specific moment of decision
   ↓ What was sacrificed to cross
   ↓ What became visible on the other side
   
IV. THE DECLARATION (What we believe / what we do)
   ↓ 5-7 belief statements — each one a gravitational force
   ↓ Each statement should cost something to say (if it's safe, it's not a manifesto)
   ↓ Use parallel structure for rhythm
   
V. THE INVITATION (Join us / here's what's possible)
   ↓ NOT a sales pitch — an extension of the identity
   ↓ "If you carry this curse too, there's a threshold waiting for you"
   ↓ The mystery remains open — the journey isn't over
```

### Phase 4 — The Belief Statements

Each belief statement follows this internal physics:
- **Names a reality** — Not aspiration; something already true
- **Costs something** — If everyone agrees, it's too safe
- **Creates an identity boundary** — People who believe this are IN; the statement itself filters
- **Carries musicality** — Read aloud, it should have rhythm

Examples of Pressfield-quality belief statements:
- "We believe the work is its own reward. If followers, applause, and rankings disappeared tomorrow, we'd still show up."
- "We believe transformation costs something. Anyone who promises otherwise is selling comfort, not change."
- "We believe beauty isn't decoration. It's the vehicle that makes people choose to stay present with hard truths."

### Phase 5 — Compressed Variants

From the full manifesto, distill:

**Tagline** (5-10 words) — The curse + the territory in one breath
**One-Liner** (1-2 sentences) — Curse + sacrifice + identity in one statement
**Social Bio Version** (3 sentences) — Enough to create gravitational pull in a bio or header
**The Spoken Version** (30 seconds) — If someone asks "what do you do?" at a dinner party

Each variant must carry the same gravitational forces as the full manifesto. Compression ≠ dilution.

### Phase 6 — Deployment Notes

Annotate where and how to deploy:

| Context | Version | Notes |
|---------|---------|-------|
| Website "About" page | Full manifesto | Lead with curse, end with invitation |
| Social bio | 3-sentence | Front-load the identity, not the credentials |
| Pitch deck slide | One-liner + 3 belief statements | Visual-first; these are convictions, not bullet points |
| Email signature | Tagline | The simplest expression of territory |
| Spoken introduction | 30-second version | Start with the curse; end with the mystery |
| Team onboarding | Full manifesto + annotations | New hires should feel the threshold they're crossing |

## Quality Gate

Run the Five Aims:
- [ ] **Drama** — Does the manifesto have genuine tension, or is it a pleasant mission statement?
- [ ] **Internal → External** — Are inner beliefs made visible through language, not just explained?
- [ ] **Meaning** — Is there a theme larger than the author/brand?
- [ ] **Universal** — Does this speak beyond the specific niche?
- [ ] **Beautiful** — Is the prose itself worth reading, independent of content?

Manifesto-specific checks:
- [ ] Does this manifesto cost something to publish? (If it's risk-free, it's not a manifesto)
- [ ] Would you REMEMBER any of these belief statements tomorrow?
- [ ] Does the invitation feel like joining a territory, not subscribing to a service?
- [ ] Read aloud — does it have rhythm, or does it stumble?
