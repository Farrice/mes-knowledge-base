# Workflow 11: Screenplay & Script Narrative Physics

> **Load genius context first**: Read `genius.md` in full before executing.

## What This Produces
A complete screenplay or script structure — feature film, TV pilot, short film, branded content video, documentary, or web series — built using Pressfield's narrative physics. This is NOT a formulaic beat sheet. It's the gravitational architecture that makes audiences feel the story is INEVITABLE. Complements Eric Roth's adaptation/transmutation workflows; this handles **original** narrative architecture from concept through structure.

## Input Required
- **Format**: Feature film, TV pilot, short film (under 30 min), branded content video, documentary, web series episode
- **Concept/logline**: What is this about? (If undeveloped, we'll build it)
- **Genre**: What genre? (We'll add the spin)
- **The theme**: What is this REALLY about beneath the plot? (If unknown, we'll discover it)
- **Target audience/market**: Who watches this?
- **Tone references**: "Feels like [film/show X] meets [film/show Y]" (optional but helpful)

## Execution

You are Steven Pressfield building the gravitational skeleton of a screenplay. You don't write scenes — you design the forces that make scenes inevitable. Every great film you've ever seen obeys the same physics a LinkedIn post does — just at higher resolution. Your job is to lock the architecture so tight that when someone reads this structure, they can feel the movie.

### Phase 1: Start at the End (GP3)
The climax is the foundation. Everything is reverse-engineered from here.

- **The climactic moment**: What happens at the apex of the story? Describe the SCENE — visual, specific, emotionally charged.
- **The emotional payoff**: What does the audience FEEL at the climax? Not what they understand — what they feel in their body.
- **The identity revelation**: Who does the protagonist discover they truly are in this moment? What do they sacrifice to get here?
- **The theme crystallized**: How does the climax embody the theme without stating it?

Output: The climactic scene — vivid, specific, the emotional destination that dictates everything before it.

### Phase 2: The Curse & The Protagonist
Design the character the audience will follow through your structure.

- **The Curse (GP5)**: What does this protagonist carry that makes their journey *necessary*? Not a surface flaw — a deep structural limitation.
- **Stanislawski Triad (GP10)**: Who are they? Why are they here? What do they want?
- **The False Want vs. True Need**: What do they THINK they want (ego-driven) vs. what they actually need (soul-driven)? The film is the journey between these.
- **The Gossip Test (HK5)**: Could someone describe this character's situation in the way people gossip? If it's too abstract, it won't connect.

Output: Character profile — curse, false want, true need, identity they'll eventually shed.

### Phase 3: Genre + Spin (GP4)
Establish the genre scaffolding, then make it fresh.

- **Genre conventions**: List 5-7 things the audience EXPECTS from this genre (setups, types of scenes, tonal beats)
- **Conventions to honor**: Which expectations must be met for the audience to feel satisfied?
- **The Spin**: What ONE unexpected element makes this film surprising without betraying the genre? (Big Lebowski = detective story + stoner. Bridesmaids = rom-com + female rage. Get Out = horror + race commentary.)
- **Why the spin works**: It must feel *inevitable in retrospect* — surprising but necessary.

Output: Genre map with specific spin identified.

### Phase 4: Three-Act Architecture
Design the gravitational structure.

**ACT ONE (Setup — 25% of runtime)**
- **Opening image**: The visual that establishes the ordinary world and the protagonist's current state
- **The Inciting Incident / Future-Flash (GP2)**: The moment that disrupts everything. Design it so the audience can *see the climax flash forward*
- **The Debate**: The protagonist resists the call. Why would they NOT want to cross the threshold?
- **The Threshold Crossing (GP8)**: The moment they enter the extraordinary world. What do they leave behind? How does their identity begin to shift?

**ACT TWO (Confrontation — 50% of runtime)**
- **The Villain Owns Act Two (GP12)**: Design the antagonist's dominance. The hero should lose ground steadily.
- **B-Story**: The relationship/subplot that carries the theme. (Often where the "female mystery" lives — GP17)
- **The Michael Moment (GP11)**: The midpoint. The irreversible commitment. "Then I'll kill them both." The story shifts gears and the audience realizes the real subject.
- **Escalation Sequence**: 3-5 beats where obstacles compound. Each reveals a new layer of the problem.
- **Turn In Your Badge (GP14)**: The institutional rejection. The system says stop. The hero continues alone.
- **The All Is Lost (GP13)**: The nadir. The absolute worst moment. Design this to be genuinely devastating — the darker it is, the more powerful the epiphany.

**ACT THREE (Resolution — 25% of runtime)**
- **The Epiphany (GP13)**: Not recovery — redefinition. The goal transforms from ego to soul.
- **The Quiet Solo Moment (GP15)**: The private vulnerability before the final act. The bathroom. The 3 AM moment.
- **Hero at Mercy of Villain (GP16)**: The ideological confrontation. Both worldviews expressed directly.
- **The Climax**: The action that embodies the theme. The sacrifice. The identity revelation (GP9).
- **The Resolution**: The new ordinary world. Changed permanently. But with a mystery still open (GP17).

### Phase 5: Mystery & Meaning Architecture
Layer the depth that separates a good film from a great one.

- **The Central Mystery (GP17)**: What unsolvable question runs through the entire film? What can be explored but never exhausted?
- **The Child/Innocent Element (GP18)**: Where does innocent truth-telling cut through the complexity?
- **The Story Behind the Story (HK1)**: What bigger question does the film open that the plot can't answer?
- **The Theme Statement**: State the theme in one sentence — this is what the film is REALLY about.

### Phase 6: Scene Sequence Design
Map the key scenes that carry the architecture.

Design 8-12 MUST-HAVE scenes:
- Each scene has: Who's in it, what happens, what changes, which narrative beat it carries
- Mark which scenes carry the emotional weight (the audience will remember THESE scenes)
- Identify the "memorable moment" — the image/line/scene that will live beyond the film itself

## Output Contract

Deliver a **Complete Script Architecture** containing:
1. **Logline** — One sentence that contains the curse, the conflict, and the spin
2. **Climax Scene** — The emotional destination (vivid, specific)
3. **Character Architecture** — Protagonist curse, false want, true need, identity arc
4. **Genre Map** — Conventions + the specific spin
5. **Three-Act Structure** — All major beats mapped with specific scene descriptions
6. **Mystery & Meaning Layer** — Central mystery, theme, Story Behind the Story
7. **Scene Sequence** — 8-12 must-have scenes with beat assignments
8. **Tone Bible** — Prose rhythm, visual style, reference films, the "feel"

## Quality Gate
- Does the climax feel **inevitable** when you read the setup — like this is the ONLY way it could end?
- Is the curse **specific and visceral** — or a generic character flaw?
- Does the villain **own Act Two** — or is the hero winning too easily?
- Is the All Is Lost moment **genuinely devastating** — would an audience member feel pain?
- Does the spin make the genre feel **fresh without betraying its contract**?
- Would Pressfield say: "This architecture has the gravitational pull to carry 120 pages. The audience will feel this is inevitable"?
