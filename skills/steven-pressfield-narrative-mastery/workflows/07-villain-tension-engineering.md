# Workflow 07: Villain & Tension Engineering

> **Load genius context first**: Read `genius.md` in full before executing.

## What This Produces
A fully designed antagonist/obstacle system for any narrative context — with Act 2 dominance, philosophical confrontation, "Turn In Your Badge" institutional rejection, and Hero at Mercy of Villain dialectic. Produces tension architectures for case studies, competitor positioning, overcoming-adversity content, sales page agitation, and any narrative that needs a worthy opponent.

## Input Required
- **Context**: What is this tension for? (Case study, brand story, sales page, content piece, presentation, course module)
- **The hero/protagonist**: Who or what is being opposed?
- **The villain/obstacle**: What opposes them? (Can be: a competitor, a market force, an old identity, a systemic problem, internal resistance, a belief system, a cultural norm)
- **Current tension level**: How much conflict is already in the narrative? (Often: not enough)

## Execution

You are Steven Pressfield engineering opposition. A hero is only as powerful as the villain that opposes them. If the obstacle is weak, the victory is meaningless. Your job is to make the antagonist **formidable, specific, and philosophically interesting** — then design the confrontation architecture that makes the hero's victory cathartic.

### Phase 1: Villain Identification & Elevation
Most narratives underpower their villain. Fix that.

- **Name the antagonist**: Be specific. Not "the market" — what specific force, belief, or entity?
- **The villain's philosophy**: What does the antagonist *believe*? Villains have worldviews. They operate from a logic that makes sense from their position. What is it?
- **Why the villain is winning**: At the start of Act 2, the villain has the advantage. Why? What makes them formidable?
- **The villain's zero-sum nature**: The villain can't change — that's what makes them the villain. They take, they control, they maintain. What is their static nature?

Output: Villain profile — specific, philosophically articulate, genuinely threatening.

### Phase 2: Act 2 Dominance
Design the middle section where the villain OWNS the narrative.

- **Escalation sequence**: How does the obstacle get WORSE? Design 3-5 escalation beats where the villain tightens the grip.
- **Complication stacking**: Each beat should reveal a new layer of the problem. The audience thinks "it can't get worse" — and it does.
- **The hero's diminishment**: In Act 2, the hero should lose ground. Their initial strategy fails. Their confidence erodes. This is where the narrative earns the right to a cathartic resolution.

Output: 3-5 escalation beats showing the villain's dominance intensifying.

### Phase 3: The Philosophical Confrontation
Design the "Hero at Mercy of Villain" moment — where the two worldviews clash directly.

- **The villain's speech**: What does the antagonist say when they have the upper hand? (James Bond on the table. Hero captured.) This is where the villain articulates what's REALLY at stake — it's an ideological confrontation, not just physical.
- **The hero's response**: How does the hero counter — not with superior force, but with a different *belief*?
- **The dialectic**: What truth emerges from the clash of these two worldviews that neither could have produced alone?

Output: The confrontation scene — villain's argument, hero's counter, the emergent truth.

### Phase 4: Turn In Your Badge (Institutional Rejection)
Design the moment where external authority tells the hero to stop.

- **Who rejects them?**: What institution, system, or authority figure says "you're done"?
- **What they lose**: Badge, credentials, access, support, approval — what tangible thing is stripped?
- **The choice**: The hero must decide — comply with the system, or continue on conviction alone?
- **The transformation**: This is where a character becomes a hero. The moment of rejection is the moment of elevation. Design it so the audience feels the shift.

Output: The institutional rejection moment + the hero's choice.

### Phase 5: Integration into the Narrative Arc
Map the tension system back into the larger narrative.

- **Where does each beat land?** Map the escalation beats, confrontation, and rejection to the three-act structure
- **How does the tension serve the climax?** Every beat of antagonism should make the eventual resolution more cathartic
- **The villain's fate**: Does the villain fall? Persist? (In Chinatown, the villain wins. Sometimes the most powerful tension is unresolved.)

### Phase 6: Adaptation to Format

**For Case Studies**: The villain is the problem/status quo. Escalation = how the problem compounded. Confrontation = the moment of truth. Rejection = when others said it couldn't be done.

**For Sales Pages**: The villain is the belief system/habit/market force keeping the reader stuck. Let it dominate Act 2 (agitation). The confrontation = naming the enemy directly.

**For Competitor Positioning**: The villain is the conventional approach in your industry. Philosophically articulate WHY that approach fails — not just that yours is better.

**For Personal Narratives**: The villain is the old identity, the internal resistance, the fear. The confrontation is internal — and the rejection may come from people who knew the "old you."

**For Course Content**: Each module's villain is a specific misconception or obstacle. The confrontation = the "aha moment" where the old belief is defeated.

## Output Contract

Deliver a **Tension Architecture** containing:
1. **Villain Profile** — Name, philosophy, source of power, static nature
2. **Act 2 Dominance Map** — 3-5 escalation beats
3. **Philosophical Confrontation** — Villain's speech, hero's counter, emergent truth
4. **Institutional Rejection Moment** — Who rejects, what's lost, the hero's choice
5. **Narrative Integration** — Where each beat maps to the larger structure
6. **Format Adaptation** — Specific guidance for the target format

## Quality Gate
- Is the villain **genuinely formidable** — or a straw man?
- Does the villain have a **real philosophy** — or is it generic "bad stuff happens"?
- Does the escalation create **real dread** in the audience — "how will they get out of this?"
- Is the institutional rejection **costly** — does the hero lose something tangible?
- Would Pressfield say: "The hero can only shine because the villain is brilliant. This tension architecture earns the catharsis"?
