# Workflow 06: Brand Narrative Builder

> **Load genius context first**: Read `genius.md` in full before executing.

## What This Produces
A complete brand narrative — the foundational story that sits underneath every piece of content, every sales page, every about page, every manifesto, and every pitch. Uses Genre + Spin, the Extraordinary World threshold, Mystery Architecture (the "female" element), and Identity Revelation to build a brand story with the gravitational pull of a great novel.

## Input Required
- **Brand/company/person**: Who is this narrative for?
- **Current positioning**: How does the brand currently describe itself? (Or is this from scratch?)
- **Audience**: Who does this brand serve?
- **The transformation the brand enables**: What does the audience become?
- **Competitive landscape**: Who else operates in this space? What do they sound like?
- **Output format**: About page, manifesto, brand bible section, pitch deck narrative, investor story?

## Execution

You are Steven Pressfield applying narrative physics to brand architecture. A brand is a protagonist. It carries a curse. It crosses a threshold. It sacrifices something. It reveals its true identity. And at its heart, it pursues a mystery it can never fully solve. THAT is what makes a brand narrative unforgettable.

### Phase 1: The Brand's Curse
Every brand worth following was born from a problem the founder couldn't tolerate.

- **The origin wound**: What frustration, failure, or injustice created this brand?
- **Why it's a curse**: This isn't just a business opportunity — it's something the founder *can't stop* pursuing. Like Shane can't escape his gunfighting past.
- **The universal resonance**: How does this curse mirror what the audience carries?

Output: The brand's origin stated as a curse — specific, visceral, pattern-matchable.

### Phase 2: The Ordinary World vs. Extraordinary World
Position the brand as the threshold.

- **The ordinary world**: What does the audience's world look like without this brand? (Status quo, frustration, settling)
- **The extraordinary world**: What does it look like AFTER they cross? (Not features — *identity shift*)
- **The threshold moment**: What is the specific crossing point? The moment of "I'm in"?
- **How the world sees them differently**: After crossing, even other people notice the change

Output: Two-world contrast + the threshold moment.

### Phase 3: Genre + Spin
Identify what "type" of brand this is — then make it fresh.

- **Brand genre**: Is this the rebel? The sage? The disruptor? The craftsman? The guide?
- **Conventions to honor**: What does the audience expect from a brand in this category?
- **The Big Lebowski Spin**: What ONE unexpected element makes this brand defy the genre while still delivering its core value? (The stoner detective. The irreverent expert. The luxury brand that swears.)
- **Why the spin works**: It must feel *necessary*, not forced

Output: Genre identification + the specific differentiating spin.

### Phase 4: Mystery Architecture (The Female Element)
At the heart of every great brand is an unsolvable question it pursues publicly.

- **The brand's unsolvable question**: What does this brand explore that can never be fully answered? (Apple: what happens when technology meets humanity? Nike: what are the limits of human potential?)
- **How every piece of content is a chapter**: Each content piece should explore a different facet of this central mystery
- **The depth test**: Can this question sustain years of content, products, and conversations? If it can be "answered" — it's not deep enough.

Output: The central mystery + how it generates infinite content angles.

### Phase 5: The Brand's Sacrifice
What does this brand REFUSE to do — at genuine cost?

- **The sacrifice**: What profitable, easy, or popular thing does this brand reject? (This is what creates faith)
- **Why it costs something real**: Not performative — genuinely challenging
- **How the audience sees themselves in the sacrifice**: The brand refuses what the audience wishes THEY could refuse

Output: The sacrifice statement — specific, costly, belief-generating.

### Phase 6: Identity Revelation (The Brand's True Self)
The climactic statement of what this brand IS.

- **The true identity**: Not the tagline — the *soul*. Who is this brand when the positioning and marketing fall away?
- **The Stanislawski**: Who is this brand? Why is it here? What does it want — REALLY?
- **The statement**: One paragraph that captures the brand's deepest identity. The thing that makes someone say "I believe in this."

Output: The identity statement.

### Phase 7: Story Behind the Story
What is the larger vision that transcends any single product?

- **The bigger narrative**: What is this brand building toward that's larger than commerce?
- **The open ending**: The brand narrative should NOT close neatly. It should open into something larger — a vision, a movement, a truth. The audience should feel like they're joining a story that's still being written.

Output: The larger vision + the open question the brand lives in.

## Output Contract

Deliver a **Complete Brand Narrative** containing:
1. **The Curse** — Origin wound (1 paragraph)
2. **Two Worlds** — Ordinary vs. Extraordinary + threshold moment
3. **Genre + Spin** — Category identification + differentiating element
4. **The Central Mystery** — The unsolvable question that generates infinite content
5. **The Sacrifice** — What the brand refuses, at cost
6. **The Identity** — The deepest truth about who this brand is
7. **The Larger Vision** — The story beyond the story
8. **Deployment Guide** — How to adapt this narrative for: About page, manifesto, pitch deck, social bio, investor presentation, team onboarding

## Quality Gate
- Does the brand feel like a **protagonist** — not a business?
- Is the mystery **genuinely unsolvable** — or just an unanswered question?
- Is the sacrifice **costly** — or performative?
- Does the narrative **open** at the end — or close neatly? (Great brand stories never end. They invite you in.)
- Would Pressfield say: "This brand has a story worth telling — and worth following"?
