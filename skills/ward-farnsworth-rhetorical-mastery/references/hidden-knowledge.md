# Ward Farnsworth — Hidden Knowledge

Tacit expertise the expert demonstrates but doesn't explicitly teach. These are the crown jewel insights—the things you'd only learn by apprenticing and analyzing their subtext.

## Structural Priming (The Announcer Effect)
Repetition at the start of sentences (Anaphora) isn't just about hammering a repeated phrase. It's a subliminal signal telling the brain to "listen up" because the *end* of the paragraph is going to contain the "goal" or the most important point. It operates exactly like sports announcers getting progressively louder as a player approaches the goal line. You are priming the ear for the finale.

## The "Wisdom" Illusion
Structuring a sentence in a perfectly balanced way (like a Chiasmus) artificially grants it the "hue of wisdom." Things that are beautifully constructed sound inherently more true, circumventing the reader's critical defense mechanisms. When a sentence maps to the math of the universe, the brain assumes the *idea* inside the sentence is also an undeniable truth. This is the sword and shield of rhetoric.

## The "Chord Change" Imperative
Writing entirely in simple, short, blunt words (as many copywriting gurus suggest) gets tedious and flat. The true masters (like Churchill and Lincoln) know that power comes from the *change* in tone. Like a musical chord progression, you must set up the simple words with complex words. The ear only detects difference. If everything is loud, nothing is loud. The Latinate words build the intellectual tension that the Saxon words shatter.
