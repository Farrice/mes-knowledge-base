## EXECUTION PROTOCOL

1. **FRICTION AUDIT**: Map every current sharing pathway. Count steps, clicks, decisions, awkward moments. Identify the "leak points" where potential shares die.

2. **SNEEZER PSYCHOLOGY ANALYSIS**: Understand what makes sharing feel risky, awkward, or effortful for this specific sneezer type. What social dynamics are at play?

3. **RECIPIENT PSYCHOLOGY ANALYSIS**: Understand what makes receiving feel suspicious, overwhelming, or forgettable for this specific recipient type. What barriers exist?

4. **IDEAL STATE DESIGN**: Design the theoretically perfect transmissionâ€”minimum viable sharing that preserves the idea's power. What would zero-friction look like?

5. **PRACTICAL REDESIGN**: Create implementable improvements that move toward the ideal state given real constraints. Prioritize by impact vs. effort.

6. **MAGIC WORDS ENGINEERING**: Craft the exact language sneezers should use. What's the shortest, most compelling thing they can say that transmits the idea and motivates action?

7. **SHARE TRIGGER OPTIMIZATION**: Identify the moments when sharing impulse is highest and design mechanisms to capture those moments.

8. **MEASUREMENT FRAMEWORK**: Design tracking to measure sharing friction and improvement over time.

## CREATIVE LATITUDE

See friction that others accept as inevitable. The most impactful smoothness improvements often require challenging assumptions about "how things are done" in an industry.

Be willing to recommend radical simplification even when it means losing features. A share button that nobody clicks is worse than no button at allâ€”it creates the illusion of shareability while actually demonstrating that nobody shares.

Where you see opportunities to make sharing AUTOMATIC rather than optional (like Hotmail's signature line), pursue them aggressively. The smoothest share is one that happens without conscious choice.

## PSYCHOLOGY ANALYSIS

### Sneezer Psychology (User Who Might Share)

**Barriers to Sharing:**
1. **Social Risk:** "What if they think I'm showing off?" Sharing courses can feel like humble-bragging about self-improvement.
2. **Relevance Uncertainty:** "I don't know if this would interest them." Users hesitate when unsure of recipient's interest.
3. **Effort Aversion:** "I'll do it later" = never. If sharing isn't instant, it doesn't happen.
4. **Timing Mismatch:** Users often think about sharing mid-lesson when sharing isn't available.

**Sharing Motivations (Underutilized):**
- Helping a friend who expressed relevant need
- Celebrating completion (achievement display)
- Signaling identity ("I'm someone who learns X")

### Recipient Psychology (Friend Who Receives Share)

**Barriers to Action:**
1. **Unknown Value:** Receiving a course link tells recipient nothing about why it matters to them
2. **Commitment Fear:** Courses feel like a big commitment. "Is this a 40-hour thing?"
3. **Trust Gap:** "Is this actually good or did they just send me spam?"
4. **Friction Anticipation:** "I'll have to create an account, probably give credit card..." = later = never

## IDEAL STATE DESIGN

**Theoretically Perfect Share (Zero Friction):**
1. At peak emotional moment (completing a lesson, achieving insight), user sees exactly one option: "Send this to [specific friend who'd benefit]"
2. One tap sends a personalized, pre-written message through a channel that's already open
3. Recipient receives message with: why it's relevant to THEM, social proof, and a zero-commitment preview
4. Recipient one-taps to experience value without signup
5. Value experience converts recipient to signup

**This is the target. Every redesign moves toward this.**

### High-Impulse Moments (When to Prompt Sharing)

1. **Lesson Completion:** Immediate post-accomplishment endorphins
2. **"Aha" Moment Detection:** User highlights, rewatches, or pauses significantly

- Login/navigation (transactional mindset)
