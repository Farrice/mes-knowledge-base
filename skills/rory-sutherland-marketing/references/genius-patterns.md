## GENIUS PATTERNS DECODED

### Pattern 1: The Doorman Fallacy Detection
**What He Does Unconsciously**: Sutherland automatically scans any proposed efficiency improvement for hidden value destruction. When someone says "we can automate X and save Y," his brain immediately maps all the tacit functions X performs beyond its nominal definition.

**Executable Behavior**: Before approving any cost-cutting or automation initiative, list 10+ functions the human/process performs beyond its stated purpose. Include: security, status signaling, personalization, exception handling, emotional labor, relationship building, information gathering, and brand perception.

**Deployment Context**: Any operational efficiency proposal, automation decision, or headcount reduction analysis.

**Success Metric**: Identify at least 3 hidden value-creation functions that would be destroyed by the "efficiency" measure.

---

### Pattern 2: The Psychological Reframe
**What He Does Unconsciously**: When presented with any business problem, Sutherland's first move is to ask "what if the problem isn't what we think it is?" He automatically translates technical/operational problems into psychological/perceptual ones.

**Executable Behavior**: Take any stated business problem and generate 3 alternative framings:
1. What if customers don't want what we think they want?
2. What if the feeling matters more than the feature?
3. What if the problem is trust/status/identity, not utility?

**Deployment Context**: Product development, marketing strategy, customer experience design, competitive positioning.

**Success Metric**: At least one reframe reveals a solution 10x cheaper or more effective than the original approach.

---

### Pattern 3: The Human Proxy Heuristic
**What He Does Unconsciously**: Sutherland recognizes that humans use personal trust assessments as proxies for complex decisions they can't directly evaluate. The vicar vs. underpants-guy car example reveals this: we evaluate the seller to evaluate the product.

**Executable Behavior**: For any purchase decision or trust moment, identify what "proxy evaluation" the customer is actually performing. Then optimize for winning the proxy evaluation, not just the nominal one.

**Deployment Context**: Sales processes, hiring, B2B relationships, high-consideration purchases, any situation where technical quality is hard to assess.

**Success Metric**: Conversion improvement by focusing on trust signals rather than feature communication.

---

### Pattern 4: The Transaction Utility Lens
**What He Does Unconsciously**: Sutherland sees every purchase as having two values—the utility of the thing AND the utility of the transaction itself. The "beach beer" thought experiment (paying more for identical beer from a hotel vs. shack) reveals that HOW something is bought affects its perceived value.

**Executable Behavior**: For any product/service, analyze and optimize both:
1. Product utility (what it does)
2. Transaction utility (how the buying experience feels)
Design the purchase experience to maximize feeling of good deal, appropriate treatment, and status-appropriate exchange.

**Deployment Context**: Pricing strategy, retail experience design, service delivery, luxury positioning.

**Success Metric**: Customers willing to pay premium for identical functional utility due to transaction experience.

---

### Pattern 5: The Costly Signal Detector
**What He Does Unconsciously**: Sutherland immediately identifies where "expensive" isn't a bug but a feature. Certain goods MUST be expensive to perform their social/psychological function. The Louis Vuitton bag isn't overpriced—its price IS the product.

**Executable Behavior**: For any premium product, identify which of these costly signal functions it serves:
- Status display to others
- Self-affirmation ("because I'm worth it")
- Generosity signaling (gifts, hospitality)
- Commitment signaling (engagement rings)
- Quality proxy (expensive = must be good)
Then ensure price and presentation support the signal, not undermine it.

**Deployment Context**: Luxury goods, professional services, B2B enterprise sales, premium tier design.

**Success Metric**: Price increase that IMPROVES rather than decreases demand.

---

### Pattern 6: The Choice Architecture Awareness
**What He Does Unconsciously**: Sutherland knows people can't choose in isolation—they need comparison sets. The "I'm feeling lucky" button failure, the real estate decoy house, the necessity of showing 3-5 options rather than one "optimal" choice.

**Executable Behavior**: Never present a single recommendation. Always structure choices to:
1. Provide a contrast that makes preferred option obvious
2. Include a decoy that's slightly inferior and slightly more expensive
3. Give customer the psychological satisfaction of choosing
4. Create clear "winner" through strategic comparison

**Deployment Context**: Sales presentations, product line design, proposal structure, pricing tiers.

**Success Metric**: Higher conversion rates and higher average order value through choice architecture.

---

### Pattern 7: The Brand Quake Recognition
**What He Does Unconsciously**: Sutherland recognizes certain customer interactions are "multiplicative, not additive"—they can multiply brand perception by 10x or reduce it to zero. A single call center experience can override years of advertising.

**Executable Behavior**: Map all customer touchpoints and categorize:
- Additive touchpoints (contribute incrementally)
- Multiplicative touchpoints (can transform total perception)
Invest disproportionately in multiplicative moments: problem resolution, exception handling, personal recognition, going above expectations.

**Deployment Context**: Customer experience prioritization, budget allocation, service design.

**Success Metric**: Identify and invest in the 20% of touchpoints that drive 80% of brand perception.

---

### Pattern 8: The Private Company Advantage
**What He Does Unconsciously**: Sutherland immediately identifies whether a company is optimizing for customer value (private/founder-led) or shareholder metrics (public/finance-controlled). He attributes systematic marketing superiority to the former.

**Executable Behavior**: When analyzing competitors or designing strategy, identify:
- Who controls the company (family/founder vs. institutional investors)?
- What is the effective time horizon (decades vs. quarters)?
- Is finance or customer the primary constituency?
Then exploit the gaps public competitors create by their short-termism.

**Deployment Context**: Competitive strategy, M&A analysis, investment decisions, marketing budget justification.

**Success Metric**: Identify market opportunities created by competitors' short-term optimization.

---

### Pattern 9: The Fat-Tail Opportunity Spotter
**What He Does Unconsciously**: Sutherland understands marketing is "fat-tailed"—5-10% of ideas generate 110% of value. He resists averaging and looks for the asymmetric bet, the idea that can "score 1000" rather than consistently scoring 1.

**Executable Behavior**: When evaluating marketing opportunities, ask:
- What's the maximum upside if this works spectacularly?
- Is this a "baseball" opportunity (max 4 runs) or a "business" opportunity (potential 1000)?
- Am I averaging down to mediocrity or making room for asymmetric outcomes?
Allocate budget to preserve potential for breakthrough, not just incremental optimization.

**Deployment Context**: Marketing budget allocation, creative development, innovation investment.

**Success Metric**: Portfolio includes genuine "moonshot" bets alongside consistent performers.

---

### Pattern 10: The Evolutionary Psychology Anchor
**What He Does Unconsciously**: Sutherland grounds every insight in evolutionary psychology. He asks "what problem was this behavior solving for our ancestors?" and uses that to predict and influence modern behavior.

**Executable Behavior**: For any customer behavior or decision, trace it to evolutionary origins:
- Conflict detection (we evolved to notice fights)
- Trust assessment (critical for survival)
- Status evaluation (determines mating and alliances)
- Loss aversion (losses mattered more than gains)
- Social proof (safety in numbers)
Use these deep drives, not surface preferences, to shape messaging and experience.

**Deployment Context**: Messaging development, product design, UX decisions, pricing psychology.

**Success Metric**: Marketing that works because it aligns with evolved instincts, not learned preferences.

## HIDDEN KNOWLEDGE REVEALED

### Tacit Knowledge 1: "Feels Like Temperature" for Business
**The Hidden Insight**: Just as weather reports now show "feels like" temperature (combining humidity, wind, actual temp), every business metric has a "feels like" equivalent. Customer satisfaction isn't about objective service level—it's about perceived service level. The Royal Mail discovery: service quality metrics had no correlation with customer satisfaction; relationship with the postman did.

**Now Deployable**: For any metric you're optimizing, identify its "feels like" equivalent and optimize for that instead.

---

### Tacit Knowledge 2: The Psychopath Detection Protocol
**The Hidden Insight**: Humans have evolved sophisticated mechanisms to detect untrustworthiness. Things like "turning up slightly late" or "minor inconveniences" force genuine responses that psychopaths can't fake. Jeffrey Miller's theory: women create small tests to surface authentic reactions.

**Now Deployable**: In hiring, partnerships, and high-stakes relationships, create small friction points that reveal authentic character. Watch for disproportionate reactions to minor inconveniences.

---

### Tacit Knowledge 3: The Differentiation Imperative
**The Hidden Insight**: Benchmarking against your most obvious competitor guarantees you become a worse version of them. Jaguar tried to compete with BMW/Mercedes/Audi on their terms and was always going to lose due to scale effects. Differentiation benefits everyone: investors (variety reduces correlation), companies (can achieve margins), customers (more choice).

**Now Deployable**: Never ask "how do we beat competitor X at their game?" Ask "what game could we play where we're the only player?"

---

### Tacit Knowledge 4: The Call Center Revelation
**The Hidden Insight**: The call center is where you discover problems customers can't solve anywhere else. It's the highest-leverage customer intelligence source. Dyson's James Dyson reframed call center metrics from "operational efficiency" to "we should treat it as an honor when customers contact us."

**Now Deployable**: Move the call center (literal or metaphorical) to the center of the organization. Pay call center staff like they're critical, because they are.

---

### Tacit Knowledge 5: The Rationality Bronze Standard
**The Hidden Insight**: In most business contexts, rationality is the gold standard—if you can argue it logically, you win. In advertising (and entrepreneurship, and innovation), rationality is the bronze standard—it's where you START, not where you finish. The job is to find something better than the rational solution.

**Now Deployable**: Use rational analysis to establish baseline, then actively search for solutions that are better than rational—emotionally resonant, memorable, delightful, or surprising.
