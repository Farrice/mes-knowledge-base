# David McRaney — Genius Patterns

## 1. The Resistance Hierarchy
**What They Do**: Categorize beliefs by type before attempting change — facts, attitudes, values, identity — because each requires fundamentally different intervention.

**Executable Behavior**: Before any persuasion attempt, classify the target belief. Facts update with evidence. Attitudes require emotional/experiential shift. Values need social permission + identity bridging. Identity beliefs require full paradigm reconstruction with face-saving narrative.

**Deploy When**: Planning any persuasion, copy, or change campaign.

**Success Metric**: You can name the precise belief type and choose the correctly-matched intervention.

---

## 2. Accommodation vs. Assimilation Detection
**What They Do**: Distinguish between real belief change (accommodation — restructuring mental models) and fake belief change (assimilation — fitting new info into existing models without actually changing).

**Executable Behavior**: After any persuasion attempt, test whether the person *restructured* their thinking or merely *absorbed* your argument into their existing framework. Ask: "What changed about how you see this?" If they repeat your argument back, that's assimilation. If they describe seeing the whole landscape differently, that's accommodation.

**Deploy When**: Evaluating whether your content, copy, or conversations are creating real change or just getting nods.

**Success Metric**: You can identify the specific mental model that shifted, not just agreement with your conclusion.

---

## 3. The 9-Word Pattern Interrupt
**What They Do**: Open conversations with a disarming reset that establishes honesty and destroys the adversarial frame before it forms. "I'm not sure this is the right thing for you."

**Executable Behavior**: Start persuasive conversations by genuinely questioning whether your offering fits. This triggers a psychological reversal — the other person stops defending and starts evaluating alongside you. Never fake this; genuine uncertainty about fit is required.

**Deploy When**: Any sales conversation, consultation, or pitch where the default frame is adversarial (seller vs. buyer).

**Success Metric**: The prospect drops defensiveness within 30 seconds and begins exploring rather than defending.

---

## 4. Processing Chain Reverse-Engineering
**What They Do**: Instead of addressing current beliefs directly, trace the *chain* of experiences, emotions, and social pressures that built the belief in the first place.

**Executable Behavior**: For any target belief, map backwards: What was the origin experience? What emotional reaction formed? What social group reinforced it? What identity integrated it? What evidence was recruited to support it? Interventions that address the chain work; arguments against the conclusion don't.

**Deploy When**: When someone holds a belief that seems immune to evidence — the belief is downstream of a processing chain you haven't mapped yet.

**Success Metric**: You can narrate exactly how someone arrived at their position, step by step, before you attempt to change it.

---

## 5. Social Death Calculation
**What They Do**: Calculate the *social cost* of changing a belief before asking for the change. Most persuasion fails not because the argument is weak but because the social price of agreeing is too high.

**Executable Behavior**: For any change you're requesting, map: Who knows they hold this position? What group membership depends on it? What face would they lose? What relationships are at risk? Only ask for change if you've either reduced the social cost or built enough social safety to absorb it.

**Deploy When**: Someone agrees with your logic privately but won't change publicly. This is always a social safety problem.

**Success Metric**: You can quantify the social risk score (1-10) across four dimensions: tribal, face, relationship, and status.

---

## 6. The Pluralistic Ignorance Exploit
**What They Do**: Identify situations where a group privately disagrees with a norm but nobody speaks up because everyone assumes everyone else agrees. Then deliberately break the false consensus.

**Executable Behavior**: Look for the gap between private sentiment and public behavior. If people think differently than they act, you're looking at pluralistic ignorance. Break it by: (1) making the private sentiment visible, (2) creating safe spaces for honest expression, (3) providing social proof that others feel the same way.

**Deploy When**: Markets where "everyone knows but nobody says" — the thing everyone secretly thinks but nobody voices creates massive demand when you name it.

**Success Metric**: People express relief when you articulate what they've been thinking — "Finally, someone said it."

---

## 7. Identity Bridge Construction
**What They Do**: Build a narrative path that allows someone to change without having to destroy their self-concept. "I'm the kind of person who X, and that's exactly why Y makes sense now."

**Executable Behavior**: Identify the positive identity traits the person holds. Find the version of those traits that *supports* the change you want. Construct a continuity narrative: "You're [identity trait they value], and because of that, [change] is actually the most consistent thing you could do."

**Deploy When**: The change you're requesting threatens who someone believes they are. (This is most high-stakes changes.)

**Success Metric**: The person describes the change as evolution, not defeat — using their own values as justification.

---

## 8. Anxiety-Is-the-Real-Objection Detection
**What They Do**: Recognize that most "objections" in sales and persuasion are surface rationalizations for deeper anxieties. The stated objection and the real barrier are almost never the same thing.

**Executable Behavior**: When someone objects, don't address the objection directly. Instead, look beneath it: "That sounds important. What are you most worried about if you did move forward?" The anxiety underneath the objection is three layers deeper and requires emotional validation, not logical rebuttal.

**Deploy When**: Someone keeps generating new objections after you address each one — classic sign the real issue is emotional, not logical.

**Success Metric**: You find the root anxiety (usually identity threat, social risk, or fear of failure) beneath the surface objection.

---

## 9. The Production/Evaluation Asymmetry
**What They Do**: Exploit the fact that people produce arguments for their OWN positions lazily but evaluate arguments from OTHERS carefully and critically.

**Executable Behavior**: Never make claims about your product yourself. Instead, get customers to make the claims. The exact same statement ("This paid for itself in 3 months") is dismissed when you say it and believed when a customer says it. Strategically design social proof so customers make every claim you wish you could make.

**Deploy When**: Designing testimonials, social proof, case studies, or review strategies.

**Success Metric**: Every key claim about your product is made by a customer voice, not brand voice.

---

## 10. Environment-First Behavior Change
**What They Do**: Change behavior by changing the environment rather than trying to change the person. The environment predicts behavior better than personality, values, or intentions.

**Executable Behavior**: Instead of persuading someone to act differently, redesign the context: change the default option, alter the physical environment, modify social cues, adjust timing. The best intervention is one the person never notices because the environment makes the desired behavior the path of least resistance.

**Deploy When**: Trying to change behavior where motivation or willpower approaches have failed.

**Success Metric**: The desired behavior happens without the person needing to "decide" to do it.

---

## 11. Face-Saving Narrative Design
**What They Do**: Provide people with a story they can tell themselves and others about WHY they changed — one that preserves dignity, status, and self-image.

**Executable Behavior**: Before asking for any significant change, pre-build the narrative: "I used to think X. Then I realized Y. Now I understand Z." The shift from X to Z must feel like discovery, not defeat. The pivot point ("Then I realized") must be something that sounds like growth, not being wrong.

**Deploy When**: Any change that requires someone to publicly reverse a previously stated position.

**Success Metric**: The person can articulate WHY they changed in a way that makes them sound *smarter* for having changed.

---

## 12. The 4-Layer Problem Architecture
**What They Do**: Diagnose problems at four distinct layers — Surface (what they SAY is wrong), Emotional (what they FEEL), Identity (what it means about WHO they are), Social (what belonging/status is at stake) — and intervene at the root layer.

**Executable Behavior**: For any stated problem, drill through all four layers. The surface problem is rarely the real problem. Most interventions fail because they solve the stated problem while ignoring the emotional, identity, or social layer underneath. The root cause is almost always at Layer 3 or 4.

**Deploy When**: Your solution addresses the surface problem but doesn't create change — the root is deeper.

**Success Metric**: Your intervention addresses the layer where the problem actually lives, not just the symptom.

---

## 13. The Threshold Equation
**What They Do**: Calculate the exact tipping point for change using a quantifiable equation: (Trigger + Trusted Source + Social Safety + Identity Bridge) must exceed (Investment + Social Risk + Identity Threat + Uncertainty).

**Executable Behavior**: Score each variable 1-10. Calculate both sides. Identify the binding constraint — the single variable that, if changed, would tip the balance. Design interventions that target the binding constraint specifically, not the whole equation.

**Deploy When**: Planning any persuasion strategy where you need to identify the minimum viable intervention.

**Success Metric**: You can name the single thing that must change for someone to cross the threshold.

---

## 14. Deep Canvassing Intelligence Sequencing
**What They Do**: Run audience research in a specific 5-prompt sequence — Excavation → Processing Chain → Root System → Belief Mapping → Threshold Calculation — where each phase's output feeds the next.

**Executable Behavior**: Never skip steps or run them out of order. Excavation produces raw intelligence. Processing Chain reveals decision architecture. Root System finds the real problem. Belief Mapping categorizes changeability. Threshold Calculation produces the intervention blueprint. The compound insight exceeds any individual analysis by 10x.

**Deploy When**: Entering a new market, launching a new offer, or when superficial audience research isn't producing results.

**Success Metric**: You produce a complete transformation blueprint that specifies exact conditions for change.

---

## 15. The Trust-Before-Persuasion Sequence
**What They Do**: Establish genuine trust BEFORE any persuasion attempt, because post-trust persuasion works entirely differently from pre-trust persuasion.

**Executable Behavior**: Start with accurate empathy (proving you understand their situation). Follow with honest acknowledgment of what DOESN'T work. Only then introduce what does work. The trust earned by honest critique of your own space creates more conversion than any number of benefits.

**Deploy When**: Markets with high skepticism, burned audiences, or commoditized offers.

**Success Metric**: Your prospect describes you as "the first honest one" or "different from everyone else."

---

## 16. Staged Information Delivery
**What They Do**: Sequence information across multiple touchpoints so each piece builds on the previous one, creating cumulative belief shift rather than attempting single-exposure persuasion.

**Executable Behavior**: Map the belief journey from current state to desired state. Identify the specific beliefs that need to shift in sequence. Design content where each piece shifts exactly one belief and creates readiness for the next shift. Never try to shift more than one belief per exposure.

**Deploy When**: The total belief shift required is too large for a single piece of content, conversation, or touchpoint.

**Success Metric**: Each stage has a specific, measurable belief shift goal and the sequence produces compound belief change.

---

## 17. Emotional Sediment Mapping
**What They Do**: Map not just what someone believes but what emotional experiences created those beliefs — the sediment layers of past experiences that formed the current belief structure.

**Executable Behavior**: For every belief you identify, ask: "What happened to them that made this feel true?" Map the emotional events — past failures, social rejections, authority figures, formative experiences — that cemented the belief. Copy that speaks to the sediment creates instant resonance; copy that argues against the conclusion creates resistance.

**Deploy When**: Writing copy, designing content, or planning conversations for an audience whose beliefs are deeply held.

**Success Metric**: Your messaging creates the "they get me" response rather than the "prove it" response.

---

## 18. The Minimum Viable Change Path
**What They Do**: Identify the smallest possible intervention that could create a belief shift, rather than designing elaborate campaigns when a single reframe might suffice.

**Executable Behavior**: After mapping the full threshold equation, ask: "What is the single sentence, experience, or proof point that would tip this?" Sometimes it's one peer endorsement. Sometimes it's one reframe. Sometimes it's one undeniable data point. Find the one thing, not the ten things.

**Deploy When**: You've done complete analysis and need to identify the highest-leverage intervention.

**Success Metric**: You can state the minimum viable intervention in one sentence.

---

## 19. The Tribal Positioning Inversion
**What They Do**: Instead of positioning against competitors, position the customer INTO a tribe identity they want to inhabit. The product becomes the membership card, not the solution.

**Executable Behavior**: Identify the aspirational identity your audience wants. Define what "people like us" do. Position your product as what members of that tribe naturally use. The purchase becomes identity expression rather than problem-solving — and identity purchases face far less price resistance.

**Deploy When**: Your market is commoditized on features, your audience is identity-driven, or price sensitivity is high.

**Success Metric**: Customers describe buying as "this is what people like me use" rather than "this solves my problem."

---

## 20. Cognitive Dissonance Engineering
**What They Do**: Deliberately create experiences where someone's current mental model CANNOT accommodate new information — forcing accommodation (genuine restructuring) rather than assimilation (fitting new info into old models).

**Executable Behavior**: Identify what experience would be impossible to explain under their current worldview. Deliver or simulate that experience. Then provide the new framework that resolves the dissonance. The key is the dissonance must be genuine — contrived dissonance triggers defensiveness, not accommodation.

**Deploy When**: Information-based persuasion has failed and genuine paradigm shift is required.

**Success Metric**: The person says something like "I can't explain that with what I used to think" before naturally reaching for the new framework.

---

## 21. The Permission Architecture
**What They Do**: Systematically identify and provide every form of "permission" someone needs before they can change — self-permission, social permission, authority permission, peer permission.

**Executable Behavior**: Map all permission requirements: Do they need to give themselves permission? Do they need a respected figure to say it's okay? Do they need to see peers doing it first? Do they need data that justifies the change? Design interventions that provide each required permission in the right sequence.

**Deploy When**: Someone agrees with the logic of change but can't seem to take the step — permission gap, not information gap.

**Success Metric**: Each required permission has been explicitly addressed and the person describes feeling "free" to choose.

---

## 22. The Evaluation Mode Trigger
**What They Do**: Shift people from production mode (generating biased defenses of their position) to evaluation mode (critically examining arguments from both sides) — because genuine attitude change only happens in evaluation mode.

**Executable Behavior**: Use open-ended questions that invite reflection rather than debate. "How did you come to feel that way?" puts someone in evaluation mode. "Here's why you're wrong" puts them in production mode. The question format, not the content, determines which cognitive mode activates.

**Deploy When**: Any persuasion scenario where the person is defending their position rather than considering yours.

**Success Metric**: The person begins exploring their own reasoning rather than defending their conclusion.

---

## 23. The Sunk-Cost Awareness Technique
**What They Do**: Make the emotional investment in current beliefs visible and name it, so the person can separate "what I've invested in this belief" from "what's actually true."

**Executable Behavior**: Gently surface the investment: "You've believed this for 20 years and built relationships around it. That's a lot invested. If there were new information that changed things, would the investment make it harder to see?" This isn't attacking the belief — it's naming the psychological mechanics that protect it from update.

**Deploy When**: Someone holds a belief primarily because of how long they've held it or how much they've sacrificed for it.

**Success Metric**: The person acknowledges that their investment in the belief might be influencing their evaluation of evidence.
