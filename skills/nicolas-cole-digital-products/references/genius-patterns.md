# Nicolas Cole — Genius Patterns (Digital Product Strategy)

## Pattern 1: The Vehicle Framework (Know What Game You're Playing)

**What They Do Unconsciously**: Before evaluating any product, Cole classifies which *vehicle type* it belongs to. He never judges a product in isolation — only within its category's rules.

**Executable Behavior**: Classify every product idea into one of 6 vehicles before making ANY decisions about content, pricing, marketing, or launch strategy. Each vehicle has its own rules, price ranges, and customer expectations. Playing by the wrong vehicle's rules is the #1 cause of product failure.

**Deployment Context**: Product planning, pricing decisions, launch strategy, "what should I build next?" decisions

**Success Metric**: Customer expectations perfectly match the vehicle you chose. No mismatch between what you deliver and what they expect.

---

## Pattern 2: Context Is the Multiplier

**What They Do Unconsciously**: Cole repackages identical content by changing its *context* — an ebook on Amazon ($9) vs. the same ebook self-hosted with 3 videos ($49-99). Same words, 5-10x price difference.

**Executable Behavior**: For any core content asset, systematically map it across 5+ contexts: marketplace listing, self-hosted product, lead magnet, bonus/bundle inclusion, live delivery. Each context creates a distinct product at a different price point. This is not lazy — this is the business model.

**Deployment Context**: Content repurposing, pricing strategy, product line expansion, "how do I make more money from what I already have?"

**Success Metric**: Single core IP is monetized at 3+ price points simultaneously through context changes.

---

## Pattern 3: Standalone Before Recurring

**What They Do Unconsciously**: Cole never recommends recurring products (paid newsletters, communities) to people who haven't proven standalone product-market fit first.

**Executable Behavior**: Before launching any recurring product, prove you can sell a one-time purchase product in the same topic. If you can't get someone to pay $49 once, what makes you think they'll pay $20/month forever? The standalone validates your value proposition; the recurring version is Level 2.

**Deployment Context**: Product roadmap planning, "should I start a paid newsletter?" decisions, subscription model launch timing

**Success Metric**: Successful standalone sales (any volume) before recurring model launch.

---

## Pattern 4: The $350 Purchasing Decision Threshold

**What They Do Unconsciously**: Cole prices all products relative to a validated threshold — $350 — where the customer's purchasing psychology fundamentally shifts from "impulse/utility buy" to "considered experience purchase."

**Executable Behavior**: Below $350: price toward the TOP of the range because higher price = more total revenue (counterintuitive but empirically proven across 22+ cohorts). Above $350: you MUST deliver an experience, not just information — live components, accountability, interaction.

**Deployment Context**: Pricing any digital product, launch strategy, product tier planning

**Success Metric**: Products below $350 priced at range ceiling with solid conversion. Products above $350 include experiential components justifying the price.

---

## Pattern 5: The Level-Up Pair (Async → Live Double Monetization)

**What They Do Unconsciously**: Cole treats every asynchronous product as having a natural live counterpart at a higher price tier. He builds async first (the forcing function to crystallize thinking), then offers the live version as a separate revenue stream.

**Executable Behavior**: When building any digital product, immediately plan its live equivalent. Course → Live Cohort. Ebook → Workshop. Template bundle → Coaching session. The content is the same; the delivery modality is different; the price is higher. This is the most capital-efficient way to expand a product line.

**Deployment Context**: Product line expansion, revenue multiplication, "how do I make more from existing products?"

**Success Metric**: Same core content generating revenue from both async and live channels.

---

## Pattern 6: Expectation Engineering

**What They Do Unconsciously**: Cole maps precise customer expectations to each price tier. A $99/month community member expects: (1) curated information (courses + mini-courses), (2) live interaction, (3) Q&A access, (4) peer networking. Miss any single expectation = churn.

**Executable Behavior**: Before launching at any price point, inventory ALL expectations customers hold at that tier. Design the product to satisfy every expectation — not just the content. Price reinforces vehicle → vehicle sets expectations → expectations must be met.

**Deployment Context**: Product design, churn prevention, customer satisfaction, community planning

**Success Metric**: Zero customer complaints about mismatched expectations. Churn driven by lifecycle completion, not disappointment.

---

## Pattern 7: The Problem-Completion Test

**What They Do Unconsciously**: Cole measures product quality not by length, volume, or production value — but by whether the customer can DO THE THING after completing it.

**Executable Behavior**: Define the ONE specific outcome your product delivers. Build ONLY what's necessary to get the customer from "can't do the thing" to "did the thing." An abandoned-cart email setup guide is done when the customer has a working sequence — not when they've watched 50 videos. Length is irrelevant; completion is everything.

**Deployment Context**: Product scoping, curriculum design, "how long should my course be?" decisions, content trimming

**Success Metric**: Customer can articulate exactly what they built/achieved after completing your product — and it matches what you promised.

---

## Pattern 8: Community as Culmination (Not Starting Point)

**What They Do Unconsciously**: Cole reveals communities are the *evergreen version of everything before them*. A community bundles: (a) low-ticket content library, (b) expanded courses, (c) live cohort elements, (d) peer networking — running perpetually.

**Executable Behavior**: Only launch a community AFTER you've validated standalone products, built complete courses, and run live cohorts. A community that tries to start from scratch (without existing products to populate it) will struggle. The content library, live cadence, and support structures should already exist from prior vehicles.

**Deployment Context**: "Should I start a community?" decisions, School/Circle/Skool platform planning, community retention strategy

**Success Metric**: Community delivers all 4 pillars (information, live, Q&A, network) from Day 1 because they were built and validated in prior vehicle stages.
