## EXECUTION PROTOCOL

### Phase 1: STRATEGIC FOUNDATION

**The Business Problem:**
What business problem does this creative need to solve?
- Awareness? (They don't know we exist)
- Consideration? (They know us but haven't decided)
- Conversion? (They're ready but need a push)
- Retention? (They're customers but not advocates)

**The Audience Insight:**
What's happening inside your audience's head?
- What do they currently believe?
- What do they currently feel?
- What do they want to believe/feel?
- What's the gap this creative must bridge?

**The Transformation:**
Before seeing this creative, they [state].
After seeing this creative, they [transformed state].

This transformation defines success. All creative choices serve it.

### Phase 2: MESSAGE ARCHITECTURE

**The Hierarchy:**
1. **Primary Message**: The ONE thing they must remember
2. **Secondary Message**: Supporting proof or context
3. **Tertiary Details**: Nice-to-have information

**The Tone:**
How should this feel? Not what it says—how it LANDS.
- Confident or humble?
- Bold or understated?
- Warm or authoritative?
- Playful or serious?

**The Call to Action:**
What specifically should they do after engaging with this creative?

### Phase 3: VISUAL DIRECTION

**Mood & Atmosphere:**
Describe the emotional quality of the visuals:
- Environmental: "Like a quiet morning in a clean, modern apartment"
- Emotional: "The feeling of finally understanding something you've struggled with"
- Reference: "The visual confidence of Apple meets the warmth of Mailchimp"

**Color Psychology:**
- Primary palette purpose (what emotional territory?)
- Secondary accents (what should draw attention?)
- What to avoid (colors that send wrong signals)

**Typography Direction:**
- Headlines: What personality should the type convey?
- Body: Readability priorities and style
- Hierarchy: How should type guide the eye?

**Imagery Guidelines:**
- Photography style (if applicable)
- Illustration style (if applicable)
- Iconography approach
- What imagery to avoid

**Layout Principles:**
- Density: Minimal/breathing vs. rich/layered
- Structure: Asymmetric/dynamic vs. grid/organized
- Flow: Where should the eye travel?

### Phase 4: PLATFORM CONSIDERATIONS

**Format Requirements:**
- Dimensions and specifications
- Mobile vs. desktop priorities
- Load time considerations
- Accessibility requirements

**Platform Psychology:**
- How do people engage on this platform?
- What's the attention span?
- What scroll/interaction patterns exist?
- What works visually in this environment?

### Phase 5: EXECUTION GUIDANCE

**Must-Haves:**
- Non-negotiable elements
- Required brand elements
- Mandatory legal/compliance items

**Nice-to-Haves:**
- Elements to include if possible
- Stretch goals

**Explicit Restrictions:**
- What NOT to do
- What to avoid
- Common mistakes to prevent

**Reference & Inspiration:**
- Specific examples that hit the right tone
- What to borrow from each reference
- What to avoid from each reference

# CREATIVE BRIEF: AUDIENCE BUILDING COURSE LANDING PAGE

## Strategic Foundation

### Business Problem
We have an email list of 8,000 aspiring creators who downloaded a free guide. They know us, they trust our content, but they haven't converted to paying customers. This landing page needs to bridge the gap between "free follower" and "course student" for a $497 investment.

### Target Audience Profile

**Who they are:**
- 25-45 years old, professionals or side-hustlers
- Want to build a personal brand/content business
- Have tried before, struggled with consistency
- Burned out on "post every day" advice
- Skeptical of guru promises but still hopeful

**Where they are emotionally:**
- Frustrated: "I've tried this before and it didn't work"
- Skeptical: "Why would this be different?"
- Time-starved: "I don't have hours for content every day"
- Hopeful: "Maybe there's a better way"

**What they need to feel:**
- Understood: "This person gets my struggle"
- Relieved: "I don't have to be a content machine"
- Confident: "This approach actually works for real people"
- Capable: "I can do this even with my schedule"

### The Transformation

**Before:** Overwhelmed by content advice, burned out trying to keep up, skeptical that audience building is possible for "regular people"

**After:** Clear on a specific system that doesn't require daily posting, confident they can execute it, excited to start building strategically

---

## Message Architecture

### Primary Message
**"Build your audience in 3 posts per week—without burning out."**

This is the counter-positioning. Everyone says post daily. We say post smart.

### Secondary Messages
1. "The system used by 500+ creators who grew while keeping their sanity"
2. "From the creator who went 0 to 50K followers posting 3x/week"
3. "Everything you need to grow—without becoming a content machine"

### Message Hierarchy (Page Flow)
1. **Hero**: The core promise (3 posts/week system)
2. **Problem**: Why the "post every day" advice is burning people out
3. **Solution**: The Audience Flywheel methodology (mechanism introduction)
4. **Proof**: Case studies + testimonials
5. **What's Inside**: Course curriculum breakdown
6. **Investment**: Price with value framing
7. **Guarantee**: Risk reversal
8. **Final CTA**: Clear enrollment path

### Tone of Voice
- **Confident but not arrogant**: We know this works, but we're not above you
- **Warm but not soft**: Friendly, approachable, but clear-eyed about what it takes
- **Anti-hustle but not anti-ambition**: We respect the goal, reject the grind
- **Direct but not aggressive**: Say what we mean, no hard sell tactics

### Call to Action
Primary: "Enroll Now" / "Join the Course"
Secondary: "Watch the Free Training" (for those not ready)

## Visual Direction

### Mood & Atmosphere

**The feeling we want to create:**
Imagine walking into a bright, organized creative workspace. There are plants. Natural light. A sense of calm productivity. This is a space where good work happens—but nobody's panicking.

This is NOT:
- The hustle-culture aesthetic (dark, aggressive, "grind" energy)
- The ultra-minimalist tech aesthetic (cold, sterile, impersonal)
- The overly playful startup aesthetic (immature, not serious)

**Reference feeling:** The visual confidence of Notion's marketing meets the warmth of Headspace.

### Color Direction

**Primary Palette:**
- Warm white/cream (#F9F7F4) — The breathing space
- Soft navy (#2B3A4D) — The grounding authority
- Warm amber accent (#E8B86D) — The inviting highlight

**Color Psychology:**
We want to feel calm, trustworthy, and optimistic. The palette should reassure, not excite. People are coming from burnout; we don't add visual stimulation. We add visual relief.

**Avoid:**
- Aggressive reds or oranges (too high-energy)
- Pure black backgrounds (too harsh)
- Neon accents (too startup-y)
- Gradient overload (too trendy)

### Typography Direction

**Headlines:**
- Modern sans-serif with personality (think: confident, not cold)
- Slightly rounded edges convey approachability
- Options to explore: Satoshi, Cabinet Grotesk, or similar

**Body:**
- Highly readable sans-serif
- Generous line height (1.6-1.8)
- Comfortable measure width (50-70 characters)

**Hierarchy Guidance:**
Headlines should have room to breathe. Don't stack text too densely. Let the eye rest. Use size, weight, and spacing (not just color) to create hierarchy.

### Imagery Guidelines

**Photography (if used):**
- Real people in natural settings (not stock-photo staged)
- Warm lighting, soft shadows
- Showing people thinking, working calmly (not "CEO at desk" or "victory pose")
- Diversity in representation

**Illustrations (alternative):**
- Abstract shapes and lines (organic, not geometric)
- Use to break up text, add visual interest
- Light touch—decorative, not distracting

**Avoid:**
- Stock photos of people pointing at laptops
- Overly posed "success" imagery
- Screenshots/UI graphics in hero area
- Busy background textures

### Layout Principles

**Density:**
Generous whitespace throughout. This page should breathe. We're counter-positioning against "hustle"—the design should feel unhurried.

**Mobile Priority:**
60%+ traffic will be mobile. Every element should be designed mobile-first, then enhanced for desktop.

**Flow:**
Single-column primary content. Eye should flow naturally downward. Each section should be visually distinct but part of one cohesive journey.

**Social Proof Placement:**
Integrate testimonials throughout the page, not just in one "testimonials section." Let proof appear when doubt would naturally arise.

## Must-Haves

**Non-Negotiable Elements:**
- Logo in header
- Clear "Enroll Now" CTA visible without scrolling (desktop)
- At least 3 testimonials with photos and specific results
- Price clearly stated (not hidden)
- Guarantee/refund policy visible
- Course curriculum breakdown
- FAQ section addressing common objections

### Nice-to-Haves
- Video testimonials embedded
- "As seen in" media logos (if applicable)
- Countdown timer if there's a deadline
- Comparison table (before/after or versus competitors)

### Explicit Restrictions

**Do NOT:**
- Use dark/black backgrounds anywhere
- Use stock photography of people
- Include more than 2 accent colors
- Write paragraphs longer than 4 lines
- Use fake urgency ("Only 3 spots left!") if not true
- Make mobile text smaller than 16px
- Put critical info below the fold on mobile
- Use carousel/sliders for testimonials (show all)

# CREATIVE BRIEF: AUTOMATION TRAINING AD CAMPAIGN

## Strategic Foundation

### Business Problem
We need top-of-funnel leads for our automation consulting. Target cost per lead: $8-12. Current audience doesn't know we exist. This campaign creates awareness and generates opt-ins for a free training that warms them toward our paid services.

### Target Audience Profile

**Who they are:**
- Service business owners (photographers, consultants, coaches, agencies)
- Revenue: $50K-$300K
- 1-5 person team (often solo)
- Drowning in client communication and admin
- Interested in automation but worried about losing personal touch

**Where they are emotionally:**
- Exhausted: Working late, working weekends
- Frustrated: Same questions over and over
- Curious: "There must be a better way"
- Skeptical: "Automation = robotic/impersonal"

### The Transformation

**Before:** Overwhelmed by admin, skeptical that automation can help a personal service business

**After:** Aware that automation can save time WITHOUT sacrificing client experience, curious enough to watch a free training

## Message Architecture

### Primary Message
**"What if your clients felt MORE taken care of... while you worked LESS?"**

This addresses the key objection: automation = impersonal. We flip it.

### Secondary Messages (Test These)
1. "I saved 45 hours/month on client emails. Here's how."
2. "Stop answering the same questions. Start automating them."
3. "Your clients want great communication. They don't care if you send it manually."
4. "The automation system that made my clients feel MORE valued"

### Call to Action
"Watch the Free Training"
"Learn How in 15 Minutes"
"See How It Works"

## Visual Direction

### Approach
We're creating **6-10 ad variations** across two visual styles:

**Style A: Human-Centric (4-5 variations)**
Focus on the PERSON, not the technology. Show the transformation (stressed → relieved) or the result (freedom, time back).

**Style B: Simple/Direct (4-5 variations)**
Text-forward, minimal visuals. Let the message do the work. Think: bold typography, clean backgrounds, one focal point.

### Style A: Human-Centric

**Photography Direction:**
- Real people (not stock-photo-staged)
- Showing relief, freedom, or ease (not "working at computer")
- Natural environments (home office, coffee shop, outdoors)
- Warm lighting, soft tones

**Visual Story Options:**
1. Person looking relieved/happy, phone in hand, NOT stressed
2. Before/after split (stressed face vs. relaxed face)
3. Person enjoying life (at kid's game, on vacation) with subtle phone presence
4. "Time reclaimed" moment (morning coffee, reading, hobby)

**Text Overlay Approach:**
- Minimal text on image
- Headline beneath image or in card format
- Let the human emotion carry the visual

### Style B: Simple/Direct

**Typography-Forward:**
- Bold statement, clean background
- One strong color accent
- No imagery or abstract shapes only

**Layout:**
- Headline takes up 60%+ of space
- Clear CTA button
- Brand colors but restrained

**Examples:**
- "45 hours back. Same great service." [CTA]
- "What if automation made your service BETTER?" [CTA]

### Color Direction

**Primary:**
- Warm white/cream backgrounds (#FAF8F5)
- Navy or charcoal for text (#2B3A4D)
- Warm amber for CTAs (#E8B86D)

**Avoid:**
- Dark/black backgrounds (feel corporate/tech-heavy)
- Blue-dominant palettes (feel robotic)
- Neon or high-saturation accents (feel aggressive)

## Testing Strategy

**Phase 1: Message Testing**
Run 4 primary messages against same visual to find winning message.

**Phase 2: Visual Testing**
Take winning message, test against human-centric vs. text-forward visuals.

**Phase 3: Refinement**
Combine winning message + winning visual, test variations.
